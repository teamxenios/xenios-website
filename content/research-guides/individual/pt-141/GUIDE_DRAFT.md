# PT-141 (bremelanotide)

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## One-minute summary

PT-141 is unusual among the compounds covered in this series. Most of them have almost no human
evidence behind them. This one does. PT-141 is the development code name for bremelanotide, which
is the active ingredient in an FDA-approved prescription medicine called VYLEESI. That approval is
real, and the trials behind it were real trials.

The honest verdict, though, turns on a distinction that most marketing about PT-141 leaves out.

- **The approval is narrow.** According to the FDA-approved label retrieved on 2026-07-19,
  bremelanotide is approved for premenopausal women with acquired, generalized hypoactive sexual
  desire disorder. The same label states, in its own Limitations of Use section, that the product
  is not indicated in postmenopausal women, not indicated in men, and not indicated to enhance
  sexual performance. Claim grade: **A (Established)**.
- **The effect in the approved population is real but modest.** Two large randomized trials both
  beat placebo on their main measures. The size of the average improvement was roughly 0.3 to 0.4
  points on the scales used, and whether a change of that size is meaningful to a person's life is
  genuinely contested. Claim grade: **B (Supported human evidence)**.
- **Side effects were common and led people to stop.** The label reports nausea in 40 percent of
  patients, anti-nausea treatment required in 13 percent, and 8 percent stopping the drug because
  of it. In the year-long follow-on study, fewer than 40 percent of those who entered it finished
  it. Claim grade: **B (Supported human evidence)**.
- **There is a cardiovascular contraindication.** The label lists uncontrolled high blood pressure
  or known cardiovascular disease as the reason not to use it at all. Claim grade: **A (Established)**.
- **For men, sound human evidence is effectively absent.** The published human trials of
  bremelanotide in men used an earlier nasal-spray formulation that was never approved and whose
  development was discontinued. The larger of those two trials carries a formal Expression of
  Concern published in 2023, meaning its integrity has been publicly questioned. The smaller one
  involved 19 people and was a safety study. Claim grade for benefit in men: **G (Unverified)**.
- **What vendors sell as "PT-141" is not the approved medicine.** VYLEESI is a specific approved
  product with an FDA-reviewed identity, purity standard, and manufacturing standard. Material
  labelled "PT-141" by an unregulated seller has none of those and has not been reviewed by any
  regulator for what it contains.
- **We could not verify several regulatory questions**, including anti-doping status and whether
  any enforcement action has been taken against sellers. Those are logged as unknowns below rather
  than guessed at.

Xenios does not sell PT-141 or bremelanotide, does not supply it, and does not tell members how to
obtain or use it. This Guide exists so that a member who encounters PT-141 elsewhere has an
accurate account of what is known, what is not, and where the marketing and the evidence part ways.

## What it is

PT-141 is the research and development code name for a substance whose approved name is
**bremelanotide**.

Bremelanotide is a **peptide**, which means a short chain of amino acids (the building blocks that
also make up proteins). It is made in a laboratory rather than extracted from a plant or animal. Its
structure is cyclic, meaning the chain is joined into a ring rather than left as a straight strand.

It acts on a family of cell-surface targets called **melanocortin receptors**. According to the
FDA-approved label retrieved on 2026-07-19, bremelanotide activates several of these receptor types
rather than just one, including the receptors known as MC1R and MC4R.

Bremelanotide is the active ingredient in **VYLEESI**, an FDA-approved prescription injectable
medicine. It is a prescription drug. It is not a dietary supplement, and describing it as a
"research chemical" is not an accurate description of what it is when a person uses it.

**Things it is commonly confused with.** Bremelanotide was structurally derived from a different
compound called melanotan II, which is not an approved medicine. It is also frequently confused
with afamelanotide, which is a different substance again. These are not interchangeable, and
evidence about one is not evidence about another.

**The distinction that matters most.** "PT-141" and "VYLEESI" are not the same thing in practice.
They refer to the same molecule, but only one of them is a regulated, reviewed pharmaceutical
product. This point runs through the whole Guide.

## Why interest exists

Interest in PT-141 comes from three places. Describing them is not endorsing them.

**It is genuinely approved, and that is rare in this category.** Most compounds discussed in peptide
communities have no approved product anywhere. Bremelanotide does. That fact gets used, accurately
in one sense and misleadingly in another, as a credibility marker.

**It works on a different pathway from the familiar erectile-dysfunction medicines.** The widely
repeated explanation is that it acts on brain circuitry involved in desire rather than on blood flow
to the genitals, which is the route used by the drug class called PDE5 inhibitors (the class that
includes sildenafil). That framing makes it sound like an answer to a problem the existing drugs do
not address, namely low desire rather than mechanical difficulty. Whether that explanation is
correct is covered in the Proposed mechanism section, and the short version is that the FDA label
itself says the mechanism is unknown.

**It is heavily marketed for uses the approval does not cover.** A large amount of the online
interest in PT-141 is about male sexual performance. That is outside the approved indication, and
the label says so explicitly. Interest in that use is real. Evidence supporting it is not.

## Terminology

- **Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids, the same building blocks that make up proteins.
- **Receptor.** A structure on or in a cell that a substance binds to in order to produce an effect.
- **Agonist.** A substance that binds to a receptor and switches it on, as opposed to blocking it.
- **Melanocortin receptors (including MC1R and MC4R).** A family of receptors found in several
  tissues. MC1R sits on pigment-producing skin cells. MC4R is found in the brain, among other places.
- **Melanocytes.** The cells in skin that produce pigment.
- **Hyperpigmentation.** Darkening of an area of skin. "Focal" means it happens in patches rather
  than all over.
- **Hypoactive sexual desire disorder (HSDD).** A formal diagnosis of persistently low sexual desire
  that causes the person marked distress or difficulty in a relationship. The approved label adds
  two qualifiers: **acquired**, meaning the person previously did not have this problem, and
  **generalized**, meaning it happens regardless of the situation, the type of stimulation, or the
  partner. The label's definition also excludes cases caused by another medical or psychiatric
  condition, by problems within the relationship, or by the effects of another medicine or substance.
- **Premenopausal and postmenopausal.** Before and after menopause. The approved indication covers
  the first group only.
- **Randomized controlled trial.** A study where participants are assigned by chance to receive the
  drug or a comparison, which is what allows a fair comparison between groups.
- **Placebo.** An inactive substance given for comparison, so that the effect of expectation can be
  separated from the effect of the drug.
- **Double-blind.** Neither the participants nor the researchers know who is receiving the drug.
- **Phase 3 trial.** A large late-stage trial intended to establish whether a drug works well enough,
  and is tolerable enough, to be approved.
- **Co-primary endpoints.** The two main outcomes a trial commits in advance to measuring. Both
  normally have to succeed for the trial to be considered positive.
- **P value.** A statistical measure of how likely a result of that size would be if the drug did
  nothing. A small P value means the result is unlikely to be chance alone. It says nothing about
  whether the size of the effect matters to a person's life.
- **Open-label extension.** A continuation study in which everyone knows they are receiving the drug
  and there is no placebo comparison. Results from this design cannot be read as placebo-adjusted.
- **Attrition.** The proportion of participants who leave a study before it ends.
- **Contraindication.** A situation in which a medicine should not be used at all.
- **Expression of Concern.** A formal notice published by a journal or indexing body signalling that
  the reliability of a published study is in question.
- **Industry-sponsored.** Funded by the company that stands to benefit from a positive result. This
  does not automatically make a study wrong, and it is a limitation a reader should hold in mind.
- **Off-label.** Use of an approved medicine for a purpose, population, or condition that the
  approval does not cover.
- **Ambulatory blood pressure monitoring.** Repeated automated blood pressure readings taken over a
  full day while a person goes about normal life, rather than a single reading in a clinic.

## Proposed mechanism

**This section describes a proposal, not an established fact.** The most important sentence in it
comes from the FDA-approved label itself.

**What the label states.** According to the FDA-approved prescribing information retrieved on
2026-07-19, bremelanotide is a melanocortin receptor agonist that activates several melanocortin
receptor subtypes, with binding at MC1R and MC4R. The same label states directly that **the
mechanism by which bremelanotide improves hypoactive sexual desire disorder is unknown**. That is
the regulator's own position, not a Xenios interpretation. Claim grade: **A (Established)** for the
statement that the therapeutic mechanism is unknown.

**One mechanism the label does describe, and it is not a therapeutic one.** The label describes
binding at MC1R on pigment-producing skin cells as increasing pigmentation. That is offered as the
explanation for an observed side effect, skin darkening in patches, not as an explanation of any
benefit. Claim grade: **B (Supported human evidence)** for the association between the compound and
focal hyperpigmentation in the trial population.

**The commonly repeated explanation, and its status.** Vendor pages and clinic pages generally
explain PT-141 as acting centrally, on MC4R receptors in a brain region called the hypothalamus, to
influence dopamine-related pathways involved in sexual desire, in contrast to the blood-flow pathway
used by PDE5 inhibitors. That explanation is a hypothesis, and it is largely derived from animal
work. [UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check] applies to the specific
central dopamine pathway claim. No primary source establishing it was retrieved during this
research session, and no animal study supporting it was retrieved either, so this Guide reports no
species-specific finding for it. Claim grade: **G (Unverified)**.

**A point that is easy to misread.** The proposed mechanism is not, in principle, specific to one
sex. That is a statement about biology in the abstract. It is not evidence of benefit in men, and it
should not be treated as a substitute for evidence in men. It is quite common for a plausible
mechanism to fail to produce a demonstrated clinical effect.

## Human evidence

Human evidence for bremelanotide exists and is, in the approved population, reasonably strong by the
standards of this Guide series. All of the efficacy evidence below is industry-sponsored.

### In premenopausal women with HSDD, the approved population

**RECONNECT, two phase 3 randomized controlled trials (PMID 31599840).** Two identically designed
phase 3, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled multicentre trials with a 24-week
double-blind core phase, published in Obstetrics and Gynecology in November 2019 (Kingsberg SA,
Clayton AH, Portman D, Williams LA, Krop J, Jordan R, Lucas J, Simon JA; DOI
10.1097/AOG.0000000000003500; registrations NCT02333071 and NCT02338960). 1,267 premenopausal women
with HSDD were randomized, with 1,247 in the safety analysis and 1,202 in the efficacy analysis.
Mean age was 39 years, 85.6 percent of participants were white, and 96.6 percent were enrolled at
United States sites.

The two co-primary endpoints were change in a sexual desire score and change in a single
desire-related distress item. In the first study, desire improved by 0.30 (P less than .001) and
distress fell by 0.37 (P less than .001) relative to placebo. In the second, desire improved by 0.42
(P less than .001) and distress fell by 0.29 (P equals .005). Combining both studies, desire improved
by 0.35 (P less than .001) and distress fell by 0.33 (P less than .001). All of these differences
against placebo were statistically significant. Nausea, flushing, and headache each occurred in 10
percent or more of the bremelanotide group in both studies, more often than in the placebo group.

Claim grade: **B (Supported human evidence)** for a small average improvement in reported sexual
desire and desire-related distress, compared with placebo, in premenopausal women with a formal
HSDD diagnosis.

*Limitations.* The absolute effect sizes are small on the underlying scales, and whether a change of
roughly 0.3 to 0.4 points is clinically meaningful is contested in the sexual medicine literature.
The trials were sponsored by Palatin Technologies, which developed the drug. The population was
narrow and not diverse: premenopausal women with a formal diagnosis, overwhelmingly white and
United States based. These results say nothing about men, nothing about postmenopausal women, and
nothing about use for sexual performance. Adverse events were common.

**The 52-week open-label extension (PMID 31599847).** A 52-week open-label extension of RECONNECT,
published in Obstetrics and Gynecology in 2019 (Simon JA et al; DOI 10.1097/AOG.0000000000003514).
Of 856 eligible people who completed the core phase, 684 entered the extension and 272 completed it.
Sustained improvement was reported: a desire-score change of 1.25 to 1.30 in those who continued
bremelanotide, and 0.70 to 0.77 in those who crossed over from placebo, with distress-score changes
of -1.4 to -1.7 and -0.9 respectively. The most common adverse events reported were nausea at 40.4
percent, flushing at 20.6 percent, and headache at 12.0 percent. The authors reported no new safety
signals, and severe nausea was the only severe treatment-emergent adverse event affecting more than
one participant.

Claim grade: **C (Early human evidence)** for sustained improvement over a year, because this design
cannot support a stronger grade.

*Limitations.* This was open-label, so there was no placebo group and no blinding. The figures above
cannot be read as placebo-adjusted effects. The dominant signal here is attrition: only 272 of the
684 people who entered the extension completed it, which is 39.8 percent, and only 684 of 856
eligible completers entered it at all. The people who reported sustained benefit are therefore a
self-selected minority who both tolerated the drug and chose to continue. Industry-sponsored.

**The RECONNECT Exit Study (PMID 33538638).** Exit surveys and qualitative telephone interviews
conducted at the end of the double-blind phase, published in the Journal of Women's Health in April
2021 (Koochaki et al; DOI 10.1089/jwh.2020.8460). 242 people completed surveys and 80 of those were
interviewed. People who had received bremelanotide described increased feelings of sexual desire and
physical arousal, and improvement in the overall quality of sexual activity within their
relationships. People who had received placebo also described benefits, including a positive
experience of seeking treatment and improved communication with their partner, without the physical
responses the treatment group described.

Claim grade: **C (Early human evidence)** for the patient-reported experience, and this is
self-report rather than an efficacy endpoint.

*Limitations.* Qualitative and self-reported, from a small self-selected subgroup of people who
completed the trial, and subject to recall and social-desirability bias. The fact that the placebo
group also described real benefit is worth sitting with. It indicates that the non-drug component in
this condition, including expectation and the act of seeking help, is substantial.
Industry-sponsored.

### Cardiovascular safety evidence

**Ambulatory blood pressure monitoring study (PMID 27977473).** A randomized, double-blind,
placebo-controlled, parallel-arm trial with 24-hour ambulatory blood pressure monitoring at 15-minute
intervals, published in the Journal of Hypertension (online December 2016, issue April 2017; White
WB, Myers MG, Jordan R, Lucas J; DOI 10.1097/HJH.0000000000001221). 397 premenopausal women with
female sexual dysfunction, who were either normotensive or had controlled high blood pressure, were
randomized. Bremelanotide produced small transient increases in blood pressure alongside a reduction
in heart rate: a maximum mean systolic increase of 3.1 mmHg and diastolic increases of 3.0 to 3.2
mmHg in the first four hours, and heart rate reductions of 4.6 to 6.6 beats per minute over the first
eight hours. Peak increases typically lasted under 15 minutes. 7 percent of participants met
predefined blood pressure criteria for withdrawal, distributed roughly equally across all groups
including placebo.

Claim grade: **B (Supported human evidence)** for a transient blood pressure increase with an
accompanying heart rate reduction, in this population.

*Limitations.* This was a cardiovascular safety and pharmacology study, not an efficacy study. The
critical limitation is who was enrolled: people who were normotensive or whose high blood pressure
was controlled. It therefore does not characterise risk in people with uncontrolled high blood
pressure or established cardiovascular disease, who are exactly the people the approved label says
should not use the product. Women only. Industry-sponsored, with authors including personnel from
the developing company.

### In men

**This is the largest gap between what is marketed and what is known.**

**A 2008 trial in men (PMID 18206919). Treat this as unreliable.** A randomized, double-blind,
placebo-controlled trial of an earlier nasal-spray formulation of bremelanotide, published in the
Journal of Urology in 2008 (Safarinejad MR, Hosseini SY; DOI 10.1016/j.juro.2007.10.063), in 342
married men aged 28 to 59 with erectile dysfunction who had not responded to sildenafil. A positive
clinical response was reported in 33.5 percent of the bremelanotide group against 8.5 percent of the
placebo group (p equals 0.03), with greater reported intercourse satisfaction (p equals 0.03) and
more drug-related adverse effects (p equals 0.01) in the bremelanotide group.

Claim grade: **G (Unverified)**. This should not be presented to anyone as sound evidence, for
several independent reasons. PubMed records an Expression of Concern issued for this study in 2023
(PMID 36626345), so its integrity is formally in question. It studied a nasal-spray formulation that
was never approved and whose development was halted, so it is not evidence about the approved
product. It is a single trial from 2008 in a narrow population of men who had already failed another
treatment. The reported statistics are internally odd, with a 25-percentage-point absolute difference
reported at p equals 0.03.

**A 2005 study (PMID 15833522). Bibliographic record only.** A study of co-administration of a
low-dose nasal formulation of PT-141 with sildenafil in men with erectile dysfunction, published in
Urology in 2005 (Diamond LE, Earle DC, Garcia WD, Spana C). Per the PubMed listing, 19 patients were
evaluated for safety and pharmacological effects. **This Guide records no finding from this study.**
Only the search listing was retrieved during research, not the abstract, so the bibliographic details
are verified and the results are not. Claim grade: **G (Unverified)**.

**The honest summary for men.** No adequately powered modern randomized trial of the approved
product in men was found. The approved label states the product is not indicated in men. The case
for male use rests on an off-label extrapolation from a compromised 2008 trial of a formulation that
was withdrawn. That is the finding, stated plainly.

## Preclinical evidence

**No preclinical studies were retrieved during this research session, and this section is
deliberately empty rather than padded.**

Two reasons. First, the human evidence in the approved population is substantial enough to stand on
its own, so animal data would add nothing to the question of whether the drug works in that group.
Second, the animal work that circulates in discussions of PT-141 is generally used to support a
mechanism that the FDA label itself describes as unknown, which means it cannot settle the question
it is usually cited to settle.

Because no animal or laboratory study was retrieved, this Guide makes no animal or cell-based
mechanistic claim at all. Any statement elsewhere that describes what PT-141 does in the brain,
without naming the species it was observed in, should be treated as unsupported until a source is
produced. Claim grade for any preclinical mechanism claim in this Guide: **not applicable, no
preclinical finding is asserted**.

## Contradictions and disagreement

### Is PT-141 "FDA approved"?

**One position.** Yes. Bremelanotide has an FDA-approved label with an initial United States
approval year of 2019, verified against the label on 2026-07-19.

**The other position.** No. What peptide vendors sell as PT-141 is not an FDA-approved product, and
the population it is most heavily marketed to, men, is explicitly excluded by the approved label's
own Limitations of Use.

**Assessment.** Both statements are true, and this distinction is the single most important fact in
this Guide. Approval attaches to a specific product, made to a specific standard, for a specific
condition, in a specific population. It does not attach to a molecule in the abstract. The approved
product is for premenopausal women with acquired, generalized HSDD. The label states it is not
indicated in postmenopausal women, not indicated in men, and not indicated to enhance sexual
performance. Marketing that cites the 2019 approval in order to sell PT-141 to men for performance
is citing an approval that specifically excludes that use.

### How strong is the efficacy evidence in the approved population?

**One position.** Two adequately powered phase 3 randomized trials with 1,267 participants both met
their co-primary endpoints, with P less than .001 in the combined analysis, and a 52-week extension
reported sustained benefit with no new safety signals.

**The other position.** The absolute effects were small, roughly 0.3 to 0.4 points on the scales
used. Only 39.8 percent of those who entered the extension finished it. Nausea caused 8 percent to
stop. And in the exit study the placebo group also described meaningful benefit.

**Assessment.** These are not really in conflict. They describe the same drug from two angles: an
average effect that is statistically real but modest, a genuine tolerability burden, and a condition
in which expectation contributes a lot. The honest one-line summary is that it is reproducibly
better than placebo, small in size, and many people stop taking it. All of the evidence is
industry-sponsored.

### Is there evidence in men?

**One position.** There are published randomized human trials of bremelanotide in men with erectile
dysfunction, and vendor and clinic pages cite them as support.

**The other position.** Those trials used a nasal-spray formulation that was never approved and was
discontinued. The larger one carries a 2023 Expression of Concern. The smaller had 19 participants.
The approved label states the product is not indicated in men.

**Assessment.** The second position is decisively stronger. The correct finding is that there is no
sound, current, adequately powered human evidence for the approved product in men. That is stated
here plainly rather than softened, because softening it would mislead.

## Limitations

These limitations apply to the Guide as a whole, on top of the study-specific limitations recorded
alongside each study above.

- **All efficacy evidence is industry-sponsored.** Every efficacy trial cited here was funded by the
  company that developed the drug. No independent replication was retrieved.
- **The trial population is narrow.** Premenopausal women with a formal HSDD diagnosis,
  overwhelmingly white, and almost entirely enrolled in the United States. Findings do not
  automatically transfer to other groups.
- **Statistical significance is not the same as personal significance.** The trials establish that
  the average effect is unlikely to be chance. They do not establish that a person would notice a
  change of that size.
- **The extension results are uncontrolled.** They come from an open-label design with heavy
  attrition and cannot be read as evidence of placebo-adjusted long-term benefit.
- **Safety data reflect regulated use of a regulated product.** The adverse-event rates quoted here
  come from trials of a pharmaceutical-grade product, given under supervision, to people screened
  for the contraindication. They do not describe what happens with an unregulated product used
  without screening.
- **Several regulatory questions could not be verified.** FDA's own website blocked automated
  retrieval during this research session, so the approval letter, the compounding lists, and any
  enforcement record could not be checked directly. The label content was obtained through DailyMed,
  which carries the same FDA-authored content.
- **No quality data on vendor-sold material was found from an acceptable source.** See the Quality
  and identity section.
- **This Guide is an AI draft that has not been reviewed by a human.** No claim in it should be
  relied on until the editorial review in this folder is complete.

## Quality and identity

This section is about what is actually in a container labelled "PT-141" from an unregulated seller.
The honest answer is that nobody has established that, and Xenios has not established it either.

**What was searched for, and what was found.** During this research session we looked specifically
for independent analytical data on the purity, identity, and mislabelling rate of peptides sold by
research-chemical vendors. Every result returned was a peptide vendor blog, an affiliate "peptide
guide" site, or a testing laboratory's marketing page. None of those meets the evidence standard for
this Guide series. **No purity figure, mislabelling rate, or counterfeiting statistic is recorded
here.** Specific numbers that circulate in that ecosystem were seen only on such pages and are
deliberately not repeated, because repeating an unverified number lends it credibility it has not
earned. Claim grade for any quantitative product-quality claim: **G (Unverified)**, and none is
made.

**The structural point that does hold, and is sourced.** The only bremelanotide product reviewed by
the FDA is VYLEESI. Material labelled "PT-141" from an unregulated seller has not been assessed by
any regulator for:

- **Identity.** Whether the contents are actually bremelanotide, rather than a related compound, a
  degraded product, or something else entirely.
- **Purity.** What proportion is the intended substance and what else is present.
- **Sterility.** Whether the material is free of living contaminants.
- **Endotoxin content.** Endotoxins are fragments of bacterial cell walls that can provoke a strong
  reaction even when no living bacteria remain.
- **Stability.** Whether the contents remain what the label says over time and under the conditions
  they were shipped and stored in.

**Why this compounds the safety picture rather than sitting beside it.** The warnings on the
approved label, including the cardiovascular contraindication and the skin-darkening signal that the
label says was not confirmed to resolve in everyone, describe a product made to pharmaceutical
standard and used under a prescriber's supervision. An unregulated product carries those same risks
plus unquantified identity and sterility risk on top, and is used by people who have not been
screened for the contraindication.

**A research-integrity note that belongs here.** The trial most often cited on vendor and clinic
pages promoting PT-141 to men (PMID 18206919) carries a 2023 Expression of Concern per its PubMed
record. Pages citing it routinely do not disclose that.

See `QUALITY_AND_DOCUMENTATION.md` in this folder for the full documentation checklist and its
current status.

## Safety categories

**This section lists categories of concern. It is not a reassurance, and it is not complete.** All
items below come from the FDA-approved label retrieved on 2026-07-19 unless stated otherwise, and
they describe a supervised, pharmaceutical-grade product used in the studied population. Nothing
here can be read as a safety statement about an unregulated product or about anyone outside that
population.

- **Cardiovascular contraindication.** The label lists uncontrolled high blood pressure or known
  cardiovascular disease as a contraindication, meaning the product should not be used at all in
  those situations. This is the label's only listed contraindication. It is directly relevant here,
  because people obtaining unapproved "PT-141" online are not screened for it.
- **Blood pressure and heart rate effects.** The label's warnings describe transient increases in
  both systolic and diastolic blood pressure alongside reductions in heart rate after use, typically
  resolving within 12 hours, and states the product is not recommended for people at high
  cardiovascular risk. This is corroborated by the independent ambulatory monitoring trial described
  above.
- **Nausea, common and clinically significant.** The label reports nausea in 40 percent of patients,
  requiring anti-nausea treatment in 13 percent, and causing 8 percent to stop the drug early. The
  open-label extension reported 40.4 percent independently. This is not a minor tolerability
  footnote.
- **Focal hyperpigmentation, possibly not fully reversible.** The label reports patchy skin darkening
  in 1 percent of patients, with a higher incidence in people with darker skin and with more
  frequent use, and states explicitly that resolution after stopping was not confirmed in all
  patients.
- **Other common adverse reactions.** The label's adverse reactions table, for reactions occurring in
  2 percent or more of patients: nausea 40 percent, flushing 20.3 percent, injection site reactions
  13.2 percent, headache 11.3 percent, vomiting 4.8 percent, cough 3.3 percent, fatigue 3.2 percent.
- **Pregnancy and reproductive potential.** The label directs that use be discontinued if pregnancy
  is suspected, notes that animal studies showed fetal harm at high exposures, and advises effective
  contraception during use. There is no data on presence in breast milk or on effects on a
  breastfed infant.
- **Organ impairment and age groups.** The label advises caution in severe kidney impairment and in
  severe liver impairment, and states that safety has not been established in children or in older
  adults.
- **Historical context, unverified.** A tertiary source (Wikipedia) states that FDA halted phase 2
  trials of the earlier nasal formulation in 2007 because of increased blood pressure in trial
  participants, that the developer discontinued that formulation in 2008, and that the compound was
  then reformulated. No FDA or peer-reviewed source for the 2007 hold could be retrieved. This is
  contextually important, because it suggests the cardiovascular signal is intrinsic to the molecule
  rather than incidental to one formulation, and it must be source-checked by a human before it is
  treated as fact. [UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]

**Unregulated products.** No safety category above accounts for contamination, misidentification,
incorrect content, or non-sterility, because those risks are unmeasured for vendor-sold material.

## Interactions

Xenios does not publish interaction guidance, because interaction assessment depends on a person's
full medicine list, health conditions, and history, and belongs with a clinician or pharmacist who
has all of it.

The following are **categories that require professional review** before anyone considers this
compound. They are not a complete list, and the absence of a category here does not mean no
interaction exists.

- **Blood pressure medicines and any diagnosed high blood pressure**, given the label's
  contraindication and its documented blood pressure effects.
- **Heart rate affecting medicines**, given the heart rate reduction documented alongside the blood
  pressure increase.
- **Any established cardiovascular disease**, which is a contraindication on the label, not merely a
  caution.
- **Erectile dysfunction medicines and other treatments in that class**, given the co-administration
  study that was identified but whose findings this Guide does not report.
- **Medicines affected by severe kidney or liver impairment**, given the label's cautions in those
  populations.
- **Pregnancy, planned pregnancy, and breastfeeding**, given the label's direction to discontinue if
  pregnancy is suspected and the absence of breastfeeding data.
- **Anti-nausea medicines**, since nausea was frequent enough that a substantial minority of trial
  participants required treatment for it.
- **Any psychiatric medicine or condition**, since the approved diagnosis explicitly excludes low
  desire caused by another psychiatric condition or by the effects of another medicine, which means
  correct assessment requires a clinician.

## Regulatory status

All statements below were checked on **2026-07-19**. Regulatory status changes. Anything here must
be re-verified before it is relied on.

### United States, FDA. Approved product and indication

**Status.** Bremelanotide is FDA-approved as VYLEESI (bremelanotide injection). The label states an
initial United States approval year of 2019.

**Approved indication, as characterised by the label.** For the treatment of premenopausal women
with acquired, generalized hypoactive sexual desire disorder, characterised by low sexual desire
causing marked distress or interpersonal difficulty, and not due to a co-existing medical or
psychiatric condition, problems within the relationship, or the effects of a medicine or drug
substance. The label defines "acquired" as HSDD developing in a patient who previously had no
problems with sexual desire, and "generalized" as HSDD occurring regardless of the type of
stimulation, situation, or partner.

**Limitations of Use, as stated on the label.** Not indicated for the treatment of HSDD in
postmenopausal women or in men, and not indicated to enhance sexual performance.

### United States. Material sold as "PT-141" by unregulated vendors

**Status.** Material sold as "PT-141" by peptide and research-chemical vendors is not an FDA-approved

**Date checked.** 2026-07-19.

**Verified.** Inference from a verified source, not directly retrieved.

## Unknowns

These are the things nobody has established, or that this research session could not establish. They
are stated as unknowns rather than filled in.

1. **The therapeutic mechanism.** The FDA label states outright that the mechanism by which
   bremelanotide improves HSDD is unknown.
2. **Efficacy and safety of the approved product in men.** No adequately powered modern randomized
   trial was found. This is the single largest gap between the evidence and the way PT-141 is
   actually marketed.
3. **Efficacy and safety in postmenopausal women.** Explicitly excluded from the approved indication,
   and no trial in that population was found.
4. **Cardiovascular risk in people with uncontrolled high blood pressure or established
   cardiovascular disease.** The blood pressure study deliberately enrolled only people who were
   normotensive or controlled, so risk in exactly the contraindicated group is uncharacterised.
5. **Whether focal hyperpigmentation fully resolves after stopping.** The label states resolution was
   not confirmed in all patients.
6. **Long-term safety beyond 52 weeks**, and safety in anyone using it more often or for longer than
   the trials permitted.
7. **The identity, purity, sterility, and endotoxin content of unregulated "PT-141" products.** No
   analytical data from an acceptable source was found.
8. **Anti-doping status** under the WADA Prohibited List.
9. **Whether bremelanotide appears on any FDA compounding bulk drug substances list**, and whether
   FDA has taken any enforcement action against sellers of unapproved PT-141.
10. **The content and scope of the 2023 Expression of Concern** on the 2008 male trial. Its existence
    was recorded in the PubMed record retrieved for that trial, but the Expression of Concern itself
    was not retrieved and read.

## Questions for a qualified professional

These are questions a member could take to a clinician or pharmacist. They are written to be usable
in an appointment, and none of them assumes a decision has been made.

**About whether it applies to you at all**

1. The approved indication is premenopausal women with acquired, generalized hypoactive sexual
   desire disorder. Do I actually fall inside that, and if not, what would using it off-label mean
   for me specifically?
2. The label says low desire caused by another medical or psychiatric condition, by a relationship
   problem, or by another medicine is excluded from the diagnosis. Could any of those explain what
   I am experiencing, and how would we find out?
3. If I am a man asking about this, what evidence exists for me, given that the approved label
   states it is not indicated in men?

**About risk in your case**

4. Do I have uncontrolled high blood pressure or any cardiovascular condition that would make this
   contraindicated? Have I actually been assessed for that, rather than assumed clear?
5. Given the documented blood pressure increase and heart rate reduction, is there anything in my
   history or medicine list that would make that a problem?
6. Nausea affected a large minority in the trials and made some people stop. How would we decide
   whether it was tolerable for me, and at what point would stopping be the right call?
7. Skin darkening in patches was reported, more often in people with darker skin, and the label says
   it did not clearly resolve in everyone. Is that a risk I should weigh differently?
8. Could this interact with anything else I take, including anything I do not think of as a
   medicine?

**About expectations**

9. The average improvement in the trials was small on the scales used. Realistically, what size of
   change would I be likely to notice, and how would we tell it apart from expectation?
10. What would we measure to decide whether it was working for me, and how long before we made that
    call?
11. What alternatives exist that are not this compound, including non-drug approaches, and how do
    they compare?

**About what you would actually be getting**

12. If a product is not the approved prescription medicine, what do we actually know about what is
    in it? What would you want to see before treating it as the same substance?
13. What would you want documented about identity, purity, and sterility before considering
    anything obtained outside a pharmacy?
