# Contradictions and Tensions: PT-141 (bremelanotide)

This file records where the record pulls in different directions, and how each tension is
resolved for the Guide. Source IDs refer to SOURCE_REGISTRY.md.

Some of what follows is not a contradiction between sources at all. It is a contradiction
between what the sources say and what the compound is commonly claimed to do. Those are
recorded here too, because they are the tensions a member is most likely to arrive with.

## 1. Whether PT-141 is FDA approved

**Position A.** Bremelanotide is FDA approved. There is an approved label with an initial
US approval year of 2019 (S1).

**Position B.** PT-141 as sold by peptide vendors is not an approved product, and the
population it is most heavily marketed to is excluded by the approved label's own
limitations of use (S1).

**Resolution.** Both statements are true. This is not a conflict in the evidence, it is a
conflict created by treating approval as a property of a molecule rather than of a specific
product used for a specific indication in a specific population. The approved product is
indicated for premenopausal women with acquired, generalized hypoactive sexual desire
disorder. The label states it is not indicated in postmenopausal women, not indicated in
men, and not indicated to enhance sexual performance. Marketing that cites the 2019
approval to sell the compound to men for performance is citing an approval that explicitly
excludes that use. The Guide should carry this distinction near the top, because it is the
single most consequential fact in the record.

## 2. How strong the efficacy evidence is in the approved population

**Position A.** Two adequately powered phase 3 randomized controlled trials, 1,267
participants randomized, both meeting co-primary endpoints, with a 52-week extension
reporting sustained improvement and no new safety signals (S2, S3, S8, S9).

**Position B.** The absolute effects were small, roughly 0.3 to 0.4 points on the scales
used. Only about 40 percent of those who entered the extension completed it. Nausea led 8
percent to stop. In the exit study, the placebo group also described real benefit (S1, S2,
S3, S4).

**Resolution.** These are not in conflict. They are two accurate descriptions of the same
drug, and each is incomplete alone. The honest summary is that the effect is reproducibly
better than placebo, modest in average magnitude, accompanied by a real tolerability
burden, and set against a condition where the non-drug and expectancy component is large.
The Guide should never present the statistical significance without the magnitude, and
should never present the extension's sustained-improvement finding without its attrition.

## 3. Evidence in men

**Position A.** Published randomized human trials of bremelanotide in men with erectile
dysfunction exist, and are cited in support of male use (S6, S7).

**Position B.** Those trials used an intranasal formulation that was never approved and was
discontinued. The larger carries an Expression of Concern recorded in its PubMed entry. The
smaller was retrieved only as a bibliographic listing, with 19 patients noted. The approved
label states the product is not indicated in men (S1, S6, S7, S7a).

**Resolution.** Position B is decisively stronger and the Guide should say so without
softening. The correct finding is that sound, current, adequately powered human evidence
for the approved injectable product in men was not found. The male-use case rests on
off-label extrapolation from an integrity-flagged trial of a withdrawn formulation. Note
also the asymmetry in how this citation is used in the wider ecosystem: pages promoting the
compound to men routinely cite the flagged trial without disclosing the flag. Any Guide
content touching male use must disclose it.

## 4. Mechanism plausibility against evidence

**The tension.** The compound has a coherent and appealing mechanistic story, that it acts
centrally on desire rather than peripherally on blood flow. That story is repeated widely
and is easy to find. The FDA-approved label states plainly that the mechanism by which the
drug improves the approved condition is unknown (S1).

**Resolution.** The regulator's position wins, and it is unusually clean here because the
label says the quiet part outright. No primary human source establishing the central
pathway was retrieved this session. The proposed pathway may be stated only as a
hypothesis, and only alongside the label's statement that the mechanism is unknown. This is
the point in the record where mechanism plausibility most outruns evidence, and it is the
main reason a reader can end up believing the compound is better understood than it is.

Note a second, subtler version of the same problem. The mechanism is not sex-specific in
principle, and that fact is often used to argue that the compound should work in men. A
mechanism that could apply to a population is not evidence that it does. Plausibility is
not a substitute for a trial.

## 5. Preclinical against human findings

There is no preclinical-versus-human divergence to report, because no preclinical primary
studies were retrieved and none are cited. That was a deliberate decision, recorded in
SOURCE_PLAN.md. Given that the label calls the therapeutic mechanism unknown, animal work
would have contributed only to a mechanism narrative the regulator declines to endorse,
which is precisely the kind of preclinical-to-human leap this Guide series exists to avoid.

The only place animal data appears at all is the label's own note of fetal harm in animal
studies at high exposures, which the Guide carries as an animal finding, described as such
in the same sentence.

## 6. Jurisdictional differences

No genuine jurisdictional contradiction can be reported, because only one jurisdiction was
checked. The United States position is documented from the approved label (S1). No non-US
regulator was checked in this session at all.

This absence is itself a risk worth naming in the Guide. Readers routinely assume that a US
approval means a compound is approved generally. The Guide should state that the status
outside the United States is unknown to us as of 2026-07-19, rather than allowing silence
to imply either approval or prohibition elsewhere.

The same reasoning applies to anti-doping status. The WADA Prohibited List could not be
retrieved, so no claim is made in either direction, and competitive athletes are directed
to their own anti-doping authority.

## 7. Vendor claims against the retrieved literature

These are the clearest conflicts in the whole record, and they run one way.

| Common claim in the vendor and clinic ecosystem | What the retrieved record supports |
|---|---|
| It is FDA approved, stated without qualification | An approval exists for one product, one indication, and one population. The label excludes men, excludes postmenopausal women, and excludes performance enhancement (S1). |
| It works by acting on desire pathways in the brain | The label states the mechanism is unknown (S1). The pathway claim is a hypothesis and no primary human source for it was retrieved. |
| Clinical trials show it works in men | The male trials used a formulation that was never approved and was discontinued, and the larger carries an Expression of Concern (S6, S7). |
| Purity and label accuracy figures for vendor-sold peptides, quoted as though they were analytical findings | No acceptable source for any such figure was found. Every result was a vendor, affiliate, or testing-laboratory marketing page. No figure is recorded in this Guide, and a widely circulated label-failure percentage is deliberately excluded. |
| A specific FDA warning letter is often referenced | Nothing was verified against an FDA document. No enforcement claim is recorded. |
| It is generally safe or well tolerated | The label reports nausea in 40 percent, anti-nausea treatment in 13 percent, discontinuation in 8 percent, a contraindication in uncontrolled hypertension or known cardiovascular disease, and skin darkening whose resolution after stopping was not confirmed in all patients (S1). |

## 8. Tensions inside the safety record

There is one tension worth naming that is internal to the evidence rather than external.

The cardiovascular safety trial reported small, brief blood pressure increases with peaks
typically lasting under a quarter of an hour, and withdrawal-criterion events distributed
evenly across groups including placebo (S5). Read alone, that sounds reassuring. But the
trial enrolled only women who were normotensive or whose hypertension was controlled, which
is exactly the opposite of the group the label contraindicates (S1). So the most reassuring
dataset in the record and the group at the highest stated risk do not overlap at all.

The Guide should present the trial finding and its enrollment restriction in the same
breath. Presenting the reassurance without the restriction would be the most likely way for
this file set to mislead a reader.
