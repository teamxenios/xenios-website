# Source Plan: PT-141 (bremelanotide)

This document records how evidence for the PT-141 Guide was gathered, what was
deliberately excluded, and what a human reviewer still has to do before anything here
is published.

## 1. What this compound is, for search purposes

PT-141 is the development code name for bremelanotide, the active ingredient in an
FDA-approved prescription drug product. That makes the search problem different from
most compounds in this series. There is a real regulatory record and a real phase 3
trial programme to find, and the main risk is not absence of evidence but misapplication
of it, specifically citing an approval that covers one narrow population as if it covered
the population the compound is actually marketed to.

Search terms used across databases included the generic name bremelanotide, the code
name PT-141, the brand name VYLEESI, and the indication term hypoactive sexual desire
disorder, alone and combined with erectile dysfunction, blood pressure, and safety.

## 2. Databases and registries consulted

| Source type | Specific resource | Outcome |
|---|---|---|
| Regulatory label | DailyMed structured product label for VYLEESI (bremelanotide injection) | Retrieved in full. This is the primary regulatory basis for the Guide. |
| Regulatory primary documents | FDA.gov (Drugs@FDA overview, approval letter PDF, 503A bulk drug substances list, bulk substances that may present significant safety risks list) | Blocked. Every attempt returned 403 or 404. Nothing from fda.gov itself was retrieved. |
| Peer-reviewed literature | PubMed | Retrieved. Phase 3 programme, open-label extension, patient-experience substudy, cardiovascular safety trial, and the older male erectile dysfunction trial. |
| Full text | PubMed Central | Retrieved for the ambulatory blood pressure study. |
| Trial registry | ClinicalTrials.gov API | Retrieved registrations for both phase 3 trials. |
| Anti-doping | WADA Prohibited List page | No usable content returned. No anti-doping claim recorded. |
| Tertiary background | Wikipedia | Retrieved for development history only, flagged as not evidence, and not used to support any member-facing claim. |

## 3. Inclusion criteria

A source was eligible to support a member-facing claim only if all of the following held.

1. It was actually retrieved during the research session on 2026-07-19, not recalled.
2. It is a regulatory label, a peer-reviewed indexed study, a trial registry record, or
   equivalent primary material.
3. Its population, formulation, and endpoint are stated clearly enough that the claim can
   be scoped to them.
4. It carries no unresolved research-integrity flag, or, if it does, the flag is disclosed
   in the same sentence as any reference to it.

## 4. Exclusion criteria

The following were treated as disqualified as evidence, regardless of how confident or
technical they appeared.

- Peptide vendor and research-chemical retailer pages, including their blogs, guides,
  and comparison articles.
- Affiliate sites and aggregator pages whose commercial model depends on the compound
  being purchased.
- Clinic and telehealth marketing pages promoting the compound for indications outside
  the approved label.
- Testing-laboratory marketing content presented as independent analytical data.
- Forum posts, social media, and user-reported experience.
- Any figure encountered only on the above source types, including quantitative purity
  and mislabeling percentages. One widely circulated label-failure percentage was seen
  only on such pages and has been deliberately left out of every file in this set.

Tertiary encyclopedia content was permitted for orientation and development history only,
labelled as such, and is not permitted to support a member-facing claim.

## 5. Specific searches that returned nothing usable

These are recorded because a null result is itself a finding, and because a later reviewer
should not assume they were skipped.

- Independent analytical quality data on vendor-sold material labelled PT-141, covering
  identity confirmation, purity, sterility, endotoxin, and mislabeling rates. Every result
  was a vendor, affiliate, or laboratory marketing page. No figure was recorded.
- FDA enforcement action, including warning letters naming bremelanotide or PT-141. Search
  returned only vendor and clinic pages asserting that such letters exist. Nothing was
  verified against an FDA document, so nothing was recorded.
- Bremelanotide's compounding status under the relevant FDA bulk drug substances lists.
  The pages were unreachable.
- WADA Prohibited List status. Page unreachable, remaining results were vendor sites.

## 6. Preclinical searching, and why the preclinical table is empty

No preclinical primary studies were retrieved, and this was a decision rather than an
omission. The human evidence in the approved population is substantial enough to stand on
its own, and the animal literature would only support a proposed central mechanism that
the FDA label itself describes as unknown. Adding rodent findings would create exactly the
preclinical-to-human leap this Guide series exists to avoid. If a reviewer decides
preclinical coverage is needed, it must be gathered fresh and every finding must carry its
species or model in the same sentence.

## 7. Handling of the male-use literature

The male erectile dysfunction literature was searched specifically, because that is the
use PT-141 is most heavily marketed for. Two records were found. Both used an intranasal
formulation that was never approved and whose development was discontinued. The larger of
the two carries a formal Expression of Concern recorded in its PubMed entry. The smaller
was retrieved only as a bibliographic listing, so no finding from it is recorded anywhere
in this file set. The conclusion carried forward is that sound, current, adequately powered
human evidence for the approved product in men was not found.

## 8. What remains for a human reviewer to search

1. Confirm the exact FDA approval date from an FDA-hosted document. Only the approval year
   is verified from the label.
2. Confirm the application number and the approval holder from an FDA-hosted document.
3. Determine bremelanotide's status on the FDA compounding bulk drug substances lists.
4. Determine whether any FDA enforcement action names bremelanotide or PT-141 sellers.
5. Determine the WADA Prohibited List status, from WADA or a national anti-doping authority.
6. Determine regulatory status outside the United States. No non-US regulator was checked
   in this session at all, so the Guide currently has a complete gap there.
7. Retrieve the Expression of Concern document itself and read its scope, rather than
   relying on the note in the linked PubMed record.
8. Retrieve the abstract of the small intranasal co-administration study if any reference
   to it is to be retained.
9. Seek independent analytical data on unregulated product quality from an academic,
   government, or peer-reviewed source. This gap should be closed before the Guide makes
   any quantitative statement about product quality.

## 9. Standing exclusions for this Guide

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

No content in this file set describes how the compound is used, prepared, or obtained.
Regulatory statements carry a jurisdiction, a date, and a source URL, and approval in one
jurisdiction is never presented as approval in another.
