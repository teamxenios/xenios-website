# Thymosin Alpha-1 (Thymalfasin)

A Xenios Research Guide. This Guide describes what is known, what is claimed, and what is
not known. It is not medical advice, and it is not a recommendation to use anything.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## One-minute summary

Thymosin alpha-1 is a real pharmaceutical molecule, not an invented one. In its synthetic
form it is called thymalfasin and is sold under the brand name Zadaxin. It is reported to be
an approved prescription medicine in some countries outside the United States, mainly for
chronic hepatitis B. It has no marketing approval in the United States.

That legitimacy is the reason this compound needs unusually careful reading. Real regulatory
history and real clinical trials can be borrowed by marketing to support claims the evidence
does not carry.

The single most important fact in this Guide is a negative one. The largest and best-designed
trial ever run on this compound, in any condition, found essentially no effect. In a
1,089-patient double-blind, placebo-controlled phase 3 trial in adults with sepsis (a
life-threatening reaction to infection), 28-day death rates were 23.4 percent with thymosin
alpha-1 and 24.1 percent with placebo (an inactive dummy treatment), with a hazard ratio of
0.99 and a p-value of 0.93 (BMJ, 2025, PMID 39814420). That trial was specifically the
confirmatory study that an earlier, smaller, weaker trial had called for, and the earlier
favourable signal did not survive it.

Three further things a member should take away:

1. Every human trial retrieved for this Guide studied people who were seriously ill (sepsis,
   COVID-19 with low blood oxygen, chronic hepatitis B). No retrieved study looked at healthy
   adults, immune support, longevity, or general resilience. The evidence base and the
   marketed use case do not overlap. **Grade G (unverified)** for any healthy-adult benefit
   claim.
2. Safety data from the trials were unremarkable within those trials, but every one of them
   was short-term use in acutely ill, medically supervised people. There is no retrieved
   evidence on long-term elective use in healthy adults. Absence of studied harm is not the
   same as demonstrated safety.
3. The United States legal and compounding status of this compound is genuinely unsettled as
   of the research date of this Guide. Vendor websites assert one status. The only independent
   journalism retrieved describes a different and unfinished picture. See
   [Regulatory status](#regulatory-status).

## What it is

Thymosin alpha-1 (often written Ta1) is a peptide, meaning a short chain of amino acids, the
building blocks that make up proteins. It is 28 amino acids long. It was originally isolated
from a crude preparation of calf thymus tissue called thymosin fraction-5, and it is also
naturally present in humans.

The synthetic pharmaceutical version is called thymalfasin, marketed as Zadaxin and developed
by SciClone Pharmaceuticals. It is described as an immunomodulatory peptide, meaning a
substance that alters the behaviour of the immune system rather than directly killing a virus
or bacterium.

**Commonly confused with three different things.** Getting these apart matters, because
claims attach to the wrong molecule constantly:

- **Thymosin beta-4, also sold as TB-500.** Structurally and functionally unrelated. It is
  associated with tissue repair rather than immune modulation and has a different regulatory
  and anti-doping situation. Findings about beta-4 are not findings about alpha-1.
- **Thymosin fraction 5.** The crude thymic extract that alpha-1 was originally purified out
  of. It is a mixture, not the single purified peptide.
- **Thymalfasin the regulated drug product versus "thymosin alpha-1" powder from an
  unregulated seller.** These are not the same product. The second carries no assurance that
  it contains what the label says.

## Why interest exists

The interest is understandable and worth describing plainly, without endorsing it.

First, unlike most compounds in the consumer peptide market, this one has a genuine
pharmaceutical identity and a genuine regulatory history somewhere in the world. That is rare
in this category and it reads as credibility.

Second, it has been studied in properly sized randomised trials, including a phase 3 trial
with over a thousand patients. Most peptides marketed to consumers have nothing of the sort.

Third, the framing is appealing. An "immunomodulator" that is described as restoring immune
competence rather than crudely stimulating the immune system sounds both sophisticated and
safe. During and after the COVID-19 period, a series of published analyses reported
mortality benefits in seriously ill patients, which kept interest high.

Fourth, in the United States it occupies contested regulatory ground, and contested ground
generates both attention and confident marketing.

None of that is evidence of benefit for a healthy person. The reason for interest and the
existence of benefit are separate questions, and this Guide keeps them separate.

## Terminology

Glossed here in the order a reader is likely to meet them.

- **Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids. Shorter than a protein.
- **Thymalfasin.** The generic pharmaceutical name for synthetic thymosin alpha-1.
- **Zadaxin.** The brand name under which thymalfasin is marketed.
- **Immunomodulator.** Something that changes how the immune system behaves. Not the same as
  something that kills an infection directly.
- **Sepsis.** A life-threatening whole-body reaction to infection.
- **Placebo-controlled.** Some participants receive an inactive dummy treatment for
  comparison.
- **Double-blind.** Neither the participants nor the researchers know who received the real
  treatment. This is the strongest guard against wishful reporting.
- **Single-blind.** Only one side is kept unaware, usually the participant. Weaker than
  double-blind.
- **Open-label.** Everyone knows who is receiving what. Weakest of the three for judging
  subjective outcomes.
- **Phase 3 trial.** A large trial intended to confirm whether a treatment actually works.
- **Primary endpoint.** The single main outcome a trial was designed and sized to answer.
  Results on other outcomes are weaker evidence.
- **Null result.** The trial found no meaningful difference between groups.
- **Hazard ratio and risk ratio.** Numbers comparing outcomes between two groups. A value of
  1.0 means no difference.
- **95 percent confidence interval.** The range of values compatible with the data. If the
  range crosses 1.0, no difference is compatible with the result.
- **P-value.** A rough measure of how surprising a result would be if there were truly no
  effect. Conventionally, below 0.05 is called statistically significant. It is a convention,
  not a proof.
- **Meta-analysis.** A study that pools results from several earlier studies.
- **Heterogeneity and I-squared.** A measure of how much the pooled studies disagree with
  each other. High values weaken the pooled answer.
- **Observational study.** Researchers watch what happens rather than randomly assigning
  treatment. More prone to being misled by who got treated and why.
- **Confounding by indication.** When sicker (or less sick) patients are more likely to
  receive a treatment, making the treatment look worse (or better) than it is.
- **Subgroup analysis.** Looking at a slice of the trial population. Prone to false positives
  and generally hypothesis-generating only.
- **Biomarker.** A measurable biological signal, such as a cell count. A biomarker moving is
  not the same as a patient getting better.
- **Monocyte HLA-DR.** A marker on certain immune cells, used as an indicator of whether the
  immune system is suppressed.
- **CD4+ T cells.** A type of immune cell, often counted as a measure of immune function.
- **Immunoparalysis.** A state in which the immune system is exhausted and underperforming,
  described in severe infection.
- **Toll-like receptors (TLRs).** Sensors on immune cells that detect signs of infection.
- **Dendritic cells.** Immune cells that present threats to the rest of the immune system.
- **HBeAg-negative.** A category of chronic hepatitis B defined by a specific blood marker.
- **Interferon alpha.** An older injectable treatment for hepatitis B, used as the comparison
  drug in one analysis in this Guide.
- **Nucleoside and nucleotide analogues.** The class of drugs that transformed hepatitis B
  treatment after 2008.
- **Orphan drug designation.** A development incentive granted to encourage work on rare
  diseases. It is not approval and does not mean a drug works.
- **Compounding and the 503A bulks list.** In the United States, compounding is the practice
  of a pharmacy preparing a medicine from raw ingredients. The 503A bulks list governs which
  raw substances may be used.
- **Category 2.** On the interim version of that list, a designation for substances that
  raise significant safety risks for compounding.
- **PCAC.** The Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee, an advisory body to the United
  States Food and Drug Administration. Its votes are advisory, not law.
- **WADA.** The World Anti-Doping Agency, which publishes the Prohibited List used in sport.

## Proposed mechanism

Everything in this section is **proposed**, not established, and should be read with that
word attached.

Thymosin alpha-1 is described as an immunomodulator rather than a direct antiviral or
antibacterial agent. The most commonly advanced proposal is that it acts through Toll-like
receptor signalling (particularly TLR9 and TLR2) on dendritic cells and monocytes, leading to
dendritic cell maturation, changes in T-cell differentiation and CD4+ and CD8+ T-cell
function, and altered cytokine output. A second proposed strand is that it restores immune
competence in states of immune exhaustion, rather than generally boosting immunity in someone
whose immune system is working normally.

[UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check] The specific TLR9 and TLR2
receptor attribution above is general background knowledge. No primary mechanistic paper
establishing it was retrieved for this Guide.

**Where the proposal comes from, and its limits.** The pathway is largely inferred from
cell-culture and animal systems, plus immune biomarker measurements taken inside human
trials. No definitive human mechanistic study was retrieved. **Grade D (preclinical)** for the
receptor-level mechanism.

Two human biomarker signals were retrieved and are the best human-level mechanistic support
available:

- In the ETASS severe sepsis trial (single-blind, 361 patients), the treated group showed
  greater improvement in monocyte HLA-DR expression at day 3 (p = 0.037) and day 7
  (p = 0.017), a recognised marker of reversal of sepsis-associated immunoparalysis.
  **Grade C (early human evidence)**, biomarker only.
- In the Shehadeh COVID-19 pilot (open-label, 49 patients analysed), among the subgroup on
  low-flow oxygen the treated group had on average 3.84 times more CD4+ T cells on day 5 than
  on day 1 (p = 0.01). **Grade C (early human evidence)**, biomarker only, small subgroup.

**The central mechanistic caution.** In both of those trials the biomarker moved while the
clinical outcome did not clearly follow. A plausible and partly measurable immune effect has
not translated into reliable clinical benefit in the largest and best-controlled trial
available. A biomarker changing is a reason to keep studying something. It is not a reason to
believe it helps people.

## Human evidence

Five human evidence sources were retrieved and verified for this Guide. They are presented
strongest first. Population is stated for every one.

### 1. TESTS, sepsis, phase 3, null result

**Citation.** Wu J, et al. The efficacy and safety of thymosin alpha-1 for sepsis (TESTS):
multicentre, double blinded, randomised, placebo controlled, phase 3 trial. BMJ, 2025.
PMID 39814420. DOI 10.1136/bmj-2024-082583. Registration NCT02867267. VERIFIED 2026-07-19,
https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39814420/

**Design and population.** Multicentre, double-blind, randomised, placebo-controlled phase 3
trial across 22 centres in China, enrolling September 2016 to December 2020. 1,089 adults
aged 18 to 85 with sepsis. 542 received thymosin alpha-1, 547 received placebo. This is the
highest-quality human trial retrieved for this compound in any condition.

**Finding.** Null on the primary endpoint. 28-day all-cause death occurred in 127 of 542
(23.4 percent) in the thymosin alpha-1 group versus 132 of 547 (24.1 percent) with placebo.
Hazard ratio 0.99, 95 percent confidence interval 0.77 to 1.27, p = 0.93. No secondary or
safety outcomes differed significantly between the groups. The authors concluded there was no
clear evidence that thymosin alpha-1 decreases 28-day all-cause mortality in adults with
sepsis.

**Limitations.** Conducted entirely in China, which may limit how well the patient mix and
background intensive care practice generalise elsewhere. Subgroup analyses suggested possible
differences by age and diabetes status, but these did not meet multiple-comparison
thresholds and are hypothesis-generating only. They must not be read as benefit in any
subgroup.

**Grade for the claim "reduces death in sepsis": PROHIBITED as a benefit claim.** The
retrieved evidence supports the opposite conclusion.

### 2. ETASS, severe sepsis, superseded

**Citation.** Wu, et al. The efficacy of thymosin alpha 1 for severe sepsis (ETASS): a
multicenter, single-blind, randomized and controlled trial. Critical Care, 2013.
PMID 23327199. PMC4056079. Registration NCT00711620. VERIFIED 2026-07-19,
https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4056079/

**Design and population.** Multicentre, single-blind, randomised controlled trial across six
tertiary teaching hospitals in China, May 2008 to December 2010. 361 patients with severe
sepsis, 181 treated and 180 control. Single-blind and not placebo-controlled in the way TESTS
was.

**Finding.** Directionally favourable but not statistically significant on the primary
endpoint as prespecified. 28-day all-cause death 47 of 181 (26.0 percent) versus 63 of 180
(35.0 percent). Relative risk 0.74, 95 percent confidence interval 0.54 to 1.02, p = 0.062
non-stratified. A log-rank p of 0.049 and an in-hospital mortality p of 0.032 were reported
as favouring treatment. Biomarker findings are described under Proposed mechanism. On safety,
no thymosin alpha-1-related severe adverse event was reported and no treatment was
discontinued for intolerance.

**Limitations.** The single-blind design is a material risk-of-bias concern. The primary
endpoint did not reach conventional significance, and the supporting p-values come from
alternative analyses rather than the prespecified primary one. The authors themselves
acknowledged a heterogeneous population, possible patient selection bias, a limited window
for laboratory data, and the need for a larger double-blind confirmatory trial.

**That confirmatory trial was subsequently run and was null (TESTS, above). This trial should
never be read in isolation.** **Grade C (early human evidence), superseded.**

### 3. Shehadeh, COVID-19 pilot, null result

**Citation.** Shehadeh F, Benitez G, Mylona EK, et al. A Pilot Trial of Thymalfasin
(Thymosin-alpha-1) to Treat Hospitalized Patients With Hypoxemia and Lymphocytopenia Due to
Coronavirus Disease 2019 Infection. The Journal of Infectious Diseases, 2023; 227(2):226-235.
DOI 10.1093/infdis/jiac362. Registration NCT04487444. VERIFIED 2026-07-19,
https://academic.oup.com/jid/article/227/2/226/6691387

**Design and population.** Prospective, open-label (not blinded) randomised controlled pilot
trial comparing thymalfasin plus standard care against standard care alone. Two Rhode Island
hospitals, enrolling September 2020 to May 2022, sponsored by Rhode Island Hospital. 49
patients analysed (23 treated, 26 control), hospitalised with low blood oxygen and low
lymphocyte counts due to COVID-19. The registry lists the study as terminated due to slow
enrolment and the principal investigator relocating, with no results posted to the registry.

**Finding.** Null on the primary endpoint of time to clinical recovery. 14 of 23 (61 percent)
treated recovered versus 17 of 26 (65 percent) control. Subdistribution hazard ratio 0.80,
95 percent confidence interval 0.42 to 1.55, p = 0.52. Subgroups by baseline oxygen support
were also non-significant: low-flow 8 of 8 (100 percent) versus 14 of 18 (78 percent),
SHR 1.48, 95 percent CI 0.68 to 3.25, p = 0.33; high-flow 6 of 15 (40 percent) versus 3 of 8
(38 percent), SHR 1.28, 95 percent CI 0.35 to 4.63, p = 0.71. Death, invasive mechanical
ventilation and intensive care admission showed no significant between-group differences.
On safety, nine serious adverse events occurred among treated patients, none deemed related
to thymosin alpha-1, with no injection site irritation or allergic reactions reported and no
clinically significant differences in acute kidney injury, liver enzyme elevation, raised
bilirubin, low neutrophils or low platelets.

**Limitations.** The authors flagged the non-blinded design, the small sample and limited
recruitment, and stated that post hoc analyses should be interpreted with caution. The trial
was terminated early for administrative reasons, not stopped for benefit. It was a pilot and
was not powered for clinical efficacy. The apparently striking 100 percent recovery in the
low-flow treated subgroup rests on eight patients and is not statistically significant. It
must not be read as evidence of benefit.

**Grade C (early human evidence), null on the outcome it was designed to measure.**

### 4. Soeroto, COVID-19 meta-analysis, positive but weak

**Citation.** Soeroto AY, Suryadinata H, Yanto TA, Hariyanto TI. The efficacy of thymosin
alpha-1 therapy in moderate to critical COVID-19 patients: a systematic review,
meta-analysis, and meta-regression. Inflammopharmacology, 2023. PMID 37845598.
DOI 10.1007/s10787-023-01354-2. VERIFIED 2026-07-19,
https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37845598/

**Design and population.** Systematic review, meta-analysis and meta-regression pooling eight
studies in patients with moderate to critical COVID-19.

**Finding.** The pooled analysis reported significantly lower COVID-19 mortality with
thymosin alpha-1: risk ratio 0.59, 95 percent confidence interval 0.37 to 0.93, p = 0.02.
Heterogeneity was substantial at I-squared 84 percent.

**Limitations, and they are decisive.** Three weaknesses compound each other. First, an
I-squared of 84 percent indicates substantial unexplained disagreement between the pooled
studies, which materially undermines the pooled number. Second, it could not be confirmed
from the retrieved abstract how many of the eight pooled studies were randomised rather than
retrospective or observational, and the design mix appears to lean observational. Pooled
observational COVID-19 treatment data were highly vulnerable to confounding by indication and
by rapidly changing standard of care. Third, the authors themselves stated that randomised
trials are still required to verify their findings. The one randomised trial retrieved in
this condition (Shehadeh, above) was null.

**A positive meta-analysis of largely non-randomised data sitting alongside a null randomised
trial is a recognised pattern of confounding, not corroboration.** **Grade G (unverified)**
for COVID-19 mortality benefit.

### 5. Yang, chronic hepatitis B meta-analysis, small and dated

**Citation.** Yang YF, Zhao W, Zhong YD, Yang YJ, Shen L, Zhang N, Huang P. Comparison of the
efficacy of thymosin alpha-1 and interferon alpha in the treatment of chronic hepatitis B: a
meta-analysis. Antiviral Research, 2008 Feb. PMID 18078676. VERIFIED 2026-07-19,
https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18078676/

**Design and population.** Meta-analysis of four randomised controlled trials, 199 patients
with chronic hepatitis B receiving either thymosin alpha-1 or interferon alpha. Three of the
four trials studied HBeAg-negative patients.

**Finding.** At six months after treatment, thymosin alpha-1 showed higher odds than
interferon alpha for virological response (odds ratio 3.71), biochemical response (3.12) and
complete response (2.69). The authors noted that the difference was not immediately
significant at the end of therapy and tended to increase gradually afterwards.

**Limitations.** The pooled sample is very small (199 patients across four trials) and the
analysis is now roughly 18 years old. It is an active-comparator analysis against interferon
alpha rather than against placebo, so it does not establish absolute efficacy. Applicability
is narrow because three of four trials enrolled HBeAg-negative patients. Most importantly,
hepatitis B treatment has been transformed since 2008 by nucleoside and nucleotide analogues,
so this comparison has limited bearing on care today. The full text was not retrieved, so the
risk-of-bias assessment of the included trials is unknown.

**Grade C (early human evidence)**, against an older comparator, applicability limited.

### A note on a source that is often misread

A Cochrane record exists for thymosin alpha-1 in chronic hepatitis B (Htet Htet N, Naing C,
Vongpunsawad S, Win TT, Poovorawan Y; Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews, 2022;
DOI 10.1002/14651858.CD014610; PMC8929401). It was retrieved and verified to be a **protocol
only**, meaning a published plan for a review. It contains no pooled results. Search engine
summaries presented protocol content as though it contained findings. No effect estimate from
this source exists and none should be cited. VERIFIED 2026-07-19,
https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8929401/

## Contradictions and disagreement

### Does it reduce death in sepsis?

**One position.** ETASS (2013, 361 patients, single-blind) reported 28-day death of
26.0 percent versus 35.0 percent, relative risk 0.74 (95 percent CI 0.54 to 1.02), primary
endpoint p = 0.062, with log-rank p = 0.049 and in-hospital mortality p = 0.032 reported as
favourable. Its authors concluded it may improve outcomes in a targeted severe sepsis
population.

**The other position.** TESTS (2025, 1,089 patients, double-blind, placebo-controlled,
phase 3) reported 28-day death of 23.4 percent versus 24.1 percent, hazard ratio 0.99
(95 percent CI 0.77 to 1.27), p = 0.93, and concluded there is no clear evidence of a
mortality reduction.

**Assessment: resolved in favour of the null.** This is not a live controversy and should not
be presented as one. TESTS is roughly three times larger, double-blind, placebo-controlled,
phase 3, and was precisely the confirmatory trial the ETASS authors called for. Its point
estimate of 0.99 is essentially perfectly null, not merely non-significant. ETASS is best read
as a promising smaller signal that did not survive rigorous confirmation, which is a very
common trajectory in medicine. Citing ETASS without TESTS would materially mislead a reader.

### Does it reduce death in COVID-19?

**One position.** Soeroto et al. (2023) pooled eight studies and reported a mortality risk
ratio of 0.59 (95 percent CI 0.37 to 0.93, p = 0.02).

**The other position.** The one randomised controlled trial retrieved in this condition
(Shehadeh 2023) was null on its primary endpoint, with no significant differences in death,
mechanical ventilation or intensive care admission.

**Assessment: unresolved, weighted toward no established benefit.** Heterogeneity in the
pooled analysis was substantial (I-squared 84 percent), the pooled evidence base appears
predominantly non-randomised, and the meta-analysis authors themselves said randomised trials
are still needed. Positive pooled observational data alongside a null randomised trial is a
recognised confounding pattern. At the same time, the null trial was a small pilot terminated
early, so it does not definitively exclude a modest effect. The honest reading is that
COVID-19 efficacy was never established by adequate randomised evidence before interest moved
on.

### Is it an approved medicine?

**One position.** It is reported to be an approved prescription medicine as Zadaxin in a
number of countries, principally for chronic hepatitis B, and holds orphan drug designations.

**The other position.** It has no United States marketing approval, and in the United States
it has been treated as a compounding substance placed in Category 2 in 2023 on safety-risk
grounds.

**Assessment: both are true, and the distinction is the entire point.** This is the framing
issue most exploited in marketing for this compound. Approval in another country reflects
that regulator's decision on that regulator's evidence standard and does not transfer to the
United States. Orphan drug designation is a development incentive, not approval, and is
routinely misrepresented as approval. Stated plainly: approved somewhere, not approved in the
United States, and designation is not approval.

### What is the current United States compounding status?

**One position.** Numerous peptide vendor and clinic websites state that thymosin alpha-1 was
reclassified out of Category 2 in February 2026 and is legally available by prescription
through compounding pharmacies.

**The other position.** The only independent journalism retrieved (ProPublica, 2026-04-03)
describes the United States Food and Drug Administration as poised to reverse the 2023
categorisation under current leadership, with the change contested by former agency
officials. It does not describe a completed February 2026 reclassification.

**Assessment: unresolved, and the vendor claim is unverified and commercially interested.** A
source with a financial interest in a product being available is the weakest possible source
for the claim that it has become available. The chronology is also internally inconsistent: an
April 2026 investigative piece describing a reversal as pending is difficult to reconcile
with a completed February 2026 reclassification. A human reviewer must verify current status
against primary regulatory sources immediately before this Guide publishes.

## Limitations

Limitations of the evidence itself:

- The strongest trial is null, and it is null with a point estimate almost exactly at no
  effect, not merely a failure to reach significance.
- The two largest sepsis trials were both conducted entirely in China, which may limit how
  well the patient mix and background critical care practice generalise.
- The COVID-19 randomised evidence consists of one small open-label pilot terminated early
  for administrative reasons.
- The positive COVID-19 pooled estimate carries substantial unexplained heterogeneity and an
  unconfirmed, apparently observational-leaning study mix.
- The hepatitis B evidence is small, roughly 18 years old, compared against an older drug
  rather than placebo, and predates the transformation of hepatitis B care.
- Where biomarkers moved, clinical outcomes did not clearly follow.

Limitations of this Guide and the research behind it:

- Every fda.gov URL returned an HTTP 404 error through the available research tooling. All
  United States regulatory statements here therefore rest on secondary reporting and are
  marked unverified.
- The World Anti-Doping Agency Prohibited List could not be retrieved. No anti-doping status
  claim is made anywhere in this Guide.
- Orphan drug designation records at the United States and European agencies could not be
  retrieved, so no designation numbers or dates are recorded.
- The list of countries where Zadaxin is approved could not be verified from any primary
  regulatory or company filing source.
- Full texts of the Soeroto 2023 and Yang 2008 analyses were not retrieved, so their internal
  study composition and risk-of-bias assessments are unknown.
- No primary preclinical study was retrieved.

## Quality and identity

This section is about the physical material a person might encounter, which is a separate
question from whether the molecule does anything.

**The specific identity risk here is unusually sharp.** Because thymalfasin genuinely is an
approved medicine as Zadaxin in some places, unregulated sellers can borrow the credibility of
a real regulated drug for material that is not that drug. Someone encountering "thymosin
alpha-1" from an unregulated source has no assurance it is the same substance as the studied
and approved product. Every trial safety observation summarised in this Guide applies to
pharmaceutical-grade product used under medical supervision. None of it transfers to
unregulated material.

**Documented product-quality concerns at the category level.** Per the retrieved ProPublica
investigation, the United States Food and Drug Administration's stated rationale for placing
peptides including thymosin-alpha in Category 2 in 2023 included that compounded peptide
manufacturing can introduce impurities such as bacteria or heavy metals, and that these
products are sensitive to temperature and vigorous handling in ways that can alter their
chemical composition. That is a concern about the material, distinct from any question about
the molecule. VERIFIED 2026-07-19,
https://www.propublica.org/article/peptide-safety-fda-compounding-pharmacies

**What is not known about this specific compound's gray-market material.** Peer-reviewed
analytical chemistry work exists on falsified and impurity-laden polypeptide drugs circulating
in gray markets. Search metadata indicated that thymosin **beta-4** appears among frequently
encountered falsified polypeptides in that literature. The full texts were not retrieved, and
no analytical data specific to thymosin **alpha-1** was found. Beta-4 findings are not
alpha-1 findings and are not presented as such here. **Grade G (unverified)** for any claim
about the purity of gray-market thymosin alpha-1 material specifically.

**Purity failure statistics circulating online should not be trusted.** Figures such as a
claimed 43 percent label-purity failure rate from a private testing lab appear widely. Every
such figure encountered in this research traced to peptide-vendor, peptide-directory or
testing-service marketing pages. These are commercial claims, not verified evidence, and none
is reproduced here as fact.

**Three marketing distortions observed directly during this research**, listed so a reader
can recognise them:

1. Asserting as settled fact that thymosin alpha-1 was reclassified to Category 1 or restored
   to legal compounding in February 2026. The only independent source retrieved describes a
   reversal as anticipated and contested, not completed.
2. Presenting orphan drug designation as though it were approval. It is a development
   incentive.
3. Presenting approval in another country as though it were United States approval. It is
   not.

## Safety categories

This section lists categories of concern. It is not a reassurance, and nothing here should be
read as a statement that this compound is safe.

**Category 1: deliberate immunosuppression.** Product labelling language reflected in the
retrieved Zadaxin monograph describes it as contraindicated in patients who are being
deliberately immunosuppressed, including organ transplant recipients, unless benefits clearly
outweigh risks. This is the most actionable safety signal in the record and follows directly
from the proposed immune-enhancing mechanism. Note that the source is a secondary drug
reference, not the primary approved label, which could not be retrieved. VERIFIED 2026-07-19,
https://www.rxlist.com/zadaxin-drug.htm

**Category 2: hypersensitivity.** The same monograph describes contraindication in patients
with a history of hypersensitivity to thymosin alpha 1 or any component of the injection.

**Category 3: local and other reported adverse experiences.** The monograph describes
reported adverse experiences as infrequent and mild, primarily local discomfort at the
injection site, with rare instances of redness, transient muscle atrophy, joint pain combined
with hand swelling, and rash. The same monograph states that across clinical experience in
over 2,000 individuals no clinically significant adverse reactions attributable to thymosin
alpha 1 were reported. That kind of sweeping characterisation is manufacturer-derived and is
not independent pharmacovigilance. Absence of reported signal in sponsor-associated
experience is not the same as demonstrated safety. **Grade E (manufacturer or supplier
reported).**

**Category 4: what trial safety data do and do not cover.** In the 1,089-patient TESTS phase
3 trial, no safety outcomes differed significantly between thymosin alpha-1 and placebo. In
ETASS, no drug-related severe adverse event was reported and no treatment was discontinued
for intolerance. In the Shehadeh COVID-19 pilot, nine serious adverse events occurred among
treated patients, none deemed related to the drug. All three were short-duration use in
acute illness under medical supervision. Trial-level safety data are not long-term safety
data. **Grade B (supported human evidence)** for the narrow statement that no safety
difference was detected in these specific short-term hospital trials. **Grade G** for any
broader safety claim.

**Category 5: the largest gap, which is a gap rather than a finding.** All retrieved human
safety data come from short-course use in acute, supervised hospital settings or in monitored
hepatitis treatment. There is no retrieved evidence on the safety of long-term, elective use
in healthy adults, which is the setting in which unregulated peptide use typically occurs.
Deliberately altering immune function in a healthy person is not a low-stakes intervention,
and the absence of studied harm in that setting reflects absence of study, not demonstrated
safety.

**Category 6: risks attached to the product rather than the molecule.** Per the retrieved
ProPublica investigation, the United States Food and Drug Administration cited immunogenicity
risk (the immune system reacting to the injected peptide itself, described as ranging from no
clinical manifestation to anaphylactic shock), manufacturing impurities including bacteria and
heavy metals, and sensitivity to temperature and handling, as the basis for the 2023
Category 2 placement of 19 peptides including thymosin-alpha. This is journalistic reporting
of the agency's reasoning, not the agency document itself.

No interaction study for thymosin alpha-1 was retrieved for this Guide. This section

## Unknowns

Stated plainly, because these are the honest boundaries of what is known.

1. **Whether thymosin alpha-1 has any benefit in healthy adults.** Every human trial
   retrieved studied acutely or chronically ill people. No retrieved study addresses immune
   support, wellness, longevity, or resilience in healthy people, which is the dominant use
   case in the consumer peptide market. The evidence base and the marketed use case do not
   overlap.
2. **Long-term safety of repeated or ongoing use.** All retrieved safety data come from
   short-course use in supervised acute or hepatitis treatment settings.
3. **Whether the observed immune biomarker changes translate into any clinical benefit.** In
   both trials where a biomarker moved, the clinical outcome did not clearly follow.
4. **Whether it has value in chronic hepatitis B relative to current standard of care.** The
   retrieved hepatitis evidence compares against interferon alpha and is roughly 18 years old,
   predating the transformation of hepatitis B management by nucleoside and nucleotide
   analogues.
5. **Efficacy in the other conditions it is discussed for**, including cancer, HIV, chronic
   obstructive pulmonary disease, myalgic encephalomyelitis and chronic fatigue syndrome,
   vaccine response, and infection after transplant. No efficacy evidence for any of these was
   retrieved.
6. **Whether unregulated material sold as thymosin alpha-1 is chemically the same substance as
   pharmaceutical thymalfasin.** No analytical study specific to thymosin alpha-1 identity or
   purity was retrieved.
7. **Whether altering immune function in a person with a normal immune system carries risk**,
   for example in relation to autoimmune predisposition. The proposed mechanism raises the
   question. No retrieved source answers it.
8. **The current, settled United States regulatory and compounding status** as of the date a
   reader is reading this.

## Questions for a qualified professional

These are questions a member could reasonably take to a physician, pharmacist, or other
qualified clinician. They are written to be asked, not answered here.

- Given my full medical history and current medicines, is there any reason a compound that
  alters immune function would be a specific risk for me?
- Do I have, or am I at elevated risk of, any autoimmune condition, and does that change the
  picture?
- Am I taking anything that suppresses my immune system, including after a transplant or for
  an inflammatory condition?
- What is the actual legal status of this compound in my jurisdiction today, and how would I
  verify that independently?
- The largest trial of this compound found no benefit in sepsis. Is there any reason to think
  a different result would apply to someone like me, who is not acutely ill?
- Is there any published evidence at all for the outcome I personally care about, in people
  like me?
- If someone offered me this outside a regulated pharmacy, how would I know what is actually
  in the vial?
- If I am tested in sport, how do I confirm the current anti-doping status rather than relying
  on a seller's claim?
- What signs or symptoms would make you want to see me if I were ever exposed to something
  like this?
- What is the actual clinical problem I am trying to solve, and is there an approach with
  better evidence behind it?
