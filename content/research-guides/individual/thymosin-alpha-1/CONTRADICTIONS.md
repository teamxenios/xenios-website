# Contradictions: Thymosin Alpha-1 (Thymalfasin)

Where sources disagree, this document records the disagreement, weighs it, and states how
the Guide must handle it. Some disagreements here are genuine and unresolved. Others look
like controversy but are not, and presenting those as open questions would itself mislead
a member. Both outcomes are recorded plainly.

Source ids refer to SOURCE_REGISTRY.md.

## 1. Does thymosin alpha-1 reduce mortality in sepsis?

**Position A.** The 2013 single blind randomised trial in 361 patients with severe sepsis
(S2) reported 28 day all cause mortality of 26.0 percent versus 35.0 percent, relative risk
0.74, 95 percent CI 0.54 to 1.02, with the prespecified primary endpoint at P equals 0.062.
Log rank analysis at P equals 0.049 and in hospital mortality at P equals 0.032 were
reported as favouring treatment. The authors concluded it may improve outcomes in a
targeted severe sepsis population.

**Position B.** The 2025 double blind, placebo controlled phase 3 trial in 1,089 adults
with sepsis (S1) reported 28 day all cause mortality of 23.4 percent versus 24.1 percent,
hazard ratio 0.99, 95 percent CI 0.77 to 1.27, P equals 0.93. The authors concluded there
is no clear evidence that thymosin alpha-1 decreases 28 day all cause mortality in adults
with sepsis.

**Assessment: resolved in favour of the null. This is not a standing controversy.**

The later trial is roughly three times larger, double blind rather than single blind,
placebo controlled, phase 3, and was precisely the confirmatory study the earlier trial's
own authors called for. Its point estimate of 0.99 is essentially perfectly null, not
merely short of significance. The earlier result is best read as a promising smaller signal
that did not survive rigorous confirmation, which is a common and unremarkable trajectory.

**Guide handling.** Lead with the phase 3 null result. The earlier trial may be mentioned
only as history, and only alongside the later one. Citing it in isolation is prohibited
(CLAIM_TABLE P-04). Do not present this as a live scientific debate.

## 2. Does thymosin alpha-1 reduce mortality in COVID-19?

**Position A.** The 2023 systematic review and meta-analysis (S5) pooled eight studies and
reported lower mortality, risk ratio 0.59, 95 percent CI 0.37 to 0.93, p equals 0.02.

**Position B.** The one randomised controlled trial retrieved in this indication (S3, S4)
was null on its primary endpoint of time to clinical recovery, subdistribution hazard ratio
0.80, 95 percent CI 0.42 to 1.55, p equals 0.52, with no significant differences in
mortality, invasive mechanical ventilation, or ICU admission.

**Assessment: unresolved, weighted toward no established benefit.**

Three factors argue against accepting the positive pooled estimate at face value.
Heterogeneity was substantial at I squared equals 84 percent, which materially undermines a
pooled figure. The composition of the pooled evidence base could not be confirmed from the
retrieved abstract and appears to lean non randomised, and observational COVID-19 treatment
data were highly vulnerable to confounding by indication and by rapidly changing standard of
care. The meta-analysis authors themselves stated that randomised trials are still required.

A positive pooled estimate drawn largely from observational data, sitting alongside a null
randomised trial, is a recognised confounding pattern rather than corroboration. The
counterweight is that the null trial was a small pilot terminated early for administrative
reasons, so it does not definitively exclude a modest effect either.

**Guide handling.** State that COVID-19 efficacy was never established by adequate
randomised evidence before research interest moved on. The pooled figure may appear only
with all three caveats attached (CLAIM_TABLE C-16 and C-17).

## 3. Where mechanism plausibility outruns the evidence

This is the most important divergence in the record, and it is not a disagreement between
sources. It is a disagreement between two levels of evidence within the same trials.

Immune biomarkers moved. The 2013 sepsis trial reported greater improvement in monocyte
HLA-DR expression in the treated arm at day 3 and day 7 (S2). The COVID-19 pilot reported
that among patients on low flow oxygen, the treated group had an average of 3.84 times more
CD4+ T cells on day 5 than on day 1 (S3).

Clinical outcomes did not follow. In the sepsis programme the definitive trial was null on
mortality (S1). In the COVID-19 pilot the primary endpoint was null (S3).

That dissociation is the honest summary of this compound: a plausible and partly measurable
immune effect that has not been shown to help patients. Mechanism plausibility here is
higher than for most compounds in this category, and that makes the gap more dangerous
rather than less, because a real biological signal can be used to imply a clinical benefit
that the strongest trial did not find.

**Guide handling.** Biomarker findings may never appear without the dissociation sentence
attached (CLAIM_TABLE C-09). No bare mechanism sentence may read as established human
physiology (CLAIM_TABLE P-18).

## 4. Where preclinical and human findings diverge

No divergence can be reported, because no preclinical study was retrieved in this session.
The preclinical evidence table for this compound is empty because nothing was retrieved,
not because nothing exists.

The proposed receptor level mechanism in general circulation was not tied to a retrieved
primary paper. It is carried in the Guide only as proposed and unverified.
[UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]

This absence is itself worth noting for a reviewer. For most compounds in this category the
risk is that preclinical findings get written up as though they were human findings. For
this compound the risk runs the other way: there is enough genuine human trial evidence that
a writer may be tempted to state mechanism as settled human fact. It is not.

## 5. Where jurisdictions differ

**Position A.** Thymalfasin is reported to be an approved prescription medicine under the
brand name Zadaxin in some countries outside the United States, principally for chronic
hepatitis B (S9).

**Position B.** It has no FDA marketing approval in the United States, and in the United
States it has been treated as a compounding substance placed in Category 2 of the interim
503A bulks list in 2023 on safety risk grounds (S8).

**Assessment: both are true, and the distinction is the point.**

This is the single most exploited framing issue for this compound. Approval in another
jurisdiction reflects that regulator's decision on that regulator's evidence standard at
that time. It does not transfer. Orphan drug designation, which is a development incentive
status, is routinely presented as though it were approval, and it is not.

**Guide handling.** State it plainly: approved in some jurisdictions, not approved in the
United States, and designation is not approval. Do not publish a country count, because no
primary regulatory source for one was retrieved. See REGULATORY_STATUS.md.

## 6. Where vendor claims conflict with the literature

Four conflicts were observed directly during the research session. In each case the
commercially interested source asserted more certainty than the independent record supports.

**6.1 Current United States compounding status.** Numerous vendor and clinic pages state
as settled fact that thymosin alpha-1 was reclassified out of Category 2 in February 2026
and is legally available by prescription through compounding pharmacies. The only
independent reporting retrieved (S8, dated 2026-04-03) describes FDA as poised to reverse
the 2023 categorisation, contested by former agency officials, and does not describe a
completed reclassification. The chronology is internally inconsistent: an April 2026
investigative piece describing a reversal as pending is difficult to reconcile with a
completed February 2026 reclassification.

A source with a financial interest in the product being available is the weakest possible
source for the claim that it has become available. This status is genuinely in flux and a
human reviewer must verify it against FDA primary sources immediately before publication,
because a Guide that misstates legal availability could directly influence a purchase.

**6.2 Orphan drug designation presented as approval.** Vendor material conflates
designation with marketing approval. Designation is a development incentive. No designation
record was retrieved in this session, so no designation number, date, or authority appears
in this Guide, and the distinction is stated regardless.

**6.3 Approval abroad presented as United States approval.** Addressed in section 5.

**6.4 Anti-doping status.** Every source found asserting that thymosin alpha-1 is absent
from the WADA Prohibited List was a peptide vendor or directory page. The Prohibited List
itself could not be retrieved. No anti-doping status claim appears anywhere in this Guide,
and athletes are directed to the authority. The adjacent confusion matters here too:
thymosin beta-4 and TB-500 are different peptides with a different anti-doping position,
and their status may not be borrowed in either direction.

## 7. Where the record does not contradict itself

Two areas are often written up as contested and are not, on this record.

**Safety within the studied settings.** All three randomised trials retrieved reported
unremarkable safety relative to control, and labeling derived adverse effects are described
as mild and mostly local (S1, S2, S3, S9). There is no conflicting safety signal in the
retrieved record. The limitation is not disagreement, it is scope: every dataset comes from
short course use in acutely ill, medically supervised populations. Absence of a detected
signal in that setting is not evidence about long term elective use in healthy adults.

**Hepatitis B efficacy.** The retrieved hepatitis B evidence (S6) is not contradicted by
another source. It is simply thin, dated, small, and compared against interferon alpha
rather than placebo, in a field transformed since by nucleoside and nucleotide analogues.
The Cochrane record (S7) is a protocol only and contains no findings that could agree or
disagree with anything. The correct characterisation is insufficient evidence, not conflict.
Note that search engine summaries presented the protocol as though it were a completed
review during this session, which is a caution about tooling rather than a contradiction in
the literature.
