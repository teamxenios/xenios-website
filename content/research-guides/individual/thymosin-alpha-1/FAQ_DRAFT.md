# Thymosin Alpha-1: Member FAQ

These answers follow the same rule as the Guide. We say what the retrieved evidence supports,
we say when it supports nothing, and we do not fill gaps with confidence.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## 1. Does it work?

For the outcomes people usually care about, the honest answer is no, or not shown.

The largest and best-designed trial ever run on this compound, in any condition, found
essentially nothing. In 1,089 adults with sepsis, in a double-blind placebo-controlled phase 3
trial, 28-day death rates were 23.4 percent with thymosin alpha-1 and 24.1 percent with the
dummy treatment. The hazard ratio was 0.99, which is as close to no effect as a result gets,
with a p-value of 0.93 (BMJ, 2025, PMID 39814420).

That trial matters more than any other because it was specifically the confirmatory study that
an earlier, smaller, weaker trial (ETASS, 2013) had asked for. The earlier trial had shown a
favourable direction that did not reach its own significance threshold. When it was tested
properly, the signal disappeared. That is a common and well-understood pattern in medicine,
and it is why the big trial is the one to weigh.

In COVID-19, several pooled analyses reported lower death rates, but the pooled studies
disagreed with each other substantially, the mix appears to lean toward non-randomised
studies, and the authors themselves said randomised trials were still needed. The one
randomised trial retrieved in COVID-19 was null on the outcome it was designed to measure.

In chronic hepatitis B there is genuine but thin evidence, comparing it against an older drug
rather than against placebo, in 199 patients across four trials, published in 2008 before the
field changed.

Grade for benefit in healthy adults: **G (unverified)**. Nothing retrieved speaks to it.

## 2. Is it safe?

We will not tell you something is safe. Here is what is actually known.

Within the trials, safety observations were unremarkable. In the 1,089-patient sepsis trial no
safety outcome differed significantly from placebo. In the earlier sepsis trial no drug-related
severe adverse event was reported. In the COVID-19 pilot, nine serious adverse events occurred
among treated patients and none were deemed related to the drug.

The limits on that are large. Every one of those settings was short-term use, in people who
were acutely ill, in hospital, under medical supervision, using pharmaceutical-grade product.
None of it tells you anything about a healthy adult using something from an unregulated seller
over months or years. There is no retrieved evidence at all on that scenario. Absence of
studied harm is not demonstrated safety.

There is one clear, actionable contraindication in the retrieved labelling-derived source:
people who are deliberately being immunosuppressed, including organ transplant recipients,
unless benefits clearly outweigh risks. That follows directly from what the compound is
proposed to do. There is also a contraindication for anyone with a history of hypersensitivity
to it.

Separately, regulators in the United States cited category-level risks for injectable
compounded peptides: the immune system reacting to the injected peptide itself, manufacturing
impurities including bacteria and heavy metals, and sensitivity to temperature and handling.

## 3. Is it legal?

It depends entirely on where you are, and in the United States the answer is genuinely
unsettled as of the research date of this Guide (2026-07-19).

Outside the United States: thymalfasin is reported to be an approved prescription medicine
under the brand name Zadaxin in a number of countries, principally for chronic hepatitis B.
We could not verify a country list from any primary regulatory source, so we do not publish
one.

In the United States: it has no marketing approval. In 2023 it was placed, along with 18 other
peptides, into Category 2 of the interim 503A bulks list, meaning substances that raise
significant safety risks for compounding. In late 2024 an advisory committee sided with the
agency on that position.

What happened after that is contested. Independent journalism from April 2026 describes the
agency as poised to reverse the 2023 categorisation, with the reversal disputed by former
agency officials. Many peptide sellers state as settled fact that a reclassification happened
in February 2026. We could not verify that claim in any primary or independent source, and it
sits awkwardly against April 2026 reporting describing the change as still pending.

We also have to disclose a limitation in our own research: every fda.gov URL we tried returned
an error, so our regulatory statements rest on secondary reporting rather than primary
documents. Check your own jurisdiction, and do not rely on a seller's characterisation of the
law.

## 5. Why is the evidence so thin, when this seems like a real drug?

This is the most interesting question about this compound, and the answer is that the evidence
is not thin in the usual way. It is unusually substantial and largely negative, which is
different.

Most compounds in the consumer peptide market have almost no human evidence at all. This one
has a phase 3 trial with over a thousand patients. That is a serious research programme by any
standard. It produced a null result.

Where the evidence really is thin is in the places people actually care about. The hepatitis B
data that supports approval abroad is small, old, and compared against a drug that has since
been largely superseded. The COVID-19 randomised evidence is one small pilot that was
terminated early for administrative reasons. And there is nothing at all on healthy adults,
immune support, longevity, or resilience, which is the entire consumer use case.

So the picture is: a real molecule, a real research programme, real regulatory history
somewhere, and an evidence base that either found nothing or never studied the question people
are asking.

## 6. What would change your mind?

Specific things, and we will say them in advance so this is not a moving target.

- A well-designed randomised, placebo-controlled trial in a population resembling the people
  who actually want to use it, meaning generally healthy adults, with a clinical outcome that
  matters rather than a biomarker.
- A second adequately powered randomised trial in any condition showing a clear clinical
  benefit, which would reopen a question the 2025 sepsis trial appears to have closed.
- A completed Cochrane review in chronic hepatitis B. A protocol was registered in 2022 but
  the record we retrieved is a plan only and contains no results. A completed version would
  become the best single source for that condition.
- Published long-term safety data from ongoing elective use in healthy people, in either
  direction.
- Independent analytical testing of gray-market material sold as thymosin alpha-1, showing
  what is actually in it. No such data specific to this compound was retrieved.
- Primary regulatory documentation clarifying the current United States status. We could not
  reach any of it.

If those appear, this Guide gets updated and the change is logged in its correction history.

## 7. There are meta-analyses showing it reduces death in COVID-19. Why are you not counting that?

We are counting it. We are just not counting it as much as it looks like it should count.

The pooled analysis we retrieved reported a mortality risk ratio of 0.59, which sounds
substantial. Three things undercut it.

First, the pooled studies disagreed with each other a great deal (I-squared of 84 percent),
which means the single pooled number is hiding a lot of variation it cannot explain.

Second, we could not confirm how many of the eight pooled studies were randomised. Based on
the authors' own closing statement, the mix appears to lean toward observational studies.
Observational COVID-19 treatment data were notoriously vulnerable to being misled by who
received treatment and when. During that period the standard of care changed rapidly, and
which patients got which add-on therapy was not random.

Third, the authors said explicitly that randomised trials were still required to verify their
findings.

When a positive pooled result from largely non-randomised data sits next to a null randomised
trial, that combination is a recognised signature of confounding rather than of a real effect.
It is not proof that the effect is absent, but it is a reason not to treat the pooled number
as established.

## 8. Sellers say it is "virtually devoid of toxicity" and has been used in thousands of people. Is that true?

That characterisation traces back to manufacturer-associated material, and it is not
independent safety monitoring.

The retrieved drug monograph does state that across clinical experience in over 2,000
individuals no clinically significant adverse reactions attributable to thymosin alpha 1 were
reported. That is worth knowing. It is also exactly the kind of statement that should be read
carefully: it is sponsor-associated experience, not systematic independent pharmacovigilance,
and absence of a reported signal is not the same as demonstrated safety.

It also does not transfer. That experience concerns the pharmaceutical product used under
medical supervision. It says nothing about material bought from an unregulated seller, where
you do not know what is in the vial.

Grade for the sweeping tolerability claim: **E (manufacturer or supplier reported)**.

## 9. It has orphan drug designation and is approved in dozens of countries. Does that not count for something?

It counts for something specific, and it is routinely stretched into something it is not.

Orphan drug designation is a development incentive granted to encourage work on rare diseases.
It is not approval, and it is not a finding that a drug works. Peptide marketing frequently
presents it as though it were approval. We could not retrieve the designation records
themselves, so we record no designation numbers or dates.

Approval abroad is real and does count. It reflects one regulator's decision, on that
regulator's evidence standard, for a specific condition (principally chronic hepatitis B). It
does not transfer to the United States, and it is not a general endorsement for other uses.
The commonly repeated figure of "more than 35 countries" appears mainly in marketing material
and we could not verify it from any primary regulatory or company filing source, so we do not
repeat it as fact.

The honest summary: approved somewhere, not approved in the United States, and designation is
not approval.

## 10. Is it banned in sport?

We cannot tell you, and we are not going to guess.

We attempted to retrieve the official World Anti-Doping Agency Prohibited List and could not
get usable content from it. Every search result asserting a status for thymosin alpha-1 came
from peptide sellers or peptide directories, which are not acceptable sources for an
anti-doping claim. Getting this wrong could end someone's career, so we make no claim at all.

One thing worth flagging: thymosin beta-4, also sold as TB-500, is a completely different
peptide with a different anti-doping situation. The two are confused constantly, and a status
claim about one is not a status claim about the other.

If you are subject to testing, check the current Prohibited List directly or consult your
anti-doping authority. Do not rely on a seller, and do not rely on this page.
