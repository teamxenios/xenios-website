# DSIP Contradictions and Divergences

Where the sources disagree, and what the Guide should say when they do. Source ids refer to
SOURCE_REGISTRY.md. Claim ids refer to CLAIM_TABLE.md.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

Three of the entries below concern claims a member is likely to meet before reading the Guide.
One of them, C-02, is the most quietly devastating point available about this compound, because
the name itself asserts something the human data did not find.

## C-01. Whether DSIP improves sleep in people with insomnia

**Position A.** A 1984 open trial (S03) reported that sleep normalised in six of seven patients
with severe insomnia over follow-up periods of three to seven months, with improvement in
daytime mood and performance. This is the result most often reproduced in commercial material.

**Position B.** A 1987 double-blind crossover study with polysomnographic recording (S01)
concluded that sleep improvement under DSIP treatment was of little clinical significance, with
no significant differences detected against either baseline or placebo on the primary
comparisons. A 1992 double-blind study of 16 patients (S02) concluded that short-term treatment
of chronic insomnia is not likely to be of major therapeutic benefit, with subjective sleep
quality unchanged and the authors describing the significant effects as weak and in part
possibly due to an incidental change in the placebo group.

**Assessment.** This is not a genuine evidential standoff, and the Guide must not present it as
one. The positive result comes from an open, uncontrolled, unblinded trial of seven people with
subjective endpoints. The negative results come from the two double-blind controlled studies.
When a striking open-label result fails to survive blinding and placebo control, the standard
inference is that the original result reflected placebo response and expectancy rather than a
drug effect. The controlled studies get decisive weight. Describing the picture neutrally as
"mixed evidence" would imply a live scientific disagreement that does not exist.

**What the Guide says.** No controlled human study supports a clinically meaningful sleep
benefit. See DSIP-H-03, DSIP-H-05, DSIP-H-06, DSIP-H-08, and the prohibitions at DSIP-X-01 and
DSIP-X-03.

## C-02. Whether DSIP actually promotes delta (slow wave) sleep, the property it is named for

**Position A.** The 1974 discovery work and the 1984 review (S06) describe delta-sleep induction
in rabbits, rats, and mice, and the compound is named for that effect.

**Position B.** The 1987 double-blind human study found that slow wave sleep was not modified
(S01). The increase in total sleep was driven by an increase in stage 2 sleep instead. The 2006
review (S05) reports that DSIP itself did not demonstrate clear effects in animal work while
certain structural analogues did.

**Assessment.** The human controlled data do not support the specific delta-sleep claim embedded
in the compound's own name. This is a significant point and it deserves to be stated directly:
the name asserts a mechanism that the best available human measurement did not find. Marketing
material that leans on delta sleep as a mechanism is leaning on the name, not on the human data.

**What the Guide says.** See DSIP-H-04, DSIP-M-05, and the prohibition at DSIP-X-04. A member
should leave the Guide able to explain, in one sentence, why the name is not evidence.

## C-03. Whether DSIP is a genuine endogenous sleep factor

**Position A.** The compound has been described since 1974 as an endogenous nonapeptide isolated
from the cerebral venous blood of sleeping rabbits, and DSIP-like immunoreactive material has
been reported in brain and peripheral tissues.

**Position B.** The 2006 review (S05) states that the sleep-factor hypothesis is extremely poorly
documented and still weak, that the peptide's natural occurrence and biological activity remain
obscure, and that no gene, precursor, or receptor has ever been isolated. The same review
proposes that a DSIP-like peptide, rather than DSIP, may account for reported effects.

**Assessment.** Immunoassay detection of DSIP-like material is much weaker evidence than gene or
receptor identification, because antibodies can cross-react with unrelated molecules. After more
than fifty years the foundational biology remains unestablished. This is an unusual weakness. For
most compounds the argument is about whether an established molecule produces a claimed effect.
Here the argument reaches back to whether the molecule is what it is said to be.

**What the Guide says.** DSIP is described as a hypothesised factor whose endogenous status was
never confirmed, never as a known human sleep hormone. See DSIP-I-01, DSIP-I-03, DSIP-I-05, and
the prohibition at DSIP-X-05.

## C-04. Where the newest research points away from the compound, not towards it

This is not a disagreement between sources. It is a gap between how the 2024 literature is
presented commercially and what it actually says.

The only recent primary research located (S07) is a study in 48 male Kun-Ming mice with insomnia
induced by p-chlorophenylalanine. The tested article was a DSIP fusion peptide engineered to
cross the blood-brain barrier and secreted from the yeast Pichia pastoris. It is not DSIP, and
it is not human. In that mouse model the engineered fusion outperformed plain DSIP, which is an
implicit finding that unmodified DSIP performed less well.

There is a second implication worth naming. A construct built specifically to cross the
blood-brain barrier implies that unmodified DSIP crosses it poorly. So the newest work, read
honestly, supplies a reason for doubt about the compound members actually encounter.

**What the Guide says.** See DSIP-P-01, DSIP-P-02, DSIP-U-06, and the prohibition at DSIP-X-06.
The 2024 study may never be cited as modern validation.

## C-05. Where the confident claim and the evidence base are mismatched in size

Also not a disagreement between scientific sources, but the most consequential mismatch a member
will encounter.

- Retailer, telehealth-marketing, clinic, and peptide-blog pages assert an absence of dependency,
  withdrawal, and organ toxicity, and an absence of any identified lethal amount in animal models
  (S15). None of these pages is scientific evidence and none was traceable to a retrieved primary
  study.
- The entire located human literature is three small studies from 1984, 1987, and 1992, with a
  combined enrolled population in the order of a few dozen people, none designed or powered as a
  safety study, and no long-term follow-up.
- A literature of that size could not detect the absence of those harms even if the absence were
  real. The claims are therefore not merely unsupported. They assert a kind of knowledge that no
  one is in a position to have.

**What the Guide says.** Safety is described as uncharacterised rather than reassuring. See
DSIP-S-01 and the prohibition at DSIP-X-02.

## C-06. Where the search results themselves are the problem

A member researching DSIP independently will meet a heavily distorted picture before they meet
any evidence. A large majority of search results returned during research were retailer,
telehealth-marketing, and peptide-blog pages presenting confident efficacy, safety, and
administration claims. None was used as evidence here.

This pollution is worth naming in the Guide rather than only in the working files, because it
explains a specific experience a member will have. The commercial sources are far more confident
than the actual literature, they are more numerous, and they appear first. Confidence in this
subject area correlates with commercial interest rather than with evidence.

**What the Guide says.** The commercial presence around DSIP is described as commercial rather
than scientific in origin, and the reader is told plainly why the internet sounds more certain
than the science.

## C-07. Jurisdictional differences

No genuine conflict between jurisdictions was found, because no jurisdiction produced a
successfully retrieved primary statement.

The United States position is recorded in REGULATORY_STATUS.md and rests entirely on
search-index snippets and secondary trade reporting, because four separate FDA URLs returned
HTTP 404 (S11, S12). The anti-doping position could not be established, because the Prohibited
List PDF returned empty content (S13). For the European Union, the United Kingdom, Canada, and
Australia, no regulator database was queried at all.

The absence of a conflict here reflects the absence of retrievable information, not agreement
among regulators.

## C-08. A note on the integrity of this record

Two things should be held together by any reviewer.

First, the weakness. Every FDA statement in this folder is marked verified=false. Four FDA URLs
failed. The WADA List could not be read. One early human study (S04) was never retrieved. All
three human evidence entries rest on abstracts rather than full texts, and the sample size of the
best-controlled study is unknown to this record.

Second, the check that was run. A deliberately fabricated identifier, PMID 99999999999, was
queried and returned HTTP 404 Not Found, while genuine records queried in the same batch returned
full content (S10). This demonstrates that the retrieval path was real and that a nonexistent
record fails rather than being confabulated into existence. It does not repair the gaps above,
and it is not evidence about DSIP. It establishes only that the verified entries were genuinely
retrieved and the failures were genuine failures.
