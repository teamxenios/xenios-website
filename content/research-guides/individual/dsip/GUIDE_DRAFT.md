# DSIP (Delta Sleep-Inducing Peptide)

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## One-minute summary

The age of the literature is the finding, and it should be the first thing anyone knows about
this compound.

Every human sleep study of DSIP that could be located was published between 1981 and 1992. There
has been no modern human replication in roughly three and a half decades. A direct query of
ClinicalTrials.gov, the main United States registry of clinical studies, returned zero registered
DSIP trials of any kind (verified 2026-07-21). A hypothesis that generated active human study in
the 1980s and then generated none for thirty years is best read as a line of inquiry that was
tried and quietly abandoned, not as a promising idea awaiting rediscovery.

- **The human evidence is three small studies.** The one most often cited as impressive, a 1984
  paper, enrolled seven patients with no control group, no blinding, and subjective endpoints.
- **When the question was put to controlled tests, the effect largely evaporated.** A 1987
  double-blind crossover study concluded that the sleep improvement was of little clinical
  significance. A 1992 double-blind study of 16 patients found effects the authors themselves
  called weak and possibly attributable to an incidental change in the placebo group, with
  subjective sleep quality unchanged, and concluded that treatment is not likely to be of major
  therapeutic benefit. That pattern, a striking open-label result that fails to survive blinding,
  is the classic signature of a placebo response rather than a drug effect. This Guide says so
  rather than describing the picture neutrally as mixed.
- **The human data do not support the compound's own name.** The 1987 study found that slow wave
  sleep, which is delta sleep, was not modified. The increase in total sleep came from stage 2
  sleep instead. Material that leans on delta sleep as a mechanism is leaning on the name, not on
  the human data.
- **The basic biology was never established.** No DSIP gene, precursor peptide, or receptor has
  been isolated in any species in the more than fifty years since the 1974 rabbit isolation. A
  2006 review in the Journal of Neurochemistry states plainly that the sleep-factor hypothesis is
  extremely poorly documented and still weak, and goes as far as suggesting that a DSIP-like
  peptide rather than DSIP may account for reported effects. This is an unusual weakness. The
  usual argument is whether an established molecule does what is claimed. Here the argument
  reaches back to whether the molecule is what it is said to be.
- **The only recent research is not about DSIP and is not in humans.** A 2024 mouse study tested
  an engineered fusion peptide built to cross the blood-brain barrier, and in that mouse model
  the engineered construct outperformed plain DSIP. It cannot be presented as modern validation.
  If anything it points the other way.
- **Safety is uncharacterised, not reassuring.** No located study was designed or powered as a
  safety study, none had long-term follow-up, and combined human exposure across the entire
  literature is in the order of a few dozen people. Confident online claims of no dependency, no
  withdrawal, and no organ toxicity are unsupported market claims. A literature this small could
  not detect such signals even if they existed.
- **It is unapproved everywhere identified.** The regulatory picture is set out below with dates
  and sources, and carries an important honesty caveat: all four FDA primary documents failed to
  retrieve during research, so every FDA statement here is unverified and must be confirmed by a
  human before publication.

Xenios does not sell DSIP, does not supply it, and does not tell members how to obtain or use it.
This Guide exists so that a member reading about DSIP elsewhere has an accurate account of what
is and is not known.

**Overall claim grade for "DSIP improves sleep in people": G (Unverified).**
**Overall claim grade for "DSIP was studied in three small human trials between 1984 and 1992, and the two controlled ones concluded against clinical benefit": C (Early human evidence).**
**Overall claim grade for "DSIP produced sleep-related effects in animal models": D (Preclinical).**

## What it is

DSIP stands for delta sleep-inducing peptide. It is a nonapeptide, meaning a chain of nine amino
acids, with the sequence Trp-Ala-Gly-Gly-Asp-Ala-Ser-Gly-Glu, written in single-letter code as
WAGGDASGE. It was first described in 1974 by the Swiss Schoenenberger-Monnier group, isolated from
the cerebral venous blood of rabbits during induced sleep. In regulatory contexts it is also
referred to as emideltide.
[UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check. The sequence, the discovery year,
and the regulatory name came from tertiary background used for orientation during research, not
from a primary chemical or regulatory source.]

The critical identity point is not the sequence. It is what has never been found.

**No DSIP gene, precursor peptide, or receptor has ever been identified in any species.** The 2006
review in the Journal of Neurochemistry by Kovalzon and Strekalova notes that the link between
DSIP and sleep has never been further characterised, in part because the DSIP gene was never
isolated (PMID 16539679).

Three consequences follow, and each matters more than it first appears.

1. **The molecule's endogenous status is unconfirmed.** The synthetic peptide sold and studied
   under this name cannot be confirmed to correspond to a genuine endogenous human signalling
   molecule. DSIP-like immunoreactive material has been reported in tissues, but immunoassay
   detection is much weaker evidence than gene or receptor identification, because antibodies can
   cross-react with unrelated molecules.
2. **There is no established target.** Without a receptor, there is no characterised site of
   action, and downstream or off-target effects in a person cannot be predicted from the existing
   literature.
3. **The reported effects may not belong to this molecule.** The same 2006 review proposes that a
   DSIP-like peptide, rather than DSIP itself, may account for effects reported in the literature.

One further note from that review: BLAST searching, a standard method for comparing a sequence
against known sequences, aligned the DSIP sequence with a hypothetical bacterial protein, which
raises a question about the peptide's presumed mammalian origin. That is a question raised in a
review, not a demonstration, and it is reported here as such.

## Why interest exists

The interest is easy to describe and worth understanding without endorsing it.

The story is unusually appealing. A peptide is isolated from the blood of sleeping rabbits in
1974, it is named for the deep, restorative stage of sleep that people most want more of, and the
proposal is that the body has its own sleep signal that can simply be supplied. Compared with
sedative medication, an endogenous signal sounds gentler by construction. Poor sleep is close to
universal, the available options carry real drawbacks, and the unmet need is genuine.

The early animal work fed that story. A 1984 review reported delta-sleep induction in rabbits,
rats, and mice (PMID 6145137), and the compound was named accordingly. The 1984 open human trial
then reported that sleep normalised in six of seven patients with severe insomnia.

Four things should be held alongside that interest.

First, an appealing origin story is not an outcome. Where the compound came from tells you nothing
about what it does when introduced into a body.

Second, the interest is unusually old. Almost every element of the appeal traces to work published
between 1974 and 1984. Nothing has been added to the human record since 1992.

Third, the compound is not a specific sleep agent even in the animal literature. The same 1984
review frames DSIP as a multifunctional neuroregulatory peptide affecting electrophysiological
activity, brain neurotransmitter levels, circadian and locomotor patterns, and hormonal levels in
animal models, rather than acting as a sedative. Breadth of reported effect is not strength of
effect.

Fourth, most of what a member will read about DSIP was written by someone selling it. That is
addressed directly in the quality and identity section below, because it changes how the whole
subject appears from the outside.

## Terminology

Every technical term used in this Guide, in plain English.

- **Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids, the building blocks of proteins. Shorter than a
  protein.
- **Nonapeptide.** A peptide made of exactly nine amino acids. DSIP is one.
- **Endogenous.** Produced naturally inside the body, as opposed to introduced from outside.
- **Gene.** The stretch of DNA that carries the instructions for making a specific protein or
  peptide. If no gene for a peptide has been found, the body has not been shown to make it.
- **Precursor peptide.** A larger peptide that the body cuts down to produce a smaller active one.
- **Receptor.** A structure on or in a cell that a signalling molecule binds to in order to produce
  an effect. If no receptor is known, the site of action is unknown.
- **Nonapeptide sequence.** The specific order of the nine amino acids, which is what defines the
  molecule chemically.
- **Emideltide.** The name used for DSIP in some regulatory contexts.
- **Delta sleep, also called slow wave sleep.** The deepest stage of non-REM sleep, identified by
  slow, large brain waves on an EEG recording. This is the sleep stage the compound is named for.
- **Stage 2 sleep.** A lighter stage of non-REM sleep. In the 1987 human study, the increase in
  total sleep came from this stage rather than from delta sleep.
- **REM sleep.** Rapid eye movement sleep, the stage most associated with dreaming.
- **Polysomnography.** Overnight recording of brain waves, breathing, and movement in a sleep
  laboratory. It measures sleep objectively rather than relying on what a person reports.
- **Sleep latency.** How long it takes to fall asleep.
- **Sleep efficiency.** The proportion of time in bed actually spent asleep.
- **Subjective sleep quality.** How well a person judges they slept, as distinct from what the
  recording shows. For a person with insomnia this is arguably the outcome that matters most.
- **Double-blind.** Neither the participant nor the researcher assessing them knows who received
  the active substance and who received placebo. This is what prevents expectation from creating
  an apparent effect.
- **Placebo.** An inactive substance given for comparison, so that improvement caused by
  expectation can be separated from improvement caused by the substance.
- **Crossover design.** Each participant receives both the active substance and the placebo at
  different times, and acts as their own comparison.
- **Parallel groups design.** Different participants receive the active substance and the placebo,
  and the groups are compared.
- **Open trial, also called open-label.** Everyone knows who is receiving what. There is no
  blinding, and often no control group at all. This is the weakest common design.
- **Uncontrolled.** No comparison group. Any change observed cannot be attributed to the substance
  rather than to time, expectation, or chance.
- **Regression to the mean.** The tendency for people recruited when their symptoms are at their
  worst to improve somewhat regardless of treatment, simply because extremes tend to be followed
  by less extreme values.
- **Investigator expectancy.** The tendency for a researcher who expects a result to observe it,
  particularly when outcomes are judged rather than measured.
- **Underpowered.** A study with too few participants to reliably detect an effect even if one
  exists.
- **Preclinical.** Research done before any human study, meaning in animals or in cultured cells.
- **Fusion peptide.** A peptide joined to another sequence to give it a property it lacks, such as
  the ability to cross into the brain. A fusion peptide is a different substance from the original.
- **Blood-brain barrier.** The lining that restricts what passes from the bloodstream into the
  brain.
- **p-chlorophenylalanine (PCPA).** A chemical used to induce insomnia-like states in laboratory
  animals so that sleep research can be done in them.
- **Pichia pastoris.** A species of yeast used to manufacture peptides and proteins in the
  laboratory.
- **Kun-Ming mice.** A strain of laboratory mouse.
- **Hippocampus.** A brain region involved in memory. Hippocampal tissue morphology means the
  appearance of that tissue under a microscope.
- **Non-monotonic response.** A response that does not increase steadily as more is given. It is a
  signal that the underlying effect was unstable.
- **Pharmacokinetics.** What the body does to a substance: absorption, distribution, metabolism,
  and elimination, including how long it lasts.
- **Immunogenicity.** Whether a substance provokes an immune response against itself, such as
  antibody formation.
- **Immunoassay.** A laboratory test that detects a substance using antibodies. Antibodies can
  cross-react with unrelated molecules, which is why an immunoassay signal is weaker evidence of
  identity than finding a gene or a receptor.
- **BLAST search.** A standard computational method for comparing a biological sequence against
  databases of known sequences.
- **Systematic review and meta-analysis.** Structured methods for combining all studies on a
  question. None exists for DSIP and sleep.
- **Pharmacopoeial standard.** An official published specification a material can be tested
  against. Without one, there is no agreed benchmark for identity or purity.
- **503A Bulks List.** A United States framework governing which raw substances pharmacies may use
  in compounding, meaning preparing customised medicines.
- **Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee (PCAC).** The FDA advisory committee that reviews
  substances nominated for that list. Its recommendations advise FDA and are not final FDA
  decisions.
- **WADA category S0.** The World Anti-Doping Agency category covering non-approved substances.

## Proposed mechanism

Everything in this section is **proposed**. None of it has been confirmed in a living human being,
and the section carries an unusual problem: the mechanism named in the compound's own title is the
one the best human measurement did not find. Claim grade for this section: **D (Preclinical)**,
with any confident human mechanism statement graded **G (Unverified)**.

**The historical proposal.** DSIP was described as an endogenous sleep-promoting factor that
modulates sleep and wake regulation, with the original animal work reporting induction of spindle
and delta EEG activity. This framing dates from the 1970s and 1980s.

**A multifunctional peptide, not a sedative.** The 1984 review by Graf and Kastin frames DSIP as a
multifunctional neuroregulatory peptide affecting electrophysiological activity, brain
neurotransmitter levels, circadian and locomotor patterns, and hormonal levels **in animal
models**, rather than acting as a sedative (PMID 6145137). Grade D.

**Neurotransmitter normalisation, in mice, with an engineered construct.** The 2024 study reported
that in male Kun-Ming mice with insomnia induced by p-chlorophenylalanine, an engineered DSIP
fusion peptide normalised serotonin, melatonin, dopamine, and glutamate levels
(DOI 10.3389/fphar.2024.1439536). The tested article was a fusion construct, not DSIP, and the
animals were mice. Grade D.

**Why none of this amounts to characterised pharmacology.**

- No DSIP receptor has ever been identified, so there is no established site of action.
- No gene or precursor has been isolated, so the body has not been shown to produce the molecule.
- The 2006 review reports that DSIP itself did not demonstrate clear effects in animal work while
  certain structural analogues did show sleep-promoting activity in animal models, and proposes
  that a DSIP-like peptide rather than DSIP may be responsible (PMID 16539679).
- In humans, the 1987 double-blind study found slow wave sleep was not modified at all
  (PMID 3583493), so the delta-sleep mechanism implied by the compound's name is not supported by
  the human data.

A mechanism must not be presented in this Guide, or anywhere else, as if it were characterised
pharmacology. The honest summary is that a mechanism was proposed in the 1970s, was never
established, and was contradicted in its central claim by the one controlled human measurement
that tested it.

## Human evidence

Human evidence exists for DSIP, which distinguishes it from many unapproved peptides. Three points
about that evidence matter more than its existence.

It is old. It is very small. And once controlled, it is negative.

**What was searched, and what came back.**

- **ClinicalTrials.gov**, queried directly through its public API for the term DSIP, returned
  **zero studies** (verified 2026-07-21). A second query on the intervention "delta sleep-inducing
  peptide" returned a single record, NCT05251207, which is a study of L-carnitine supplementation
  and insulin resistance and involves no DSIP whatsoever. That is an irrelevant fuzzy text match.
  The correct reading is that there are no registered DSIP trials at all.
- **No systematic review or meta-analysis** of DSIP and sleep was located. Only narrative reviews
  from 1984 and 2006 exist. With three small heterogeneous trials, a meta-analysis would not be
  meaningful in any case.
- **No study of any kind** located for DSIP was designed or powered as a safety study.

**How to read these three studies together.** The strongest-looking result is the weakest study.
Kaeser 1984 is the paper most often reproduced in commercial material, and it is an open,
uncontrolled, unblinded trial of seven people with subjective endpoints. A design of that type
cannot separate a drug effect from placebo response, regression to the mean, or investigator
expectancy. When the same question was put to double-blind control, in 1987 and again in 1992, both
sets of authors concluded against clinical benefit in their own words.

That pattern, a striking open-label result that fails to survive blinding and placebo control, is
the classic signature of a placebo response rather than a drug effect. This Guide states that
rather than describing the evidence neutrally as mixed, because calling it mixed would imply a live
scientific disagreement that the record does not contain.

Two further details deserve emphasis. In 1987, slow wave sleep, the very thing the compound is
named for, was not modified. In 1992, subjective sleep quality, arguably the outcome that matters
most to a person with insomnia, was unchanged.

Claim grade for any statement of the form "DSIP improves sleep in people": **G (Unverified)**. No
claim in this Guide reaches grade A or B.

## Preclinical evidence

Every finding below carries its species or model in the same sentence. None of it establishes an
effect in a person.

**Rabbits, rats, mice, and cats (1984 narrative review).** A review by Graf and Kastin in
Neuroscience and Biobehavioral Reviews reported delta-sleep induction across rabbits, rats, and
mice, with a more pronounced REM effect in cats, indicating that the response is not consistent
across species (PMID 6145137). The review also describes a non-monotonic response in animal models,
meaning the response did not increase steadily with either how much was given or when it was given,
which is a signal that the underlying effect was unstable. It further describes effects on
neurotransmitter levels, circadian and locomotor patterns, and hormonal levels in animal models,
framing DSIP as a multifunctional neuroregulatory peptide rather than a specific sleep agent.
Grade D.

**Mice, 2024, an engineered fusion peptide (primary research).** In 48 male Kun-Ming mice of
approximately eight weeks of age, with insomnia induced by p-chlorophenylalanine, a DSIP fusion
peptide engineered to cross the blood-brain barrier and secreted from the yeast Pichia pastoris was
reported to reduce wakefulness time, normalise serotonin, melatonin, dopamine, and glutamate
levels, decrease anxiety-like and depressive-like behaviours, and improve hippocampal tissue
morphology (DOI 10.3389/fphar.2024.1439536, Frontiers in Pharmacology 2024). Grade D.

**Two interpretive caveats on that 2024 study, both of which cut against the compound.**

1. **The fusion peptide outperformed plain DSIP in that mouse model.** That is an implicit finding
   that unmodified DSIP performed less well. The newest research points away from the compound, not
   towards it.
2. **A construct built specifically to cross the blood-brain barrier implies unmodified DSIP
   crosses it poorly.** The engineering exists because the plain molecule was judged inadequate.

This study is the only recent primary research located, and it is a mouse study of a different
substance. It cannot be presented as modern validation of DSIP for human sleep.

**A critical review of the preclinical record (2006).** Kovalzon and Strekalova, in the Journal of
Neurochemistry, report that certain structural analogues showed sleep-promoting activity in animal
models while DSIP itself did not demonstrate clear effects, and propose that a DSIP-like peptide
rather than DSIP may account for observed results. They state that the hypothesis regarding DSIP as
a sleep factor is extremely poorly documented and still weak, and that the peptide's natural
occurrence and biological activity remain obscure (PMID 16539679). Grade D.

**A general caution.** The preclinical record here is not a body of consistent animal evidence
awaiting human confirmation. It contains a species inconsistency, a non-monotonic response, a
critical review reporting that the compound itself did not show clear effects, and a modern study
in which an engineered replacement beat it.

## Contradictions and disagreement

Where sources genuinely disagree, both positions are stated with an assessment of which the
retrieved evidence supports. Full versions are in CONTRADICTIONS.md.

**1. Whether DSIP improves sleep in people with insomnia.**

- *Position A.* Kaeser 1984 (PMID 6391926) reported sleep normalised in six of seven patients with
  severe insomnia over three to seven months of follow-up, with improved daytime mood and
  performance.
- *Position B.* Monti et al 1987 (PMID 3583493) concluded sleep improvement was of little clinical
  significance, with no significant differences against baseline or placebo on the primary
  comparisons. Bes et al 1992 concluded that short-term treatment of chronic insomnia is not likely
  to be of major therapeutic benefit, with subjective sleep quality unchanged.
- *Assessment.* This is not a genuine evidential standoff. The positive result comes from an open,
  uncontrolled, unblinded trial of seven people. The negative results come from the two
  double-blind controlled studies. When a striking open-label result fails to survive blinding and
  placebo control, the standard inference is placebo response and expectancy rather than drug
  effect. The controlled studies get decisive weight.

**2. Whether DSIP actually promotes delta sleep, the property it is named for.**

- *Position A.* The 1974 discovery work and the 1984 review describe delta-sleep induction in
  rabbits, rats, and mice, and the compound is named for that effect.
- *Position B.* Monti et al 1987 found in humans that slow wave sleep was **not** modified, with the
  increase in total sleep driven by stage 2 sleep instead. Kovalzon and Strekalova 2006 report that
  DSIP itself did not show clear effects in animal work while some structural analogues did.
- *Assessment.* The human controlled data do not support the specific delta-sleep claim embedded in
  the compound's own name. The name asserts a mechanism that the best available human measurement
  did not find. Marketing material that leans on delta sleep is leaning on the name, not on the
  human data.

**3. Whether DSIP is a genuine endogenous sleep factor.**

- *Position A.* Described since 1974 as an endogenous nonapeptide isolated from the cerebral venous
  blood of sleeping rabbits, with DSIP-like immunoreactive material reported in brain and peripheral
  tissues.
- *Position B.* Kovalzon and Strekalova 2006 state that the sleep-factor hypothesis is extremely
  poorly documented and still weak, that natural occurrence and biological activity remain obscure,
  and that no gene, precursor, or receptor has ever been isolated. They propose that a DSIP-like
  peptide, not DSIP, may account for reported effects.
- *Assessment.* Immunoassay detection of DSIP-like material is much weaker evidence than gene or
  receptor identification, because antibodies can cross-react with unrelated molecules. After more
  than fifty years the foundational biology remains unestablished. The honest description is a
  hypothesised factor whose endogenous status was never confirmed, not a known human sleep hormone.

## Limitations

Limitations of the evidence base itself:

- All human evidence is 34 to 45 years old. The methodological standards of that era, in particular
  single-digit uncontrolled open trials, would not be accepted as evidence of efficacy today.
- The total human evidence base is three small studies with a combined enrolled population in the
  order of a few dozen people.
- The sample size of the best-controlled study, the 1987 crossover trial, was not stated in the
  retrieved abstract and is unknown to this Guide.
- The 1992 study enrolled 16 patients, which is severely underpowered for detecting a real effect.
- No study located was designed or powered as a safety study, and none had long-term follow-up.
- No systematic review or meta-analysis exists.
- No study has been replicated. Not one.
- The only recent primary research is a mouse study of an engineered fusion peptide rather than of
  DSIP itself.
- The preclinical record contains an inconsistent cross-species response and a non-monotonic
  response, both of which weaken rather than support the case.
- The foundational biology, meaning a gene, a precursor, and a receptor, was never established.

Limitations of this Guide's research process, stated so a reader can judge its reliability:

- **All four FDA primary URLs failed during research**, returning HTTP 404 or unreadable binary.
  Every FDA statement in this Guide therefore rests on search-index snippets and secondary trade
  reporting and is marked unverified. A human must confirm each one against FDA primary documents
  before publication.
- **The 2026 WADA Prohibited List PDF returned empty content.** DSIP's specific anti-doping status
  was not confirmed from WADA directly, and the reasoning offered below is an inference from a
  category definition, not a retrieved ruling.
- **PMID 7028502 was never retrieved.** The fetch returned a reCAPTCHA rather than the record, so
  one early human study remains entirely unexamined and the human evidence section is incomplete by
  exactly one study.
- **No full texts were obtained.** All three human evidence entries rest on abstracts, so no
  risk-of-bias assessment at full-text level has been performed.
- **No regulator database for the European Union, the United Kingdom, Canada, or Australia was
  queried.** The non-approval statement for those jurisdictions is background knowledge and is
  flagged as such.
- **No adverse event reporting database, such as FAERS, was searched.**
- Only ClinicalTrials.gov was searched. The EU Clinical Trials Register, ISRCTN, the WHO ICTRP, and
  the Japanese and Chinese registries were not searched.

## Quality and identity

This section is about the material itself, separately from the science, and about the information
environment surrounding it.

**There is no reference standard to check anything against.** Because no DSIP gene or receptor has
been identified and there is no approved reference product in any jurisdiction, there is no
pharmacopoeial standard against which unapproved material can be verified for identity, purity, or
content. Reporting on the FDA review indicates FDA cited that the substances under consideration
are not well characterised.
[UNVERIFIED - FDA primary documents returned HTTP 404 this session. Requires human confirmation.]

**No product testing study was located.** No peer-reviewed study testing material sold as DSIP for
identity, purity, sterility, or endotoxin content was found. That is a gap in knowledge, not a
clean result. Nobody appears to have checked.

**The information environment is the finding here.** A large majority of search results returned
during research were retailer, telehealth-marketing, and peptide-blog pages presenting confident
efficacy, safety, and administration claims. None was used as evidence in this Guide. This is worth
saying plainly, because a member researching DSIP independently will encounter those sources first
and will find them far more confident than the actual literature. In this subject area, confidence
correlates with commercial interest rather than with evidence.

**Specific market claims that are rebutted rather than reported.** Commercial pages assert an
absence of dependency, withdrawal, and organ toxicity, and an absence of any identified lethal
amount in animal models. These are recorded as grade E or G market claims. None was traceable to a
retrieved primary study. More importantly, a human literature of three small studies and a few
dozen people could not establish the absence of those harms even if the absence were real. The
claims assert a kind of knowledge that nobody is in a position to have.

**Identity confusion is a practical risk.** Results for structural analogues must not be presented
as DSIP results, and the 2024 mouse work concerns an engineered fusion peptide rather than DSIP.
The 2006 review's proposal that a DSIP-like peptide rather than DSIP may account for reported
effects makes this more than a labelling nicety.

## Safety categories

Nothing in this section is reassurance. These are categories of concern that remain open because
nobody has closed them.

**1. Human safety data are essentially absent. This is the primary safety finding.** The only human
studies located are three small trials from 1984, 1987, and 1992, with a combined enrolled
population in the order of a few dozen people. None was designed or powered as a safety study, and
none had long-term follow-up. Zero registered trials exist on ClinicalTrials.gov, confirmed
directly against the registry API on 2026-07-21. **Absence of reported harm in a literature this
small is not evidence of safety.** A study of seven or sixteen people cannot detect an uncommon
harm.

**2. Unassessed immunogenicity.** Reporting on the FDA's 2026 advisory committee review indicates
FDA cited insufficient human safety data including unassessed immunogenicity risk across the
peptides under review. Immunogenicity means the body may mount an immune response against the
substance. It has not been characterised for DSIP in any retrieved study.
[UNVERIFIED - the FDA primary documents could not be retrieved this session, all returning HTTP 404.
This rests on secondary reporting and requires human confirmation against the FDA source before
publication.]

**3. No identified target, so no predictable consequences.** Because no receptor, gene, or precursor
has ever been identified, downstream and off-target effects in a person cannot be predicted from
the existing literature. This is not a statement that harm is likely. It is a statement that the
usual basis for reasoning about harm does not exist here.

**4. No characterised human pharmacokinetics.** Reviews note rapid degradation, and how much
administered peptide reaches the central nervous system is not established. Whether meaningful
exposure is achieved at all, by any route, is unknown. The 2024 mouse work is relevant in an
unflattering way: a construct was engineered specifically to cross the blood-brain barrier, which
implies unmodified DSIP crosses it poorly.

**5. One participant-level note from the human record.** The 1984 open trial flagged complications
in patients with a long-standing history of drug dependence. That is the only participant-level
complication note located in any human study. It is reported plainly here. It should not be
extrapolated into a characterised risk, and its existence must not be read to imply that other
risks were examined and ruled out.

**6. Product quality as a safety issue in its own right.** Separately from the molecule, material
obtained outside a regulated supply chain has unverified identity, purity, sterility, and endotoxin

## Interactions

No interaction data for DSIP was located in any retrieved source. No study of DSIP alongside any
medication or supplement was found. Interactions are therefore unstudied, which is not the same as
absent.

The following categories would require review by a qualified professional, because they intersect
with the proposed area of action or with the population most likely to be interested:

- **Sedatives, hypnotics, and sleep medication of any kind**, prescribed or over the counter.
- **Alcohol**, which affects sleep architecture directly.
- **Antidepressants, antipsychotics, and mood stabilisers**, many of which affect the
  neurotransmitter systems named in the animal work.
- **Any other central nervous system medication**, including for pain, anxiety, seizures, or
  attention.
- **Medication for a diagnosed sleep disorder**, including sleep apnoea, where an unstudied
  substance could obscure or complicate the clinical picture.
- **Melatonin and other sleep supplements.**
- **Any other unapproved peptide or research compound taken at the same time**, which makes both
  benefit and harm impossible to attribute.
- **Pregnancy and breastfeeding**, where no data exists at all.

There is a further point specific to sleep. Sleep problems are frequently a symptom of something
else, including depression, anxiety, pain, sleep apnoea, thyroid disease, and medication effects.
Anything that alters sleep without addressing an underlying cause can delay a diagnosis that
matters.

## Unknowns

An explicit list of what is not known.

- Whether DSIP is a genuine endogenous mammalian peptide at all. No gene, receptor, or precursor has
  been identified in over fifty years.
- Whether any of the reported human sleep effects would survive a modern, adequately powered,
  blinded trial. This has never been tested.
- The human safety profile over any duration. No located study was designed as a safety study and
  none had long-term follow-up.
- Immunogenicity in humans. Flagged as an unassessed risk in the regulatory context and not
  characterised in any retrieved study.
- Human pharmacokinetics and stability. Reviews note rapid degradation, and how much administered
  peptide reaches the central nervous system is not established.
- Whether findings in the 2024 mouse fusion-peptide work have any bearing on unmodified DSIP in
  humans. The fusion construct was specifically engineered to cross the blood-brain barrier, which
  implies unmodified DSIP does so poorly.
- Whether material sold outside regulated channels contains what it claims, in the absence of any
  approved reference product or pharmacopoeial standard.
- The content of PMID 7028502, an early human sleep study that could not be retrieved this session.
- The sample size of the 1987 crossover study, the best-controlled human study located.
- Interactions with any medicine or supplement. No interaction data was located.
- Behaviour in specific populations, including pregnancy, breastfeeding, children, older adults,
  people with psychiatric or neurological conditions, and people taking sedative, hypnotic, or other
  central nervous system medication.
- Whether DSIP is specifically addressed by any anti-doping authority, as opposed to falling under a
  catch-all category by inference.
- The verified FDA position on emideltide, because the FDA primary documents could not be retrieved.

## Questions for a qualified professional

If a member wants to discuss DSIP with a clinician, these questions produce a useful conversation.
They are written to be asked as they stand.

1. Every human study of this compound is from between 1981 and 1992, and there are no registered
   trials at all. Does that match what you can find, and what do you make of a compound that was
   studied and then dropped?
2. The two double-blind studies concluded that the benefit was of little clinical significance. How
   much weight would you give a positive open-label trial of seven people against that?
3. The compound is named for delta sleep, and the controlled human study found delta sleep was not
   changed. Does that matter as much as it seems to?
4. No gene or receptor for this peptide has ever been found. How does that change how you think
   about anything reported for it?
5. What is actually causing my sleep problem, and have we ruled out the causes that need treating in
   their own right, such as sleep apnoea, depression, anxiety, pain, thyroid problems, or a
   medication I already take?
6. What approved options with real human evidence, including non-drug approaches for insomnia, have
   we not tried yet?
7. I take the following medications and have the following conditions. Which of them would make any
   unstudied sleep-active compound a particularly poor idea for me?
8. If something changed my sleep without addressing the cause, what would you be worried about
   missing?
9. I compete in a tested sport. Who is the right authority for me to ask about whether this falls
   under the non-approved substances category?
10. If new human evidence on DSIP appeared, what would you want to see in it before it changed your
    advice?

This Guide does not give medical advice and does not tell anyone what to do. These questions belong
with a clinician who knows your history.
