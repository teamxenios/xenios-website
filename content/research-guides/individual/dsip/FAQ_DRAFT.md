# DSIP: questions members actually ask

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

These answers follow the same rule as the Guide itself. Where the honest answer is that the
evidence points the other way, that is the answer given.

---

## 1. Does it work?

The controlled human evidence says no, and that is a stronger answer than "nobody knows".

Three human sleep studies exist and all are from the 1980s and early 1990s. The two that were
double-blind and controlled both concluded against clinical benefit, in their own authors' words. A
1987 double-blind crossover study with overnight sleep-laboratory recording concluded that the
sleep improvement was of little clinical significance, with no significant differences against
either baseline or placebo on the primary comparisons (PMID 3583493). A 1992 double-blind study of
16 patients found effects the authors described as weak and possibly attributable to an incidental
change in the placebo group, and concluded that short-term treatment of chronic insomnia is not
likely to be of major therapeutic benefit (DOI 10.1159/000118919). In that study, subjective sleep
quality, arguably the outcome a person with insomnia cares about most, did not change at all.

Nothing has been added since. A direct query of ClinicalTrials.gov returned zero registered DSIP
trials of any kind (verified 2026-07-21).

The claim grade for "DSIP improves sleep in people" is **G (Unverified)**.

---

## 2. But I have seen a study where it worked in almost everyone. What about that one?

That is Kaeser 1984 (PMID 6391926), and it is the paper reproduced most often in commercial
material. It reported that sleep normalised in all but one of seven patients with severe insomnia
over follow-up of three to seven months, with improved daytime mood and performance.

Read the design. Seven patients. No control group. No blinding. No placebo. Subjective endpoints.

A study of that shape cannot separate a drug effect from a placebo response, from regression to the
mean (people recruited when symptoms are at their worst tend to improve somewhat anyway), or from
investigator expectancy (a researcher who expects an improvement tends to see one). It is not that
the researchers did anything wrong. It is that this design cannot answer the question, which is
exactly why the double-blind studies were run afterwards.

And they did not reproduce it. When a striking open-label result fails to survive blinding and
placebo control, the standard inference is that the original result reflected placebo response and
expectancy rather than the substance. That is what appears to have happened here.

This Guide does not describe the picture as "mixed evidence", because that phrasing implies a live
scientific disagreement. There is not one. There is a weak positive study and two better studies
that did not confirm it.

---

## 3. Does it actually increase deep sleep? It is called delta sleep-inducing peptide.

The best human measurement says it did not.

In the 1987 double-blind study, sleep was recorded overnight with polysomnography, meaning brain
waves were measured rather than self-reported. Total sleep time and non-REM sleep did increase.
But the increase came from **stage 2 sleep**, which is a lighter stage. **Slow wave sleep, which is
delta sleep, was not modified. Neither was REM sleep** (PMID 3583493).

That is a notable finding, because delta sleep is the property the compound is named for. The name
comes from early animal work in the 1970s, not from a demonstrated human effect. Marketing material
that leans on delta sleep as the mechanism is leaning on the name.

---

## 4. Is it safe?

Nobody knows, and the reason is specific rather than vague.

The entire located human literature is three small studies with a combined enrolled population in
the order of a few dozen people. None was designed or powered as a safety study. None had long-term
follow-up. There is no adverse event dataset, no human pharmacokinetic characterisation (how the
body absorbs, distributes, and clears a substance), and no immunogenicity assessment (whether the
body mounts an immune response against it).

Absence of reported harm in a literature this small is not evidence of safety. A study of seven or
sixteen people could not detect an uncommon harm even if it were there.

One thing does appear in the human record and is worth reporting: the 1984 open trial flagged
complications in patients with a long-standing history of drug dependence. That is a single note in
a seven-person uncontrolled study. It should not be inflated into a characterised risk, and its
existence certainly does not imply that other risks were examined and found absent.

There is also a structural problem underneath all of this. No receptor for DSIP has ever been
identified, so there is no known site of action, and off-target effects in a person cannot be
predicted from the existing literature in the way they can be for a characterised drug.

Xenios does not describe DSIP as safe, as well tolerated, or as free of side effects.

---

## 5. Vendor pages say it is non-addictive, has no withdrawal, and no organ toxicity. Is that true?

Those claims are not findings. They are market claims, and they should be treated as grade E or G.

None was traceable to a retrieved primary study. More importantly, they assert a kind of knowledge
that nobody is in a position to have. Establishing that a substance does not cause dependency,
withdrawal, or organ toxicity requires systematic safety study in a meaningful number of people over
a meaningful period. For DSIP, that work does not exist. Three small studies totalling a few dozen
people could not detect those signals even if the reassuring conclusion happened to be correct.

The same applies to statements about the absence of any identified lethal amount in animal models.
Not having looked hard is not the same as having looked and found nothing.

## 6. Is it a natural substance my body already makes?

That is the most surprising part of this compound, and the honest answer is that it has never been
established.

**No DSIP gene, precursor peptide, or receptor has been identified in any species**, in the more
than fifty years since the 1974 isolation from the blood of sleeping rabbits. The 2006 review in
the Journal of Neurochemistry treats this as the central unresolved problem, notes that the link
between DSIP and sleep was never further characterised in part because the gene was never isolated,
and states that the sleep-factor hypothesis is extremely poorly documented and still weak
(PMID 16539679).

Material that reacts with DSIP antibodies has been reported in tissues. That is much weaker
evidence than finding a gene or a receptor, because antibodies can cross-react with unrelated
molecules.

The same review goes further and suggests that a DSIP-like peptide, rather than DSIP itself, may
account for the effects reported in the literature. In other words, even the reported effects may
not belong to this molecule.

So the accurate description is a hypothesised factor whose endogenous status was never confirmed,
not a known human sleep hormone.

---

## 7. There is a 2024 study. Does that mean the science is moving again?

Not in the way it appears. The 2024 paper in Frontiers in Pharmacology is worth reading carefully,
because it says close to the opposite of how it is usually cited.

It is a mouse study. Specifically, 48 male Kun-Ming mice of approximately eight weeks of age, with
insomnia induced by a chemical called p-chlorophenylalanine. And the substance tested was not DSIP.
It was an engineered DSIP fusion peptide, built to cross the blood-brain barrier and produced from
a yeast, Pichia pastoris. In those mice the fusion peptide reduced wakefulness, normalised several
neurotransmitters, reduced anxiety-like and depressive-like behaviour, and improved the appearance
of hippocampal tissue (DOI 10.3389/fphar.2024.1439536).

Two things follow, both unflattering to plain DSIP.

First, **the engineered fusion outperformed plain DSIP in that mouse model.** That is an implicit
finding that unmodified DSIP performed less well.

Second, **a construct built specifically to cross the blood-brain barrier implies that unmodified
DSIP crosses it poorly.** The engineering exists because the plain molecule was judged inadequate.

So this is not modern validation of DSIP for human sleep. It is a mouse study of a different
substance, and it supplies a reason for doubt rather than support.

## 8. Why did research stop?

That question contains its own answer, and it is worth sitting with.

Human study of DSIP was active in the 1980s. The last human sleep study located is from 1992. Since
then there have been zero registered trials of any kind, no systematic review, no meta-analysis, and
no modern replication in a period of roughly three and a half decades.

A hypothesis that attracted real academic investigation and then attracted none for thirty years is
usually best read as a line of inquiry that was tried and quietly abandoned. That reading fits the
record here. The controlled studies came back negative, the foundational biology (a gene, a
precursor, a receptor) was never established, and researchers moved on.

It is not that the idea was never tested. It is that it was tested, and it did not hold up.

## 9. Why does everything online sound so much more confident than this?

Because most of it was written by someone with a commercial interest.

During research for this Guide, a large majority of search results were retailer,
telehealth-marketing, clinic, and peptide-blog pages presenting confident claims about efficacy,
safety, and how to use the compound. None of those pages is scientific evidence, and none was used
as evidence here.

This is worth stating plainly because it describes an experience a member will have. If you look up
DSIP, the confident sources appear first, they are more numerous, and they are far more certain
than the actual literature. In this subject area, confidence correlates with commercial interest
rather than with evidence.

The literature itself is three small old studies, two of which concluded against benefit, and a
review whose own title calls the compound an unresolved riddle.

## 10. Is it legal?

The accurate answer is jurisdiction-specific, dated, and in this case carries an unusual honesty
caveat. All checks were made on 2026-07-21.

DSIP has no FDA approval for any indication in the United States. Emideltide, the regulatory name
for DSIP in free base and acetate forms, was scheduled for discussion at the FDA Pharmacy
Compounding Advisory Committee meeting on July 23 to 24, 2026, regarding possible inclusion on the
503A Bulks List for compounding. Reporting on the FDA briefing materials indicates FDA proposed that
emideltide not be added to that list, citing that available studies were small, uncontrolled, or
contradictory.

**The honesty caveat.** Repeated attempts to retrieve the FDA advisory committee calendar page, the
FDA 503A bulk substances page, and the FDA briefing document all returned HTTP 404 during research.
Every FDA statement above therefore rests on search-index snippets and secondary reporting, not on a
successfully retrieved FDA document. It is marked unverified and must be confirmed by a human
against the FDA primary source before publication. Note also that an advisory committee
recommendation is advisory and is not a final agency determination.
(https://www.fda.gov/advisory-committees/advisory-committee-calendar/july-23-24-2026-meeting-pharmacy-compounding-advisory-committee-07232026)

No evidence of marketing authorisation was found in the European Union, the United Kingdom, Canada,
or Australia. That is an absence-of-evidence finding from search, not a confirmed retrieval from
each regulator's database.

## 11. I compete in a tested sport. Is it banned?

This Guide cannot tell you, and it will not guess.

DSIP is not named individually anywhere on the WADA Prohibited List that could be confirmed during
research. WADA category S0, Non-Approved Substances, covers any pharmacological substance not
addressed by other sections of the List and not approved by any governmental regulatory health
authority for human therapeutic use, including drugs under pre-clinical or clinical development and
discontinued drugs. Because DSIP holds no such approval in any jurisdiction identified, an S0
classification is the logically expected status.

That is an inference from a category definition. It is **not** a retrieved WADA ruling about DSIP.
The direct fetch of the 2026 Prohibited List PDF returned empty content, so the definition itself
came from search-index retrieval rather than from reading the List
(https://www.wada-ama.org/en/prohibited-list, checked 2026-07-21).

**Athletes in tested sport must confirm with their own anti-doping authority.** Do not rely on an
inference made here.

## 13. What would change your mind?

A specific list, so that this position can be tested rather than simply asserted.

- **A modern, adequately powered, registered, double-blind human trial**, with polysomnographic
  outcomes and pre-specified endpoints. Today there is not even a registration.
- **A replication.** Not one of the three human studies has ever been replicated.
- **Identification of a DSIP gene, precursor, or receptor.** This would move the compound from a
  hypothesised factor to an established molecule, and would be genuinely significant news after more
  than fifty years.
- **Published human safety and pharmacokinetic data**, including systematic adverse event collection
  and an immunogenicity assessment.
- **Evidence that unmodified DSIP does anything comparable to the engineered fusion construct** used
  in the 2024 mouse work, and evidence that it reaches the brain at all in humans.
- **Independent analytical testing of commercial material**, which is currently impossible to
  benchmark because there is no approved reference product or pharmacopoeial standard.
- **The retrieved FDA record**, read directly rather than through search snippets, so that the
  regulatory section can move from unverified to verified.
- **PMID 7028502**, the early human study that could not be retrieved during research. It is a real
  gap, and reading it is part of completing this picture honestly.

If those appeared, this Guide would be updated, the change would be recorded in its correction
history with a date, and the claim grades would move.

## 14. What should I do with all this?

Nothing in this Guide is a recommendation, in either direction. It is an account of the evidence.

If DSIP came up because you are sleeping badly, the more useful conversation is about why. Sleep
problems are frequently a symptom of something else, including depression, anxiety, pain, sleep
apnoea, thyroid disease, and medication effects. Anything that alters sleep without addressing an
underlying cause can delay a diagnosis that matters. There are also approaches to insomnia with real
human evidence behind them. The Guide includes a list of questions written to be taken into a
clinical conversation as they stand.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides. This
FAQ is educational. It is not medical advice, it is not a recommendation, and it is not an offer to
supply anything. Xenios does not sell DSIP.
