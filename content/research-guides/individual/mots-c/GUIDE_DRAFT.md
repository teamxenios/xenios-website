# MOTS-c

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## One-minute summary

MOTS-c is a short peptide that the human body makes on its own, encoded by the small separate genome
inside mitochondria (the compartments in your cells that produce most of their usable energy). It has
become a subject of real scientific interest, and it has also become a heavily marketed product.
Those two facts are not the same thing, and this Guide separates them.

The honest evidence verdict, stated plainly: **there is no completed human study showing that giving
MOTS-c to a person does anything beneficial.** This is not a case of weak or mixed results from
trials that were run. It is a case of trials that have not been completed.

- Every human study located for this Guide **measured** MOTS-c as a naturally occurring substance in
  blood. **None gave MOTS-c to anyone.** Measuring how much of something you already have is a
  completely different question from what happens when you add more of it.
- Those measurement studies do not even agree with each other. One reported circulating MOTS-c
  **lower** in obese boys and moving in the opposite direction to markers of insulin resistance
  (PMID 29691953). A 2025 adult study reported the **opposite** direction of association
  (PMID 41004666). The second paper's own authors point to inconsistent laboratory measurement
  methods as a likely cause.
- One human trial of MOTS-c is registered and recruiting: a Phase 2a study in adults with prediabetes
  and overweight or obesity, sponsor Hudson Biotech, start February 2026 (NCT07505745, verified
  2026-07-19). **No results are posted.** A registration is a plan, not a result.
- The one completed interventional trial in this space tested **CB4211, a modified analog and a
  different molecule**, not MOTS-c (NCT03998514). It has **no posted results**, so even the widely
  repeated claim that it was well tolerated could not be verified against trial data.
- The mechanistic story that appears everywhere in marketing comes from **mice and cultured cells**.
  The 2023 review that describes that mechanism also states plainly that no effective method of
  applying MOTS-c in the clinic has been developed (PMC9905433).
- On safety, the correct statement is not that MOTS-c is safe. According to USADA (retrieved
  2026-07-19), it is **unknown under what conditions, if any, it is safe to use**, because no
  clinical trials have been completed. Reported adverse effects circulating in this area (including
  palpitations, injection site irritation, insomnia, and fever) are aggregated anecdote, not trial
  data, and no incidence rate can be attached to any of them.
- MOTS-c is **not approved by the FDA for any human use**, and it is **banned in sport** under the
  World Anti-Doping Agency's metabolic modulators section, with **no therapeutic use exemption
  available** (USADA, retrieved 2026-07-19). For a tested athlete this is a doping violation with no
  medical route around it.
- Because it is unapproved, any material is obtained outside a regulated supply chain, where
  identity, purity, and sterility are unverified.

Xenios does not sell MOTS-c, does not supply it, and does not tell members how to obtain or use it.
This Guide exists so that a member who encounters MOTS-c elsewhere has an accurate account of what is
and is not known.

**Claim grade for "administering MOTS-c produces a benefit in people": G (Unverified).**
**Claim grade for "MOTS-c has metabolic effects in rodent and cultured-cell models": D (Preclinical).**
**Claim grade for "circulating MOTS-c is associated with insulin resistance in people": C (Early human
evidence), and the direction of that association is contested.**

## What it is

MOTS-c is a peptide, meaning a short chain of amino acids (the building blocks that make up proteins).
It is 16 amino acids long. Its name is an abbreviation of "Mitochondrial Open Reading Frame of the
12S rRNA type-c", which is a description of where in the genetic code it comes from.

The unusual and genuinely interesting feature is where it is encoded. Most peptides and proteins in
your body are encoded by DNA in the cell nucleus. MOTS-c is encoded by the separate, much smaller
genome carried inside mitochondria. Peptides of this kind are called **mitochondrial-derived
peptides**, usually shortened to MDPs. Other members of this family include humanin and the SHLP
group.

MOTS-c occurs naturally in humans and can be measured in serum and plasma (the liquid portion of
blood). This matters more than it might sound, and it explains the entire shape of the human
literature. Because MOTS-c is already present in the body, researchers can study it by measuring how
much of it people have, without ever giving any to anyone. That is exactly what the human studies
located for this Guide did.

**Compounds MOTS-c is commonly confused with.** Their evidence is not MOTS-c's evidence and must not
be merged with it.

- **CB4211.** A modified MOTS-c **analog** developed by CohBar and studied in a Phase 1 trial
  (NCT03998514). An analog is a related but chemically different molecule. CB4211 is not MOTS-c, and
  trial activity involving CB4211 is not evidence about MOTS-c. Vendor and marketing pages
  frequently blur this distinction and present analog trial activity as if it supported MOTS-c
  itself. This is the single most common evidence error in this subject area.
- **Humanin and other mitochondrial-derived peptides.** Same family, different molecules, different
  literature.
- **Nuclear-encoded metabolic peptides.** A different class entirely.

## Why interest exists

The interest in MOTS-c is easy to describe, and it is worth understanding without endorsing it.

The underlying biology is genuinely novel. The idea that the mitochondrial genome encodes signalling
peptides that talk to the rest of the cell, including to the nucleus, is a real and relatively recent
line of research. That novelty alone generates scientific attention.

The proposed activity sits in an area of large unmet need. In rodent and cell-culture work, MOTS-c
has been investigated in relation to glucose handling in skeletal muscle and resistance to
diet-induced obesity. Metabolic health is an area where people have real problems and existing
options are imperfect, so an early signal attracts attention quickly.

There is also an "your body already makes this" framing that circulates widely and that deserves
scrutiny. The fact that a substance occurs naturally says nothing about what happens when additional
amounts are introduced from outside. Insulin occurs naturally. So does cortisol. Endogenous origin is
a statement about biology, not a safety argument.

Finally, some of the interest is commercial rather than scientific. MOTS-c is sold, marketed, and
discussed online, and a portion of the enthusiasm traces to material produced by parties who benefit
from that enthusiasm. Two specific distortions recur: presenting CB4211 analog trial activity as
MOTS-c evidence, and presenting correlational blood-measurement studies as though they showed a
benefit from administration.

Three things should be held alongside the interest.

First, a mechanism is a reason to run a trial, not a substitute for one. The history of metabolic
medicine contains many compounds with an elegant mechanism in mice and no benefit in people.

Second, the reviewing authors themselves are not claiming clinical readiness. The 2023 review states
that no effective method of applying MOTS-c in the clinic has been developed (PMC9905433). That is
the field's own assessment of maturity.

Third, correlation in a blood measurement is not a demonstration of effect. This point is developed
in the human evidence section below, because it is the central interpretive issue for MOTS-c.

## Terminology

Every technical term used in this Guide, in plain English.

- **Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids. Shorter than a protein.
- **Amino acid.** A small molecule that links with others to form peptides and proteins.
- **Mitochondria.** Compartments inside your cells that produce most of the cell's usable energy.
  They carry their own small, separate genome.
- **Mitochondrial-derived peptide (MDP).** A peptide encoded by the mitochondrial genome rather than
  by DNA in the cell nucleus. MOTS-c is one. Humanin is another.
- **Nucleus.** The compartment in a cell that holds the main genome.
- **Endogenous.** Made by the body itself, as opposed to introduced from outside.
- **Serum and plasma.** The liquid parts of blood, used for laboratory measurement.
- **Circulating levels.** How much of a substance is present in the bloodstream at the time of
  measurement.
- **Analog.** A related but chemically different molecule. CB4211 is an analog of MOTS-c.
- **Preclinical.** Research done before any human study, meaning in animals or in cultured cells.
- **In vitro.** Literally "in glass". Research done in cells in a dish, outside a living body.
- **Cell culture.** Cells grown and studied in a laboratory dish.
- **Rodent model.** A study performed in mice or rats.
- **Observational study.** A study that measures what is already happening without changing anything.
  It can show that two things move together. It cannot show that one causes the other.
- **Interventional study.** A study in which researchers give participants something and observe what
  follows. Only this design can test whether administering a substance does anything.
- **Cross-sectional.** Measured at a single point in time, like a photograph rather than a film.
- **Case-control.** A design comparing a group with a condition against a group without it.
- **Correlation.** Two things moving together. It does not establish that one causes the other.
- **HOMA-IR.** A calculated number, derived from fasting blood glucose and insulin, used as an
  indirect indicator of insulin resistance.
- **Insulin resistance.** A state in which the body responds less effectively to insulin, the hormone
  that manages blood sugar.
- **HbA1c.** A blood measure reflecting average blood sugar over roughly the preceding few months.
- **BMI (body mass index).** A ratio of weight to height, used as a rough population-level measure.
- **Prediabetes.** A state in which blood sugar is higher than usual but below the threshold for a
  diabetes diagnosis.
- **NAFLD (nonalcoholic fatty liver disease).** Fat accumulation in the liver not caused by alcohol.
- **AMPK (AMP-activated protein kinase).** An enzyme inside cells that acts as an energy sensor and
  switches on energy-producing processes when cellular energy runs low.
- **Kinase.** An enzyme that chemically modifies other proteins to switch their activity on or off.
- **MAP kinase and c-Fos.** Components of another internal cell signalling route. The proposed MOTS-c
  pathway describes inhibiting this route.
- **Transcription factor.** A protein that binds DNA and controls which genes are switched on.
- **Gene expression.** The process of switching genes on so their instructions are used.
- **Translocation.** Movement of a molecule from one part of a cell to another, for example from the
  main body of the cell into the nucleus.
- **Metabolic stress.** A state in which a cell's energy supply is challenged.
- **Phase 1 trial.** The first stage of human testing, designed mainly to look at safety and
  tolerability in a small group. It is not designed to demonstrate benefit.
- **Phase 2a trial.** A later, still early stage, typically the first look at whether a compound does
  anything useful, in a modest number of participants.
- **Randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled.** The standard design for reducing bias:
  participants are assigned by chance, neither participants nor researchers know who received what,
  and an inactive comparison is included.
- **Placebo.** An inactive comparison given so that the effect of expectation can be separated from
  the effect of the compound.
- **Pharmacodynamics.** What a substance does to the body.
- **Pharmacokinetics.** What the body does to a substance: absorption, distribution, breakdown, and
  elimination.
- **Bioavailability.** How much of a substance actually reaches the bloodstream in an active form.
- **Immunogenicity.** Whether the body mounts an immune response against a substance.
- **Assay.** A laboratory test used to measure how much of something is present.
- **Unstandardised assay.** A situation in which different laboratories measure the same substance
  using different methods, so their numbers cannot be directly compared.
- **ClinicalTrials.gov.** The main United States public registry of clinical studies.
- **NCT number.** The unique identifier a study receives on that registry.
- **PMID.** The unique identifier a paper receives in PubMed, the main biomedical literature database.
- **Registry record.** An entry describing a planned or completed study. Its existence says nothing
  about results.
- **Posted results.** Actual outcome data uploaded to the registry. Many completed trials never post
  results.
- **Sponsor.** The organisation running and funding a trial.
- **WADA (World Anti-Doping Agency).** The body that publishes the international Prohibited List for
  sport.
- **USADA (United States Anti-Doping Agency).** The United States anti-doping authority.
- **Therapeutic use exemption (TUE).** Permission for an athlete to use an otherwise prohibited
  substance for a documented medical reason.
- **Compounding pharmacy.** A pharmacy that prepares customised medicines rather than dispensing
  manufactured products.
- **Certificate of analysis (COA).** A document from a testing laboratory stating what a specific
  batch of material was found to contain.
- **Endotoxin.** A contaminant from bacterial cell walls that can cause fever and serious reactions
  in an injectable product, even when no live bacteria remain.
- **Sterility.** Freedom from living micro-organisms.
- **Unregulated channel.** Sale outside a licensed, inspected supply chain.

## Proposed mechanism

Everything in this section is **proposed**. All of it derives from **mice and cultured cells**. No
mechanism has been confirmed in a living human being. Claim grade for this entire section:
**D (Preclinical)**.

**The described pathway.** As set out in a 2023 review in Frontiers in Endocrinology (PMC9905433,
retrieved 2026-07-19), MOTS-c is reported to move into the cell nucleus under metabolic stress in a
manner dependent on AMPK (the cell's internal energy sensor), to bind transcription factors, and to
influence which genes are switched on. The review describes the route as activation of the AMPK
pathway together with inhibition of the MAP kinase and c-Fos pathway. **The species and systems
behind these core mechanistic findings are mice and human cell cultures.**

**Where the AMPK framing also appears.** The same AMPK-activator description is the basis for the
anti-doping classification: USADA places MOTS-c under the World Anti-Doping Agency's Section 4.4.1,
activators of AMP-activated protein kinase (retrieved 2026-07-19). That is a regulatory
categorisation built on the proposed mechanism, not independent confirmation that the mechanism
operates as described in people.

**The qualifier the review itself attaches.** The same 2023 review states that no effective method of
applying MOTS-c in the clinic has been developed. This is important and easy to skip past. It means
the mechanism is a hypothesis about a molecule whose practical clinical application remains
unresolved, including basic questions of how meaningful exposure would be achieved at all.

**The inferential gap, which is the single most important caveat in this Guide.** Human data on
MOTS-c are correlational: researchers measured how much MOTS-c people naturally have and compared it
against their metabolic state. Even where such a correlation is real, it does not establish that
administering MOTS-c produces the corresponding effect. A substance can be lower in a disease state
because the disease lowered it, because something upstream lowered both, or because of measurement
artefact. None of those explanations implies that adding more of it changes anything. Marketing
material that moves from "levels are lower in people with X" to "therefore supplementing it helps X"
is making a leap the evidence does not support.

**Species used across the mechanistic literature:** mice (in vivo) and human cell cultures. No human
in vivo mechanism data was located.

### Studies that measured MOTS-c in people, without administering it

These are real, peer-reviewed human studies. They are observational, which means they measured what
was already there. They can describe an association. They cannot establish cause, and because nothing
was given to anyone, they say nothing at all about what administering MOTS-c does.

**Study 1. Obese children and adolescents, China (PMID 29691953).**
Observational case-control study, **not** an interventional trial. MOTS-c was measured as a naturally
occurring marker in blood and was **not administered to participants**. Population: 97 participants
in Hubei Province, China (40 obese children and adolescents, 57 controls), with results separated by
sex. Finding: circulating MOTS-c levels were significantly **lower** in obese male children and
adolescents compared with controls, and were **negatively** correlated with markers of insulin
resistance and obesity (BMI, waist circumference, fasting insulin, HOMA-IR, and HbA1c) in the male
group.
*Limitations:* observational and cross-sectional, so no causal conclusion is possible. Because
nothing was administered, this says nothing about whether giving MOTS-c does anything. The effect was
seen in males only. Single-region paediatric group, modest sample size, and not generalisable to
adults. Claim grade: **C (Early human evidence)**, for the association only.

**Study 2. Adults with and without obesity (PMID 41004666, PMCID PMC12468430).**
Observational cross-sectional case-control study with prospective enrolment, **not** an interventional
trial. MOTS-c was measured only, **not administered**. Population: 85 adults (48 with a BMI at or
above 30, and 37 with a BMI between 18.5 and 24.9). Finding: serum MOTS-c showed a significant
**positive** correlation with HOMA-IR, and levels **did not differ significantly** between the
obesity and normal-weight groups. Age and insulin resistance independently predicted MOTS-c levels.
*Limitations:* this directly contradicts the direction of association reported in the paediatric
study above. Cross-sectional, modest sample size, and the authors themselves note that MOTS-c
measurement methods are not standardised across the literature, which undercuts comparison between
studies. Claim grade: **C (Early human evidence)**, for the association only, with the direction
contested.

**Study 3. Coronary artery disease and type 2 diabetes cohort (NCT04027712).**
Observational cohort with two-year follow-up, **not** interventional. MOTS-c was measured as a
possible prognostic marker. Estimated 120 patients with coronary artery disease and type 2 diabetes,
sponsor University of Athens. Finding: **no results are posted**, and the registry record's overall
status is **UNKNOWN** (last known status: active, not recruiting). The study was designed to test
whether reduced MOTS-c predicts cardiovascular mortality. **No outcome can be cited.**
*Limitations:* no posted results, stale registry status, and observational by design. It cannot
support any claim about administering MOTS-c. Claim grade: **G (Unverified)**, since nothing is
reported.

### Registered but not completed

**NCT07505745.** A Phase 2a randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled study to evaluate the
efficacy, safety, and pharmacodynamics of MOTS-c in adults with prediabetes and overweight or
obesity. Sponsor Hudson Biotech. Estimated enrolment 120. Start date February 2026. Status:
**RECRUITING**. **No results are posted** (verified 2026-07-19).

This is the first registered interventional trial of MOTS-c itself located for this Guide, and its
existence is worth knowing. It is also worth being precise about what it is: a statement of intent to
run a study. A registration is not evidence of effect and not evidence of safety. Many registered
trials do not complete, and many that complete do not post results. Claim grade: **G (Unverified)**
for any statement about what this trial shows, because it has not shown anything yet.

The existence of this registry record was confirmed directly against the ClinicalTrials.gov
programmatic interface. As a check against fabrication, a deliberately non-existent NCT identifier
was also queried and correctly returned an HTTP 404 error, confirming that the retrieval process was
returning real records rather than inventing them.

### The analog trial, which is not MOTS-c evidence

**NCT03998514, CB4211.** A Phase 1a/1b randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled interventional
trial of **CB4211, a MOTS-c analog and a different molecule**. Status: completed (verified May 2021).
Population: 88 participants, including healthy non-obese subjects and subjects with nonalcoholic
fatty liver disease, across four sites in California and Texas. **No results are posted on
ClinicalTrials.gov.**

Because no results are posted, **no efficacy or safety finding can be cited from this record**. The
registry confirms only that the trial ran and completed. Claims circulating online that the compound
was safe and well tolerated trace to sponsor communications rather than to posted trial results, and
they are **not verified here**.

*Limitations:* it tested an analog, not MOTS-c, so it does not transfer to MOTS-c. Phase 1 is a
safety and tolerability stage and is not designed to demonstrate benefit. Results are not posted, the
trial was industry-sponsored (CohBar, Inc.), and the sponsor has since ceased independent operations.
Claim grade: **G (Unverified)** for any MOTS-c claim resting on this trial.

### What this section adds up to

Any statement of the form "MOTS-c does X when given to people" carries claim grade **G (Unverified)**.
No claim in this Guide reaches grade A or B. The two observational association findings reach grade
**C**, and they point in opposite directions.

## Preclinical evidence

This is where most of the MOTS-c literature lives. It is genuine research, and it is in animals and
cultured cells. Every finding below names its model in the same sentence. None of it establishes an
effect in a person.

**Mice (in vivo) and human cell culture, metabolic and inflammatory findings.** A 2023 review in
Frontiers in Endocrinology (PMC9905433, retrieved 2026-07-19) reports improvements in skeletal muscle
glucose metabolism **in mice**, prevention of high-fat-diet-induced obesity **in mice**, and
anti-inflammatory effects **in mice** in formalin pain testing, alongside AMPK-dependent movement
into the nucleus regulating gene expression **in mice and cultured human cells**. All of this is
preclinical. Claim grade: **D (Preclinical)**.

**The reviewing authors' own limiting statement.** The same review states that no effective method of
applying MOTS-c in the clinic has been developed. A review's authors saying this about their own
subject is a meaningful signal about how early this work is.

**An interpretive caution specific to metabolic research in rodents.** Rodent models of obesity and
glucose handling have a long history of producing results that do not carry over to people. A benefit
in a mouse fed a high-fat diet is a reason to run a human trial, not a substitute for one. This
caution applies directly to the findings above.

**A note on the depth of this section.** The preclinical evidence recorded for this Guide rests on a
single retrieved review article rather than on individually retrieved primary experiments. A review
is a summary written by authors making selections. The primary papers behind these findings were not
individually retrieved and read during research, so individual findings within them are reported here
to map the literature, not as independently substantiated results. This is recorded as a gap.

## Contradictions and disagreement

Where sources genuinely disagree, both positions are stated, along with an assessment of which the
retrieved evidence supports.

**1. The direction of the association between circulating MOTS-c and insulin resistance.**

- *Position A.* PMID 29691953 (obese male children and adolescents, 97 participants): MOTS-c is
  **decreased** in obesity and **negatively** correlated with HOMA-IR and other insulin-resistance
  markers.
- *Position B.* PMID 41004666 and PMC12468430 (adults, 85 participants): MOTS-c shows a significant
  **positive** correlation with HOMA-IR, and levels **do not differ** between the obesity and
  normal-weight groups.
- *Assessment.* This is a genuine, unresolved conflict in the human observational literature. The
  populations differ (children in one, adults in the other), and the second paper's authors
  explicitly flag unstandardised MOTS-c measurement methods as a likely driver of the inconsistency.
  Neither position is dismissed here, because the retrieved evidence does not settle it. The
  practical implication is direct: the popular narrative that low MOTS-c means metabolic dysfunction,
  and that supplementing it therefore helps, is **not consistently supported even at the level of
  correlation**, let alone at the level of cause. A claim that rests on a correlation whose direction
  the literature disputes is on very unstable ground.

**2. Whether an active human trial of MOTS-c exists.**

- *Position A.* Some secondary sources encountered during research claim there is no active human
  MOTS-c trial as of 2026, and that any registry hits point only to the CB4211 analog.
- *Position B.* A direct query of the ClinicalTrials.gov programmatic interface returns NCT07505745,
  a Phase 2a MOTS-c trial listed as **RECRUITING**, sponsor Hudson Biotech, start February 2026.
- *Assessment.* Position B is correct on the registry record, which was directly retrieved and
  verified, including a control query using a fabricated NCT identifier that correctly returned an
  HTTP 404 error. Two qualifications matter. First, this resolves only that a trial is **registered
  and recruiting**. It has no posted results, so **neither position supports any efficacy claim**.
  Second, the sources on both sides of this dispute in general web search were vendor and marketing
  material, which is precisely why the registry was queried directly rather than relying on either.

**3. Whether CB4211 evidence counts as MOTS-c evidence.**

- *Position A.* Vendor and marketing pages routinely present CB4211 trial activity, and the
  associated tolerability language, as though it established something about MOTS-c.
- *Position B.* CB4211 is a modified analog, a chemically different molecule. Its trial
  (NCT03998514) has no posted results, so it establishes nothing on its own terms either.
- *Assessment.* Position B. This conflation is the most common evidence error in MOTS-c marketing,
  and it fails twice over: the molecule is different, and the trial has no published outcome to
  transfer even if it were the same. Any claim built on this route carries claim grade
  **G (Unverified)**.

## Limitations

Limitations of the evidence base itself:

- There is no completed human interventional evidence, so there is no human effect size, no responder
  rate, no dose-response relationship, and no duration of effect to report.
- Both human studies located are observational and cross-sectional, which is the weakest design for
  drawing conclusions about cause.
- Both human studies are small (97 and 85 participants) and drawn from single regions.
- The two human studies contradict each other on the direction of the association they report.
- Laboratory measurement of MOTS-c is not standardised across studies, a limitation raised by the
  study authors themselves. This means even the published blood levels are not directly comparable
  between papers.
- One human study found its effect in males only, so it does not describe a general population.
- The preclinical evidence here rests on a single review article rather than on individually
  retrieved primary experiments.
- Rodent metabolic models have historically been poor predictors of human metabolic outcomes.
- The one completed interventional trial in this space tested a different molecule and posted no
  results.
- The registered Phase 2a trial has no results and, at the date of this Guide, is only recruiting.

Limitations of this Guide's research process, stated so a reader can judge its reliability:

- **FDA primary sources could not be retrieved during research.** Attempts at the relevant FDA
  compounding category pages and an FDA media document returned HTTP 404 responses. Every FDA-related
  statement in this Guide is therefore reported **secondhand via USADA**, and a human must verify
  these directly against fda.gov before publication.
- No FDA warning letters specific to MOTS-c were retrieved or confirmed.
- The research session's web search budget was exhausted before FDA-domain-restricted searching could
  be completed, and before a systematic literature sweep for systematic reviews or meta-analyses
  could be run. **No systematic review or meta-analysis of MOTS-c was retrieved.**
- The human-readable ClinicalTrials.gov study page for NCT07505745 could not be independently
  confirmed, because the page returns a script-driven shell rather than readable content. Confirmation
  rests on the programmatic interface plus the 404 control test. The trial is new, and a human should
  re-check its status and any posted results before publication.
- Widely repeated claims about FDA concerns regarding impurity, characterisation, and immunogenicity
  for MOTS-c appear on secondary and vendor pages and were **not** verified against an FDA primary
  source. They are excluded from the regulatory section of this Guide.
- Claims circulating in vendor and marketing material about a July 2026 FDA advisory review of MOTS-c
  and a change to its compounding category were **not verified** and are deliberately excluded. They
  must not be added without primary sourcing.
- No analytical or counterfeiting survey of MOTS-c sold in the unregulated market was retrieved, so
  no evidence-based statement about market product quality can be made in either direction.
- Only ClinicalTrials.gov was searched for registrations. Other national and international registries
  were not searched.

## Quality and identity

This section is about the material itself, separately from the science. It matters because an
unapproved substance is, by definition, obtained outside a regulated supply chain.

**The regulatory starting point.** MOTS-c is not FDA-approved, and according to USADA (retrieved
2026-07-19), FDA prohibits compounding pharmacies from making medications containing MOTS-c. There is
therefore no approved manufacturer, no approved specification, and no regulatory authority inspecting
production against a filed standard. Any material obtained comes from an unregulated channel with no
regulatory assurance of identity, purity, or sterility.

**No MOTS-c-specific market testing was found.** No analytical survey or counterfeiting study of
material sold as MOTS-c was retrieved during research. Nobody appears to have systematically checked
what is in vials sold under this name. **That is an absence of information, not a clean bill of
health.** Both a favourable and an unfavourable claim about market product quality would be
unsupported by retrieved evidence, and neither is made here.

**Measurement heterogeneity is a documented problem even inside the peer-reviewed literature.** The
authors of PMID 41004666 note that MOTS-c measurement methods are not standardised across studies,
and they raise this as an explanation for conflicting results. The practical consequence is that even
published serum MOTS-c numbers are not directly comparable between papers. If the research literature
cannot reliably agree on how to measure this molecule, a certificate from an unregulated seller
claiming to quantify it deserves proportionate scepticism.

**Identity confusion is a practical quality risk, not just a semantic one.** MOTS-c must not be
conflated with CB4211, with humanin, or with other mitochondrial-derived peptides. Because marketing
material routinely blurs the MOTS-c and CB4211 distinction, a purchaser has no straightforward way to
confirm from labelling alone which molecule is being described, let alone which is present.

**A note on circulating claims.** Widely repeated statements about FDA-identified impurity,
characterisation, and immunogenicity concerns for MOTS-c appear on secondary and vendor pages. These
could not be verified against an FDA primary source during research, because FDA pages returned HTTP
404 responses on every attempt. They are therefore treated as unverified and are not asserted here.

## Safety categories

Nothing in this section is reassurance. These are categories of concern that remain open because
nobody has closed them.

**1. Safe conditions of use are unknown. This is the primary safety finding.** According to USADA
(retrieved 2026-07-19), it is unknown under what conditions, if any, it is safe to use MOTS-c,
because there are no completed clinical trials. This is an **absence-of-evidence finding**, and it
must be read that way. Nobody reporting harm is not the same as nobody being harmed. Where a
substance has never been systematically studied in people, there is no mechanism in place to notice,
collect, or report a problem.

**2. Reported adverse effects with no incidence rate attached.** USADA lists increased heart rate or
heart palpitations, injection site irritation, insomnia, and fever as reported adverse effects. The
basis for this list is aggregated anecdotal user report, **not controlled trial adverse-event data**.
The evidence grade is low. **No incidence rate, severity distribution, or duration can be attached to
any of these**, and no statement can be made about how common or uncommon they are. They are recorded
here because they are what has been reported, not because their frequency is known. Claim grade:
**E (Manufacturer or supplier reported)**, extended here to aggregated user report.

**3. No long-term safety data exist.** USADA states that no long-term safety data are available.
Nothing retrieved during research contradicts that. Cumulative effects, effects of repeated exposure,
and effects appearing after a delay are entirely uncharacterised.

**4. No characterised human pharmacokinetics or bioavailability.** How the body absorbs, distributes,
breaks down, and eliminates administered MOTS-c is unknown. The 2023 review states that no effective
method of clinical application has been developed, which raises the prior question of whether
meaningful exposure is achieved at all. Where basic exposure is uncharacterised, downstream effects
cannot be predicted from the existing literature.

**5. Unassessed immunogenicity.** Whether the body mounts an immune response against introduced
MOTS-c has not been established in any retrieved source. This is a standard concern for peptides
introduced from outside the body, and it is specifically listed among this Guide's unknowns.
Statements attributing a specific immunogenicity concern to FDA circulate widely but could not be
verified against an FDA primary source during research, so nothing here is attributed to FDA as its
words. [UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]

**6. Metabolic signalling with uncharacterised consequences.** The proposed mechanism, described in
mice and cultured cells, involves an energy-sensing pathway that influences gene expression. Broad
metabolic signalling raises questions about effects on blood sugar regulation, about interaction with
existing metabolic conditions, and about consequences of sustained activation. None of these has been
studied in humans for MOTS-c, so no reassurance can be offered in either direction.

**7. Product quality as a safety issue in its own right.** Separately from the molecule, material
obtained outside a regulated supply chain has unverified identity, purity, sterility, and endotoxin
status. Contamination and misidentification are harms that have nothing to do with whether the
intended molecule does anything.

**8. Sport eligibility as a concrete, non-hypothetical consequence.** For a member who competes in a
tested sport, this is not an abstract risk. MOTS-c is prohibited under the World Anti-Doping Agency's
metabolic modulators section, and USADA states that a therapeutic use exemption cannot be obtained,
because there is no approved therapeutic use for this experimental compound. There is no medical
route around this.

**9. Entirely unstudied populations.** Pregnancy, breastfeeding, children, older adults,
immunocompromised people, people with diabetes or prediabetes, people with cardiovascular disease,
people with liver disease, and people taking any metabolic medication. No safety data of any kind was
located for any of these groups.

## Interactions

No interaction data for MOTS-c was located in any retrieved source. No study of MOTS-c alongside any
medication or supplement was found. **Interactions are unstudied, which is not the same as absent.**
An empty interaction record reflects that nobody has looked, not that nothing is there.

The following categories would require review by a qualified professional before anyone considers
anything in this area, because they intersect directly with the proposed metabolic action:

- **Diabetes and blood sugar medication of any kind**, including insulin and oral agents, given that
  the proposed mechanism concerns glucose handling.
- **Any medication that affects the body's energy-sensing pathways**, since the proposed route is
  described as activating AMPK.
- **Cardiovascular medication**, particularly anything affecting heart rate or rhythm, given that
  increased heart rate and palpitations appear among the reported adverse effects.
- **Weight management medication.**
- **Medication for liver conditions**, given that the analog trial in this space enrolled participants
  with fatty liver disease.
- **Thyroid and other metabolic hormone medication.**
- **Any medication requiring stable, monitored blood levels**, where an unpredictable metabolic effect
  could complicate monitoring.
- **Any other unapproved peptide or research compound taken at the same time**, which makes both
  benefit and harm impossible to attribute.
- **Pregnancy and breastfeeding**, where no data exists at all.

## Unknowns

An explicit list of what is not known. It is longer than the evidence section, which is itself the
most accurate summary of the state of knowledge.

- Whether administering MOTS-c to humans produces any clinical benefit at all. No completed human
  interventional trial of MOTS-c with posted results was located.
- Whether the rodent metabolic findings, including glucose handling and resistance to diet-induced
  obesity, carry over to humans in any degree.
- The human safety profile. No incidence rates, no long-term data, and no organ-system safety
  characterisation from controlled trials exist.
- Whether circulating MOTS-c rises or falls with insulin resistance, given directly conflicting
  published findings in children and in adults.
- Whether serum MOTS-c is even a reliable measurement, given the unstandardised laboratory methods
  flagged in the literature.
- Whether any finding from the analog CB4211 has any bearing on MOTS-c itself.
- Immunogenicity risk in humans, which is a recurring stated concern but was not verifiable against a
  primary source during research.
- Bioavailability, and whether any practical route of clinical application exists at all. The 2023
  review states that no effective clinical application method has been developed.
- The purity and identity of material sold through unregulated channels, since no analytical survey
  addressing this was retrieved.
- Interactions with any medication or supplement. No interaction data was located.
- Behaviour in specific populations: pregnancy, breastfeeding, children, older adults, people with
  diabetes or prediabetes, people with cardiovascular disease, people with liver disease, and people
  taking metabolic medication.
- The outcome of the registered Phase 2a trial, which had not reported at the date of this Guide.
- FDA's actual category classification and its specific stated concerns, which could not be retrieved
  from a primary source during research.

## Questions for a qualified professional

If a member wants to discuss MOTS-c with a clinician, these are questions that produce a useful
conversation. They are written to be asked as they stand.

1. I have read that every human study of MOTS-c measured it in blood rather than giving it to anyone,
   and that the one registered trial is still recruiting. Does that match what you can find, and does
   it change how you would think about it?
2. Two published human studies disagree about whether MOTS-c levels go up or down with insulin
   resistance. How do you weigh a compound whose basic association in humans is contested?
3. Most of what I have read traces back to studies in mice and in cells in a dish. How much weight do
   you put on that kind of evidence when there is no completed human trial behind it?
4. What is actually going on with my metabolic health, and is there an approved option with real human
   evidence that we have not tried yet?
5. I take the following medications and have the following conditions. Which of them would make any
   compound acting on blood sugar or energy metabolism a particularly poor idea for me?
6. Palpitations and increased heart rate appear among the reported effects, though only as anecdote
   rather than trial data. Given my heart history, how would you think about that?
7. This is not approved anywhere I could find, which means any material comes from outside a regulated
   supply chain. How do you think about unverified identity, purity, and sterility for something
   introduced into the body?
8. I compete in a tested sport. My understanding is that this is prohibited and that a therapeutic use
   exemption is not available. Who is the right authority for me to confirm that with directly?
9. If I decided to do nothing about this for now, what would you want me to watch for, and when should
   I come back?
10. There is a Phase 2a trial recruiting. If it reports, what specifically would you want to see in
    the results before it changed your advice?

This Guide does not give medical advice and does not tell anyone what to do. These questions belong
with a clinician who knows your history.
