# MOTS-c: member questions

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research
Guides.

Every identifier referenced below (trial registrations, PMIDs) is recorded with its
retrieval status in `SOURCE_REGISTRY.md`. Nothing is cited here that is not registered
there, and a source marked UNVERIFIED in that registry carries no claim in this file.

## Does it work?

There is **no completed human interventional evidence** that giving MOTS-c to a person
does anything beneficial.

That is a stronger statement than "the evidence is weak." Every human study we found
measured MOTS-c as a molecule naturally present in blood. **None gave it to anyone.** So
there is no human result to be weak or strong about.

One Phase 2a trial is registered and recruiting (NCT07505745, start February 2026). It has
no posted results.

## But I have seen studies on MOTS-c. What are those?

Almost certainly one of three things:

1. **Observational studies** measuring naturally occurring MOTS-c in blood. These look at
   whether people with more or less of it differ. They cannot tell you what happens if
   you administer it, and the two we retrieved **disagree with each other about the
   direction** of the association with insulin resistance.
2. **Mouse and cell-culture work.** This is where the mechanistic story comes from. The
   2023 review describing that mechanism also states plainly that no effective method of
   clinical application has been developed.
3. **Trials of CB4211**, which is an **analog, a different molecule**. Its Phase 1 trial
   has no posted results. Vendor pages routinely present it as though it were MOTS-c
   evidence. It is not.

## Is it safe?

Nobody knows, and that is the accurate answer rather than a cautious one.

USADA states outright that it is unknown under what conditions, if any, MOTS-c is safe to
use, because no clinical trials have been completed.

Effects you may see listed online (palpitations, injection site irritation, insomnia,
fever) are **aggregated self-reports**, not adverse event data from a controlled study.
Their absence from a trial record is not reassurance; it reflects the absence of trials.

## Is it legal?

MOTS-c is **not FDA approved for any human use**. On the reported position, FDA also
prohibits compounding pharmacies from making medications containing it.

That leaves no lawful supply route we could identify, which is why anything obtained comes
from an unregulated channel.

Note that our FDA statements here reach us via USADA rather than from FDA pages directly,
because those pages were not retrievable. They are flagged for human verification before
this Guide publishes.

## Can I get it from Xenios?

**No.** Xenios does not sell MOTS-c, does not supply it, and does not tell members how to
obtain or use it. This Guide exists so that a member reading about MOTS-c elsewhere has an
accurate account of what is and is not known.

## I am a tested athlete. What do I need to know?

It is **prohibited**. MOTS-c falls under the WADA Prohibited List section on metabolic
modulators, as an AMPK activator.

USADA states that you **cannot obtain a therapeutic use exemption**, because there is no
approved therapeutic use. Using it is a doping violation.

## Why is the evidence so thin if the biology is interesting?

The biology genuinely is interesting. MOTS-c is encoded by the mitochondrial genome rather
than the nuclear one, which is unusual and worth studying.

But interesting biology and a demonstrated human benefit are separated by a large amount
of expensive, slow work: showing the mechanism holds in people, that it can be delivered
usefully, that the effect is meaningful, and that it is safe over time. MOTS-c has not
been through that. The most cited review says as much.

Marketing tends to compress that distance. This Guide does not.

## The label says it is a "research peptide." Does that mean it is tested?

No, and the phrase often works in the opposite direction to how it reads.

"Research use only" describes a product sold outside the framework that would require
proof of identity, purity, sterility, and safety for human use. It is a statement about
what the product has **not** been qualified for.

We also cannot tell you what is actually in such a product. No supplier documentation for
any MOTS-c product is on file with Xenios, and measurement of MOTS-c is unstandardised
even in the published literature.

## What would change your mind?

A completed, adequately powered, controlled human trial that **administered MOTS-c** and
reported posted results, with a meaningful clinical outcome rather than a biomarker shift
alone. NCT07505745 could eventually be that, or could not.

Until results are posted, our position does not move, and we will say so plainly rather
than describing a recruiting trial as though it were a finding.
