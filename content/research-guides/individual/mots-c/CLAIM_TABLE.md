# MOTS-c Claim Table

One row per discrete claim. Source ids refer to SOURCE_REGISTRY.md.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

## How to read the grades in this table

| Grade | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| A | Established |
| B | Supported human evidence |
| C | Early human evidence |
| D | Preclinical |
| E | Manufacturer or supplier reported |
| F | Traditional or historical |
| G | Unverified |
| PROHIBITED | May not appear on any member-facing surface |

Two notes on how grade A is applied. It is used for documented record facts: what a registry
contains, what an official body has stated, what a review's authors concluded about their own
field. It is never used for a statement about what MOTS-c does in a human body.

**No claim in this table about an effect of administering MOTS-c to a person reaches any
grade above PROHIBITED, because no completed human interventional evidence was retrieved.**
Every human study in this file measured MOTS-c that participants already produced. None gave
it to anyone. That single distinction governs the entire table.

Some low-grade rows are marked allowed on a member-facing surface. In every such case the
claim is a limiting, negative or risk-restraining statement. Restraining claims may be
published at low grade. Promotional claims may not.

## The one distinction that drives this table

MOTS-c occurs naturally in the human body and is measurable in blood. A study can therefore
report an association between the amount of MOTS-c a person already has and some health
state, without ever administering anything. That is what every human study retrieved here
did.

An association between a naturally occurring peptide level and a metabolic state does not
establish that giving someone that peptide produces the corresponding change. The level
could be a consequence rather than a cause, or both could follow from something else
entirely. Treating a correlational biomarker finding as a reason to administer a compound is
the single most common error made about MOTS-c, and every row below is graded with that
error in mind.

## 7. PROHIBITED claims

These claims must never appear on any member-facing surface for this compound, in any
wording, in any format, including headlines, product copy, social posts, email, FAQ answers
and support replies. Each is listed with why it fails, because an editor who understands the
reason will also catch the paraphrase.

| ID | Prohibited claim | Why it is prohibited |
| --- | --- | --- |
| P-01 | Any bare mechanism sentence presented as human physiology, for example that MOTS-c activates AMPK, improves mitochondrial function, or regulates metabolism, without the rodent or cell-culture model named in the same sentence. | The mechanism rests on mice and cell culture (S07). Stripping the model is the single most common failure in this subject area, and it converts a preclinical observation into an implied human fact. |
| P-02 | That MOTS-c improves insulin sensitivity, blood sugar control, metabolic health or body composition in people. | No completed human interventional evidence exists (HU-01). The human data are observational measurements that disagree with each other about direction (HU-04). |
| P-03 | That MOTS-c causes fat loss, prevents weight gain, or helps with obesity in people. | The obesity findings are rodent (PC-02). Carrying them to humans is exactly the leap this Guide exists to prevent. |
| P-04 | That MOTS-c is an exercise mimetic, exercise in a bottle, or that it delivers the benefits of training. | Not supported by any retrieved source in any species, and it is a marketing frame rather than a finding. |
| P-05 | That MOTS-c is safe, well tolerated, or has no side effects. | USADA states plainly that it is unknown under what conditions, if any, use is safe, because no clinical trials have been completed (SF-01). Reported adverse effects exist and are anecdotal (SF-02). |
| P-06 | That the CB4211 trial showed MOTS-c to be safe or effective, or any use of that trial as support for MOTS-c. | CB4211 is a different molecule (ID-04), and its registry record carries no posted results (HU-05). The tolerability claim that circulates traces to sponsor communications, not to trial data (UV02). |
| P-07 | That MOTS-c is "clinically studied", "in clinical trials", or "backed by clinical research", used in a way that implies demonstrated benefit. | One trial is registered and recruiting with no posted results (RG-05). A registration is a plan. Using it as an endorsement misrepresents the record. |
| P-08 | That low MOTS-c indicates metabolic dysfunction, or that a person should have their MOTS-c measured, or that raising MOTS-c would correct anything. | The direction of association is contested in the human literature (HU-04) and the assays are unstandardised (QU-02). The chain from a correlational biomarker to an intervention is not supported at any link. |
| P-09 | That because MOTS-c occurs naturally in the human body, administering it is safe, natural, or physiologically neutral. | The premise is true (ID-02) and the conclusion does not follow. A naturally occurring molecule administered from outside is a different exposure from the one the body produces. |
| P-10 | That MOTS-c is anti-aging, extends lifespan or healthspan, or slows biological aging. | No retrieved source supports this in humans, and nothing in the record supports it as an outcome in any species. |
| P-11 | Any claim of benefit for athletic performance, endurance, recovery or training capacity. | Unsupported, and additionally reckless: MOTS-c is prohibited in sport with no therapeutic use exemption available (RG-03, RG-04). A performance claim would be an inducement to a doping violation. |
| P-12 | Any dosing, amount, concentration, frequency, timing, cycle, titration, stacking, reconstitution, route or administration technique. | Excluded from Xenios Research Guides by policy, and the record contains no basis for any of it in any case. Where a reader expects it, the Guide states that the information is intentionally excluded. |
| P-13 | Any claim citing a July 2026 FDA advisory review of MOTS-c, or a change to a 503A category listing. | These appeared only in vendor and marketing material and were not verified against any primary source (UV03). FDA pages returned 404 on every attempt. |
| P-14 | Any statement written as "FDA states" or "according to FDA" for this compound. | No FDA primary source was retrieved (F01, F02). Every FDA-attributed fact here reaches us through USADA (UV01). The second-hand attribution must remain visible until a human verifies against fda.gov. |
| P-15 | Any use of "research use only", "for research purposes", or similar, in a way that implies human benefit or acts as a permission structure. | Prohibited framing by policy. It is a labelling device, not evidence, and using it to gesture at human use is dishonest. |
| P-16 | Any claim of benefit for a named condition, including type 2 diabetes, prediabetes, fatty liver disease, cardiovascular disease or inflammation. | The registrations that touch these populations have no posted results (RG-05, HU-05, HU-06), and the observational cohort is a prognostic study, not a treatment study. |
| P-17 | Any wording of the form "will", "proven to", "restores", "cures", "eliminates", "reverses", or "corrects", applied to any effect of MOTS-c. | Guaranteed-outcome language, prohibited by policy for every compound, and unsupportable for this one at any grade. |
| P-18 | Anything that reads as sourcing, acquisition or self-treatment guidance. | Out of scope for a Research Guide, and directly at odds with the regulatory status described in RG-01 and RG-02. |

## 8. What an honest member-facing summary looks like

For the editor writing the Guide body, the defensible framing that the whole table supports:

MOTS-c is a peptide the body makes itself, and the underlying biology is genuinely
interesting. The evidence for taking it is not thin, it is absent. Every human study
retrieved measured the peptide people already have, none administered it, and those studies
disagree with each other about even the direction of the association. The mechanistic story
comes from mice and cultured cells, and the authors of the review describing that story say
plainly that no method of applying it in the clinic has been developed. One trial is now
recruiting and has posted nothing. The one completed trial in this space tested a different
molecule and also posted nothing. It is not approved in the United States, the market for it
is unregulated, and for anyone subject to anti-doping testing, use is a violation with no
exemption available.

That is a complete and useful thing to tell a member. It requires no claim this table
cannot support.
