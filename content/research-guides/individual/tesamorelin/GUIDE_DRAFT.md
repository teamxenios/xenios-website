# Tesamorelin

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

## One-minute summary

Tesamorelin is unusual in this category. Most compounds a member encounters in the peptide space
have almost no human evidence. Tesamorelin has a real, replicated body of randomized human trials
and a genuine United States FDA approval dating to 2010. The honest verdict is still strongly
cautionary, and the reason is worth understanding.

Every randomized trial that measured body composition was conducted in one population: adults living
with HIV who had abdominal fat accumulation while on antiretroviral therapy. That is the population
the approval covers, and it is almost certainly not the member reading this Guide. The single
randomized trial we located in a population without HIV (adults with type 2 diabetes) did not measure
body composition at all.

Four facts should sit together in a member's mind, because they interlock:

1. The approved United States label states the product is not indicated for weight loss management.
   A 2026 meta-analysis of five randomized trials independently found no significant change in body
   mass index and no significant change in fat sitting under the skin. This is a compound studied for
   a specific internal fat compartment, not a weight-loss compound. (Grade A for the label statement,
   Grade B for the meta-analysis finding.)
2. The same label states that long-term cardiovascular safety has not been established. Every
   benefit reported in the trials is a surrogate marker (an imaging or blood measurement that stands
   in for a health outcome), not a health outcome itself. (Grade A.)
3. In the twelve-month trial, the improvement was rapidly lost in participants switched from active
   drug to placebo. Benefit appears to require continuous use. (Grade B.)
4. Continuous use means continuously raised IGF-1, a growth signal. The FDA label states plainly that
   the effects of prolonged elevations in IGF-1 are unknown, and the same label carries a warning
   about neoplasms (new abnormal growths, including cancers) and an absolute contraindication in
   active malignancy. (Grade A for the label statements.)

There is a fifth point specific to this compound. Because tesamorelin genuinely is FDA-approved, the
phrase "FDA-approved peptide" is technically true and can be attached to material that is not the
approved product, for a use that is not the approved use, in a population that is not the approved
population. That is the most likely way a member will be misled about this compound.

Xenios has not confirmed identity, purity, sterility, or any other quality documentation for any
product containing this compound. See QUALITY_AND_DOCUMENTATION.md.

## What it is

Tesamorelin is a peptide (a short chain of amino acids, the building blocks that make up proteins).
It is a synthetic analog of human growth hormone-releasing hormone, a natural signalling hormone. FDA
labeling describes it as a "growth hormone-releasing factor analog." It was developed as TH9507 by
Theratechnologies Inc.

In the United States it is a prescription product regulated as a biologic, approved and supplemented
through the Biologics License Application pathway. It is marketed as EGRIFTA (the original product),
EGRIFTA SV, and EGRIFTA WR. Only two tesamorelin products are currently listed on DailyMed, the
National Library of Medicine's official labeling registry, and both come from the same manufacturer.

Tesamorelin is commonly confused with other compounds in or near the same mechanistic family, none of
which hold a current FDA-approved indication: CJC-1295, CJC-1293, ipamorelin, ibutamoren (also called
MK-677), and hexarelin. Sermorelin sits in this family as well and was previously approved, though
its current United States marketing status is [UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human
source check]. Tesamorelin is the only member of this family with a current FDA-approved indication.

It is also frequently conflated in consumer marketing with recombinant human growth hormone
(somatropin). That is a different molecule with a different regulatory status, and evidence about one
does not transfer to the other.

## Why interest exists

Interest in tesamorelin outside its approved use comes from a genuine finding applied to a population
where it was never tested.

In the trials, tesamorelin reduced visceral adipose tissue, the fat stored deep in the abdomen around
the organs. Visceral fat is the fat compartment most consistently associated with cardiometabolic
risk, and it is the compartment people find hardest to influence. A compound reported to reduce it
specifically, while lean mass modestly increased, is naturally appealing to anyone thinking about
body composition, metabolic health, or aging.

The FDA approval amplifies that appeal. In a category where almost nothing is approved for anything,
an approved compound reads as validated.

Xenios does not endorse that reasoning. The trials reporting these effects were conducted exclusively
in adults with HIV-associated lipodystrophy, the approval is written for that population only, and
the label itself states the product is not a weight-loss product. The interest is real and
understandable. The evidence supporting the extrapolation does not exist in anything we retrieved.

## Terminology

- **Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids, the building blocks that make up proteins.
- **GHRH (growth hormone-releasing hormone).** A natural hormone from the hypothalamus that signals
  the pituitary gland to release growth hormone. An "analog" is a synthetic molecule built to
  resemble it.
- **Analog.** A synthetic molecule designed to resemble a natural one closely enough to act at the
  same receptor.
- **Pituitary gland.** A small gland at the base of the brain that releases growth hormone and other
  hormones.
- **IGF-1 (insulin-like growth factor 1).** A growth signal in the blood that rises when growth
  hormone rises. It is commonly measured as a marker of growth hormone activity.
- **Visceral adipose tissue (VAT).** Fat stored deep in the abdomen around the organs, as opposed to
  fat directly under the skin.
- **Subcutaneous adipose tissue.** Fat stored directly under the skin.
- **Lipodystrophy.** An abnormal distribution of body fat. HIV-associated lipodystrophy is a
  recognized pattern seen in some people living with HIV, historically linked to certain
  antiretroviral therapies.
- **Antiretroviral therapy (ART).** The standard medication regimen used to treat HIV.
- **Surrogate endpoint.** A measurement that stands in for a health outcome, for example an imaging
  measure of fat or a blood lipid value, rather than a heart attack, hospitalization, or death.
- **Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial.** The strongest common trial design.
  Participants are assigned by chance to active drug or an inactive placebo, and neither participants
  nor investigators know who received which.
- **Meta-analysis.** A statistical pooling of results from multiple trials.
- **Contraindication.** A condition under which a product must not be used.
- **Neoplasm.** A new abnormal growth of tissue, including cancers.
- **Biologic / BLA / sBLA.** A regulatory category for products made from or resembling biological
  molecules. The Biologics License Application is the approval pathway. A supplemental application
  (sBLA) covers changes to an already-approved product.
- **Grey market.** Sale outside a licensed, regulated supply chain.
- **Limitation of Use.** A statement printed on an FDA-approved label describing what the product is
  not established to do.

## Proposed mechanism

The following is the proposed mechanism as described in FDA labeling and in the manufacturer's trial
literature. Parts of it are directly demonstrated in humans, and parts remain hypothetical. Both are
marked.

Tesamorelin is proposed to bind GHRH receptors on the anterior pituitary gland and stimulate the
body's own pulsatile release of growth hormone, which in turn raises circulating IGF-1. Raised growth
hormone and IGF-1 signalling is proposed to increase the breakdown of fat, preferentially in the
visceral compartment.

The first part of that chain is demonstrated rather than merely proposed, and in humans. In the 2007
New England Journal of Medicine trial (PMID 18057338), conducted in HIV-infected adults, IGF-I rose
81.0 percent in the tesamorelin arm compared with a 5.0 percent decrease with placebo. Target
engagement in humans is therefore shown, not assumed. (Grade B, in that population.)

What remains proposed rather than established:

- That the reduction in visceral fat translates into any reduced cardiovascular or liver-related
  clinical event. FDA labeling carries an explicit Limitation of Use stating long-term cardiovascular
  safety has not been established. (Grade G for the clinical-benefit inference.)
- That the mechanism produces meaningful benefit in people without HIV-associated lipodystrophy. No
  randomized body-composition trial in such a population was located. (Grade G.)
- The consequences of prolonged IGF-1 elevation. The FDA label states these effects are unknown. This
  is the central open mechanistic safety question, because IGF-1 is a growth signal and the same
  label separately warns about neoplasms. (Unknown, not graded as evidence.)

Species note: the human IGF-1 finding above is from a human trial. No preclinical animal or cell
studies were retrieved in this research session, so no animal mechanism claims appear in this Guide.

## Human evidence

All five randomized human studies below were retrieved and verified on 2026-07-19 at the PubMed
record level. Full texts were not retrieved, so effect sizes and quoted phrases come from those
abstract-level records. No dosing information is reproduced.

Four of the five were conducted exclusively in adults living with HIV. The fifth, in adults with type
2 diabetes, did not measure body composition.

### Falutz 2007, New England Journal of Medicine (PMID 18057338)

- **Design.** Multicenter, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial over 26 weeks. One of
  the two pivotal registrational trials.
- **Population.** 412 HIV-infected patients (86 percent male) with abdominal fat accumulation, on
  antiretroviral therapy. Not a general or healthy population.
- **Findings.** Visceral adipose tissue decreased 15.2 percent with tesamorelin versus a 5.0 percent
  increase with placebo. Triglycerides fell 50 mg/dL versus a 9 mg/dL increase with placebo. The
  cholesterol-to-HDL ratio improved significantly (P less than 0.001 for these comparisons). IGF-I
  rose 81.0 percent versus a 5.0 percent decrease with placebo.
- **Limitations.** Population is exclusively HIV-infected adults on antiretroviral therapy, so it
  says nothing about people without HIV-associated lipodystrophy. Predominantly male, which limits
  inference to women. 26 weeks only, so no long-term outcome or safety data. All endpoints are
  surrogate. More participants withdrew from the tesamorelin arm for adverse events, though overall
  adverse event frequencies did not differ significantly. Manufacturer-sponsored registrational
  trial.
- **Grade.** B, restricted to HIV-associated abdominal fat accumulation. G for any read-across to
  other populations.

### Falutz 2010, Journal of Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndromes (PMID 20101189)

- **Design.** Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial with a safety extension. Twelve
  months total, a six-month efficacy phase followed by a six-month extension. Second pivotal
  registrational trial.
- **Population.** 404 HIV-infected adults on antiretroviral therapy with excess abdominal fat.
- **Findings.** Visceral adipose tissue fell 10.9 percent versus a 0.6 percent decline with placebo
  over six months (p less than 0.0001). With continued treatment, an approximately 18 percent
  visceral fat reduction was sustained through month twelve. In participants switched from active
  drug to placebo, the abstract reports that the initial six-month improvements in visceral adipose
  tissue were rapidly lost.
- **Limitations.** The reversal on discontinuation is the single most important limitation for a
  member. Benefit appears to require continuous use, and continuous use runs directly into the
  label's unknown-long-term-IGF-1 statement and its unestablished-cardiovascular-safety statement.
  Again exclusively HIV-associated lipodystrophy. Surrogate endpoints only. Manufacturer-sponsored.
- **Grade.** B, restricted to that population.

### Stanley 2014, JAMA (PMID 25038357)

- **Design.** Double-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled trial over six months.
  Investigator-initiated and academic rather than manufacturer-run.
- **Population.** 50 antiretroviral-treated HIV-infected men and women with abdominal fat
  accumulation (48 received treatment).
- **Findings.** Visceral adipose tissue decreased by approximately 34 cm2 in the treatment group
  versus an 8 cm2 increase in controls. Liver fat showed a median reduction of 2.0 percentage points
  versus a 0.9 percentage point increase with placebo.
- **Limitations.** Small, and the authors themselves describe the study as preliminary, stating that
  further studies are needed to determine the clinical importance and long-term consequences of the
  findings. Same narrow HIV population. Liver fat here is an imaging surrogate, not a tissue or
  clinical liver outcome.
- **Grade.** C, restricted to that population.

### Badran 2026, Obesity Research and Clinical Practice (PMID 41545261)

- **Design.** Systematic review and random-effects meta-analysis of five randomized controlled
  trials.
- **Population.** Adults with HIV-associated lipodystrophy, tesamorelin versus placebo. The title of
  the paper itself scopes the entire pooled evidence base to HIV-associated lipodystrophy.
- **Findings.** Pooled visceral adipose tissue reduction of 27.71 cm2 (P less than 0.001). Hepatic
  fat decreased 4.28 percent (P less than 0.001). Lean body mass increased 1.42 kg (P less than
  0.001). Reported without serious adverse events or perturbation of glucose. Adverse events noted
  were joint pain, muscle pain, tingling or altered sensation, and injection-site redness. There was
  no significant reduction in subcutaneous fat and no significant reduction in body mass index.
- **Limitations.** The null findings matter as much as the positive ones. No change in body mass
  index and no change in subcutaneous fat means this is a visceral-compartment-specific effect, not
  weight loss, which is consistent with the label's own limitation. All pooled trials are in
  HIV-associated lipodystrophy, so the pooled result cannot be read across to other populations.
  Pooling does not resolve trial-level heterogeneity or the manufacturer sponsorship of the
  underlying pivotal trials. The abstract did not enumerate limitations explicitly and the full text
  was not retrieved.
- **Grade.** B, restricted to that population.

### Clemmons 2017, PLoS One (PMID 28617838)

- **Design.** Randomized, placebo-controlled trial with three arms (placebo and two active arms) over
  12 weeks.
- **Population.** 53 patients with type 2 diabetes. This is the only randomized human trial retrieved
  in a population without HIV, which makes it directly relevant to any question about use outside the
  approved indication.
- **Findings.** Essentially null. The abstract reports no significant differences between groups in
  relative insulin response over the 12-week period, and that at week 12 fasting glucose, HbA1c, and
  overall diabetes control were not significantly different between groups. Total and non-HDL
  cholesterol showed modest reductions in one active arm versus placebo.
- **Limitations.** This trial did not report body composition outcomes at all, so it provides no
  evidence for body-composition use outside the approved indication. It is short, small, and
  manufacturer-sponsored. It should be read as short-term glycemic neutrality information in type 2
  diabetes and for nothing else.
- **Grade.** C for the short-term glycemic observation in type 2 diabetes. It supports no
  body-composition claim at any grade.

### What is absent

We searched specifically for randomized human body-composition trials of tesamorelin in populations
without HIV. None were found in PubMed or ClinicalTrials.gov in this session. That absence is a
finding and is reported as one rather than filled in with weaker material.

## Contradictions and disagreement

### Does tesamorelin impair blood sugar control?

**Position A.** The FDA-approved label carries "Glucose Intolerance or Diabetes Mellitus" as an
explicit Warning and Precaution. This is consistent with the known tendency of growth hormone axis
stimulation to affect blood sugar. Source: DailyMed labeling, retrieved 2026-07-19.

**Position B.** The 2026 meta-analysis of five randomized trials (PMID 41545261) reported the effect
was achieved without perturbation of glucose, and the dedicated type 2 diabetes trial (PMID 28617838)
found no significant between-group difference in fasting glucose, HbA1c, or overall diabetes control
at 12 weeks.

**Assessment.** Both are likely correct at different timescales and in different populations. The
null trial results are short (12 weeks in the diabetes study, and the pooled trials largely six to
twelve months) and are group averages, which can conceal individual susceptibility. The label warning
is the conservative position and it is FDA's own position on the approved product. The correct
framing for a member: short-term randomized data has not shown a group-level blood sugar penalty, FDA
still requires the warning, and long-term data does not exist. The null findings are not a clearance.

### Does the approval constitute evidence for general body composition use?

**Position A.** Retailer, telehealth, and peptide-vendor pages surfaced during searching frame
tesamorelin as a clinically proven peptide for visceral fat with general applicability, and assert
that smaller studies in metabolic syndrome and abdominal obesity show similar visceral fat reduction
in populations without HIV.

**Position B.** Every randomized trial and the 2026 meta-analysis we retrieved that measured body
composition was conducted exclusively in HIV-infected adults with lipodystrophy or abdominal fat
accumulation on antiretroviral therapy. The single randomized trial retrieved in a population without
HIV did not report body composition outcomes at all. The FDA label states the product is not
indicated for weight loss management, and the meta-analysis found no significant change in body mass
index or subcutaneous fat.

**Assessment.** Position B is correct and Position A is not supported by anything we retrieved. We
searched specifically for the trials Position A claims to exist, and the only results returned were
retailer, telehealth, and peptide-vendor pages, which are excluded source types for this Guide. We
could not verify a single such trial through PubMed or ClinicalTrials.gov. Position A is graded G
(unverified) and, as stated in marketing form, is a prohibited claim on a Xenios member-facing
surface. This is the most important disagreement in the record for a member to understand.

## Limitations

- Every body-composition finding in this Guide comes from a single population: adults living with HIV
  who had abdominal fat accumulation while on antiretroviral therapy. There is no verified evidence
  base outside it.
- All reported benefits are surrogate endpoints: imaging measures of fat compartments, blood lipids,
  IGF-1, and liver fat fraction. No trial retrieved measured heart attacks, liver disease outcomes,
  hospitalization, or death.
- The largest pivotal trial was 86 percent male, which limits what can be inferred about women.
- The longest randomized exposure retrieved was twelve months. Nothing retrieved speaks to longer use.
- The two pivotal trials and the type 2 diabetes trial were manufacturer-sponsored. The JAMA trial
  was investigator-initiated but small.
- Full texts were not retrieved for any trial. All evidence entries are drawn from directly fetched
  PubMed abstract records.
- No preclinical primary studies were retrieved.
- None of the human evidence was generated with material sold outside the licensed supply chain, so
  none of it can be assumed to describe such material.

## Quality and identity

This section describes the unregulated-market reality. It is not a sourcing guide, and Xenios does
not provide acquisition guidance.

**The central problem with this specific compound is regulatory-status laundering.** Tesamorelin
genuinely is FDA-approved, which makes "FDA-approved peptide" a technically true phrase that can be
attached to a product that is not the approved product, for a use that is not the approved use, in a
population that is not the approved population. The approval attaches to specific licensed products
(EGRIFTA SV and EGRIFTA WR, from Theratechnologies Inc., listed on DailyMed) for reduction of excess
abdominal fat in HIV-infected adult patients with lipodystrophy. It does not attach to material sold
under the name tesamorelin outside that licensed supply chain, and it does not attach to
body-composition, longevity, or athletic use.

**The most useful single checkable fact.** Only two tesamorelin products are listed on DailyMed, both
from a single manufacturer. A member who encounters a tesamorelin product that cannot be traced to
that licensed supply chain is looking at something outside the approved product entirely, and none of
the human evidence in this Guide was generated with such material. This is verifiable directly at the
DailyMed URL in the References section.

**General grey-market risk.** [UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]

## Safety categories

These are categories of concern drawn from the FDA-approved label and from the retrieved trials. This
is not a complete list of possible effects, and nothing here should be read as reassurance. Absence
of a category from this list is not evidence of absence.

**Contraindications printed on the approved label.** Use is contraindicated in disruption of the
hypothalamic-pituitary axis (from pituitary surgery or removal, pituitary underfunction, pituitary
tumor, head irradiation, or head trauma), in active malignancy, in known hypersensitivity to
tesamorelin or to mannitol, and in pregnancy. The pregnancy contraindication is stated on the basis
that there is no benefit in pregnancy and there is potential for fetal harm. (Grade A as label
content.)

**Neoplasms.** An explicit Warnings and Precautions heading on the label. Active malignancy is an
absolute contraindication, and prior malignancies must be inactive with treatment complete before
use. The label notes increased background malignancy risk in HIV-positive patients and directs
discontinuation if a recurrent malignancy appears. Because this is a growth-signal compound, this
warning is mechanistically coherent and should not be read as boilerplate. (Grade A as label
content.)

**Raised IGF-1 with unknown long-term consequences.** An explicit Warnings and Precautions heading.
The label states that the effects of prolonged elevations in IGF-1 levels are unknown. The 2007 trial
quantified an 81.0 percent IGF-I increase versus placebo, so the elevation is substantial and real
rather than theoretical. Combined with the rapid loss of effect on discontinuation, sustained use
implies sustained elevation of unknown long-term consequence. (Grade A as label content, Grade B for
the measured elevation in that population.)

**Fluid retention, including swelling, joint pain, and carpal tunnel syndrome.** An explicit Warnings
and Precautions heading. Joint pain, peripheral swelling, muscle pain, and limb pain are also among
the most commonly reported adverse reactions on the label. The 2026 meta-analysis independently
reported joint pain, muscle pain, and altered sensation across the pooled trials, so this category
replicates across sources. (Grade A as label content, Grade B for the pooled replication.)

**Glucose intolerance and diabetes mellitus.** An explicit Warnings and Precautions heading. See
Contradictions above for the tension between this warning and the short-term null trial findings.
(Grade A as label content.)

**Hypersensitivity and allergic reactions.** An explicit Warnings and Precautions heading. Known
hypersensitivity to tesamorelin or excipients is a contraindication, and the label directs immediate
medical attention for allergic reactions. (Grade A as label content.)

**Injection site reactions.** Redness, itching, pain, and bruising at the injection site appear both
as a Warnings and Precautions heading and among the most common adverse reactions on the label.
Injection-site redness was also reported in the 2026 pooled analysis. (Grade A as label content.)

**Increased mortality in acute critical illness.** An explicit Warnings and Precautions heading on
the label, directing consideration of discontinuation in severely ill patients. (Grade A as label
content.)

**Long-term cardiovascular safety not established.** This is not an adverse event report. It is a
printed Limitation of Use, and it is a safety signal by absence. The compound reduces a
cardiometabolic risk marker without demonstrated long-term cardiovascular benefit or safety. Anyone
reasoning from it for cardiometabolic purposes is reasoning from a surrogate. (Grade A as label
content.)

**Effect reverses on discontinuation, implying open-ended exposure.** From the twelve-month trial:
improvements in visceral adipose tissue were rapidly lost in participants switched to placebo. This
is safety-relevant because it means benefit requires continuous use, which compounds every
unknown-long-term-exposure item above. (Grade B.)

## Interactions

Xenios does not provide interaction advice. The following are categories that require review by a
qualified clinician who knows the individual's full medication and health history. This list is not
exhaustive.

- **Any history of cancer, current or past.** Active malignancy is an absolute contraindication on
  the label, and prior malignancies require inactive status with treatment complete.
- **Any pituitary or hypothalamic condition or history**, including pituitary surgery or removal,
  pituitary underfunding, pituitary tumor, head irradiation, or head trauma. These are absolute
  contraindications on the label.
- **Pregnancy, possible pregnancy, and pregnancy planning.** An absolute contraindication on the
  label. Breastfeeding was not addressed in the record assembled for this Guide and should be raised
  with a clinician.
- **Diabetes, prediabetes, and any blood-sugar-affecting medication**, given the label's glucose
  warning and the unresolved disagreement described above.
- **Any acute or severe illness**, given the label warning about increased mortality in acute
  critical illness.
- **Known allergy or sensitivity to tesamorelin or to mannitol.**
- **HIV and antiretroviral therapy.** The approved population is defined by this context, and any
  discussion belongs with the HIV treating clinician.
- **Any other growth hormone axis product**, including recombinant human growth hormone and other
  growth hormone secretagogues. No specific interaction data was retrieved, which is itself a reason
  for professional review.

## Unknowns

- Long-term cardiovascular safety. Stated as unestablished on the FDA label itself, despite the
  compound reducing visceral adiposity, a cardiometabolic risk marker. No cardiovascular outcome
  trial was found.
- The consequences of prolonged IGF-1 elevation. The label states these effects are unknown. Because
  benefit reverses on discontinuation, sustained use implies sustained elevation, so this unknown is
  not avoidable by design.
- Whether tesamorelin produces any body composition benefit in adults without HIV-associated
  lipodystrophy. No randomized body-composition trial in such a population was located.
- Whether visceral fat reduction on this compound translates into any clinical outcome, such as
  cardiac events, liver outcomes, or mortality. All retrieved endpoints are surrogate.
- Long-term risk of neoplasms. The label warns on it and contraindicates active malignancy, but no
  long-term cancer-incidence data was retrieved.
- Effects in women. The largest pivotal trial was 86 percent male.
- Whether any of the human evidence transfers to material sold under the name tesamorelin outside the
  licensed supply chain, given the absence of identity, purity, or sterility assurance there.
- Duration of use for which any safety statement can be made. The longest retrieved randomized
  exposure was twelve months.
- The registration record numbers for the pivotal trials. Candidate numbers appeared in search
  results, but ClinicalTrials.gov records could not be parsed in this session, so no registration
  identifiers are recorded here rather than risk publishing an unverified one.

## Questions for a qualified professional

These are questions a member could reasonably take to a physician, ideally one familiar with
endocrinology. They are framed to produce useful answers, not to prompt a particular one.

1. The approved use is for adults living with HIV who have lipodystrophy. Does anything in my history
   place me in or near that population, and if not, what would you be extrapolating from?
2. The FDA label states this is not indicated for weight loss management. If my goal is body
   composition, what does the label's own limitation mean for my situation?
3. The label says long-term cardiovascular safety has not been established. What would you want to
   monitor, and what would you not be able to monitor?
4. This compound raises IGF-1 substantially, and the label says the effects of prolonged elevation
   are unknown. Would you measure IGF-1, and what value or trend would cause you to stop?
5. Given my personal and family history of cancer, how do you read the label's neoplasm warning and
   the active-malignancy contraindication?
6. The benefit reversed rapidly when people stopped in the twelve-month trial. If continuous use is
   what maintaining an effect requires, what is your view on open-ended use of a compound with no
   data beyond twelve months?
7. The label carries a glucose intolerance warning while short trials found no group-level effect.
   Given my blood sugar history, which of those two do you weight more heavily, and why?
8. I have been shown material described as FDA-approved tesamorelin. How would you verify whether a
   specific product is the licensed approved product, and what would you conclude if it is not?
9. What are the pituitary, head injury, and pregnancy contraindications actually screening for, and
   does anything in my history trigger one?
10. If I decided against this, what better-evidenced options would you point me toward for the same
    underlying goal?
