# Semax: questions members actually ask

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

These answers follow the same rule as the Guide itself. Where the honest answer is that nobody
knows, that is the answer given.

---

## 1. Does Semax work?

For the thing most people are asking about, focus and memory in a healthy person, there is no
evidence at all. That is not a hedge. **No human study of Semax with a cognitive endpoint in
healthy people was located** (checked 2026-07-21).

For other things, there is some human evidence, and it is weak. Four human studies were
retrieved. Two are Russian-language clinical studies in stroke and cerebrovascular disease, in
110 and 187 patients, neither described as randomized, placebo-controlled, or blinded. Two are
small neuroimaging studies in healthy volunteers, in 24 and 52 people, which reported differences
in brain scans rather than any change in how anyone thought, felt, or functioned.

**No randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial of Semax was found for any indication.**
Zero Semax studies are registered on ClinicalTrials.gov. No systematic review or meta-analysis of
Semax exists in PubMed's review-filtered results.

The honest claim grade for "Semax improves cognition in a healthy person" is **G (Unverified)**.
The grade for the cerebrovascular findings as reported is **C (Early human evidence)**, which
here means only that a human study reports them, not that they are reliable.

---

## 2. But the studies found something. Why does that not count?

It counts as a reason to run a proper trial. It does not count as a finding about you.

Three specific problems, and they compound each other.

**The studies are not designed to separate the compound from everything else.** In the stroke
study, patients were not assigned to groups by chance, and rehabilitation timing varied. So the
reported Semax effect cannot be separated from how much rehabilitation someone got, or from
whatever made them end up in one group rather than the other.

**Nothing was registered in advance.** Prospective registration means publicly recording what a
study will measure before it runs. Without it, there is no way to tell whether the reported
results were the ones the researchers set out to measure or the ones that happened to look good.
One of the two imaging studies explicitly describes its findings as post hoc, meaning the analysis
was decided after seeing the data.

**Brain scan differences are not benefits.** Both healthy-volunteer studies measured functional
connectivity, which is a statistical description of how activity in two brain regions varies
together on a scan. A difference in that measure is not something a person experiences. It is a
stand-in for an outcome, not an outcome.

---

## 3. Who did the research?

This is the question that changes the picture most, and almost nobody asks it.

The four human studies retrieved trace back to roughly **two Russian research groups**. The two
neuroimaging studies share nearly the same author list, and one of those authors is named in
search summaries as a leader of the group that originally developed Semax. The two clinical
studies share a senior author.

**No replication by an unaffiliated group in another country was found.**

That is not an accusation against anyone. It is a structural fact about the evidence, and it is
the single most important limitation of this compound's record. When the people who developed a
compound are also the people who publish the studies showing it works, and nobody independent has
repeated the work, the findings stay provisional no matter how many of them there are.

---

## 4. Is it safe?

Nobody knows, and this is the answer that matters most.

**No adequate human safety dataset was retrieved.** That is the finding, not a gap in the search.
Across all four human studies, exactly one makes any tolerability statement: the 2005 study of 187
patients described a minor percentage of side effects and good tolerability across age groups.
That is an uncontrolled impression from a single non-randomized study, read from an English
abstract. It is not a safety characterization.

No adverse-event tables, no long-term follow-up, no pharmacovigilance database, and no regulator
safety review were retrieved.

**There is no human safety data at all in healthy people**, who are the group most likely to be
reading this. The two healthy-volunteer studies enrolled 24 and 52 people in short imaging
studies, and neither was retrieved as reporting a safety endpoint. Dozens of people in short
studies cannot detect uncommon or delayed harms.

The critical point: nobody reporting harm is not the same as nobody being harmed. Absence of
reported harm in a literature this small reflects absence of study.

Xenios does not describe Semax as safe, as well tolerated, or as free of side effects, because no
evidence supports any of those statements.

---

## 5. It is an approved medicine in Russia. Does that not mean it is proven?

No, and this is the most consequential misreading of this compound.

Two separate things are true here and both matter.

**First, registration in one country carries no weight in another.** Semax is reported to be a
registered prescription medicine in the Russian Federation and to appear on the Russian List of
Vital and Essential Drugs approved on 2011-12-07. That is a national instrument. It confers no
legal or scientific standing in the United States, the European Union, the United Kingdom, or
anywhere else. It should never be paraphrased as "approved abroad" or "a licensed medicine
overseas" in a way that implies standing here.

**Second, even the Russian status could not be confirmed directly.** Research could not retrieve
the Russian State Register of Medicines record itself. The claim rests on a tertiary reference
plus a search-engine summary. No certificate number was obtained and no Russian regulator page was
read. It is reported, not verified.

And separately from both: a national registration is a regulatory decision made under one
country's evidence standards at one point in time. It is not the same thing as a body of
randomized, independently replicated evidence, which for Semax does not exist.

## 6. Is the FDA about to approve it?

No. This is a specific piece of misinformation worth addressing directly.

What actually happened, from the primary document: a Federal Register notice published 2026-04-16
(Docket FDA-2025-N-6895) records that "Semax (free base)" and "Semax acetate" appear on a list of
bulk drug substances **nominated** for possible inclusion on the section 503A Bulk Drug Substances
List, with nominated uses listed as cerebral ischemia, migraine, and trigeminal neuralgia. An
advisory committee meeting to discuss those nominations was scheduled for July 23 to 24, 2026.

What that is not:

- It is **not** FDA review of a drug application.
- It is **not** an approval, and the 503A list is not an approval pathway. It is a mechanism
  governing which raw substances pharmacies may use when preparing customised medicines.
- It is **not** an efficacy finding or an endorsement.
- The nomination was made by an outside party, not by FDA.

As of 2026-07-21 the meeting had not occurred and no outcome exists. Vendor pages describing this
as Semax "heading toward FDA approval" are wrong, and this Guide states no outcome. A human
reviewer must re-check after the meeting.

## 7. Is it banned in sport?

**This Guide does not say, and that is deliberate.**

The WADA Prohibited List could not be read during research; the fetch returned no usable content.
Every Semax anti-doping status claim that surfaced in search results came from peptide-vendor and
supplement-marketing pages, some reasoning by analogy to other peptide categories. Those are
vendor inferences, not WADA statements, and they are inadmissible as regulatory evidence.

Publishing an inference here would be worse than publishing nothing, because an athlete could act
on it and lose a career over it.

If you compete in a tested sport, ask your own anti-doping authority directly.

## 9. What about the product itself, separately from the science?

Nobody appears to have checked.

**No independent testing of material sold as Semax was located.** No product-testing dataset was
retrieved during research. That is an absence of information, not a favourable result.

A tertiary source states that Semax is widely sold by online vendors and used as a purported
nootropic. Material obtained that way sits outside any pharmacy or regulatory chain, so identity,
purity, sterility, and content are unverified. No regulator is inspecting a production line
against a filed specification.

There is also a naming problem. United States regulatory filings treat "Semax (free base)" and
"Semax acetate" as two separate bulk substances, so a product labelled simply "Semax" does not by
itself tell you which material it contains.

QUALITY_AND_DOCUMENTATION.md sets out the full documentation standard a supplier would have to
meet. Every item on it is currently unmet for this compound.

## 10. Why is the research so hard to pin down?

Three reasons, and they are worth naming because they explain the shape of everything above.

**Language and access.** The two clinical-outcome studies are published in Russian in the same
journal, and only English abstracts were readable during research. Full methods, randomization
procedures, blinding, adverse-event tables, and statistical analysis plans were never seen.

**No systematic appraisal exists.** There is no systematic review or meta-analysis of Semax, so
nobody has formally assessed the risk of bias in this literature. The 14 review-type records
returned by a PubMed search were narrative reviews mentioning Semax alongside other peptides, and
narrative reviews do not appraise bias.

**The information environment is dominated by sellers.** Most search results across every query
were peptide retailers, marketing blogs, and peptide-encyclopedia sites. Several assert specific
regulatory and safety positions sourced to no regulator. Several publish dosing protocols. None of
them carries a fact anywhere in this Guide.

---

## 11. There are around 230 papers on PubMed. Does that not count for something?

It counts for what it is, which is a real research literature about a molecule.

Of the first 20 of those records reviewed, roughly 19 were preclinical (animal, cell, or
biochemical work) or narrative review, and only one was a human clinical study. So the size of the
literature is a measure of scientific interest, not of human evidence.

This Guide is also deliberately restrained about the preclinical work. Three of the most
frequently cited preclinical records were read from a search results listing only, so their
species were never confirmed. A finding whose species is unknown cannot responsibly be written
down, because "Semax increases BDNF" reads to most people as a fact about human beings. Those
three findings therefore do not appear in the Guide at all until a human fetches each record and
names its species.

## 12. What would change your mind?

Specific, checkable things. If these appeared, this Guide would be revised.

- A randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial of Semax, prospectively registered, with a
  clinical or cognitive endpoint rather than a brain scan measure.
- Any human study conducted by a group with no affiliation to the compound's originators,
  particularly outside Russia.
- A systematic review or meta-analysis that formally appraises risk of bias across the existing
  literature.
- Full-text access to the two Russian clinical studies, allowing their designs and adverse-event
  reporting to be assessed properly.
- A retrievable Russian State Register of Medicines record establishing the registration
  particulars.
- Any published human safety or pharmacovigilance dataset.
- Independent analytical testing of material sold as Semax.

Until then, the accurate summary is: real preclinical interest, a genuine but jurisdictionally
confined regulatory history in one country, and no adequately controlled, independently replicated
human evidence for any of the cognitive or performance uses the market promotes.
