# Semax Contradictions and Divergences

Where the sources disagree, and what the Guide should say when they do. Source ids refer to
SOURCE_REGISTRY.md. Claim ids refer to CLAIM_TABLE.md.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

There are genuine contradictions here. Three of them concern claims a member is likely to
encounter before reading the Guide, and one of them (C-03) is resolved by refusing to state
anything at all.

## C-01. Whether the 2018 default-mode-network study was randomized

**Position A.** The fetched PubMed record summary characterized PMID 30225715 as a randomized
controlled trial with 14 participants receiving Semax and 10 receiving placebo (S01).

**Position B.** A search-engine summary in the same session described the best-powered recent
Semax study as non-randomized and not placebo-controlled. Separately, the retrieved abstract for
PMID 30225715 does not itself describe a randomization method, allocation concealment, or
blinding, and the unequal 14 versus 10 allocation is atypical for randomization.

**Assessment.** This is partly a conflation. The "non-randomized, 110 patients" description
belongs to the separate 2018 stroke study PMID 29798983 (S04), not to the imaging study. But the
randomization status of PMID 30225715 remains genuinely unconfirmed from the abstract alone,
and the label "randomized controlled trial" carries far more weight with a reader than the
retrieved evidence supports.

**What the Guide says.** PMID 30225715 is described only as a small placebo-comparison imaging
study in healthy volunteers. It is not described as a randomized controlled trial anywhere. See
SEM-H-05 and the prohibition at SEM-X-07. A human must read the full text to resolve this.

## C-02. What the United States FDA 503A activity means

**Position A.** Vendor and peptide-marketing pages frame the July 2026 Pharmacy Compounding
Advisory Committee consideration as Semax being "under FDA review" and heading toward
legitimacy (S20).

**Position B.** The primary Federal Register notice describes only that "Semax (free base)" and
"Semax acetate" were nominated for possible inclusion on the section 503A Bulk Drug Substances
List, with nominated uses listed as cerebral ischemia, migraine, and trigeminal neuralgia, and
that a committee would meet on July 23 to 24, 2026 (S08).

**Assessment.** The primary source wins decisively. Nomination by an outside party, and
discussion by an advisory committee, are not FDA review of a drug application and are not
approval. The 503A list is a compounding-eligibility mechanism, not a drug approval pathway. As
of 2026-07-21 the meeting had not occurred and no outcome exists.

**What the Guide says.** The nomination is stated as fact and the outcome is stated as unknown.
No momentum toward approval is implied. See SEM-U-09 and the prohibitions at SEM-X-02 and
SEM-X-03, and REGULATORY_STATUS.md. A human must re-check after the meeting and re-date the
regulatory section.

## C-03. Semax anti-doping status

**Position A.** Several vendor and peptide-blog pages assert a specific WADA classification for
Semax, variously reasoning by analogy to melanocortin peptides, to an S2 peptide category, or
asserting an S0 catch-all position (S20).

**Position B.** The WADA Prohibited List itself could not be retrieved this session (S15), and
none of these assertions is sourced to WADA.

**Assessment.** Unresolved, and it must be left unresolved. These are vendor inferences, not
WADA statements. Reasoning by analogy from another peptide class is not a regulatory fact, and
publishing an inference as a status would be worse than publishing nothing, because an athlete
could act on it.

**What the Guide says.** No WADA classification for Semax appears anywhere in this folder. Any
competing athlete is directed to their own anti-doping authority. See SEM-U-08 and the
prohibition at SEM-X-10.

## C-04. Strength of the stroke and cerebrovascular efficacy claims

**Position A.** The retrieved Russian studies report meaningful clinical benefit, including
improved functional recovery after ischemic stroke (S04) and, in the 2005 study, reduced risk of
stroke and transient ischemic attacks in chronic cerebrovascular insufficiency (S03).

**Position B.** Neither study is described as randomized, placebo-controlled, or blinded. Neither
is registered on ClinicalTrials.gov (S07). Both share a senior author. Only English abstracts
were readable. No independent replication outside Russia was found.

**Assessment.** The claims are not supportable at the strength stated. The honest characterization
is that these are uncontrolled or non-randomized reports from a small number of affiliated Russian
groups, which generate hypotheses and cannot establish efficacy. In the stroke study specifically,
rehabilitation timing and Semax exposure were not separated by randomization, so the reported
Semax effect cannot be disentangled from rehabilitation intensity or baseline group differences.

**What the Guide says.** Findings are attributed to their authors, labelled non-randomized in the
same sentence, and never written as outcome verbs. See SEM-H-03, SEM-H-04, and the prohibition at
SEM-X-06.

## C-05. Where jurisdictional status is most likely to be misread

**Position A.** Semax is reported to be a registered prescription medicine in the Russian
Federation and to appear on the Russian List of Vital and Essential Drugs approved on 2011-12-07,
which reads to a general audience as evidence that the compound is established medicine.

**Position B.** Registration in the Russian Federation confers no legal or scientific standing in
any other jurisdiction. Separately, this session could not retrieve the Russian State Register of
Medicines record itself (S18). The claim rests on a tertiary source (S10) plus a search-engine
summary naming a registration-certificate holder. No certificate number was retrieved and no
Russian regulator page was read.

**Assessment.** Both halves matter and they are independent of each other. Even if the Russian
registration is fully confirmed by a human, it still says nothing about any other country. And it
is not yet fully confirmed.

**What the Guide says.** The Russian status is reported as reported-but-not-primary-verified, and
is immediately followed by the statement that it carries no standing elsewhere. See SEM-U-07 and
the prohibition at SEM-X-01, which is the most important prohibition in this folder.

## C-06. Where a mechanism list reads as a benefit list

**Position A.** The breadth of reported preclinical mechanisms, covering BDNF, dopaminergic and
serotoninergic systems, immune response genes, opioid receptor gene expression, and copper
binding, is routinely presented as evidence that the compound does a great deal (S20).

**Position B.** Those are themes visible in the titles of records in a PubMed results listing
(S05). The species is not stated in every title, and three of the individual records were never
fetched (S12, S13, S14). A compound reported to touch that many pathways in animal and cell
models has a correspondingly wide surface for unstudied interactions in humans.

**Assessment.** Mechanistic breadth is an unresolved safety question, not a selling point. It is
also the weakest kind of evidence in this folder, since it comes from titles rather than from
read methods.

**What the Guide says.** The themes are described as themes visible in titles, never as findings,
and are paired with the interaction question. No finding from the three unfetched records appears
at all. See SEM-P-02, SEM-S-03, and the prohibition at SEM-X-09.

## C-07. Where the absence of evidence is itself contested

**Position A.** A search-engine summary claimed that English-language Semax reviews appear in the
Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews, which would imply a level of systematic appraisal that
would materially change this Guide.

**Position B.** A PubMed search restricted to review and systematic-review publication types
returned 14 records, none of which was a systematic review or meta-analysis of Semax (S06). The
Cochrane claim was not confirmed by any retrieval.

**Assessment.** Treat the Cochrane claim as unsupported. If a human reviewer confirms that such a
review exists, this folder must be revised substantially, because a systematic review would be the
first risk-of-bias appraisal in the record.

**What the Guide says.** No systematic review or meta-analysis of Semax exists in the retrieved
results. See SEM-Q-03 and SEM-Q-04.

## C-08. A note on the integrity of the retrieval record

Several regulatory pages could not be read this session: the FDA 503A landing page, the July 2026
advisory committee meeting page, the 2026 meeting-materials page, Drugs@FDA, the EMA search
endpoint, the WADA Prohibited List, and the Russian State Register of Medicines (S15 to S19).

These are declared as failed retrievals throughout this folder rather than papered over, and none
of them is treated as a confirmed negative. In particular, the Drugs@FDA HTTP 404 is a failed
retrieval and not proof of anything, and the absence of an EMA authorisation is a weak negative
resting on a tertiary source rather than on the regulator.

The one primary regulatory document that was successfully retrieved is the Federal Register full
text (S08), and every United States 503A statement in this folder comes from it.

As an adversarial check that the citations here reflect real retrieval rather than reconstruction,
a deliberately fabricated identifier was queried and correctly returned HTTP 404, while genuine
identifiers returned full records (S11).
