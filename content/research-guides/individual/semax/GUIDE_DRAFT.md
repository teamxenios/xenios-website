# Semax

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## One-minute summary

Semax is a synthetic seven-amino-acid peptide that circulates online as a cognitive enhancer.
The honest position, and the single most important thing in this Guide, is this: **for the use
the market actually promotes, cognitive enhancement in healthy people, there is no supporting
human study at all.** Human studies of Semax do exist, but they are about stroke and
cerebrovascular disease, or they measure brain scans rather than cognition, and none of them is
strong enough to establish that Semax does anything.

- **No randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial of Semax was found for any
  indication** (checked 2026-07-21).
- **Zero Semax studies are registered on ClinicalTrials.gov** (checked 2026-07-21). Without
  prospective registration there is no protection against selective outcome reporting, meaning
  the practice of reporting the results that came out well and quietly dropping the rest.
- **No systematic review or meta-analysis of Semax exists** in a PubMed search restricted to
  review publication types. The 14 records returned were narrative reviews mentioning Semax
  alongside other peptides, and narrative reviews do not appraise risk of bias.
- **Independence is the defining weakness.** The four human studies retrieved trace back to
  roughly two Russian research groups. The two neuroimaging studies share nearly the same author
  list, which includes a person named in search summaries as a leader of the group that
  originally developed Semax. The two clinical studies share a senior author. No replication by
  an unaffiliated group in another country was found.
- **The jurisdictional framing matters more here than for almost any compound.** Semax is
  reported to be a registered prescription medicine in the Russian Federation. It is approved
  nowhere else. Registration in Russia carries no legal or scientific weight in any other
  country and must never be read as approval elsewhere.
- **In the United States, Semax is not FDA approved.** The only verified United States
  regulatory fact is that "Semax (free base)" and "Semax acetate" were *nominated* for possible
  inclusion on a pharmacy compounding list, with an advisory committee meeting scheduled for
  July 23 to 24, 2026. A nomination is not a review of a drug application and not an approval.
  As of 2026-07-21 that meeting had not occurred and no outcome exists.
- **On safety, no adequate human dataset was retrieved.** One uncontrolled tolerability
  impression from a single 2005 non-randomized study is the entire retrieved safety signal.
  Total healthy-volunteer exposure in the retrieved record is on the order of dozens of people.
  Absence of reported harm in a literature this small is not evidence of safety.

Xenios does not sell Semax, does not supply it, and does not tell members how to obtain or use
it. This Guide exists so that a member reading about Semax elsewhere has an accurate account of
what is and is not known.

**Overall claim grade for "Semax improves cognition in healthy people": G (Unverified).**
**Overall claim grade for "Semax improves outcomes in stroke or cerebrovascular disease": C
(Early human evidence), from non-randomized, unregistered, unreplicated studies that cannot
establish efficacy.**

## What it is

Semax is a **heptapeptide**, meaning a chain of seven amino acids. Its sequence is
Met-Glu-His-Phe-Pro-Gly-Pro, usually written with single-letter codes as MEHFPGP.

It is described as an analogue of the ACTH(4-10) fragment of adrenocorticotropic hormone
(ACTH), a hormone the body makes, extended at one end with a three-amino-acid Pro-Gly-Pro tail.
"ACTH(4-10)" means amino acids 4 through 10 of that hormone. Semax was first described in the
scientific literature in 1991.

Two cautions attach to that description.

First, all of it rests on a tertiary source, meaning a reference work summarising other people's
work rather than a primary chemical registry. A reviewer should confirm the sequence and the
identity against a primary registry before publication.

Second, and more important for a reader: being derived from a hormone fragment tells you where a
molecule came from. It tells you nothing about what it does in a person. Semax is not ACTH, it
is not a hormone the body makes, and the parent hormone's known effects are not Semax's effects.

**A frequently repeated origin claim that is not confirmed here.** Semax is widely said to have
been developed at a named Russian research institute. That attribution appeared during research
only in search-engine summaries and vendor pages, in no primary or institutional source, so the
institute is deliberately not named in this Guide.
[UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]

**Identity is not fixed by the name alone.** United States regulatory filings treat "Semax (free
base)" and "Semax acetate" as two separate bulk substances. In practice this means the single
word "Semax" does not pin down which material is in a given product.

## Why interest exists

The interest in Semax is easy to describe and worth understanding without endorsing it.

The compound has a long history in one country. Semax is reported to be a registered
prescription medicine in the Russian Federation and to appear on the Russian List of Vital and
Essential Drugs approved by the Russian government on 2011-12-07. For a general reader, a
substance being a listed essential medicine somewhere reads as strong reassurance.

Alongside that, there is a real and substantial research literature. A PubMed search for Semax
returned approximately 230 records, covering neurotrophin biology, monoamine systems, gene
expression in brain injury models, and behavioural work. That is not nothing, and it is not a
compound invented by a marketing department.

Four things should be held alongside that interest.

**First, the regulatory reassurance does not cross borders.** Registration in the Russian
Federation confers no legal or scientific standing in any other jurisdiction. It is not an
approval in the United States, the European Union, the United Kingdom, or anywhere else, and it
should never be paraphrased as one. Separately, the Russian registration itself could not be
confirmed against the Russian State Register of Medicines during research, so even that fact is
reported rather than verified.

**Second, the research literature is preclinical.** Of the first 20 of those approximately 230
records reviewed, roughly 19 were preclinical (animal, cell, or biochemical work) or narrative
review, and only one was a human clinical study. A large literature about a molecule is not the
same as evidence about a person.

**Third, the human studies that do exist are about a different thing than the market sells.**
They concern ischemic stroke and chronic cerebrovascular insufficiency, in patients. The
consumer market promotes Semax for focus, memory, and mental performance in healthy people. No
human study of Semax with a cognitive endpoint in healthy people was located.

**Fourth, an interesting mechanism is not an outcome.** Interest describes why researchers
thought a compound was worth studying. It does not describe what happened when it was given to
people under conditions that could tell you anything reliable.

## Terminology

Every technical term used in this Guide, in plain English.

- **Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids, the building blocks of proteins. Shorter than a
  protein.
- **Heptapeptide.** A peptide made of exactly seven amino acids. Semax is one.
- **Amino acid.** A small molecule that links with others to form peptides and proteins.
- **Analogue.** A molecule deliberately built to resemble another one, usually with a
  modification.
- **ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone).** A hormone produced by the pituitary gland. Semax is
  described as an analogue of a fragment of it.
- **Fragment.** A piece of a larger peptide or protein chain. ACTH(4-10) means amino acids 4
  through 10 of ACTH.
- **Nootropic.** An informal marketing term for a substance claimed to improve mental
  performance. It is not a regulatory or scientific category.
- **Preclinical.** Research done before or outside any human study, meaning in animals, in
  cultured cells, or in cell-free chemical systems.
- **Randomized.** Participants are assigned to groups by chance, so the groups are comparable
  and the treatment effect can be separated from other differences between people.
- **Placebo-controlled.** One group receives an inactive comparison so that the effect of
  expectation can be separated from the effect of the substance.
- **Blinded.** Participants, and ideally the assessors, do not know who received what.
- **Non-randomized study.** A study where group assignment was not made by chance, so any
  difference between groups may reflect who ended up in which group rather than the treatment.
- **Prospective registration.** Publicly recording a study's design, endpoints, and analysis plan
  before it runs. It is what makes selective outcome reporting detectable.
- **Selective outcome reporting.** Reporting the measures that produced favourable results and
  omitting the ones that did not.
- **Post hoc analysis.** Analysis decided after seeing the data. It generates hypotheses. It
  does not test them.
- **Endpoint.** The thing a study actually measures.
- **Surrogate endpoint.** A measurable stand-in, such as a brain scan finding, used in place of
  an outcome a person would actually notice, such as feeling or functioning better. A change in
  a surrogate is not itself a benefit.
- **Functional MRI (fMRI).** A scan that infers brain activity from blood flow.
- **Resting-state functional MRI.** An fMRI scan taken while the person is at rest and not
  performing a task.
- **Functional connectivity.** A statistical measure of how much the activity in two brain
  regions varies together. It is a correlation in a scan, not a demonstrated communication
  channel.
- **Default mode network.** A set of brain regions that tend to be active together when a person
  is at rest rather than focused on a task.
- **Systematic review.** A review that searches the literature by a pre-stated method and
  formally appraises the risk of bias in each study.
- **Meta-analysis.** A statistical combination of results across studies.
- **Narrative review.** An informal review with no pre-stated search method and no risk-of-bias
  appraisal. It cannot establish efficacy.
- **Risk of bias.** The set of design features that determine how likely a study's result is to
  be wrong in a systematic direction.
- **BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor).** A protein involved in the growth and maintenance
  of nerve cells.
- **Neurotrophin.** A family of proteins, including BDNF, that support nerve cell survival and
  growth.
- **Dopaminergic and serotoninergic systems.** Brain signalling systems using dopamine and
  serotonin respectively.
- **Ischemic stroke.** A stroke caused by blocked blood flow to part of the brain.
- **Cerebrovascular insufficiency.** Reduced blood supply to the brain, as a chronic condition.
- **Transient ischemic attack (TIA).** A temporary interruption of blood flow to the brain,
  sometimes called a mini-stroke.
- **Hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis.** The body's core stress-hormone system, which
  includes the gland that produces ACTH.
- **Pharmacokinetics.** What the body does to a substance: absorption, distribution, metabolism,
  and elimination, including how long it lasts.
- **503A bulk drug substances list.** A United States framework governing which raw substances
  pharmacies may use in compounding, which means preparing customised medicines. It is a
  compounding-eligibility mechanism and not a drug approval pathway.
- **Nomination (in the 503A context).** A submission by an outside party proposing that a
  substance be considered for that list. A nomination is not a review of a drug application, not
  an efficacy finding, and not an endorsement.
- **Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee (PCAC).** The FDA advisory committee that discusses
  substances nominated for that list. Its recommendations advise FDA and are not final FDA
  decisions.
- **Marketing authorisation.** The European term for approval to sell a medicine.
- **List of Vital and Essential Drugs.** A Russian government list of medicines. It is a national
  instrument with no effect outside the Russian Federation.
- **Certificate of analysis (COA).** A document from a testing laboratory stating what a specific
  batch of material was found to contain.

## Proposed mechanism

Everything in this section is **proposed**. All of it derives from animal, cell, or biochemical
work, and none of it has been confirmed in a living human being. The claim grade for this entire
section is **D (Preclinical)** where a species-bearing statement is possible, and **G
(Unverified)** where it is not.

**What can honestly be said about the mechanism is very little, and the reason is methodological
rather than a matter of opinion.** Of the retrieved preclinical records, three of the most
frequently cited were read from a PubMed results listing only. Their titles were recorded, but
their abstracts were never fetched, so the species, tissue, and effect sizes are unconfirmed for
each. A finding whose species is unknown cannot be written down responsibly, because a sentence
like "Semax increases BDNF" reads to a general audience as a fact about people. Those three
records are therefore named in the source registry but their findings do not appear in this
Guide.

**Themes visible in the titles of the retrieved literature**, and stated as themes rather than
as findings, include copper chelation and amyloid-beta aggregation, neurotrophin transcription,
opioid receptor gene targeting, and behavioural models. Species were not stated in every title.
No individual claim should be built on any of these without a human fetching the specific record
and naming its species. Grade D as a description of the literature, and nothing more.

**No mechanism has been observed in a human being.** The two human studies conducted in healthy
volunteers measured brain connectivity on scans. A connectivity map is a statistical description
of a scan, not a mechanism, and not an effect a person would notice.

Material that describes a confident mechanism of action for Semax is ahead of what the retrieved
published science supports.

## Human evidence

Human evidence for Semax exists. That distinguishes it from many compounds in this category. But
it cannot carry the weight placed on it, and for the use the market promotes it is absent
entirely.

Four human studies were retrieved. All four are listed here with their limitations attached,
because in this case the limitations are the finding.

| Study | Design | Population | Reported finding | Grade |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| PMID 29798983 (2018, Russian) | Non-randomized comparison of a Semax-treated group with an untreated group at early and late rehabilitation timepoints. No randomization, placebo control, or blinding stated. | 110 adults after ischemic stroke (43 men, 67 women), mean age approximately 58 years | Authors report that Semax administration together with high BDNF levels was associated with faster improvement and a better final outcome on functional recovery measures, and that early initiation of rehabilitation independently improved outcomes. | C |
| PMID 15792140 (2005, Russian) | Comparative or evaluation study by PubMed publication type. Randomization, placebo control, and blinding not stated. | 187 adult and older-adult patients at various stages of chronic cerebrovascular insufficiency | Authors report clinical improvement, stabilization of disease progression, a reduced reported risk of stroke and transient ischemic attacks, and good tolerability across age groups. | C |
| PMID 30225715 (2018) | Resting-state functional MRI study with a placebo comparison group. A fetched summary called it randomized; the retrieved abstract states no randomization method, allocation concealment, or blinding, and the unequal group sizes are atypical for randomized allocation. | 24 healthy adults (11 men, 13 women), mean age approximately 44 years; 14 Semax, 10 placebo | Greater volume of the rostral (medial frontal cortex) subcomponent of the default mode network in the Semax group. An imaging measure, not a clinical outcome. | C |
| PMID 32342318 (2020) | Resting-state functional MRI connectivity study of Selank and Semax, described in the fetched record as randomized and controlled; method detail not verifiable from the abstract. | 52 healthy adults | Between-group differences in functional connectivity between the right amygdala and right temporal cortex, fusiform gyrus, and parahippocampal regions, identified via post hoc analysis. | C |

**What is missing from that table, and it is most of what matters.**

- **No randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial of Semax was found for any
  indication.**
- **Zero Semax studies are registered on ClinicalTrials.gov** (checked 2026-07-21). None of the
  four studies above has a corresponding prospective registration, so pre-specified endpoints and
  analysis plans cannot be checked against what was published. This matters acutely for
  PMID 32342318, whose findings are explicitly described as post hoc.
- **No systematic review or meta-analysis of Semax exists** in PubMed's review-filtered results.
  A search restricted to review and systematic-review publication types returned 14 records, all
  narrative reviews mentioning Semax alongside other peptides.
- **No study with a cognitive endpoint in healthy people was located.** The two healthy-volunteer
  studies measured brain connectivity. Neither measured memory, attention, focus, mood, or
  performance. The claim that Semax improves cognition in a healthy person has, in the retrieved
  record, nothing behind it.

**Two interpretive points that should travel with these studies wherever they are cited.**

In the stroke study, rehabilitation timing and Semax exposure were not separated by
randomization. The reported Semax effect therefore cannot be disentangled from rehabilitation
intensity or from baseline differences between the groups. This is not a quibble. It is the
reason the study cannot support a causal claim.

In the 2005 cerebrovascular study, the reported outcomes include reduced risk of stroke and
transient ischemic attacks. Outcome claims of that magnitude would normally require a
randomized, adequately powered, prospectively registered trial. This study is none of those
things, it is 21 years old, it is published in Russian, only its English abstract was readable,
and nothing retrieved replicates it outside Russia. It is hypothesis-generating.

Claim grade for any statement of the form "Semax improves cognition in a healthy person":
**G (Unverified)**. Claim grade for the cerebrovascular findings as reported: **C (Early human
evidence)**, which here means only that a human study reports them, not that they are reliable.

## Preclinical evidence

The Semax literature is overwhelmingly preclinical. A PubMed search returned approximately 230
records. Of the first 20 reviewed, roughly 19 were preclinical (animal, cell, or biochemical
work) or narrative review, and only one was a human clinical study.

**This section is deliberately short, and the reason is a rule rather than a shortage of
material.** Every preclinical finding in a Xenios Guide must name its species or model in the
same sentence as the finding. Three of the individual preclinical records retrieved for Semax
were read from a results listing only, so their species were never confirmed:

- PMID 16635254, titled to report that Semax binds specifically and increases BDNF protein
  levels. Species, tissue, and effect size unconfirmed.
- PMID 16362768, titled to report that Semax activates dopaminergic and serotoninergic brain
  systems. Species and methods unconfirmed.
- PMID 28255762, titled to report that Semax regulates immune response genes during ischemic
  brain injury. Species unconfirmed, although such models are typically rodent.

Because none of those three species is confirmed, **none of those three findings is stated in
this Guide**. They are recorded in the source registry with their status so a reviewer can fetch
each record and name its species, at which point they may enter at grade D at best.

**What can be said about the preclinical body as a whole**, at grade D: it is mixed animal, cell,
and biochemical work whose visible themes include copper chelation and amyloid-beta aggregation,
neurotrophin transcription, opioid receptor gene targeting, and behavioural models. Species were
not stated in every title. This is a map of a literature, not a set of results.

**A general caution.** A large preclinical literature is a reason to run good human trials. It is
not a substitute for them, and the size of a preclinical literature says nothing about whether the
compound works in a person. For Semax the preclinical body is substantial and the controlled human
body is effectively empty, which is exactly the pattern that produces confident marketing copy
with nothing underneath it.

## Contradictions

Where sources genuinely disagree, both positions are stated, along with an assessment of which
the retrieved evidence supports. The full set is in CONTRADICTIONS.md.

**1. Whether the 2018 default-mode-network study was randomized.**

- *Position A.* The fetched PubMed record summary characterized PMID 30225715 as a randomized
  controlled trial with 14 Semax and 10 placebo participants.
- *Position B.* A search-engine summary in the same session described the best-powered recent
  Semax study as non-randomized and not placebo-controlled. The retrieved abstract for
  PMID 30225715 does not describe a randomization method, allocation concealment, or blinding,
  and the unequal 14 versus 10 allocation is atypical for randomization.
- *Assessment.* Partly a conflation. The "non-randomized, 110 patients" description belongs to
  the separate stroke study PMID 29798983. But the randomization status of PMID 30225715 remains
  genuinely unconfirmed from the abstract alone. This Guide describes it only as a small
  placebo-comparison imaging study in healthy volunteers, and does not call it a randomized
  controlled trial.

**2. What the United States FDA 503A activity means.**

- *Position A.* Vendor and peptide-marketing pages frame the July 2026 advisory committee
  consideration as Semax being "under FDA review" and heading toward legitimacy.
- *Position B.* The primary Federal Register notice describes only that "Semax (free base)" and
  "Semax acetate" were nominated for possible inclusion on the section 503A Bulk Drug Substances
  List, with nominated uses listed as cerebral ischemia, migraine, and trigeminal neuralgia, and
  that a committee would meet on July 23 to 24, 2026.
- *Assessment.* The primary source wins decisively. Nomination by an outside party, and
  discussion by an advisory committee, are not FDA review of a drug application and not approval.
  As of 2026-07-21 the meeting had not occurred and no outcome exists. This Guide implies no
  momentum toward approval.

**3. Semax anti-doping status.**

- *Position A.* Several vendor and peptide-blog pages assert a specific WADA classification,
  variously reasoning by analogy to melanocortin peptides, to an S2 peptide category, or
  asserting an S0 catch-all position.
- *Position B.* The WADA Prohibited List itself could not be retrieved during research, and none
  of those assertions is sourced to WADA.
- *Assessment.* Unresolved, and left unresolved. These are vendor inferences, not WADA
  statements. **This Guide carries no WADA classification for Semax.** Any competing athlete
  should ask their own anti-doping authority directly.

**4. Strength of the stroke and cerebrovascular efficacy claims.**

- *Position A.* The retrieved Russian studies report meaningful clinical benefit, including
  improved functional recovery and, in the 2005 study, reduced risk of stroke and transient
  ischemic attacks.
- *Position B.* Neither study is described as randomized, placebo-controlled, or blinded, neither
  is registered on ClinicalTrials.gov, both share a senior author, only English abstracts were
  readable, and no independent replication outside Russia was found.
- *Assessment.* The claims are not supportable at the strength stated. The honest
  characterization is that these are uncontrolled or non-randomized reports from a small number
  of affiliated Russian groups, which generate hypotheses and cannot establish efficacy.

## Limitations

Limitations of the evidence base itself:

- No randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial of Semax was found for any indication.
- No Semax trial is registered on ClinicalTrials.gov, so selective outcome reporting cannot be
  excluded for any of the published reports.
- No systematic review or meta-analysis of Semax exists in the review-filtered PubMed results.
  Without one, no formal risk-of-bias appraisal of this literature has been done by anyone.
- Retrieved human sample sizes are 24, 52, 110, and 187. None is powered for a reliable clinical
  efficacy conclusion.
- The two healthy-volunteer studies used surrogate imaging endpoints. Whole-brain connectivity
  analyses carry a high false-positive risk, and one of the two studies describes its findings as
  post hoc.
- The two clinical-outcome studies are published in Russian in the same journal, and only English
  abstracts were readable. Randomization procedures, blinding, adverse-event tables, and
  statistical analysis plans were never seen, so risk of bias could not be assessed. Any claim
  drawn from these papers is a claim drawn from an abstract.
- Three of the preclinical records were read from a results listing only, so their species and
  methods are unconfirmed and their findings are not stated in this Guide.
- The entire retrieved human evidence base reduces to roughly two affiliated Russian research
  groups, one of which appears to include the compound's originators. No independent replication
  by an unaffiliated group in another country was found.

Limitations of this Guide's research process, stated so a reader can judge its reliability:

- Several regulatory pages could not be read: the FDA 503A bulk-substances landing page, the July
  2026 advisory committee meeting page, the 2026 meeting-materials page, and Drugs@FDA all
  returned HTTP 404; the EMA search endpoint returned HTTP 401; the WADA Prohibited List returned
  no usable content; and the Russian State Register of Medicines was not fetched. Each of these is
  declared as a failed retrieval and none is treated as a confirmed negative.
- The Federal Register full text **was** successfully retrieved, and every United States 503A
  statement in this Guide comes from it.
- The Russian registration claim rests on a tertiary reference plus a search-engine summary. No
  certificate number was obtained and no Russian regulator page was read.
- The absence of an EMA marketing authorisation is a weak negative resting on a tertiary source
  rather than on the regulator.
- The attribution of Semax's development to a named Russian institute could not be confirmed and
  the institute is therefore not named.
- Only ClinicalTrials.gov was searched among trial registries. The EU Clinical Trials Register,
  ISRCTN, the WHO ICTRP, and Russian national registries were not searched. Given that the human
  literature is Russian, a Russian registry search is the most likely of these to change the
  picture.
- As an adversarial check that this Guide's citations reflect real retrieval rather than
  reconstruction, a deliberately fabricated identifier was queried during research and correctly
  returned HTTP 404, while genuine identifiers returned full records.

## Quality and identity

This section is about the material itself, separately from the science.

**No independent testing of material sold as Semax was located.** No product-testing dataset for
Semax was retrieved during research. Nobody appears to have systematically checked what is in
vials or containers sold under this name. That is an absence of information, not a favourable
result.

**The consumer supply route sits outside any pharmacy or regulatory chain.** A tertiary source
states that Semax is widely sold by online vendors and used as a purported nootropic. Material
obtained that way has unverified identity, purity, sterility, and content, because no regulator
is inspecting a production line against a filed specification and no pharmacy stands between the
manufacturer and the buyer.

**The name does not fix the identity of the material.** United States regulatory filings treat
"Semax (free base)" and "Semax acetate" as separate bulk substances. A product labelled simply
"Semax" does not by itself tell you which one it contains.

**The information environment is dominated by sellers, and this is a quality issue in its own
right.** Most search results across every research query were peptide retailers, compounding
adjacent marketing blogs, and peptide-encyclopedia sites. Several assert specific regulatory and
safety positions that are not sourced to any regulator. Several publish dosing protocols. None is
scientific evidence, none carries a fact anywhere in this Guide, and no dosing information from
any of them has been reproduced anywhere in this folder.

A full documentation standard, with the honest current status of every item, is in
QUALITY_AND_DOCUMENTATION.md. Every item there is currently unmet.

## Safety categories

Nothing in this section is reassurance. These are categories of concern that remain open because
nobody has closed them.

**1. No adequate human safety dataset was retrieved. This is the primary safety finding.**
Across the four human studies retrieved, only PMID 15792140 makes any tolerability statement,
describing a minor percentage of side effects and good tolerability across age groups. That is an
uncontrolled tolerability impression from a single non-randomized 2005 study, read from an
English abstract. It is not a safety characterization. No adverse-event tables, no long-term
follow-up, no pharmacovigilance database, and no regulator safety review were retrieved.
**Absence of reported harm in a literature this small is not evidence of safety.**

**2. There is no human safety data at all in healthy non-patient users**, who are the population
most likely to be reading this. The only healthy-volunteer studies retrieved enrolled 24 and 52
people respectively, both were short neuroimaging studies, and neither was retrieved as reporting
a safety or adverse-event endpoint. Total retrieved healthy-volunteer exposure is on the order of
dozens of people, which cannot detect uncommon or delayed harms.

**3. Mechanistic breadth is an unresolved safety question, not a selling point.** Preclinical
titles retrieved describe effects, in animal and cell models whose species are not individually
confirmed, on BDNF, dopaminergic and serotoninergic systems, immune response genes, opioid
receptor gene expression, and copper binding. A compound reported to touch monoaminergic, opioid,
immune, and metal-homeostasis pathways in those models has a correspondingly wide surface for
unstudied interactions in humans, including with psychiatric and neurological medications. No
human drug-interaction study was retrieved.

**4. Effects on the body's stress-hormone system over time are unstudied.** Semax is described as
an analogue of a fragment of ACTH, a hormone central to the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis.
What repeated or prolonged exposure does to that axis in a person was not addressed by anything
retrieved.

**5. Human pharmacokinetics are uncharacterised.** Absorption, central nervous system exposure,
metabolism, and half-life are unknown. Nothing on this was retrieved. Whether meaningful exposure
is achieved at all, by any route, is not established in the retrieved record.

**6. Product identity and content risk.** Separately from the molecule, material obtained outside
a pharmacy or regulatory chain has unverified identity, purity, sterility, and content, and no
independent product-testing dataset for Semax was retrieved.

**7. Entirely unstudied populations.** Pregnancy, breastfeeding, adolescents, older adults, and
people with cardiovascular, psychiatric, endocrine, or seizure conditions. No data of any kind was
retrieved for any of these groups.

## Interactions

No human drug-interaction study for Semax was retrieved. No study of Semax alongside any
medication or supplement was found. Interactions are therefore **unstudied**, which is not the
same as absent.

The following categories would require review by a qualified professional before anyone considers
anything in this area, because they intersect with the pathways named in the retrieved preclinical
titles:

- **Psychiatric medication**, including antidepressants and other medicines acting on serotonin
  or dopamine systems, given the monoaminergic themes in the retrieved preclinical titles.
- **Neurological medication**, including anti-seizure medicines.
- **Opioid medication**, given the opioid receptor gene themes in the retrieved preclinical
  titles.
- **Anticoagulant and antiplatelet medication**, given the anticoagulant-adjacent themes noted in
  the retrieved literature and the cerebrovascular context in which Semax has been studied.
- **Any medicine for a cardiovascular or cerebrovascular condition**, including after a stroke or
  transient ischemic attack.
- **Immune-modulating medication**, given the immune-response-gene themes in the retrieved
  preclinical titles.
- **Corticosteroids or any medicine acting on the stress-hormone axis**, given the ACTH-fragment
  derivation.
- **Any other unapproved peptide or research compound taken at the same time**, which makes both
  benefit and harm impossible to attribute.
- **Pregnancy and breastfeeding**, where no data exists at all.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

## Regulatory status

All statements below were checked on **2026-07-21**. Regulatory status changes. Anything read
after that date should be re-verified. Several statuses below could not be confirmed against the
regulator's own pages and are marked accordingly.

**The decisive principle for this compound: approval, authorisation, or registration in one
jurisdiction is not approval in another.** A status recorded here for the Russian Federation says
nothing whatever about any other country.

## Unknowns

An explicit list of what is not known.

- Whether Semax has any clinically meaningful cognitive, mood, or nootropic effect in healthy
  people. Nothing retrieved addresses this. The healthy-volunteer studies measured brain
  connectivity, not cognition or symptoms.
- Whether the reported stroke and cerebrovascular benefits survive randomization, blinding, and
  placebo control. No such trial was found.
- The human safety profile at any level of detail: adverse-event frequency, serious adverse
  events, long-term effects, effects with repeated or prolonged use, and effects on the
  hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis over time.
- Drug and supplement interactions in humans, particularly with psychiatric, neurological, and
  anticoagulant medications, given the monoaminergic, opioid-gene, immune, and
  anticoagulant-adjacent themes in the retrieved preclinical titles.
- Human pharmacokinetics: absorption, central nervous system exposure, metabolism, and half-life.
  Nothing on this was retrieved.
- Safety and effect in pregnancy, breastfeeding, adolescents, older adults, and people with
  cardiovascular, psychiatric, endocrine, or seizure conditions. No data retrieved for any of
  these groups.
- The exact Russian registration particulars: certificate number, approved indications as written
  by the Russian regulator, date of first registration, and current status. Not retrieved from the
  state register.
- The current WADA classification.
- The outcome of the FDA Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee meeting of July 23 to 24, 2026,
  which had not occurred as of the date checked.
- The identity, purity, and content of material sold to consumers by online vendors. No
  independent product-testing data was retrieved.
- The species and methods behind three frequently cited preclinical records, which were read from

- Whether Semax was in fact developed at the Russian institute commonly named for it. No primary

## Questions for a qualified professional

If a member wants to discuss Semax with a clinician, these are questions that produce a useful
conversation. They are written to be asked as they stand.

1. I have read that there are no registered clinical trials of Semax and no randomized,
   placebo-controlled trials. Does that match what you can find, and how does it change how you
   would think about it?
2. The human studies I found are non-randomized, in Russian, and come from research groups
   connected to the people who developed the compound. How much weight do you put on evidence
   like that?
3. My interest came from claims about focus and memory. As far as I can tell no study has
   measured cognition in healthy people. Does that match what you find?
4. Two studies reported differences on brain scans in healthy volunteers. Is a difference on a
   scan something I would notice, and what would you want to see instead?
5. I take the following medications and have the following conditions. Which of them would make
   an unstudied compound acting on brain signalling a particularly poor idea for me?
6. This compound is described as derived from a fragment of ACTH. Is there anything about my
   hormone or stress-axis health that would make that a concern?
7. This is not approved where I live, which means any material comes from outside a regulated
   supply chain. How do you think about unverified identity, purity, and content for something
   taken into the body?
8. I compete in a tested sport. Who is the right authority for me to ask about whether this is
   permitted, given that I could not find a stated position?
9. If I decided to do nothing about this for now, what would you want me to watch for, and when
   should I come back?
10. If new human evidence on Semax appears, what would you want to see in it before it changed
    your advice?

This Guide does not give medical advice and does not tell anyone what to do. These questions
belong with a clinician who knows your history.
