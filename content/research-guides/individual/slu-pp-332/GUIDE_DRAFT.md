# SLU-PP-332

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## One-minute summary

SLU-PP-332 is an early-stage laboratory research chemical. It is discussed online as an "exercise
mimetic", meaning a substance proposed to produce some of the changes that exercise produces. The
single most important fact in this Guide is that **no living human being has ever been given this
compound in any published study**.

- **There is zero human evidence of any kind.** Not sparse evidence, not preliminary evidence. None.
  A direct query of ClinicalTrials.gov, the United States registry of clinical studies, returned zero
  registered studies for SLU-PP-332.
- Of the ten publications indexed in PubMed (the main biomedical literature database), **not one
  administered the compound to a person**. They are mouse studies, cell-culture studies, computer
  modelling, and laboratory analyses of liver preparations.
- One study did enrol 20 human participants, and it is the study most likely to be misrepresented as
  human evidence. SLU-PP-332 was applied only to cells grown from tissue removed during their surgery.
  No participant received the compound. It produces no human safety or effectiveness data.
- **It is not a peptide**, despite being sold almost entirely through peptide-branded channels. It is a
  synthetic small molecule. A buyer who believes they hold a peptide has the wrong mental model of what
  the substance is and of what test would even verify it.
- **The published research says the compound does not work when taken by mouth.** A 2026 mouse study
  states that SLU-PP-332 lacks oral bioavailability, and that this is precisely why a chemically
  different successor compound, SLU-PP-915, was developed. Oral tablets are nonetheless sold under the
  SLU-PP-332 name.
- **Safety cannot be characterised, because it has never been studied in humans.** There is no human
  dose-finding work, no human pharmacokinetics, no adverse event dataset, no organ toxicity assessment,
  and no carcinogenicity or reproductive toxicity work. Unknown toxicity is not the same as demonstrated
  safety.
- The rodent findings are real and come from credible journals. They are rodent findings about a gene
  activity program. The step from that to an effect in a human being has not been taken.
- For competitive athletes: two independent 2026 papers built laboratory methods to detect this
  compound for anti-doping purposes. Detection capability is established. The specific prohibited-list
  classification circulating on vendor websites could not be confirmed and is not repeated here as fact.

Xenios does not sell SLU-PP-332, does not supply it, and does not tell members how to obtain or use it.
This Guide exists so that a member who encounters SLU-PP-332 elsewhere has an accurate account of what
is and is not known.

**Overall claim grade for "SLU-PP-332 produces any effect in humans": G (Unverified).**

## What it is

SLU-PP-332 is a **synthetic small molecule**, meaning a laboratory-made chemical compound of modest
size, as distinct from a peptide (a short chain of amino acids). This distinction matters commercially
and is covered again under Quality and identity below.

- CAS registry number: 303760-60-3 [UNVERIFIED]
- IUPAC chemical name: 4-Hydroxy-N-[(Z)-naphthalen-2-ylmethylideneamino]benzamide [UNVERIFIED]
- Associated patent: US 8680150, assigned to Ligand Pharmaceuticals [UNVERIFIED]

The "SLU" prefix reflects the compound's origin at Saint Louis University. In the scientific literature
it is described as a **chemical probe**, meaning a tool compound used to study what a biological target
does, and as an **exercise mimetic**.

Its stated pharmacological class is a **pan-agonist of the estrogen-related receptors**. An agonist is a
substance that switches a receptor on. "Pan" means it activates all members of the family, in this case
ERRalpha, ERRbeta and ERRgamma. These are **orphan nuclear receptors**, meaning receptors inside cells
that control which genes are switched on, and for which no natural activating substance has been
definitively identified. The ERR family is involved in regulating mitochondrial production and the
cellular pathways that burn fuel.

Despite the name, ERRs are distinct from the classical estrogen receptors.

**Three compounds are routinely confused with it, and the confusion matters.**

1. **Peptides generally.** SLU-PP-332 is not one. Evidence, handling, and analytical verification for
   peptides do not transfer to it.
2. **SLU-PP-915.** A chemically distinct successor from the same research programme, developed because
   SLU-PP-332 is reported not to work when taken by mouth. Evidence generated for one is routinely
   presented in commercial copy as though it applies to the other. It does not.
3. **Other so-called exercise mimetics**, for example GW501516 and SR9009. These act on entirely
   different biological targets and carry their own separate evidence and separate regulatory histories.
   Nothing said about them applies here.

## Why interest exists

Describing why interest exists is not the same as endorsing it. The drivers are identifiable.

1. **The phrase "exercise mimetic" is unusually appealing.** A compound framed as delivering some of
   what exercise delivers speaks directly to a widely held wish. The phrase is a scientific shorthand
   for a research goal, not a description of a demonstrated human outcome.

2. **The rodent results read impressively when species is not attended to.** In mice, published studies
   have reported increased exercise endurance, shifts in muscle fibre composition, and changes in
   whole-body fuel use. Those are genuine published findings in mice.

3. **The journals are credible.** Findings have appeared in ACS Chemical Biology, the Journal of
   Pharmacology and Experimental Therapeutics, and Circulation. Publication in a strong journal
   establishes that the animal work was taken seriously. It does not convert animal work into human
   evidence.

4. **Novelty and scarcity.** A compound with almost no published human context reads to some audiences
   as early access rather than as an absence of information. The absence is the actual finding.

5. **Peptide-adjacent marketing channels.** Because it is sold through peptide-branded retailers, it
   inherits an audience and a vocabulary built around a different class of substance.

6. **Anti-doping attention has raised the profile.** The existence of detection-method papers is
   sometimes read as evidence that a compound must be effective. It is evidence that testing
   authorities regard it as worth detecting, nothing more.

None of the above constitutes evidence that SLU-PP-332 does anything in humans, or that it is safe.

## Terminology

Every technical term used in this Guide is glossed here, and again at first use in the body.

- **Small molecule.** A laboratory-made chemical compound of modest size. Most conventional medicines
  are small molecules.
- **Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids. SLU-PP-332 is not a peptide.
- **Agonist.** A substance that binds a receptor and switches it on.
- **Pan-agonist.** A substance that activates every member of a receptor family rather than one member
  selectively.
- **Receptor.** A protein that a substance binds to in order to produce an effect.
- **Nuclear receptor.** A receptor that works inside the cell and controls which genes are switched on
  or off.
- **Orphan receptor.** A receptor for which no natural activating substance has been definitively
  identified.
- **ERR (estrogen-related receptor).** A family of three orphan nuclear receptors, ERRalpha, ERRbeta
  and ERRgamma, distinct from the classical estrogen receptors despite the similar name.
- **Selectivity.** How strongly a substance prefers one receptor over another. Low selectivity means it
  acts broadly.
- **Mitochondria.** Structures inside cells that produce usable energy. **Mitochondrial biogenesis**
  means making more of them.
- **Oxidative phosphorylation.** The main cellular process for producing usable energy.
- **Fatty acid oxidation.** The cellular process of burning fat for fuel.
- **Transcriptional program.** The pattern of which genes are switched on in a cell at a given time.
- **Exercise mimetic.** A research term for a substance proposed to reproduce some biological changes
  normally caused by exercise. It is a goal, not an established outcome.
- **Preclinical.** Research done in animals or in cells, not in people.
- **In vitro.** In a dish or test tube, outside a living organism.
- **Ex vivo.** In tissue or cells removed from a living organism and studied outside it. Ex vivo human
  cell work is not human evidence, because no living person received the substance.
- **In vivo.** In a living organism.
- **Cell line.** Cells grown indefinitely in a laboratory. C2C12 is a mouse muscle cell line, HEK293 is
  a human kidney-derived cell line.
- **Myoblast and myotube.** Immature muscle cells, and the fused muscle-like structures they form in
  culture.
- **Knockout animal.** An animal bred to lack a specific gene, used to test whether an effect depends
  on that gene's protein.
- **Structure-activity relationship (SAR).** Chemistry work that changes a molecule step by step to see
  how its activity changes.
- **Oral bioavailability.** Whether a substance taken by mouth reaches the bloodstream in a meaningful
  amount. A compound without it does not produce a systemic effect by that route.
- **Pharmacokinetics.** What the body does with a substance over time, including absorption,
  distribution, breakdown, and elimination.
- **Metabolite.** A substance produced when the body chemically alters a compound. Metabolites can have
  their own activity, which may differ from the parent compound.
- **Liver microsomes and liver S9 fraction.** Preparations of liver components used in a test tube to
  approximate how the liver might break a compound down. They are a laboratory approximation, not a
  living body.
- **Kinetic solubility.** How readily a compound dissolves under test conditions. Poor solubility is a
  practical obstacle to developing a compound as a medicine.
- **Microsomal stability.** How quickly liver preparations break a compound down in a test tube.
- **Apoptosis assay.** A laboratory test measuring whether cells are dying. An Annexin V assay is one
  such test. It measures acute cell death in a dish and nothing more.
- **Pharmacodynamics.** What a substance does to the body, as opposed to what the body does to it.
- **LC-MS and HRMS.** Laboratory instruments used to identify and measure chemical substances precisely.
- **Pharmacopoeial monograph.** An official published quality standard defining what a substance must
  contain and how it must be tested.
- **Certificate of analysis (COA).** A supplier document reporting test results for a specific batch.
- **ClinicalTrials.gov.** The United States public registry of clinical studies. Zero entries means no
  registered human study exists.
- **WADA.** The World Anti-Doping Agency, which publishes the Prohibited List used in sport.

## Proposed mechanism

Everything in this section is **proposed**. Every step has been characterised only in cultured cells and
in rodents. **None of it has been demonstrated in a living human being.** The translation of a rodent
gene activity program into human physiology is an assumption, not a finding.

**The proposed sequence, as described in the preclinical literature:**

SLU-PP-332 binds and activates all three ERR receptor types, with a reported preference for ERRalpha
over ERRgamma. ERR activation is proposed to switch on a set of genes that overlaps with the set switched
on by a single session of aerobic exercise, including genes governing mitochondrial production, cellular
energy production, and fat burning.

**What was actually observed, with the model named in each case:**

- In the C2C12 mouse muscle cell line, the compound was reported to enhance mitochondrial respiration.
  Claim grade: **D (Preclinical)**.
  PMID 36988910, https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36988910/ (verified 2026-07-19)

- In mice, the same study reported an increase in type IIa oxidative muscle fibres and improved exercise
  endurance. The response was reported to be diminished in mice bred to lack the ERRalpha gene, which the
  authors read as evidence that the effect depends on ERRalpha in that species.
  Claim grade: **D (Preclinical)**.
  PMID 36988910, https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36988910/ (verified 2026-07-19)

- In cultured cells (a human kidney-derived cell line, mouse muscle cells, and rat heart cells), a 2026
  chemistry paper reported induction of Ddit4, a gene associated with exercise response.
  Claim grade: **D (Preclinical)**.
  PMID 41850449 / PMC13112601, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13112601/ (verified 2026-07-19)

**Three cautions belong in any honest reading of this mechanism.**

First, the entire mechanism rests on work in a human kidney-derived cell line, mouse muscle cells, rat
heart cells, and mice. A gene activity pattern in a mouse muscle is not a demonstrated physiological
outcome in a person.

Second, the compound activates the whole ERR family rather than one member, and its selectivity for
ERRalpha over ERRgamma is described in the chemistry literature as modest. Switching on a family of gene
regulators that operates across many organs is a broad intervention, not a targeted one.

Third, and most practically: the primary literature reports that SLU-PP-332 lacks oral bioavailability,
meaning it does not meaningfully reach the bloodstream when taken by mouth. A proposed mechanism cannot
operate if the compound does not reach the tissue. This point is developed under Contradictions below.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

## Human evidence

**There is none.**

This is not a hedge or a summary of weak evidence. No published study has administered SLU-PP-332 to a
living human being.

| Source checked | What was found |
|---|---|
| ClinicalTrials.gov, direct API query for "SLU-PP-332", 2026-07-21 | **Zero registered studies.** No trial, no posted results, no clinical development programme in the registry. Confirmed a true negative against adversarial and positive controls run the same day. |
| PubMed, all indexed publications for SLU-PP-332, exactly 10 records, 2026-07-21 | **Zero human administration studies.** Mouse studies, cell-culture studies, computational work, and laboratory liver-preparation analyses. |
| Systematic review literature, PMID 42024694, 2026-07-21 | A 2026 systematic review covering animal and cell models **concludes that clinical trials are needed to confirm efficacy and safety in humans**, independently corroborating the absence. |

Consequently there is:

- no established human dose,
- no human pharmacokinetic profile,
- no human tolerability data,
- no adverse event dataset,
- no exposure duration for which safety has been characterised,
- and no human effectiveness data for any purpose.

**The one study that is most likely to be misread as human evidence.**

A 2025 pilot study published in Frontiers in Physiology (PMID 40692696) enrolled 20 inactive women
undergoing hip surgery. Human participants were enrolled, and human tissue was used, which is why the
study is indexed in a way that invites a human-evidence reading.

**SLU-PP-332 was applied only to primary myoblast cultures established in a laboratory from muscle tissue
removed during those surgeries. It was not given to any living person.** The methods state that primary
cultures of myoblasts were set up from the muscle tissue of inactive women and treated with the ERR
agonist in culture. That was confirmed by a direct quote from the paper's own methods on 2026-07-21. The
study is an exploration of ERR targeting against muscle decline associated with inactivity, conducted in
cultured cells. It supplies no human safety data, no human tolerability data, and no human effectiveness
data, and it must never be presented as human research or as a clinical trial.

This is ex vivo human cell work. It is preclinical. If a member encounters a claim that SLU-PP-332 has
been "studied in humans", this study is almost certainly what is being referenced, and the claim is
wrong.

Claim grade for this study as human evidence: **D (Preclinical)**. It does not qualify as grade C.

## Preclinical evidence

Preclinical means animal or laboratory research. **Every finding below names its species or model in the
same sentence as the finding**, because moving a preclinical result into a human-sounding sentence is the
most common and most misleading error written about this compound. Nothing in this section tells a member
what SLU-PP-332 does in a person.

**1. Foundational characterisation. Mice, plus mouse muscle cell line and primary muscle cell cultures.**

In mice, SLU-PP-332 was reported to increase type IIa oxidative muscle fibres, improve exercise endurance
and grip strength, and induce a gene program associated with a single session of aerobic exercise. In the
C2C12 mouse muscle cell line, mitochondrial respiration was reported to increase. The effects were
reported to be diminished in mice bred to lack the ERRalpha gene. The compound was identified in this
study as a pan-ERR agonist with greatest potency at ERRalpha. Published in ACS Chemical Biology, 2023.
Claim grade: **D (Preclinical)**.
PMID 36988910, https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36988910/ (verified 2026-07-19)

**2. Metabolic effects. Mice, in diet-induced obese and genetically obese models.**

In mouse models of obesity and metabolic syndrome, administration of SLU-PP-332 was reported to
reproduce several exercise-associated whole-body metabolic effects in those mice, including increased
energy expenditure and increased fat burning, alongside decreased fat mass accumulation. Published in the
Journal of Pharmacology and Experimental Therapeutics, 2024.
Claim grade: **D (Preclinical)**.
Note on retrieval: this source was retrieved directly on 2026-07-21, upgrading the provisional status it
carried in the prior draft. The prior session's direct fetch had been blocked by a bot challenge.
PMID 37739806, https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37739806/ (verified 2026-07-21)

**3. Cardiac effects. Mice, in a pressure-overload heart failure model, plus rat heart cells.**

In mice subjected to transaortic constriction, a pressure-overload model of heart failure, SLU-PP-332
**and the chemically distinct SLU-PP-915** were reported to improve cardiac function through enhanced
cardiac fatty acid metabolism and mitochondrial function in those mice. The work also used neonatal rat
ventricular myocytes, which are heart muscle cells from newborn rats, in the laboratory. Published in
Circulation, 2024.
Claim grade: **D (Preclinical)**.
**The finding covers both compounds, not SLU-PP-332 alone.** That makes it especially prone to being
reported as an SLU-PP-332 result, which it is not.
Note on retrieval: the abstract was retrieved directly on 2026-07-21, upgrading the provisional status it
carried in the prior draft. Full text is still not retrieved. The abstract stated dose figures, which are
deliberately excluded from this Guide by policy and must not be reintroduced.
PMID 37961903, https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37961903/ (verified 2026-07-21)

**4. Chemistry and compound liabilities. Cultured cells only, plus computer modelling.**

A structure-activity relationship study, working in a human kidney-derived cell line, mouse muscle cells,
and rat heart cells, plus computational docking and molecular dynamics, documented significant practical
liabilities of the parent compound: extremely low kinetic solubility, only moderate stability in liver
preparations, and modest selectivity for ERRalpha over ERRgamma. Analogue compounds were reported to show
no toxicity in an Annexin V apoptosis screen in cultured cells, which **the authors themselves frame as
limited preliminary screening rather than a safety evaluation**. The paper explicitly states that
biological validation was restricted to laboratory systems that may not reflect what happens in a living
organism. Published in the International Journal of Biological Macromolecules, 2026.
Claim grade: **D (Preclinical)**.
PMID 41850449 / PMC13112601, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13112601/ (verified 2026-07-19)

**5. Human muscle cells in culture, from surgical tissue. Not human administration.**

Muscle cells cultured from tissue taken from 20 women undergoing hip surgery for osteoarthritis were
exposed to SLU-PP-332 in the laboratory. No participant received the compound. See the Human evidence
section above for why this study is frequently misrepresented. Published in Frontiers in Physiology, 2025.
Claim grade: **D (Preclinical)**.
PMID 40692696, https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40692696/ (verified 2026-07-19)

**6. Metabolite identification for anti-doping purposes. Human liver preparations in a test tube.**

Using human liver S9 fractions and laboratory instrumentation, researchers identified 22 distinct
metabolites of SLU-PP-332 in order to support development of detection methods. This work is purely
analytical. It reports no effectiveness and no safety endpoints. Published in Drug Testing and Analysis,
2026.
Claim grade: **D (Preclinical)**.
PMID 41688415, https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41688415/ (verified 2026-07-19)

**7. Companion metabolism characterisation. Human liver preparations in a test tube.**

A companion analysis of SLU-PP-332 and SLU-PP-915 in human liver microsomes and human liver S9 fraction,
again for doping-control purposes. The authors caution explicitly that this laboratory approach "might not
fully reflect the authentic human metabolic pattern and, thereby, plasma or urinary metabolic profiles",
and call for more complex studies including organ-on-a-chip models or clinical trials. This is a direct
acknowledgment from within the field that human metabolism of this compound is not established.
Published in Rapid Communications in Mass Spectrometry, 2026.
Claim grade: **D (Preclinical)**.
PMID 41588687 / PMC12835572, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12835572/ (verified 2026-07-19)

**8. Successor compound, and the oral bioavailability finding. Mice.**

A 2026 mouse study states that **SLU-PP-332 improves aerobic performance in mice but lacks oral
bioavailability**, and gives that as the reason the chemically distinct successor SLU-PP-915 was
developed. Published in the Journal of Pharmacology and Experimental Therapeutics, 2026.
Claim grade: **D (Preclinical)**, and directly relevant to the consumer context. See Contradictions below.
Note on retrieval: this source was retrieved directly on 2026-07-21, upgrading the provisional status it
carried in the prior draft.
PMID 41421047, https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41421047/ (verified 2026-07-21)

**9. Kidney findings in ageing. Aged mice, plus human tissue sections examined under a microscope.**

In aged mice (21 months old), a pan-ERR agonist was reported to reverse age-related albuminuria (protein
loss into the urine), podocyte loss (loss of a specialised kidney filtering cell), mitochondrial
dysfunction, and inflammatory markers in the kidney. Separately in the same work, human kidney tissue
sections were examined histologically, meaning looked at under a microscope. **No person received the
compound at any point.** Published in the American Journal of Pathology, 2023.
Claim grade: **D (Preclinical)**.
PMID 37717940, https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37717940/ (verified 2026-07-21)

**10. Systematic review of the animal and cell literature. No human studies to review.**

A 2026 systematic review in Revista Medica de Chile covering experimental studies in animals and cell
models concludes that clinical trials are needed to confirm efficacy and safety in humans. This is the
review literature independently confirming the central finding of this Guide: no human data exists.
Claim grade: **D (Preclinical)**, and cited **for its conclusion only**.
A caution for anyone reading it directly: its abstract phrases effects such as improved endurance and
reduced body fat without always attaching the species in the same clause. That is exactly the framing this
Guide prohibits, and it is a plausible upstream source of commercial copy that reads as human fact. Its
effect phrasing is deliberately not quoted or paraphrased anywhere here.
PMID 42024694, https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42024694/ (verified 2026-07-21)

## Contradictions and disagreement

**1. Whether human evidence exists.**

*Position A:* The 2025 Frontiers in Physiology pilot study enrolled 20 human participants and is indexed
in a way that invites a human-evidence reading. It can be cited, and has been marketed, as human research
on SLU-PP-332.

*Position B:* Direct retrieval of that paper confirms SLU-PP-332 was applied exclusively to primary
myoblast cultures established from surgical tissue. No living person received the compound.

*Assessment:* **RESOLVED in favour of Position B**, confirmed on 2026-07-21 by a direct quote from the
study's own methods. This is
ex vivo human cell work, not human administration, and it generates zero human safety or effectiveness
evidence. This is the single most important accuracy point in this Guide and the most likely place for a
reviewer, a marketer, or a member to go wrong.

**2. Oral use.**

*Position A:* Products are sold as oral SLU-PP-332 tablets and marketed with reference to the compound's
documented metabolic effects.

*Position B:* The 2026 successor-compound paper states that the parent compound lacks oral
bioavailability, which is the reason SLU-PP-915 was developed.

*Assessment:* **The primary literature contradicts the commercial premise.** The rodent findings for
SLU-PP-332 were generated by injection in those studies, not by mouth. Those findings therefore cannot be
transferred to an oral product even setting aside the species gap entirely. Either such a product does not
do what is claimed by that route, or it does not contain what the label says. Both are problems. This is
stated as a factual description of the published research and of the market, not as guidance about any
route of administration.
PMID 41421047, https://jpet.aspetjournals.org/article/S0022-3565(25)40300-0/abstract (verified 2026-07-19)

**3. World Anti-Doping Agency Prohibited List status.**

*Position A:* Vendor and affiliate pages state definitively that SLU-PP-332 is banned by WADA under class
S4, with some specifying subsection S4.5, metabolic modulators.

*Position B:* The peer-reviewed doping-control literature makes only a general statement: that
non-approved substances with performance-enhancing potential can be subject to the WADA Prohibited List.
That describes a general possibility. It does not assert current listing of this specific compound.

*Assessment:* **UNRESOLVED, and it stays unresolved here.** WADA's own Prohibited List landing page and
its 2026 Prohibited List news page both returned empty content on direct fetch, on two consecutive
research sessions, so the claim cannot be adjudicated against a primary source. **No specific
classification, by name, class, or subsection, is stated as fact in this Guide.** A search snippet
encountered during research described the general content of the S4 metabolic modulators class, but it
does not name SLU-PP-332 and did not come from a retrieved WADA page, so it establishes nothing here and
is not repeated. What is solidly established, and separate, is that validated detection methods now
exist. The correct position for a member is that competitive status must be confirmed directly with WADA
or with the athlete's own national anti-doping organisation.

**4. What the rodent literature supports.**

Marketing sources present the mouse endurance, metabolic, and cardiac findings as though they describe
what the compound does. Those findings describe what was observed in mice.

*Assessment:* This is not a genuine scientific controversy. The preclinical literature is real and was
published in credible journals. Its translation into human evidence has not happened. The preclinical
versus human distinction should be unmistakable on every claim a member encounters.

## Limitations

These are limitations of the evidence, and by extension of this Guide.

- **No human study of any kind exists.** Effectiveness in humans is not merely unestablished, it is
  entirely untested.
- **The evidence base is very small.** Exactly ten PubMed-indexed publications in total, several of which
  are analytical chemistry papers with no biological endpoints.
- **No systematic review or meta-analysis of human data exists to draw on, because the human literature
  does not exist.** The one systematic review that has been written (PMID 42024694) covers animal and
  cell models only, and concludes that clinical trials are needed.
- **The full text of the Circulation cardiac paper (PMID 37961903) was not retrieved.** Its abstract was,
  directly, on 2026-07-21. The other two previously provisional sources (PMID 37739806, PMID 41421047)
  were retrieved directly on 2026-07-21 and are no longer provisional.
- **The chemical identity values were not re-retrieved.** Molecular formula, molar mass, CAS registry
  number and IUPAC name were carried forward from a tertiary source and carry an unverified flag pending
  a check against a primary chemical registry.
- **WADA's Prohibited List document could not be retrieved, on two consecutive research sessions.** Both
  the landing page and the 2026 Prohibited List news page returned empty content each time. Anti-doping
  classification is therefore logged as unresolved rather than asserted in either direction. A retrieval
  failure is evidence of neither presence nor absence on the List.
- **Non-United States regulators were not searched.** The European Medicines Agency, the United Kingdom
  MHRA, Australia's TGA, and Health Canada were not checked for statements on this compound.
- **No independent analytical testing of marketed product was located.** Every statement in this Guide
  about product quality is inferred from the structure of an unregulated market, not measured from a
  tested sample.
- **The absence of an FDA document is not an FDA clearance.** Targeted searching of fda.gov located no
  approval, no warning letter, no safety communication, and no compounding list entry. That is a
  negative search result, and the most reasonable reading is that FDA has not addressed this compound
  at all.
- **Preclinical results frequently fail to reproduce in humans.** Here the usual translational
  uncertainty is compounded by a broad receptor target, modest selectivity, poor solubility, and an
  absence of oral bioavailability in the parent compound.

## Quality and identity

This section describes the unregulated-market reality. It is not a critique of any particular seller, and
it is not sourcing guidance. Xenios does not direct members to any supplier and does not sell this
compound. Detailed supplier requirements are set out in `QUALITY_AND_DOCUMENTATION.md` in this folder.

- **It is systematically mislabelled as a peptide.** Search results returned numerous vendor and affiliate
  pages titled as peptide products or peptide guides for SLU-PP-332. One search result summary explicitly
  flags that despite appearing on peptide-focused sites, SLU-PP-332 is not a peptide but a synthetic
  small-molecule chemical entity. Wikipedia records a small-molecule formula, C18H14N2O2, CAS 303760-60-3.
  A buyer who believes they are purchasing a peptide has an incorrect model of what the substance is, how
  it would be handled, and what analytical test would even verify it.

## Safety categories

This section lists categories of concern. **It is not a reassurance, and the absence of a category here
does not mean that category is safe.** Nothing in the evidence base supports describing SLU-PP-332 as
safe, as well tolerated, or as free of side effects. Those statements cannot honestly be made about a
compound that has never been given to a person.

**1. The complete absence of human safety data, which is itself the primary signal.**

There is no human safety data at all. Not sparse, not preliminary. Absent. Zero registered clinical
studies, zero human administration studies among the ten publications retrieved, and no established
human dose, no human pharmacokinetic profile, no tolerability data, no adverse event dataset, and no
exposure duration for which safety has been characterised.

**Unknown human toxicity is not the same as demonstrated human safety.** These are opposite epistemic
positions that can feel similar. Nothing has been ruled out, because nothing has been tested.
Source: ClinicalTrials.gov API query,
https://clinicaltrials.gov/api/v2/studies?query.term=SLU-PP-332&pageSize=20 (verified 2026-07-19)

**2. Human metabolism is explicitly not established.**

Metabolites have been identified only in liver preparations in a test tube. The authors of that work state
directly that the approach "might not fully reflect the authentic human metabolic pattern and, thereby,
plasma or urinary metabolic profiles", and call for clinical studies. What a living human body would
produce from this compound, and whether those products would have activity of their own, is unknown.
Downstream toxicology therefore cannot be predicted.
Source: PMC12835572, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12835572/ (verified 2026-07-19)

**3. The only toxicity screening cited is a narrow cell assay that its own authors decline to treat as a
safety evaluation.**

The 2026 chemistry paper reports no toxicity in an Annexin V apoptosis screen of analogue compounds in
cultured cells, and characterises its biological validation as restricted to laboratory systems. An
apoptosis screen in a cell line addresses acute cell death in a dish. It says nothing about organ
toxicity, endocrine effects, cancer risk, reproductive effects, or the consequences of repeated exposure
over time.
Source: PMC13112601, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13112601/ (verified 2026-07-19)

**4. Broad receptor target across many tissues, with modest selectivity.**

SLU-PP-332 activates all three ERR receptor types, and the chemistry literature describes its preference
for ERRalpha over ERRgamma as modest. ERRs regulate mitochondrial and metabolic gene programs across many
tissues, and the retrieved literature covers effects in heart and muscle in rodents and in kidney-derived
cells. Switching on a family of gene regulators across multiple organs, sustained over time, is a broad
intervention whose off-target consequences have not been characterised in humans.
[UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check] that broad nuclear receptor agonism is
a recognised general source of off-target risk.
Source: PMC13112601, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13112601/ (verified 2026-07-19)

**5. Unknown identity and contents of marketed material.**

Because no pharmacopoeial standard exists, no independent testing survey was located, and the compound is
routinely mislabelled as a peptide and conflated with a different compound, the actual contents of any
material sold under this name are unknown. A safety discussion about a compound is separate from a safety
discussion about an unverified product, and neither can be resolved here.

**6. Competitive and career risk for tested athletes.**

Two independent 2026 peer-reviewed papers developed detection methods and metabolite panels for
SLU-PP-332 explicitly for doping-control purposes. Regardless of unresolved questions about its exact
Prohibited List classification, **detection capability is established**. This is a real consequence, not
a hypothetical one.
Source: PMID 41688415, https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41688415/ (verified 2026-07-19)

## Interactions

**No human interaction data exists for SLU-PP-332, because no human has been given it in any published
study.** Human pharmacokinetics have not been characterised, so interaction risk cannot be estimated at
all. This is an absence of information, not an absence of risk.

Because interaction risk is unknown rather than absent, the following categories warrant review by a
qualified clinician who knows the person's full history and complete medication list. This is a list of
categories for professional review. It is not a statement that any specific interaction has been observed,
and it is not a checklist that makes anything acceptable.

- Any prescription medication of any kind.
- Medicines for diabetes or for blood sugar regulation, and any condition affecting blood sugar, given
  that the proposed mechanism concerns cellular fuel handling.
- Cardiovascular medication, and any heart condition, given that the retrieved rodent literature includes
  cardiac effects in mice.
- Medication affecting the kidneys, and any kidney impairment, given that ERR receptors are described in
  the retrieved literature as active in kidney tissue.
- Medication affecting the liver, and any liver impairment, given that metabolism has been characterised
  only in liver preparations in a test tube.
- Hormonal therapy of any kind, and any hormone-sensitive condition. Despite the receptor family name,
  ERRs are distinct from classical estrogen receptors, but downstream endocrine interactions in humans
  are uncharacterised.
  [UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]
- Cancer treatment, current or past, and any active or previously treated malignancy, given the complete
  absence of cancer-risk data.
- Pregnancy, planned pregnancy, and breastfeeding, given the complete absence of reproductive and
  developmental toxicity data.
- Any other unapproved compound, supplement, or research chemical, including any other so-called exercise
  mimetic. Combined exposure makes both benefits and harms unattributable.
- Competitive sport participation of any kind, where anti-doping rules apply and where detection methods
  for this compound now exist.

## Questions for a qualified professional

These are questions a member could reasonably take to a physician, pharmacist, or other qualified
clinician. They are framed to open a conversation, not to obtain a protocol. Xenios does not provide
medical advice.

1. Given that no human has ever been given this compound in a published study, is there any basis on
   which you would consider it reasonable for me?
2. What am I actually trying to achieve here, and is there an approved, evidence-supported option for
   that which we should discuss first?
3. This compound activates a family of gene regulators that operate in the heart, kidney, and muscle.
   Does anything in my history make that particular kind of broad, systemic intervention more concerning?
4. The published research reports that this compound does not work when taken by mouth. How should I
   interpret an oral product sold under its name?
5. It is a small molecule, not a peptide, though it is sold on peptide sites. Does that change how you
   would think about it, or how anyone would verify what a product contains?
6. I take the following medicines and have the following conditions. Given that no human interaction data
   exists at all, what categories of risk would concern you?
7. There is no cancer-risk, genetic-toxicity, or reproductive-toxicity data for this compound. How much
   weight would you put on those gaps?
8. What would you want to see published, and in what kind of study, before you considered this reasonable
   to discuss?
9. If I have already used a product sold under this name, is there anything you would want to monitor,
   test, or document in my record?
10. I compete in tested sport. Detection methods for this compound now exist. What are the consequences
    for me, and who should I ask about current prohibited-list status?
11. If I decided against this, what would you suggest instead, and how would we measure whether it is
    working?
