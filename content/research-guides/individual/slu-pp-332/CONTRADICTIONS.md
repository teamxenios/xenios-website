# SLU-PP-332 Contradictions and Divergences

Where the sources disagree, and what the Guide should say when they do. Source ids refer to
SOURCE_REGISTRY.md. Claim ids refer to CLAIM_TABLE.md.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

Two of the divergences below concern claims a member is likely to meet before they ever reach
the Guide. One of them, the oral bioavailability contradiction, is the single most useful thing
a member can take away from this compound's record.

## C-01. Whether human evidence exists

**Position A.** One retrieved study (PMID 40692696, a 2025 Frontiers in Physiology pilot study)
enrolled 20 human participants and is indexed in a way that invites a human-evidence reading. It
can be cited, and has been marketed, as human research on this compound.

**Position B.** The compound was applied only to primary myoblast cultures established from the
muscle tissue of those participants, obtained during hip surgery. The methods state that the
cultures were set up from muscle tissue and treated with the ERR agonist in culture. No living
person received the compound.

**Assessment.** **RESOLVED in favour of Position B**, confirmed on 2026-07-21 by a direct quote
from the study's own methods. This is ex vivo human cell work. It supplies no human safety
data, no human tolerability data, and no human efficacy data. This is the single most likely
point of downstream error in the entire record, because the study is the one thing in the
literature that looks like human research from a distance.

**What the Guide says.** Any citation of this study carries the ex vivo qualifier in the same
sentence, without exception. See HU-03 and the prohibition at PR-07.

## C-02. Oral bioavailability versus the oral products on the market

**Position A.** Oral products are sold under the SLU-PP-332 name.

**Position B.** The peer-reviewed literature states that SLU-PP-332 improves aerobic performance
in mice but lacks oral bioavailability, and gives that as the reason a chemically distinct
successor compound, SLU-PP-915, was developed (PMID 41421047, retrieved directly on 2026-07-21,
upgrading this source from the provisional status it carried in the prior packet).

**Assessment.** This is the sharpest practical contradiction in the record, and it is not
resolvable in favour of the market. Either a product sold under this name does not behave as
claimed by that route, or it is not what its label says. Both are quality problems. Naming the
route here is permitted, and necessary, because the point being made is that the evidence for it
is absent. It is a description of what the published research reports, not instruction.

**What the Guide says.** The tension is stated, and no conclusion is drawn about any named
seller. See PC-06, QU-03, and the prohibitions at PR-01 and PR-16.

## C-03. How the review literature frames the rodent findings

**Position A.** The primary literature attaches the species or model to every finding: mice,
cultured cells, rat ventricular myocytes, human liver preparations.

**Position B.** The 2026 systematic review in Revista Medica de Chile (PMID 42024694) phrases
effects such as improved endurance and reduced body fat without always attaching the model in
the same clause, even while concluding that clinical trials are needed to confirm efficacy and
safety in humans.

**Assessment.** These positions are not in substantive conflict. The review covers experimental
studies in animals and cell models only, and its own conclusion independently corroborates the
central finding that no human data exists. But its abstract phrasing is exactly the
species-stripped framing this Guide prohibits, and it is a plausible upstream source of
commercial copy that reads as human fact. A reader who meets that phrasing second-hand will
reasonably assume a human result.

**What the Guide says.** The review is cited for its conclusion, which is that clinical trials
are needed. Its effect phrasing is not quoted, reproduced, or paraphrased anywhere. See PR-04.

## C-04. Anti-doping status

**Position A.** Commercial and secondary sources circulate a specific WADA classification for
this compound.

**Position B.** WADA's own Prohibited List landing page and its 2026 Prohibited List news page
both returned empty content on direct fetch on 2026-07-21, exactly as in the prior session. No
WADA document naming SLU-PP-332 has ever been retrieved. The peer-reviewed doping-control paper
(PMID 41688415) states only that non-approved substances with performance-enhancing potential
can be subject to the WADA Prohibited List, which describes a general possibility rather than a
current listing of this compound.

**Assessment.** **UNRESOLVED, and it must stay unresolved in the Guide.** A retrieval failure
across two consecutive sessions is evidence of neither presence nor absence on the List. No
specific classification may be stated as fact, in any wording, including any class or subsection
identifier. A search snippet encountered during research described the general content of the S4
metabolic modulators class, but that snippet does not name SLU-PP-332 and did not come from a
retrieved WADA page, so it establishes nothing about this compound and is not repeated in the
Guide.

**What the Guide says.** The classification is unconfirmed. The athlete is directed to WADA or
to their own national anti-doping organisation. What is separately established, and is stated,
is that validated detection methods and metabolite panels now exist, so a tested athlete faces
real competitive exposure regardless of how the listing question resolves. See REG-03, SA-07,
and the prohibition at PR-09.

## C-05. Where the evidence covers two compounds and only one is named downstream

This is not a disagreement between sources. It is a structural feature of the record that
reliably produces error.

The Circulation heart failure finding (PMID 37961903) covers SLU-PP-332 **and** the chemically
distinct SLU-PP-915 together, in a mouse pressure-overload model. It is therefore especially
prone to being reported as an SLU-PP-332 result alone. The two compounds have different
pharmacokinetic properties, which is the entire reason the successor exists.

**What the Guide says.** Wherever the cardiac finding appears, it names both compounds and names
the mouse model in the same sentence. See PC-03 and the prohibition at PR-08.

## C-06. Where the identity of the substance is itself disputed by the market

**Position A.** The compound is sold widely through peptide-branded channels and is routinely
presented as a peptide.

**Position B.** It is a synthetic small molecule, not a peptide.

**Assessment.** Position B is correct and Position A is simply a market error, but it is a
consequential one. A buyer who believes they hold a peptide has a wrong model of what the
substance is, how it would behave, and what analytical test would even verify it. This belongs
early in any member-facing text rather than in a footnote.

**Caveat carried forward honestly.** The specific chemical identity values in the prior packet
(molecular formula, molar mass, CAS registry number, IUPAC name) came from a Wikipedia entry and
were **not re-retrieved on 2026-07-21**. The small-molecule-not-peptide correction is not in
doubt, but those individual values are marked
[UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check] and must be confirmed against a
primary chemical registry by a reviewer.

**What the Guide says.** The identity correction leads. The specific registry values carry the
unverified flag until a reviewer confirms them. See ID-01 and the prohibition at PR-06.

## C-07. Jurisdictional differences

No genuine conflict between jurisdictions was found, because only one jurisdiction produced any
retrievable statement, and that statement is a negative search finding rather than a regulator's
position.

- **United States, FDA.** No approval, and no warning letter or safety communication naming the
  compound, was located by targeted site-restricted search on 2026-07-21. This is the absence of
  a retrieved document, not an FDA statement about the compound.
- **United States, ClinicalTrials.gov.** A direct registry query returned zero studies on
  2026-07-21. This is a positive retrieved result and the strongest single item in the
  regulatory record.
- **EMA, MHRA, TGA, Health Canada.** Not searched. No statement about any non-US jurisdiction
  may be made from this record.

The absence of conflict here reflects the absence of retrievable information from other
jurisdictions, not agreement among them. See REGULATORY_STATUS.md.

## C-08. A note on the integrity of the retrieval itself

Retrieval integrity was checked adversarially on 2026-07-21, which is worth recording because
the central finding of this packet is a negative one and a negative finding is only as good as
the query that produced it.

- A fabricated compound identifier submitted to the ClinicalTrials.gov API returned zero
  studies.
- A fabricated PMID returned HTTP 404, confirming that PubMed retrieval does not invent records.
- A positive control query for semaglutide returned real registry records, confirming the query
  path works.

Together these establish that the zero result for SLU-PP-332 is a true negative rather than a
broken retrieval path. That distinction is the difference between "we found nothing" and "there
is nothing", and this packet is entitled to claim the second.

The one thing this control set does not rescue is the WADA gap, which is a genuine retrieval
failure and remains open. It is recorded in SOURCE_PLAN.md and REGULATORY_STATUS.md and must be
closed by a human reviewer opening the current List document directly.
