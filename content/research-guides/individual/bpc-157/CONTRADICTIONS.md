# BPC-157 Contradictions and Divergences

Where sources disagree, where preclinical and human findings diverge, where mechanism
outruns evidence, where jurisdictions differ, and where marketing conflicts with the
literature. Source ids refer to SOURCE_REGISTRY.md.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

## Summary

There is very little genuine scientific controversy about BPC-157, because genuine
controversy requires competing bodies of evidence and there is only one body of evidence
here. What exists instead are four distinct kinds of divergence: a documentation
discrepancy about US regulatory history, a split between a scientific review and a
political signal, a very large gap between preclinical volume and human evidence, and a
gap between marketing claims and the literature. Each is treated separately below, because
collapsing them into "the science is contested" would misdescribe all four.

## 1. Documentation discrepancy: what the current US compounding status actually is

**Position A.** Numerous secondary sources, including law firm bulletins, industry
commentary and seller pages, state that FDA placed BPC-157 in Category 2 of its compounding
interim policies in September 2023, and that this is its current status.

**Position B.** FDA's live page (S02, content current as of 04/22/2026) does not list
BPC-157 in the Category 2 table. It lists it in a separate table headed as bulk drug
substances nominated but withdrawn, described as substances previously in category 2 of the
interim policies that were withdrawn by the nominators. FDA's July 2026 briefing document
(S01) confirms that both nominations were withdrawn and that FDA is now evaluating the
substances on its own initiative.

**Assessment.** The practical consequence is identical under either description. BPC-157 is
not on the 503A bulks list, has no applicable USP or NF monograph, and is not a component
of an FDA-approved drug, so compounding with it does not qualify for the 503A exemptions.
FDA has stated exactly this in two enforcement letters, dated 04/01/2020 (S04) and
09/14/2023 (S05). The discrepancy is about the label and the date, not about the effect.

**Editorial rule.** Do not repeat "Category 2 since September 2023" as current fact. That
date could not be verified from any FDA source in this session, and FDA's own page now
presents the matter differently. State the effect (not on the bulks list, compounding not
eligible for the exemptions), cite S02, S04 and S05, and carry the date checked. A human
reviewer should resolve the history against the Federal Register and the FDA docket. See
SOURCE_REGISTRY.md, UV02.

## 2. Direction of travel: a scientific review against a political signal

**Position A.** FDA staff, in a review memo dated 5/11/2026 placed before the Pharmacy
Compounding Advisory Committee meeting of July 23-24, 2026, recommended against adding
BPC-157 free base and BPC-157 acetate to the 503A bulks list, citing physicochemical
characterisation, information on historical use, lack of evidence of effectiveness, and
safety information (S01, S03).

**Position B.** Science journalism (S13, dated February 3, 2026) reports that HHS leadership
has signalled openness to loosening restrictions on unapproved peptides, and that FDA
leadership responses in December 2025 were open but non-committal, describing an intention
to review peptides to determine which are harmless.

**Assessment.** These are not two readings of the same evidence. One is a scientific review
recommendation. The other is a political signal about policy direction. They can both be
true at once and neither refutes the other.

Two things must be stated explicitly. First, the retrieved article provides no evidence that
any formal reclassification occurred. A search engine summary encountered during research
asserted that a reversal was announced in February 2026. That assertion is not supported by
the article actually retrieved and is treated as false or unverified (SOURCE_REGISTRY.md,
UV05). Second, the advisory committee met on July 23-24, 2026, after the research date of
2026-07-19, so the outcome is unknown to this Guide.

**Editorial rule.** Regulatory status in this Guide is stated as of 2026-07-19 with the date
visible to the reader, and as a proposal pending advisory committee input. This must be
re-verified before publication. Claim P-13 in CLAIM_TABLE.md prohibits any statement that
the classification has been or is about to be reversed.

## 3. Preclinical volume against human evidence

This is the largest divergence in the record, and it is not a contradiction between sources.
It is a gap between two different kinds of evidence.

**What the preclinical literature reports.** A substantial rodent and in vitro literature
reports cytoprotective and wound-healing effects across many tissues. Specific retrieved
examples: in rat tendon explants and cultured rat tendon fibroblasts, accelerated fibroblast
outgrowth, increased survival under oxidative stress, and increased migration, with
increased phosphorylation of FAK and paxillin (S10). FDA summarises rodent studies reporting
prevention of lesions in gastrointestinal and cardiovascular challenge models,
nephroprotective properties, healing of transected tendons, and neuroprotective properties
in rodent models (S01). A 2025 narrative review reports regenerative effects across numerous
animal models involving VEGFR2, the Akt-eNOS axis, and ERK1/2 signalling in animal and cell
systems (S09).

**What the human literature contains.** FDA identified five human studies in total (S01).
Three are conference abstracts never published in full. One is a retrospective chart review
of 17 people at a single private clinic, with a second peptide co-administered in some
subjects. One is a two-person case series. The only PubMed-indexed human study is an
uncontrolled, unblinded pilot in 12 women with interstitial cystitis whose primary outcome
was a single self-rated questionnaire (S08). The only randomized controlled trial identified
did not separate from placebo (S01, S01-a). A PubMed search restricted to systematic
reviews, randomized controlled trials and clinical trials returns zero records (S14).

**Assessment.** This is not a scientific controversy. Both sides of it agree. FDA and an
independent 2025 peer-reviewed review (S09) reach the same conclusion from the same
material, with the review stating that BPC-157 should be considered investigational. The
preclinical literature is real. Its translation into human clinical evidence has not
happened.

**Editorial rule.** Every preclinical statement on a member-facing surface carries its
species or model in the same sentence as the finding. See CLAIM_TABLE.md section 3 and
prohibited claim P-05.

## 4. Mechanism plausibility outrunning evidence

There is a specific and unusual tension inside the preclinical literature itself, and it is
worth surfacing to a reader rather than smoothing over.

Proposed pathways are named with confidence in the primary literature and in review
(VEGFR2, nitric oxide synthesis via Akt-eNOS, ERK1/2, FAK-paxillin), yet FDA states that the
molecular targets of BPC-157-related substances have not been identified and that the
mechanisms of action remain poorly understood (S01). FDA also notes, repeatedly across the
rodent literature, that dose-response relationships could not be established because widely
separated doses produced comparable effects.

**Assessment.** A named downstream signalling pathway is not the same as an identified
molecular target. A flat response across widely separated doses is an anomaly. In a
well-characterised pharmacology, effect tracks exposure. The absence of that relationship
across a literature is a reason for caution about the literature, not a demonstration of a
wide safety margin, and it must never be presented as the latter.

**Editorial rule.** Where mechanism appears, FDA's characterisation (claim PC-03) appears
alongside it. Publishing the pathway names without that caveat is a misrepresentation.

## 5. Jurisdictional differences

There is no jurisdiction in which BPC-157 is approved, so there is no genuine international
disagreement to report.

- FDA states that no approved product containing BPC-157-related substances exists in any
  country, and that neither form appears in the European, Japanese, or International
  Pharmacopeias (S01).
- FDA reports that New Zealand's Medsafe stated there are no approved products or pending
  applications for medicines containing BPC-157 in New Zealand (S01, S01-e).
- USADA states that BPC-157 is prohibited under the unapproved substances category of the
  Prohibited List (S12).
- The US Department of Defense lists it as a prohibited dietary supplement ingredient and
  states it is not a lawful dietary ingredient (S11).

**Assessment.** The jurisdictions align. Where they differ is only in how far each has gone
in formal evaluation, not in whether the substance is approved. The general principle still
applies and should be stated to the reader: approval or registration in one jurisdiction is
not approval in another, and absence of a formal statement from a jurisdiction is not
permission. See REGULATORY_STATUS.md.

## 6. Marketing claims against the literature

**What is claimed commercially.** BPC-157 is marketed as a broadly effective healing
peptide, commonly with reference to a large volume of published studies.

**What the retrieved literature supports.** The study volume is real and it is almost
entirely rodent and in vitro. The human evidence is described in section 3 above. FDA
identified five human studies. No efficacy claim in CLAIM_TABLE.md grades above C, and that
single row is an uncontrolled pilot in 12 people with a self-rated outcome.

**A second and independent problem.** Even if a benefit claim were supported, it could not
reliably be attached to a purchased product. BPC-157 is a common name, not a standardised
one, and FDA has encountered multiple salts, derivatives and different active moieties sold
under it (S01). There is no pharmacopoeial monograph to test against (S01). Market
certificates of analysis are purity-only and do not demonstrate control of the impurity
profile (S01). Products are frequently co-formulated with other unapproved peptides (S01,
S11), which appears in two of the three adverse event reports FDA retrieved and in one of
the small human studies.

**Assessment.** This is not a contradiction between two evidence bases. It is a gap between
commercial claims and the evidence. Vendor and retailer material is disqualified as evidence
for this Guide (SOURCE_PLAN.md section 5) and was not used to support any claim.

**Editorial rule.** Prohibited claims P-01, P-02, P-03, P-07, P-11 and P-14 in
CLAIM_TABLE.md exist specifically to close this gap.

## 7. Where there is no contradiction, and why that matters

On the central question, whether BPC-157 has been shown to work in humans, the sources do
not disagree. FDA, the US Department of Defense, and an independent peer-reviewed narrative
review all describe the human evidence as absent or extremely limited, and the review
concludes the compound should be considered investigational. A direct database query returns
zero indexed trials or systematic reviews.

A Guide that presents this as a live debate would be inventing a controversy that the
retrieved record does not contain. The honest presentation is that the human evidence base
is close to empty, that the preclinical literature is real but has not translated, and that
one adequately designed trial is now running and has produced nothing yet.
