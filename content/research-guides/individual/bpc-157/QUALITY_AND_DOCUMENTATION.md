# BPC-157: quality and documentation

## Purpose and current position

This document sets out the quality documentation that would need to exist before any product containing
BPC-157 could be discussed as a characterised material, and records the honest current state.

**Current state, stated plainly: Xenios has NOT CONFIRMED any of the items below for this compound.
Every item on the checklist is currently UNMET. No supplier has been assessed. No batch has been
reviewed. No certificate of analysis has been verified. Xenios does not sell BPC-157 and has no supplier
relationship for it.**

Two structural facts make this harder than it would be for most ingredients, and they sit above every
individual checklist item.

1. **There is no official standard to test against.** FDA states there is no United States Pharmacopeia
   (USP) or National Formulary (NF) drug substance monograph for BPC-157 (free base) or BPC-157 acetate,
   no USP dietary supplement monograph, and that neither form appears in the European, Japanese, or
   International Pharmacopeias. A monograph is the published specification that defines what a substance
   must contain and how it must be tested. Without one, there is no official identity, purity or quality
   specification that a supplier can be held to, and any specification a supplier offers is its own.

2. **The name does not identify the substance.** FDA states BPC-157 is a common name and not a United
   States Adopted Name, that FDA treats the free base and the acetate as different active pharmaceutical
   ingredients, and that FDA has encountered multiple salts and derivatives including different active
   moieties (different active chemical entities) sold commercially under the same common name. FDA states
   that inconsistent naming conventions represent a safety risk for patients because they may be dosed
   with a different bulk drug substance than intended.

Source for both: FDA Briefing Document, Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee Meeting, July 23-24 2026.
https://www.fda.gov/media/193343/download (verified 2026-07-19)

Nothing in this document is a route to obtaining BPC-157, and nothing here should be read as Xenios
preparing to supply it. Meeting every item below would establish that a material is characterised. It
would not establish that the compound works or is safe in humans, which remains unknown.

## Status key

| Status | Meaning |
|---|---|
| UNMET | Not satisfied, not documented, or not verified by Xenios |
| PARTIAL | Some documentation exists but is incomplete or unverified |
| MET | Documented and independently verified by Xenios |

Every row in this document is currently **UNMET**.

## 1. Identity

What a supplier would have to establish: that the material is the specific substance intended, named
unambiguously, and not a different salt, derivative, or active moiety.

| # | Requirement | Status | Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1.1 | Unambiguous chemical identification, including which specific form (free base or acetate) and full chemical name, not the common name alone | UNMET | FDA treats free base and acetate as different active ingredients and notes both nomination packages it reviewed were internally inconsistent about which was meant |
| 1.2 | CAS number, molecular formula and molecular weight stated and matched to the material supplied | UNMET | FDA states the free base formula is C62H98N16O22 with molecular weight 1419.5 g/mol; Xenios has verified no supplier material against this |
| 1.3 | Confirmatory identity testing on the actual batch, including mass spectrometry and amino acid sequence confirmation | UNMET | No batch data reviewed |
| 1.4 | Counterion identity and content quantified where a salt form is supplied | UNMET | No data reviewed |
| 1.5 | Declaration that the product contains only the named substance, with any co-formulated peptide disclosed | UNMET | FDA records that products are frequently combined with other unapproved peptides, notably TB-500 (thymosin beta-4) |
| 1.6 | A named applicable monograph or, in its absence, a written in-house specification with a documented scientific basis | UNMET | No USP, NF, European, Japanese or International Pharmacopeia monograph exists |

## 2. Purity and impurity profile

What a supplier would have to establish: not only how much of the intended substance is present, but what
else is present and within what limits.

| # | Requirement | Status | Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2.1 | Assay and purity by a validated, stability-indicating method, with the method disclosed | UNMET | No batch data reviewed |
| 2.2 | A defined impurity profile with each impurity identified and specified, not a purity figure alone | UNMET | FDA states most certificates of analysis for BPC-157 (free base) contain only purity testing results with no impurity limits or results demonstrating control of the impurity profile, and found the same pattern in certificates published by commercial peptide sellers |
| 2.3 | Peptide-related impurities characterised, including deletion sequences, insertion sequences, and isomeric impurities from solid-phase peptide synthesis | UNMET | FDA identifies these as expected impurities of the manufacturing route |
| 2.4 | Residual solvents, coupling reagents, activators, catalysts and scavengers quantified against limits | UNMET | FDA identifies these as introduced by solid-phase peptide synthesis |
| 2.5 | Nitrosamine risk assessment and testing | UNMET | FDA names nitrosamines among likely impurities of concern and states available data do not address them for BPC-157 |
| 2.6 | Potentially mutagenic impurity assessment (substances able to damage DNA) | UNMET | FDA names this category and states available data do not address it |
| 2.7 | Potentially teratogenic impurity assessment (substances able to harm a developing foetus) | UNMET | FDA names this category and states available data do not address it |
| 2.8 | Elemental impurity and heavy metal testing against defined limits | UNMET | No data reviewed |
| 2.9 | Aggregate content quantified, given that aggregation drives the immunogenicity concern | UNMET | FDA states no aggregate data were available for the proposed product forms |

## 4. Stability and storage

What a supplier would have to establish: that the material remains what it claims to be across its stated
shelf life under stated conditions.

| # | Requirement | Status | Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| 4.1 | Real-time stability data supporting the stated shelf life, using stability-indicating methods | UNMET | No data reviewed |
| 4.2 | Accelerated and stress stability data characterising degradation pathways | UNMET | FDA states peptides such as BPC-157 can be extremely sensitive to formulation, process and environmental conditions including pH, heat, concentration, in-process related impurities and excipients, which may lead to aggregation and degradation |
| 4.3 | Aggregation monitored specifically across storage and under stress conditions | UNMET | FDA states significant amounts of aggregates can form especially during storage or under stress conditions, and that aggregates drive the immunogenicity concern |
| 4.4 | Defined storage and transport conditions, with excursion limits and evidence the cold chain, if required, was maintained | UNMET | No data reviewed |
| 4.5 | Container closure system defined, with compatibility and integrity data | UNMET | FDA states no container-closure data were available for the proposed product forms |

## 6. Certificate of analysis and third-party verification

What a supplier would have to provide, and what independent verification would have to confirm.

| # | Requirement | Status | Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| 6.1 | A batch-specific certificate of analysis matching the exact lot supplied, not a representative or example certificate | UNMET | No certificate reviewed |
| 6.2 | The certificate lists every test performed, the method used, the specification, and the result, including impurity attributes | UNMET | FDA found that market certificates for BPC-157 generally contain purity testing only, with no impurity attribute control |
| 6.3 | The certificate is signed and dated by a named, qualified person at a named laboratory | UNMET | No certificate reviewed |
| 6.4 | Independent third-party laboratory confirmation of identity and purity on the same lot, commissioned by the buyer and not by the seller | UNMET | No third-party testing commissioned |
| 6.5 | Third-party laboratory accreditation documented, for example to a recognised testing standard | UNMET | No laboratory engaged |
| 6.6 | Confirmation that no undisclosed additional active substance is present, including screening for co-formulated peptides | UNMET | FDA adverse event reports involving BPC-157 include products containing TB500, and the published knee-pain chart review included subjects who received thymosin beta-4 |
| 6.7 | Contamination screening consistent with FDA's stated concern about compounded products containing BPC-157 | UNMET | The Department of Defense states FDA has cautioned against compounded drugs containing BPC-157 due to safety risks and potential contamination with other substances |
| 6.8 | Labelling reviewed for accuracy and for compliance in the jurisdiction of sale | UNMET | The Department of Defense notes products are often labelled with wording indicating they are not for human consumption while being marketed into health and wellness use. ## 8. What passing this checklist would and would not mean

**Would mean:** the material in a given batch is identified, characterised, traceable, and independently
verified to be what it says it is.

**Would not mean:** that BPC-157 works for anything in humans, that it is safe in humans, that its
mechanism is understood, that a dose-response relationship exists, that it is immunogenic or not
immunogenic in humans, or that it is lawful to sell. Those are separate questions, and on every one of
them the current answer is that it is not established. See `GUIDE_DRAFT.md` in this folder.

Quality documentation answers "is this the substance". It does not answer "should anyone take it".
