# BPC-157

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## One-minute summary

BPC-157 is a synthetic peptide (a short chain of amino acids made in a laboratory) that is
widely discussed online as a healing or recovery compound. The honest position, and the most
important thing in this Guide, is that almost nothing is known about what it does in people.

- No product containing BPC-157 is approved as a medicine in any country, according to the
  United States Food and Drug Administration (FDA) as of the date checked below.
- When FDA searched the published literature, it identified a total of five human studies of
  BPC-157. Three of those exist only as conference abstracts that were never published in full.
  One is a review of medical charts from 17 people at a single private clinic. One is a report
  on two people.
- The only randomised controlled trial FDA could find, in ulcerative colitis, exists only as a
  2005 meeting abstract, and its result did not separate from placebo. FDA described that data
  as inadequate to support the efficacy and safety of BPC-157.
- A search of PubMed (the main biomedical literature database) restricted to systematic reviews,
  randomised controlled trials and clinical trials returned zero records for BPC-157.
- There is a large body of animal and laboratory-dish research. That research is real, but it is
  preclinical, meaning it was done in rodents or in cultured cells, not in people. FDA's own
  assessment is that the molecular targets are unidentified, the mechanisms are poorly understood,
  and dose-response relationships could not be established.
- On safety, the correct statement is not that it is safe. It is that no one knows. Total
  documented human exposure across all identified studies is on the order of dozens of people
  for periods of days to about two weeks, with unclear safety monitoring.
- FDA's specific named concern is immunogenicity, meaning the possibility that the body mounts
  an immune response against the peptide, potentially made worse by peptide clumping (aggregation)
  and by uncontrolled impurities.
- The name itself does not identify the substance. FDA has encountered multiple salts, derivatives
  and even different active chemical entities sold commercially under the single name "BPC-157".
- It is prohibited in sport under the World Anti-Doping Agency's non-approved substances class,
  and it appears on the United States Department of Defense prohibited dietary supplement
  ingredients list.

Xenios does not sell BPC-157, does not supply it, and does not tell members how to obtain or use
it. This Guide exists so that a member reading about BPC-157 elsewhere has an accurate account of
what is and is not known.

**Overall claim grade for "BPC-157 is effective for any human condition": G (Unverified).**

## What it is

BPC-157 stands for "Body Protection Compound 157". It is a synthetic peptide reported to be made
of 15 amino acids, which is why it is sometimes called a pentadecapeptide (penta-deca means
fifteen). It is described as a partial sequence taken from a larger protein originally isolated
from human gastric juice (the fluid produced in the stomach).

FDA records BPC-157 as also known as Bepecin and PL-14736, an earlier development code. FDA notes
that BPC-157 is not a United States Adopted Name, meaning it is a common name rather than a
standardised official drug name.

FDA states the molecular formula of the free base form is C62H98N16O22, with a molecular weight of
1419.5 g/mol.

One identity point matters more than any other and is easy to miss. FDA treats "BPC-157 (free base)"
and "BPC-157 acetate" as two different active pharmaceutical ingredients. FDA also notes that the
common name "BPC-157" is used commercially for multiple salts, derivatives, and even different
active moieties (different active chemical entities). In practice, two products labelled BPC-157
are not guaranteed to contain the same substance.

There is no United States Pharmacopeia (USP) or National Formulary (NF) drug substance monograph
for either form, and no USP dietary supplement monograph. FDA further states that neither form is
found in the European, Japanese, or International Pharmacopeias.

BPC-157 is also frequently sold alongside, or combined in the same product with, other unapproved
peptides, most commonly TB-500 (thymosin beta-4). That combination makes it very difficult to
attribute either a reported benefit or a reported harm to BPC-157 itself.

Source: FDA Briefing Document, Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee Meeting, July 23-24 2026.
https://www.fda.gov/media/193343/download (verified 2026-07-19)

## Why interest exists

Describing why people are interested is not the same as endorsing the interest. The interest has
several identifiable drivers.

1. **A large and striking animal literature.** Rodent studies have reported protective and
   wound-healing effects across many different tissues, including the gut, tendon, and nervous
   system in rodent models. The volume of that literature is genuinely large, and it reads as
   impressive when the species is not attended to.

2. **A very broad claimed range.** Because the reported effects in rodent models span many organ
   systems, the compound is discussed as a general repair agent rather than a treatment for one
   condition. Broad claims travel well online.

3. **An origin story that sounds reassuring.** The description of BPC-157 as a fragment of a protein
   found in human gastric juice is often read as implying it is natural and therefore benign. That
   inference does not follow. A synthetic fragment of a human protein is still an unapproved drug
   substance with an uncharacterised safety profile.

4. **Recovery and injury are high-motivation problems.** People dealing with persistent tendon,
   joint, or gut symptoms are strongly motivated, and approved options can be slow or partly
   effective. That creates demand that outruns evidence.

5. **Ready availability.** The compound is sold widely online, frequently with labelling such as
   "not for human consumption" or wording referring to laboratory use, while in practice being
   marketed toward people. Availability is not evidence of anything, and Xenios does not treat it
   as such.

6. **Regulatory attention has raised the profile.** Coverage of FDA's compounding decisions and of
   political statements about peptides has increased visibility, which tends to be read as
   significance.

None of the above constitutes evidence that BPC-157 works or is safe in humans.

## Terminology

Terms are glossed here and again at first use in the body of the Guide.

- **Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids. Proteins are long chains, peptides are short ones.
- **Pentadecapeptide.** A peptide made of 15 amino acids.
- **Synthetic.** Manufactured in a laboratory rather than extracted from a living source.
- **Preclinical.** Research done in animals or in cells in a dish, not in people.
- **In vitro.** In a dish or test tube, outside a living organism.
- **Ex vivo.** In tissue removed from an organism and studied outside it.
- **In vivo.** In a living organism.
- **Free base and acetate.** Two different chemical forms (salt forms) of the same core molecule.
  FDA treats them as different active ingredients.
- **Active moiety.** The part of a molecule responsible for its activity. Different active
  moieties mean genuinely different substances.
- **Monograph (USP or NF).** An official published quality standard that says what a substance must
  contain and how it must be tested. BPC-157 has none.
- **Compounding.** Preparing a customised medicine at a pharmacy for an individual patient.
  Section 503A of the United States Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act sets conditions under
  which this is exempt from certain drug requirements.
- **503A bulks list.** The FDA list of bulk drug substances that may be used in this kind of
  compounding. BPC-157 is not on it.
- **Randomised controlled trial (RCT).** A study where participants are assigned by chance to the
  treatment or to a comparison group, which is the main way to separate a real effect from
  expectation and natural improvement.
- **Placebo-controlled.** Includes a group receiving an inactive comparison.
- **Blinded.** Participants (and ideally investigators) do not know who received what.
- **Single-arm and uncontrolled.** Everyone receives the substance and there is no comparison
  group, so improvement cannot be attributed to the substance.
- **Retrospective chart review.** Looking backward at existing medical records rather than running
  a planned study.
- **Confidence interval (CI).** A range of values consistent with the data. If a confidence
  interval for a difference between two groups includes zero, the study did not demonstrate a
  difference.
- **Pharmacokinetics (PK).** What the body does with a substance over time, including whether it
  can even be detected in blood.
- **Immunogenicity.** The tendency of a substance to trigger an immune response against itself.
- **Aggregation.** Peptide molecules clumping together, which can increase immunogenicity risk.
- **Impurity profile.** The identity and quantity of everything in a product other than the
  intended substance.
- **Certificate of analysis (COA).** A supplier document reporting test results for a batch.
- **Endotoxin.** A bacterial cell wall component that can cause fever and serious reactions if
  present in an injectable product.
- **FAERS.** The FDA Adverse Event Reporting System, a voluntary database of reported adverse
  events. Reports in it do not establish that a substance caused an event.
- **ClinicalTrials.gov.** A public registry of clinical studies. A registration is a plan, not a
  result, and entries are supplied by the sponsor.

## Proposed mechanism

Everything in this section is **proposed** and derives from animal or cell research. None of it has
been demonstrated to operate in humans. FDA's summary position is that the molecular targets for
BPC-157-related substances have not been identified and the mechanisms of action remain poorly
understood.

- In cultured rat tendon fibroblasts and rat tendon explants, one study reported increased cell
  migration and spreading and increased phosphorylation of FAK and paxillin (two proteins involved
  in how cells attach and move), and the authors concluded the effect is likely mediated by
  activation of the FAK-paxillin pathway. The same study reported no effect on cell proliferation
  by MTT assay. This is a mechanistic study in rat cells and rat tissue, not a demonstration of
  tendon healing in people.
  Claim grade: **D (Preclinical)**.
  Source: PMID 21030672, https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21030672/ (verified 2026-07-19)

- A 2025 narrative review of the preclinical musculoskeletal literature reports that, across animal
  models and cell systems, BPC-157 has been described as activating VEGFR2 and nitric oxide
  synthesis via the Akt-eNOS axis and engaging ERK1/2 signalling (all names of cell signalling
  routes involved in blood vessel formation and tissue repair). The review's own conclusion is that
  human data are extremely limited and that BPC-157 should be considered investigational.
  Claim grade: **D (Preclinical)**.
  Source: PMID 40789979, https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40789979/ (verified 2026-07-19)

Two further points belong in any honest reading of the proposed mechanism.

First, FDA notes across the rodent literature that a dose-response relationship could not be
established, because widely separated doses produced comparable effects. In pharmacology, an
absence of dose-response is unusual and is a reason for caution about how the underlying effect is
being interpreted.

Second, in the human studies where it was assessed (administration by rectal enema), BPC-157 was
not detected in plasma. FDA states there is no human pharmacokinetic data for other proposed
routes. A proposed mechanism acting on distant tissues sits awkwardly with an absence of measured
systemic exposure in people.

Source for both: FDA Briefing Document, https://www.fda.gov/media/193343/download (verified 2026-07-19)

## Human evidence

**Summary: human evidence is minimal, and no adequately designed human trial has reported a
positive efficacy result for any condition.**

FDA searched PubMed, Embase, Cochrane and ClinicalTrials.gov and identified five human studies in
the entire published literature. A PubMed search performed for this Guide, restricted to the
publication types systematic review, randomised controlled trial and clinical trial, returned zero
records for BPC-157.

## Preclinical evidence

Preclinical means animal or laboratory-dish research. Every finding below carries its species or
model in the same sentence as the finding, because moving a preclinical result into a human-sounding
sentence is the most common and most misleading error in writing about this compound. Nothing here
tells a member what BPC-157 does in a person.

- In rat tendon explants and cultured rat tendon fibroblasts, one study reported accelerated
  outgrowth of tendon fibroblasts from explants, increased fibroblast survival under hydrogen
  peroxide stress, and increased migration and spreading of tendon fibroblasts, with no effect on
  cell proliferation by MTT assay.
  Claim grade: **D (Preclinical)**.
  PMID 21030672, https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21030672/ (verified 2026-07-19)

- Summarising the rodent literature, FDA states that pharmacological studies have suggested BPC-157
  prevents lesions induced by various challenges in rodent gastrointestinal and cardiovascular
  systems, has nephroprotective (kidney-protective) properties in rodents, promotes healing of
  transected tendons in rodents, and has neuroprotective properties in rodent models of stroke,
  encephalopathy and spinal cord injury. FDA also summarises rat studies on surgically created
  colonic fistulas and on prevention of NSAID-induced gastric and hepatic lesions in rats.
  Claim grade: **D (Preclinical)**.
  FDA Briefing Document, https://www.fda.gov/media/193343/download (verified 2026-07-19)

- FDA's caveat on that same rodent literature is as important as the findings. FDA states it is
  difficult to assess the biological plausibility of these pharmacological effects because
  dose-response relationships have not been established, the molecular targets have not been
  identified, and the mechanisms of action remain poorly understood. FDA repeatedly notes that a
  dose-response relationship could not be established in the individual rodent studies, and that
  several studies lacked histopathological confirmation (microscopic examination of tissue to
  confirm the claimed change).
  FDA Briefing Document, https://www.fda.gov/media/193343/download (verified 2026-07-19)

- A 2025 peer-reviewed narrative review of animal models and cell systems reports that BPC-157 has
  demonstrated regenerative properties across numerous animal models, and concludes in its own words
  that human data are extremely limited, that only three pilot studies have examined BPC-157 in
  humans in this area, and that BPC-157 should be considered investigational and its use approached
  with caution.
  Claim grade: **D (Preclinical, review level)**.
  PMID 40789979, https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40789979/ (verified 2026-07-19)

## Contradictions and disagreement

**1. Whether BPC-157 is currently "Category 2" on the FDA 503A list, and the date.**

Many secondary sources (law firm bulletins, industry blogs, seller websites) state that FDA placed
BPC-157 in Category 2 in September 2023 and that this is its current status. FDA's own page (content
current as of 04/22/2026) does not list BPC-157 in the Category 2 table. It lists BPC-157 in a
separate table headed "Bulk drug substances nominated but withdrawn", described as substances
previously in category 2 of the interim policies that were withdrawn by the nominators.

Assessment: the practical consequence is the same either way. BPC-157 is not on the 503A bulks list,
has no USP or NF monograph, and is not a component of an approved drug, so compounding with it does
not qualify for the 503A exemptions. That is confirmed by two separate FDA enforcement letters. But
the specific "Category 2 since September 2023" framing should not be repeated as current fact. The
September 2023 date could not be verified from an FDA source for this Guide and is logged as a gap
requiring a human to check the Federal Register and FDA docket record.

**2. Direction of United States federal policy.**

FDA staff, in a review memo dated 5/11/2026 placed before the Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee
meeting of July 23-24 2026, recommended against adding BPC-157 (free base) and BPC-157 acetate to the
503A bulks list, citing physicochemical characterisation, information on historical use, lack of
evidence of effectiveness, and safety information. Separately, science journalism (Undark, February 3
2026) reports that HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr. has signalled openness to loosening
restrictions on unapproved peptides, and that FDA Commissioner Marty Makary's December 2025 responses
were open yet non-committal, saying the agency would review peptides to determine which are harmless.

Assessment: these are a scientific review recommendation and a political signal, not two readings of
the same evidence. The Undark article provides no evidence that HHS formally reversed any
classification. A claim encountered in a web search summary that HHS announced plans to reverse the
classification in February 2026 is not supported by the retrieved article and is treated here as
unverified. Regulatory status may change, so every legal statement in this Guide carries the date it
was checked.

**3. Strength of the evidence base as portrayed by enthusiasts versus by regulators and reviewers.**

Marketing and enthusiast sources present BPC-157 as a broadly effective healing peptide and cite a
large volume of published studies. That volume is almost entirely rodent and in vitro work. FDA
identified only five human studies in the entire published literature, four of which are meeting
abstracts or small uncontrolled clinic series. A 2025 peer-reviewed narrative review independently
reached the same conclusion and called BPC-157 investigational. A PubMed search restricted to
systematic reviews, randomised controlled trials and clinical trials returned zero records.

Assessment: this is not a genuine scientific controversy. The preclinical literature is real. Its
translation into human clinical evidence has not happened. The preclinical versus human distinction
should be unmistakable on every claim a member encounters.

## Limitations

These are limitations of the evidence, and by extension of this Guide.

- **No adequately powered controlled human trial has reported a positive efficacy result** for any
  indication. Efficacy in humans is unestablished.
- **The only randomised controlled trial identified is a 2005 conference abstract** whose
  between-group confidence interval crosses zero, meaning it did not separate from placebo, and
  which FDA judged inadequate to support efficacy and safety.
- **Four of the five identified human studies were not retrieved in full text** for this Guide and
  are described only as FDA summarised them.
- **The one PubMed-indexed human study is uncontrolled, unblinded and single-site**, with a
  self-rated questionnaire as the primary outcome, in a condition with substantial placebo and
  procedural-response effects, and no long-term follow-up reported in the abstract.
- **Human exposure is tiny and brief.** On the order of dozens of people, for days to about two
  weeks. That cannot detect uncommon or delayed harms.
- **Co-administration confounds attribution.** In some studies and in many marketed products,
  BPC-157 appears alongside thymosin beta-4 (TB-500), so neither benefit nor harm can be attributed
  cleanly.
- **The material studied is not necessarily the material sold.** In at least one published human
  study the material came from a compounding pharmacy that later received an FDA warning letter
  naming BPC 157, and material in two other studies came from a 503A pharmacy that declined to be
  named.
- **Preclinical results do not transfer.** Rodent and cell findings frequently fail to reproduce in
  people, and here the usual translational uncertainty is compounded by unidentified targets and an
  absence of dose-response.
- **The regulatory picture was moving at the time of writing.** The FDA advisory committee meeting
  of July 23-24 2026 had not occurred as of 2026-07-19, so FDA's position was a staff recommendation
  and not a final determination.

## Quality and identity

This section describes the unregulated-market reality. It is not a critique of any particular seller,
and it is not sourcing guidance. Xenios does not direct members to any supplier.

- **The name does not identify the substance.** FDA states BPC-157 is a common name and not a United
  States Adopted Name, that FDA has encountered multiple salts and derivatives including different
  active moieties sold commercially under the same common name, and that inconsistent naming
  conventions that do not follow established chemical nomenclature standards represent a safety risk
  for patients because they may be dosed with a different bulk drug substance than intended. FDA also
  notes that both nomination packages it reviewed were internally inconsistent about whether they
  meant the free base or the acetate.

- **There is no official standard to test against.** No USP or NF drug substance monograph, no USP
  dietary supplement monograph, and absence from the European, Japanese and International
  Pharmacopeias. There is therefore no official identity, purity or quality specification that anyone
  can be held to.

- **Certificates of analysis in the market are purity-only.** FDA states that most certificates of
  analysis for BPC-157 (free base) contain only purity testing results, with no information about
  impurity limits or testing results to demonstrate control of the impurity profile. For the acetate,
  one certificate listed a related-substances test but the impurity profiles were unclear because the
  impurities were not identified or specified, and another had only a purity test with no impurity
  attribute control. FDA examined certificates published by commercial peptide sellers and found the
  same pattern.

- **Impurities of toxicological concern are not addressed.** FDA notes that likely impurities of
  concern for a bulk drug substance include nitrosamines, potential mutagenic substances (able to
  damage DNA) and potential teratogenic substances (able to harm a developing foetus), and that
  available data do not address these for BPC-157. Solid-phase peptide synthesis, the usual
  manufacturing method, also introduces residual solvents, coupling reagents, activators, catalysts,
  scavengers, and isomeric and deletion peptide impurities (peptides missing or misordering an amino
  acid).

- **Basic product-quality data were absent.** FDA reported no particle size, microbial quality,
  bacterial endotoxin, sterility, aggregate or container-closure data for the proposed product forms,
  and concluded that both BPC-157 (free base) and BPC-157 acetate are not well characterised from the
  physical and chemical characterisation perspective.

- **Consumer products are commonly mislabelled.** The United States Department of Defense Operation
  Supplement Safety states that products are often labelled with wording indicating they are not for
  human consumption or are for laboratory purposes, while being marketed into health and wellness use,
  and that BPC-157 is not a lawful dietary ingredient despite appearing in products sold as
  supplements. Xenios does not treat that labelling convention as implying anything about human
  benefit.

- **Products are frequently combined with other unapproved peptides.** TB-500 (thymosin beta-4)
  appears in FDA adverse event reports involving BPC-157 and in the published human knee-pain chart
  review, which makes both benefit claims and harm signals unattributable.

- **Contamination has been flagged.** Operation Supplement Safety states FDA has cautioned against
  compounded drugs containing BPC-157 due to safety risks and potential contamination with other
  substances.

## Safety categories

This section lists categories of concern. It is not a reassurance, and the absence of a category here
does not mean that category is safe. Nothing in the evidence base supports describing BPC-157 as safe
or as well tolerated in general.

**1. Immunogenicity (immune response against the peptide).** This is FDA's central stated concern.
FDA states that compounded drugs containing BPC-157 may pose risk for immunogenicity for certain
routes of administration, and that as a 15-amino-acid peptide given by certain routes BPC-157 may
pose a significant risk for immunogenicity, potentially amplified by aggregation as well as potential
peptide-related impurities. FDA adds that it is not aware of information suggesting the substance does
not present these risks. This is a mechanistic and theoretical risk flagged by the regulator, not an
observed clinical outcome.

**2. Peptide aggregation and degradation from handling, formulation and storage.** FDA states that
peptides such as BPC-157 can be extremely sensitive to product formulation, process and environmental
conditions (for example pH, heat, concentration, in-process related impurities, excipients), which
may lead to aggregation and degradation, that significant amounts of aggregates can form especially
during storage or under stress conditions, and that aggregates drive the immunogenicity concern.

**3. Reported adverse events in FAERS.** FDA searched its adverse event reporting system through
December 4 2025 and retrieved three reports in total, all associated with injectable BPC-157.
(a) A 55-year-old woman with injection-site redness and swelling, confounded by concurrent thymosin.
(b) A 28-year-old man who developed shortness of breath resulting in an emergency room visit, with no
further information provided.
(c) A 40-year-old woman who developed diffuse hyperpigmentation (widespread skin darkening) and
gingival darkening (darkening of the gums) on a product containing both BPC-157 and TB500, with
identical reproducible reactions on rechallenge and resolution on discontinuation. FDA judged this
likely due to the drug product because the events recurred on rechallenge, but noted that because the
product contained two peptides it is not possible to assess a relationship to BPC-157 specifically.
FDA's own caveats apply. FAERS reports do not establish causation, and 503A compounders generally do
not report adverse events to FDA, so underreporting is expected.

**4. Contamination and unknown contents.** Operation Supplement Safety states FDA has cautioned about
potential contamination with other substances in compounded products containing BPC-157.

**5. The absence of a characterised safety profile, which is itself the signal.** FDA's conclusion is
that there is insufficient clinical safety information to characterise the safety profile of BPC-157
(free base) and BPC-157 acetate, that nonclinical toxicological studies were too limited in scope and
duration to inform safety considerations, and that there is no information to assess the
pharmacokinetics of BPC-157 in humans beyond the enema studies. No long-term human safety data of any
kind was identified.

**6. Why "no serious adverse events were reported" is uninformative here.** FDA states that no serious
adverse events appear to have been reported in the identified studies, but that those studies were of
short duration, had small sample sizes, evaluated doses that were likely exploratory in nature, and
provided limited information on safety data, with safety monitoring unclear for most. A small number
of short exposures cannot detect uncommon or delayed harm, so this observation must not be read as
evidence of safety.

## Interactions

No human drug-interaction studies for BPC-157 were identified in the research underpinning this Guide.
FDA states there is no information to assess the pharmacokinetics of BPC-157 in humans beyond the
rectal enema studies, and no human pharmacokinetic data for oral, subcutaneous, nasal or transdermal
administration. Without human pharmacokinetic data, interaction risk cannot be estimated at all.

Because interaction risk is unknown rather than absent, the following situations warrant review by a
qualified clinician who knows the person's full history and medication list. This is a list of
categories for professional review, not a statement that any specific interaction has been observed.

- Any prescription medication, including medicines being taken for the same condition BPC-157 is being
  discussed for.
- Anticoagulant or antiplatelet medicines, and any bleeding or clotting disorder.
- Immunosuppressant or immunomodulating therapy, given FDA's flagged immunogenicity concern.
- Biologic therapies and other peptide or protein medicines, where an immune response to one product
  can matter for others.
- Cancer treatment, current or past, and any active or previously treated malignancy, given the
  absence of carcinogenicity data.
- Autoimmune and inflammatory conditions.
- Inflammatory bowel disease and other gastrointestinal conditions, including where approved therapies
  exist.
- Kidney or liver impairment, which affect how substances are handled.
- Pregnancy, planned pregnancy, and breastfeeding, given the absence of reproductive and developmental
  toxicity data.
- Any co-administered unapproved peptide, including TB-500 (thymosin beta-4), which appears in FDA
  adverse event reports involving BPC-157.
- Competitive sport participation of any kind, where anti-doping rules apply.
- Military service, where the Department of Defense prohibited ingredients list applies.

## Unknowns

These are the open questions. Listing them plainly is the honest position.

- **Whether BPC-157 works for anything in humans.** No adequately powered controlled human trial has
  reported a positive efficacy result for any indication.
- **What it does mechanistically.** FDA states the molecular targets have not been identified and the
  mechanisms of action remain poorly understood. Proposed pathways (VEGFR2, Akt-eNOS, ERK1/2,
  FAK-paxillin) come from animal and cell work.
- **Whether there is any dose-response relationship at all.** FDA notes repeatedly across the rodent
  literature that a dose-response relationship could not be established because widely separated doses
  produced comparable effects. That is itself an anomaly worth flagging.
- **Human pharmacokinetics by any route other than rectal enema.** FDA states it found no human
  pharmacokinetic data after oral, subcutaneous, nasal or transdermal administration. In the enema
  studies BPC-157 was not detected in plasma.
- **Whether it is immunogenic in humans, and at what rate.** No human immunogenicity testing was
  identified.
- **Long-term safety of any kind.** Maximum reported human exposure in the identified studies is on the
  order of two weeks.
- **Carcinogenicity, reproductive and developmental toxicity, and genotoxicity.** FDA states
  nonclinical toxicology is too limited in scope and duration.
- **What is actually in the products people buy.** No pharmacopoeial standard exists, market
  certificates of analysis are purity-only, and products are frequently combined with other unapproved
  peptides.
- **Whether the ongoing Phase 2 hamstring trial (NCT07437547) will complete or report.** Estimated
  study completion is February 2028.
- **The outcome of the FDA advisory committee meeting of July 23-24 2026**, which had not occurred as of
  2026-07-19.

## Questions for a qualified professional

These are questions a member could reasonably take to a physician, pharmacist, or other qualified
clinician. They are framed to open a conversation, not to obtain a protocol. Xenios does not provide
medical advice.

1. Given what is actually known about BPC-157 in humans, does the evidence justify considering it at
   all for my situation?
2. Is there an approved, evidence-supported treatment for the problem I am hoping BPC-157 would
   address, and what would you recommend first?
3. What is my actual diagnosis, and has it been confirmed? Am I considering this for something that has
   not been properly worked up?
4. FDA has flagged immunogenicity as its main concern. What would that mean for me, and does anything
   in my history make it more concerning?
5. I take the following medicines and have the following conditions. Is there any category of risk you
   would be worried about here, recognising that no human interaction data exists?
6. Products sold under this name are not standardised and certificates of analysis in this market
   generally test purity only. How would anyone verify what is actually in a given product?
7. Many products combine BPC-157 with other unapproved peptides such as TB-500. What would that add to
   the risk picture?
8. If I decided against it, what would you suggest instead, and how would we measure whether it is
   working?
9. If I have already used a product containing BPC-157, is there anything you would want to monitor or
   document?
10. I compete in sport, or I serve in the military. What are the consequences for me of a substance on
    the WADA Prohibited List or on the Department of Defense prohibited ingredients list?
11. What would you want to see published before you considered this reasonable?
12. Who should I tell about this, and should it go in my medical record?
