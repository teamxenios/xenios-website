# Ipamorelin: questions members actually ask

Answers below follow the same evidence record as the main Guide. Where the honest answer is "we do
not know", that is the answer given.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

Every identifier referenced below (PMIDs, trial registrations) is recorded with its retrieval
status in `SOURCE_REGISTRY.md`. Nothing is cited here that is not registered there, and a source
marked UNVERIFIED in that registry carries no claim in this file.

---

### 1. Does it work?

For the reasons people take it, we cannot say that it does, and the reason is not that the evidence
is mixed. Our review of the published record located no human trial of ipamorelin measuring body
composition, lean mass, fat loss, strength, recovery, sleep quality, injury healing, skin, or aging.
None. The human evidence base for those uses is empty rather than thin.

What is confirmed is narrower. In a small study of healthy men (PMID 10496658), ipamorelin produced
a short, temporary rise in growth hormone in the blood. That is a biomarker change. A hormone moving
on a blood test is not the same as a person recovering better, sleeping better, or changing shape.

The one published human trial that measured an actual outcome (PMID 25331030) tested whether
ipamorelin helped bowel function recover after abdominal surgery. It did not meet its main goal
(median time to tolerating solid food of about 25 hours versus about 33 hours for placebo,
p = 0.15, which is not statistically significant). The development programme was then discontinued.

---

### 2. Is it safe?

We cannot tell you that it is safe, and we would not be honest if we implied it.

The accurate statement is that safety has not been established. There is no long-term human safety
data for ipamorelin. Every human exposure in the record we reviewed was short: a single-dose
pharmacology study, an in-hospital course after surgery, and analytical detection work. Nobody has
run a controlled trial of repeated use in healthy adults over the kind of timeframe consumer
protocols involve.

United States regulators looked at the safety file and were not satisfied. Per reporting on FDA's
review, FDA cited immunogenicity risk (the risk that an injected peptide provokes an immune
reaction, ranging from mild rashes to life-threatening anaphylaxis), and concluded that the
available data were insufficient to resolve known safety concerns. The same reporting notes that
subjects in the studies FDA examined experienced adverse events including death, while stating
causation was not definitively proven. That last point needs its context: those trials enrolled
patients recovering from bowel surgery, a group with meaningful baseline risk independent of any
study drug. It is a signal that the safety database was judged inadequate. It is not proof that
ipamorelin killed anyone. Both of those things are true at once.

Insufficient evidence of safety is a different finding from evidence of harm. We are reporting the
first, not the second.

### 3. Is it legal?

The answer is jurisdiction-specific and it is dated. As of 2026-07-19:

- Ipamorelin is not an approved drug in any jurisdiction identified in our review.
- In the United States, FDA placed it in Category 2 of the interim 503A bulk drug substances list in
  2023, the category for substances FDA identified as presenting significant safety risks for
  compounding. On 2024-10-29 the Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee voted against adding it to
  the list. So it is not eligible for lawful pharmacy compounding.
- Numerous vendor and clinic pages claim ipamorelin was among peptides removed from Category 2 in
  April 2026 and is now available. A legal-practice analysis of that action states ipamorelin was
  not among those removed. We resolve toward the legal analysis and flag the point as unconfirmed.
- Even where a peptide was removed from Category 2, that removal does not authorise compounding.
  The legal analysis is explicit that such substances remain unlawful for compounding until they are
  formally added to the Bulks List. This distinction is widely misrepresented in commercial material,
  and misreading it is the single easiest way for a member to believe something is permitted when it
  is not.
- In sport, ipamorelin is prohibited under WADA, and its metabolites are detectable in urine testing.

An important caveat: fda.gov could not be reached during our review, so every FDA statement above
comes from secondary reporting. A human reviewer must confirm current status against FDA directly.
If you are making a decision that depends on legal status, verify it with a professional and against
the primary source rather than relying on this page.

### 5. Why is the evidence so thin?

Because the compound was developed for something else, that attempt failed, and the programme
stopped before anyone studied the uses it is now marketed for.

The sequence matters:

- Ipamorelin was developed by Novo Nordisk in the late 1990s and characterised in animals in 1998.
- Human development was taken up by Helsinn Therapeutics, and the target indication was
  postoperative ileus, the temporary shutdown of bowel movement after abdominal surgery. Not muscle,
  not fat loss, not aging.
- The published Phase 2 trial in 117 patients missed its primary endpoint.
- A second, much larger Phase 2 trial in 320 participants completed in May 2014 and has never
  reported results anywhere we could find.
- Development was discontinued.

So the compound never accumulated the kind of evidence base that comes from a sustained development
programme, and the specific questions consumers care about were never on anyone's research agenda.
The evidence is not thin because researchers are behind. It is thin because the studies were never
done.

### 6. What about the claim that it raises growth hormone "cleanly", without cortisol?

That claim is real research, and it is animal research.

It comes from the 1998 study that first characterised ipamorelin (PMID 9849822), conducted in
cultured rat pituitary cells, in anaesthetised rats, and in conscious pigs. In those models,
ipamorelin raised growth hormone without raising ACTH or cortisol beyond what GHRH stimulation
produced, unlike two comparison compounds which did raise them.

The study was well conducted. The problem is what happens to the claim in marketing, where a result
from rats and pigs is presented as a confirmed property of the compound in people. Our review located
no human study testing ipamorelin's effect on cortisol, ACTH, or prolactin, and none testing whether
the selectivity holds with repeated rather than single exposure.

So the correct reading is: a preclinical characteristic that has not been confirmed by an equivalent
human study.

### 7. Vendors say it has completed Phase 1 and Phase 2 trials and was well tolerated. Is that untrue?

The individual facts are largely true. The overall impression is misleading.

It is true that ipamorelin reached Phase 2 human trials, and it is true that the published trial
described it as well tolerated in that surgical setting. What is usually omitted is that the trials
were for postoperative ileus rather than any consumer use, that the published trial failed its
primary endpoint, that the largest trial never reported results, that the sponsor discontinued
development, and that FDA concluded the safety data were insufficient with its advisory committee
concurring.

Citing "Phase 2 trials" as reassurance while leaving out that those trials failed inverts the meaning
of the evidence. A failed trial is information, and it is not favourable information.

### 8. If I buy something labelled ipamorelin, is that what is in the vial?

Nobody in the published record we reviewed has checked, specifically for ipamorelin products.

What the literature does establish about synthetic peptide products generally is not encouraging. In
one analytical quality-control study of commercially obtained synthetic peptides (PMID 18342612),
one of five products was found to be a completely different peptide from the label, and two thirds
of the rest were of insufficient quality. Counterfeit and illegal injectable peptide preparations
are enough of a problem in Europe that controlling agencies built a dedicated screening method for
25 peptides already found in seized products (PMID 26003685).

There is a second identity problem specific to this compound. Ipamorelin is very commonly sold
blended with CJC-1295, which is a different compound acting on a different receptor. A product
labelled as a blend contains two active substances, and no trial of that combination was located in
our review. Evidence about ipamorelin alone does not carry over to a blend.

Because ipamorelin is not on the 503A bulks list, regulated compounding pharmacy supply is not
currently a lawful route either, so there is no oversight channel that would independently assure
sterility, endotoxin content, identity, or purity.

### 9. What would change your mind about this compound?

A specific and answerable list. Any of the following would move our assessment:

- Publication of the results of NCT01280344, the completed 320-participant trial. This is the
  largest human dataset that exists and it is missing. Its release would change the picture in one
  direction or the other.
- A randomised, placebo-controlled human trial measuring an outcome members actually care about
  (body composition, recovery, sleep quality, injury healing) rather than a hormone level.
- A human study testing whether the selectivity finding from rats and pigs holds in people, and
  whether it holds with repeated exposure.
- Any controlled long-term human safety data at all, over months rather than days.
- Retrieval of the FDA advisory committee briefing document on ipamorelin, which contains FDA's
  detailed safety assessment and which we could not access. That would replace secondhand reporting
  with a primary regulatory source.
- A published analytical survey testing what is actually in consumer products labelled as ipamorelin.
- A formal change in regulatory status confirmed directly against fda.gov, in either direction.

We would update this Guide, log the change in its correction history, and say plainly what changed
and why. Our position tracks the evidence, and if the evidence moves, so do we.
