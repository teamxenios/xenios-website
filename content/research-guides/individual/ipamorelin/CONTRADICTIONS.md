# Ipamorelin: Contradictions and Divergences

Where the record disagrees with itself, where animal findings and human findings diverge,
where a plausible mechanism outruns the evidence, and where commercial claims conflict with
the literature. Source ids refer to SOURCE_REGISTRY.md.

There are genuine contradictions here, and they matter. For this compound the disagreements
are not mostly between two research groups. They are between what the literature shows and
what the market says the literature shows.

## 1. Whether the selectivity finding applies to people

**Position A (dominant in commercial material).** Ipamorelin is the most selective growth
hormone secretagogue, raising growth hormone without raising cortisol, ACTH, or prolactin.
This is the compound's principal selling point.

**Position B (the retrieved literature).** The selectivity data come from a 1998 study in
cultured rat pituitary cells, anesthetized rats, and conscious swine (S07). In those models
ipamorelin did not raise ACTH or cortisol at levels significantly different from those seen
after growth hormone releasing hormone stimulation, while GHRP-6 and GHRP-2 did. No human
study located in this research tested ipamorelin against ACTH, cortisol, or prolactin.

**Assessment.** Position B is accurate about the evidence base. The 1998 work appears to be
well conducted and its finding is real within its models. The error is not in the study, it
is in the transfer. Position A reports an animal and in vitro result as though it were an
established human clinical property. The Guide describes selectivity as a preclinical
characteristic of the compound, with the species named in the same sentence, and states
plainly that no equivalent human confirmation was located. See CLAIM_TABLE.md C-13, C-14,
and PROHIBITED P-05.

## 2. Whether ipamorelin is "clinically studied and well tolerated"

**Position A (commercial pages).** Ipamorelin is among the better-characterized peptides,
having completed Phase 1 and Phase 2 clinical trials, and was described as well tolerated.

**Position B (the retrieved record).** The trials were run for recovery of gastrointestinal
function after bowel resection surgery. The one published Phase 2 efficacy trial did not
meet its primary endpoint, with median time to a tolerated meal of approximately 25 hours
versus approximately 33 hours for placebo (p = 0.15), and no advantage on secondary measures
(S04). A larger Phase 2 dose-finding trial with 320 participants completed in May 2014 and
has never posted results, with no publication located (S02). Development was not continued.
Per secondary accounts of FDA's review, the agency concluded the safety data were
insufficient and recommended against compounding eligibility, and the advisory committee
agreed on October 29, 2024 (S12, S13).

**Assessment.** Both positions contain true elements, which is what makes Position A
effective and misleading. It is true that the compound reached Phase 2. It is true the
published trial called it well tolerated in that hospital setting. It is also true that the
trial failed, that the program was abandoned, that the indication studied has nothing to do
with the uses now marketed, and that regulators judged the safety database inadequate.
Presenting "Phase 2 trials" as reassurance while omitting the outcome of those trials
inverts the meaning of the evidence. The Guide presents the full arc, and the tolerability
observation only with its population, setting, and duration attached. See C-20 through C-26,
C-23, and PROHIBITED P-06, P-07, P-08.

## 3. Whether ipamorelin was removed from FDA Category 2 in April 2026

**Position A.** A legal practice analysis of the April 15, 2026 FDA action states that
ipamorelin was not among the peptides removed from Category 2, and that the seven peptides
scheduled for the July 23-24, 2026 advisory committee review do not include ipamorelin (S14).

**Position B.** Multiple peptide vendor, telehealth clinic, and supplement marketing pages
assert that ipamorelin was among roughly twelve to fourteen peptides removed from Category 2
in April 2026, and that it is now or will soon be available for compounding.

**Assessment.** Resolve toward Position A, and flag it as unconfirmed. Position B sources are
commercially interested parties, disqualified as evidence under the source plan, and several
of them appear to conflate a reported political intention with a completed legal action.
Position A is a disinterested analysis citing a specific date and a specific Federal Register
docket. It is also internally consistent with the separately reported fact that ipamorelin
already had its advisory committee review on October 29, 2024 and was voted against, which
puts it on a different procedural track from peptides still awaiting a first review.

Neither position was confirmed against fda.gov, because fda.gov was unreachable throughout
the session. The Guide states the status conservatively, attributes it, and date-stamps it.
See REGULATORY_STATUS.md and PROHIBITED P-10.

## 4. What "removal from Category 2" would mean even if it happened

**Position A (widespread in commercial material).** Removal from Category 2 means a peptide
is cleared, legal, or FDA-sanctioned for compounding.

**Position B (the retrieved legal analysis).** Removed substances remain unlawful for
compounding until they are formally added to the bulks list, which requires completion of
formal rulemaking after advisory committee evaluation (S14).

**Assessment.** Position B is correct and Position A is a consequential misreading. Category 2
removal moves a substance out of the identified-safety-risk bucket. It does not confer
authorization. Any Guide section touching 2026 regulatory news states this distinction
explicitly, or it will leave members with a false belief about legality. See C-74.

## 5. Preclinical promise against human result, within this compound's own program

This is the clearest divergence in the record, and it is internal rather than adversarial.

A rodent model of postoperative ileus supported the hypothesis that a ghrelin mimetic could
improve gastric motility (S08, title level). That rationale carried the compound into two
Phase 2 human trials. The published human trial did not meet its primary endpoint (S04), the
larger one never reported (S02), and the program was not continued.

**Assessment.** No source disputes any of this. The value of recording it is that the same
inferential pattern now supports the consumer claims: an animal or mechanistic result is
treated as a prediction of human benefit. In this compound's own history, that prediction was
tested and did not hold. The Guide uses this as a worked example rather than an abstract
caution. See C-42.

## 6. Mechanism plausibility outrunning evidence

There is no contradiction between sources here, because there is no opposing evidence. There
is a gap, and the gap is where nearly all consumer claims live.

What is supported: ipamorelin binds a known receptor (S07) and, in a small human study in
healthy men, was followed by a transient rise in circulating growth hormone (S05).

What is claimed in the market: that this produces changes in muscle, fat, recovery, sleep,
healing, and aging.

What connects them: an inference of the form "it raises growth hormone, and growth hormone
does things." No retrieved study tests any step of that inference for this compound in
people. No human trial of any of those endpoints was located (S01, S08).

**Assessment.** This is not a case of conflicting evidence. It is a case of a biomarker
argument being presented as an outcome argument. The Guide names the inference explicitly so
a member can see the shape of it, rather than only being told the conclusion is unsupported.
See C-15 and PROHIBITED P-01 through P-04.

## 7. Where the record's own sources are thin against each other

**FDA statements have no primary source in this record.** Three secondary accounts (S12
journalism, S13 a trade association, S14 a law firm) are the entire basis for every FDA claim.
They do not contradict one another on the points used, and they come from different vantage
points, which is mild corroboration. It is not verification. If any of them misread the
underlying documents, this Guide inherits the error. Flagged in every regulatory row as
Grade G.

**Prohibited-in-sport status rests on a statement inside a research paper, not on the list.**
A peer-reviewed anti-doping paper states that growth hormone releasing peptides are on the
WADA Prohibited List and includes ipamorelin among the compounds studied (S06). The list
document itself could not be retrieved. Nothing contradicts the status, and the same paper
establishes that ipamorelin metabolites are detectable in human urine, so the practical risk
to a tested athlete is real either way. But the specific subsection reference is unconfirmed
and may not be published. See C-54 and PROHIBITED P-19.

**Product quality evidence is analogue, not specific.** The quality findings (S09, S10)
concern synthetic peptide products generally and seized illegal peptide preparations in
Europe. Neither analyzed products labeled as ipamorelin. No such analysis was located. This
is not a contradiction, it is a labeling obligation: the analogue evidence must be presented
as analogue, and the absence of product-specific evidence stated separately. See C-60, C-61,
C-63.

## 8. Jurisdictional divergence

No divergence can be reported, because only one jurisdiction was examined. The registry sweep
identified no approval or marketing authorization anywhere (S01), and the regulatory detail
in this record is entirely United States. UK, EU, Australian, and Canadian status was not
searched. The Guide does not claim a global position beyond "no approval was identified."
Approval or registration in one jurisdiction would not constitute approval in another, and
absence of a search in a jurisdiction is not evidence of a prohibition there. See
REGULATORY_STATUS.md section 3.
