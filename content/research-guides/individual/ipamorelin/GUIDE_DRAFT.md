# Ipamorelin

A Xenios Research Guide. This document describes what is known, what is claimed, and what is
not known about ipamorelin. It is not medical advice, it is not a recommendation, and it is not
an offer of a product.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## One-minute summary

Ipamorelin is a small synthetic peptide (a short chain of amino acids made in a laboratory) that
acts on the same receptor as ghrelin, a natural hunger and growth hormone signalling hormone. Its
one reliable, human-confirmed effect is that it produces a short, temporary rise in growth hormone
in the blood.

That is where the confirmed story ends, and it matters that it ends there.

The honest evidence verdict, as of 2026-07-19:

- Ipamorelin has never been approved as a medicine in any jurisdiction identified in our review.
- It was developed and taken into human trials, but not for muscle, fat loss, recovery, sleep, or
  aging. It was tested for a hospital condition called postoperative ileus (the temporary shutdown
  of normal bowel movement after abdominal surgery).
- The one published human efficacy trial did not meet its main goal.
- The largest human trial ever run on ipamorelin (320 participants, completed in 2014) has never
  reported its results anywhere we could find.
- The commercial development programme was discontinued.
- For every reason people actually take ipamorelin today, the human evidence base is not thin. It
  is empty. Our review located zero human trials measuring body composition, lean mass, fat loss,
  strength, recovery, sleep quality, injury healing, skin, or aging.
- There is no long-term human safety data for ipamorelin at all.
- United States regulators reviewed it and were not persuaded. An FDA advisory committee voted
  against making it eligible for pharmacy compounding on 2024-10-29.
- It is prohibited in sport and is detectable in athlete urine testing.

The strongest true sentence we can write is this: ipamorelin is a compound that reliably moves a
hormone in the blood, whose only completed efficacy trial failed, whose largest trial was never
reported, whose development was abandoned, which no regulator has approved, and which has never
been tested for the purposes it is sold for.

A material limitation of this Guide is disclosed in full below: every FDA-related statement here
comes from secondary reporting, because fda.gov could not be reached during our review.

## What it is

Ipamorelin is a synthetic pentapeptide, meaning a laboratory-made chain of five amino acids. It
belongs to a class called growth hormone secretagogues (compounds that prompt the body's own
pituitary gland to release growth hormone, rather than supplying growth hormone from outside).

It acts as an agonist (an activator) at a receptor called GHS-R1a, also known as the ghrelin
receptor. Because ghrelin is the body's natural signal at that receptor, ipamorelin is described
as a ghrelin mimetic (a compound that imitates ghrelin).

It was developed by Novo Nordisk in the late 1990s and described in the peer-reviewed literature
as the first selective growth hormone secretagogue (Raun et al., European Journal of Endocrinology,
1998, PMID 9849822).

What ipamorelin is not:

- It is not growth hormone (somatropin) and it is not IGF-1. It prompts the body to release its
  own growth hormone rather than adding growth hormone from outside.
- It is not a GHRH analogue. Sermorelin, tesamorelin, and CJC-1295 act on a different receptor
  (the growth hormone releasing hormone receptor). This matters commercially, because ipamorelin
  is very commonly sold blended with CJC-1295. A product labelled "CJC-1295 / Ipamorelin" contains
  two distinct active compounds, not one.
- It is not the same as other compounds in its own class. GHRP-2, GHRP-6, hexarelin, anamorelin,
  macimorelin, and ibutamoren (MK-677) act at the same receptor but are chemically different, and
  evidence about one does not transfer to another. MK-677 is not even a peptide.

Class label to carry through this Guide: ipamorelin is an investigational growth hormone
secretagogue with a discontinued development programme, not an approved therapy.

A CAS registry number is sometimes quoted for ipamorelin. We are not publishing it here.

## Why interest exists

We describe the interest so a member understands the landscape. Describing it is not endorsing it.

Interest in ipamorelin rests on three ideas, in roughly this order.

**The selectivity story.** The compound's original 1998 characterisation reported that it raised
growth hormone without meaningfully raising cortisol or ACTH (stress-axis hormones), unlike other
compounds in the same class. This became its main commercial selling point, usually phrased as
"raises growth hormone cleanly, without the stress hormone spike". That finding is real, and it
was well conducted. It was also carried out in cultured rat pituitary cells, in anaesthetised
rats, and in conscious pigs. It is an animal and laboratory result. Grade D.

**The growth hormone inference.** Ipamorelin does raise growth hormone in humans, briefly, and
growth hormone is associated in the wider public imagination with lean tissue, recovery, sleep,
and youthfulness. The commercial argument is essentially: the compound raises growth hormone, and
growth hormone does desirable things, therefore the compound does desirable things. That is a
biomarker argument, not evidence of benefit. A change in a blood measurement is not the same as a
change in how a person looks, performs, recovers, or feels. Grade G for any outcome claim built
this way.

**The "clinically studied" framing.** Vendors correctly note that ipamorelin reached Phase 2 human
trials, which sounds reassuring compared to compounds that never left the laboratory. The part
usually left out is what those trials studied, what they found, and what happened next. That
omission changes the meaning of the fact entirely, and it is addressed directly in the
Contradictions section.

## Terminology

Each term is glossed here and on first use above.

- **Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids. Shorter than a protein. Ipamorelin has five amino
  acids, so it is called a pentapeptide.
- **Growth hormone (GH).** A hormone released by the pituitary gland at the base of the brain.
  It has broad effects on growth, tissue turnover, and metabolism.
- **Secretagogue.** A compound that causes a gland to secrete something. A growth hormone
  secretagogue prompts the body to release its own growth hormone.
- **Receptor.** A docking site on a cell that a signalling molecule binds to. Binding a receptor
  is how a hormone tells a cell to do something.
- **GHS-R1a / ghrelin receptor.** The specific receptor ipamorelin activates. Ghrelin, a hormone
  linked to hunger and to growth hormone release, is its natural signal.
- **Agonist.** A compound that binds a receptor and switches it on. The opposite is an antagonist.
- **Ghrelin mimetic.** A compound that imitates ghrelin at that receptor.
- **GHRH analogue.** A different class (sermorelin, tesamorelin, CJC-1295) acting on a different
  receptor. Frequently blended with ipamorelin in consumer products.
- **Pituitary somatotroph.** The specific pituitary cell type that stores and releases growth
  hormone.
- **ACTH and cortisol.** Stress-axis hormones. Whether a compound raises them is the core of the
  selectivity claim.
- **Prolactin, TSH, FSH, LH.** Other pituitary hormones, measured in the original animal study to
  check for off-target effects.
- **Preclinical.** Research in cells, tissue, or animals, before human testing. Preclinical
  results frequently do not reproduce in people.
- **In vitro.** In cultured cells or isolated tissue, outside a living body.
- **Pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics.** What the body does to a compound (absorption,
  elimination) and what the compound does to the body (measured effects such as a hormone rise).
- **Biomarker.** A measurable signal, such as a blood hormone level. A biomarker change is not by
  itself a health benefit.
- **Endpoint.** The specific outcome a trial is designed to measure. The primary endpoint is the
  one the trial is judged on.
- **Placebo-controlled, double-blind, randomised.** The design that reduces bias. Participants are
  assigned by chance, and neither participants nor researchers know who got what.
- **p value.** A statistical measure of how likely a difference could be chance alone. Trials
  conventionally require p below 0.05 to call a result statistically significant. The published
  ipamorelin trial reported p = 0.15 on its primary endpoint, which does not meet that bar.
- **Proof-of-concept trial.** An early trial testing whether an idea shows any signal at all.
- **Postoperative ileus.** Temporary shutdown of normal bowel movement after abdominal surgery.
  This is what ipamorelin was actually tested for in humans.
- **Immunogenicity.** The tendency of an injected substance to provoke an immune reaction, which
  can range from mild to severe.
- **503A bulks list.** A United States FDA list governing which bulk drug substances may lawfully
  be used by compounding pharmacies. Ipamorelin is not on it.
- **PCAC.** The Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee, the FDA advisory body that reviews
  candidate substances for that list.
- **WADA.** The World Anti-Doping Agency, which publishes the list of substances prohibited in
  sport.

## Proposed mechanism

Everything in this section is proposed rather than established, and the species is stated with
each finding.

The proposed mechanism is that ipamorelin binds the GHS-R1a (ghrelin) receptor on pituitary
somatotrophs and triggers a pulse of the body's own growth hormone. This was characterised in
cultured rat pituitary cells, in anaesthetised rats, and in conscious pigs (Raun et al., 1998,
PMID 9849822). In those three models, ipamorelin released growth hormone with potency and efficacy
comparable to GHRP-6.

The distinguishing proposed property is selectivity. In those same rat and pig models, ipamorelin
did not raise ACTH or cortisol beyond the levels seen after GHRH stimulation, even at very high
exposures, whereas GHRP-6 and GHRP-2 did raise them. No secretagogue tested in that animal work
affected FSH, LH, prolactin, or TSH. Grade D (preclinical).

Three qualifiers must travel with that mechanism.

1. The selectivity finding is preclinical. It was shown in rats, in pigs, and in cultured cells.
   Our review located no human study testing ipamorelin's effect on ACTH, cortisol, or prolactin.
   The widely repeated consumer claim that ipamorelin is selective in people reports an animal
   result as though it were a confirmed human property.
2. One human pharmacology study in healthy male volunteers (PMID 10496658) confirmed that
   ipamorelin produces a measurable, temporary episodic rise in growth hormone with predictable
   handling by the body, and noted that the size of the growth hormone response varied more between
   individuals than the handling of the compound did. Grade C, and only for the narrow claim that
   growth hormone rises transiently in healthy men.
3. Showing that a compound raises growth hormone is a pharmacodynamic observation. It is not
   evidence that anything a member cares about changes. No source in our review links ipamorelin to
   any clinical outcome benefit in humans.

A second proposed mechanism came from the abandoned development programme. Because ipamorelin
imitates ghrelin, and ghrelin is involved in gut motility, it was hypothesised to stimulate
gastrointestinal movement. That hypothesis was tested in a rodent model of postoperative ileus and
then in humans. In humans it did not work. See Human evidence.

## Human evidence

Four human studies were located. Read the table before reading the detail, because the shape of
the evidence is the point.

| Study | What it measured | Result | Grade |
|---|---|---|---|
| PMID 25331030 (NCT00672074) | Time to tolerate solid food after bowel surgery | Did not meet primary endpoint | B (for the negative finding) |
| NCT01280344 | Dose-finding after bowel resection, 320 participants | Results never reported anywhere located | No grade, no usable evidence |
| PMID 10496658 | Growth hormone response in healthy men | Transient growth hormone rise confirmed | C (biomarker only) |
| PMID 25869809 | Detectability in urine for anti-doping | Metabolites detectable | Not an efficacy or safety study |
| Body composition, fat loss, strength, recovery, sleep, injury healing, skin, aging | Nothing located | No human trials found | No evidence |

**The published efficacy trial (PMID 25331030, registered as NCT00672074).** A Phase 2,
multicentre, randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled proof-of-concept trial published in the
International Journal of Colorectal Disease in 2014, sponsored by Helsinn Therapeutics (U.S.), Inc.
and run from April 2008 to December 2009. It enrolled 117 adults undergoing small and large bowel
resection, with 114 included in the analyses. The primary endpoint was time from first study drug
administration to tolerating standardised solid food. Median time was approximately 25 hours with
ipamorelin and approximately 33 hours with placebo, p = 0.15, which is not statistically
significant. No meaningful advantage over placebo was reported on the primary or the secondary
outcomes. The publication described ipamorelin as well tolerated in that setting. Treatment-emergent
adverse events were high in both arms (87.5 percent with ipamorelin, 94.8 percent with placebo),
which is consistent with a population recovering from bowel surgery rather than with a drug effect.

What this trial can and cannot tell a member: it is the only published human efficacy trial of
ipamorelin we located, it studied a hospital gastrointestinal outcome, and it tells us nothing at
all about body composition, recovery, sleep, injury healing, or aging, none of which were measured.
It was short and conducted in acutely ill hospitalised patients, so it is not a source of long-term
safety information for healthy adults. The negative result contributed to the discontinuation of
the development programme.

**The missing trial (NCT01280344, HT-IPAM-202).** A Phase 2 double-blind, placebo-controlled
dose-finding study in 320 participants following bowel resection with primary anastomosis,
sponsored by Helsinn Therapeutics (U.S.), Inc., started April 2011 and completed May 2014, listed
as completed. No results have ever been posted to ClinicalTrials.gov, and our review located no
corresponding publication.

This is the largest human dataset that exists on ipamorelin, and it is effectively absent from the
public record. We list it for transparency, not as evidence. It supports nothing. It must never be
cited as showing that ipamorelin works. One could reason that a completed but unreported trial in a
programme that was subsequently discontinued is more consistent with an unfavourable or null result
than a favourable one, but that is inference, and we state plainly that it is not established.

**The pharmacology study (PMID 10496658).** A pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic modelling study
in healthy male subjects, published in Pharmaceutical Research in 1999. Ipamorelin produced a
single episodic rise in circulating growth hormone that peaked and then declined, with predictable
absorption and elimination. Variability in the growth hormone response between individuals was more
pronounced than variability in how the body handled the compound. This measured no clinical endpoint
of any kind: no body composition, strength, recovery, sleep, or symptom measure. It is short term
and provides no safety information beyond the immediate study window.

**The anti-doping analytical study (PMID 25869809).** A study in humans characterising detectable
urinary metabolites of GHRP-1, GHRP-2, GHRP-6, hexarelin, and ipamorelin for doping control,
published in Drug Testing and Analysis in 2015. It provides no efficacy or safety evidence
whatsoever. It is included because it independently corroborates prohibited status in sport and
confirms that ipamorelin is detectable in human doping control testing.

**On the uses ipamorelin is actually sold for.** We state this without softening it. Our review
located no human trial of ipamorelin measuring body composition, lean mass, fat mass, strength,
recovery, sleep quality, injury healing, skin, or aging. The consumer use case is unstudied in
humans. An empty evidence table here is the accurate result, not an oversight in our search.

## Preclinical evidence

Preclinical means cells, tissue, or animals. Preclinical results often do not reproduce in people,
and the ipamorelin story contains a clean example of exactly that (the ileus work below). Every
finding carries its species or model.

- **In cultured rat pituitary cells, in anaesthetised rats, and in conscious pigs**, ipamorelin
  released growth hormone with potency and efficacy comparable to GHRP-6, and did not raise ACTH or
  cortisol beyond levels seen after GHRH stimulation, in contrast to GHRP-6 and GHRP-2 which did
  raise them. No secretagogue tested affected FSH, LH, prolactin, or TSH. Raun et al., European
  Journal of Endocrinology, 1998, PMID 9849822. This animal and cell study is the origin of the
  consumer claim that ipamorelin is selective and does not raise cortisol or prolactin. Grade D.
- **In rats**, a 1999 paper in Growth Hormone and IGF Research reports that ipamorelin induces
  longitudinal bone growth. We retrieved this as a PubMed record only, so the existence, title,
  journal, and year are verified and the detailed findings are not. PMID 10373343. Rodent
  longitudinal bone growth does not transfer to adults whose growth plates have closed. Grade D.
- **In ferrets**, a 2024 paper in Physiology and Behavior reports that the GHS-R1a agonists
  anamorelin and ipamorelin inhibit cisplatin-induced weight loss. Retrieved as a PubMed record
  only, so the title, journal, and year are verified and the detailed findings are not.
  PMID 39043357. Grade D.
- **In a rodent model of postoperative ileus**, a 2012 paper in the Journal of Experimental
  Pharmacology reports efficacy of ipamorelin on gastric dysmotility. Retrieved as a PubMed record
  only. PMID 27186127. This rodent work is the rationale that preceded the human ileus trials,
  which then failed in humans. It is the clearest illustration in this compound's record of
  preclinical promise not translating. Grade D.
- **In cichlid fish**, a 2024 paper in Animal Reproduction Science examines the influence of
  ipamorelin acetate on the hypothalamic-pituitary-testicular axis. Retrieved as a PubMed record
  only. PMID 38996787. Relevance to human physiology is very low. Listed only for completeness of
  the retrieved literature set. Grade D.

## Contradictions and disagreement

**Was ipamorelin removed from FDA Category 2 in April 2026?**
A legal-practice analysis of the 2026-04-15 FDA action states that ipamorelin was not among the
peptides removed from Category 2 of the interim 503A bulks list, and that the seven peptides
scheduled for PCAC review on 2026-07-23 and 2026-07-24 do not include ipamorelin. Numerous vendor,
telehealth clinic, and supplement marketing pages assert the opposite, that ipamorelin was among
roughly 12 to 14 peptides removed and is now, or soon will be, available for compounding. Our
assessment resolves toward the legal analysis while flagging it as unconfirmed. The vendor position
comes from commercially interested parties (grade E or G, never evidence), and several of those
pages appear to conflate a political announcement of intent with a completed legal action. The
legal analysis cites a specific date and a specific Federal Register docket, and it is internally
consistent with the separately sourced fact that ipamorelin already had its PCAC review on
2024-10-29 and was voted against, which places it on a different procedural track from peptides
still awaiting a first review. Neither position could be checked against fda.gov during our review.

**Is the selectivity claim established in humans?**
The consumer position is that ipamorelin is the most selective growth hormone secretagogue,
raising growth hormone without raising cortisol, prolactin, or ACTH. The evidence position is that
this derives from a 1998 study in rat pituitary cells, anaesthetised rats, and conscious pigs, and
that no human study located here tested selectivity against those hormones. The evidence position
is accurate. The finding is real and well conducted, and it is preclinical. The consumer claim
reports an animal result as a human clinical property.

**Is ipamorelin "clinically studied and well tolerated"?**
Vendor pages describe it as one of the better-characterised peptides, having completed Phase 1 and
Phase 2 trials, and cite that it was well tolerated. It is true that it reached Phase 2 and that
the published trial used that phrase in that surgical setting. It is also true that the trials were
run for postoperative ileus, that the published Phase 2 missed its primary endpoint at p = 0.15,
that the sponsor discontinued development, that the largest trial has never reported results, and
that FDA concluded the safety data were insufficient with the advisory committee concurring. Citing
"Phase 2 trials" as reassurance while omitting that those trials failed and the programme was
dropped inverts the meaning of the evidence. This Guide presents the full arc.

**What does removal from Category 2 mean?**
A widespread commercial reading treats Category 2 removal as clearance, legality, or FDA sanction
for compounding. The retrieved legal analysis states that removed substances remain unlawful for
compounding until formally added to the Bulks List, which requires completing formal rulemaking
after advisory committee evaluation. The legal reading is correct. Category 2 removal moves a
substance out of the identified-safety-risk bucket. It does not confer authorisation. Any member
reading 2026 regulatory news about peptides should hold that distinction, because without it the
news reads as permission that has not been granted.

## Limitations

Limitations of the compound's evidence base:

- One published human efficacy trial exists, it studied an unrelated hospital indication, and it
  was negative.
- The largest human trial is unreported, which means the single biggest body of human data on this
  compound cannot be examined by anyone.
- All human exposure in the retrieved record was short. There is no long-term human data.
- The selectivity property, which is the compound's main selling point, has not been confirmed in
  humans in anything we located.
- The consumer use case has no human trials at all.

Limitations of this Guide itself, disclosed because they are material:

- fda.gov was unreachable throughout our review. Every attempted FDA URL returned an error,
  including the 503A bulk drug substances pages, the Category 2 list, the advisory committee
  calendar entry for 2024-10-29, and two FDA document downloads. Every FDA-related statement in
  this Guide therefore rests on secondary accounts (investigative journalism, a compounding trade
  association, and a legal-practice analysis) rather than on FDA directly. A human reviewer must
  confirm current status against fda.gov before this Guide reaches a member.
- The FDA advisory committee briefing document on ipamorelin could not be retrieved. That document
  is the single most important primary source on this compound's safety assessment, and it is
  missing from this record.
- The numeric vote tally from the 2024-10-29 committee vote could not be obtained. The direction of
  the vote is sourced. The count is not.
- The WADA Prohibited List document itself could not be retrieved. Prohibited status is
  corroborated by a peer-reviewed anti-doping paper. The exact list section number and the verbatim
  list entry are unconfirmed, and no section number is published in this Guide for that reason.
- Four preclinical papers are recorded at title level only. Their identifiers, titles, journals, and
  years are verified. Their detailed findings are not.
- PubMed reports 53 total ipamorelin publications and 27 under a humans filter. Only the first page
  of each was examined. A small primary human study could have been missed, though the pattern of
  results makes a missed substantial efficacy trial unlikely.
- No search for FDA warning letters or import alerts naming ipamorelin vendors was possible,
  because fda.gov was unreachable.

## Quality and identity

Ipamorelin is not available through a regulated pharmaceutical supply chain. That is not a
technicality. It changes what a buyer can know about what is in a vial.

What the peer-reviewed literature establishes about the synthetic peptide supply chain generally:

- In an analytical quality-control study of commercially obtained synthetic peptides (the obestatin
  case, PMID 18342612), one of five peptide products examined was found to be a completely different
  peptide from the one on the label, and two thirds of the remainder were of insufficient quality,
  with purity below 95 percent and/or individual impurities above 1 percent. This is analogue
  evidence about synthetic peptide products in general, not an analysis of ipamorelin products.
- Counterfeit and illegal injectable peptide preparations are a recognised enforcement problem in
  Europe, serious enough that controlling agencies developed a dedicated screening method covering
  25 peptides already detected in illegal and counterfeit products seized by European countries
  (PMID 26003685). The retrieved material did not confirm whether ipamorelin was among those 25.
- A peer-reviewed critical review describes peptide and peptide-analogue products used in
  recreational sport and bodybuilding as often mislabelled or contaminated, because the supply chain
  is largely unregulated (PMID 41880199). This was retrieved at search-summary level only, so it is
  reported at low resolution and should be re-verified before publication.

Specific to ipamorelin:

- **Identity confusion is built into the market.** Ipamorelin is commonly sold as a fixed blend with
  CJC-1295, a GHRH analogue acting on a different receptor. A blend product contains two distinct
  pharmacologically active compounds. Evidence about ipamorelin alone does not transfer to the
  blend, and evidence about a blend does not transfer to ipamorelin. Our review located no trial of
  such a blend. The pharmacological non-equivalence point is sound. The claim about how common the
  blend is in the market comes from observing vendor material rather than a scientific source.
  [UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]
- **No published analytical survey testing the identity and purity of consumer products labelled as
  ipamorelin was located.** So the honest statement is that nobody in our review has publicly tested
  what is actually in these products.
- **Regulated compounding is not currently a lawful route either**, because ipamorelin has not been
  added to the 503A bulks list. See Regulatory status.
- Products sold as ipamorelin in the United States frequently carry laboratory-chemical labelling.
  Such products sit outside pharmaceutical manufacturing oversight, meaning sterility, endotoxin
  content, identity, and purity are not independently assured. Xenios does not treat that labelling
  as implying anything whatsoever about human suitability, and this Guide does not use it as such.
  [UNVERIFIED - background knowledge about labelling practice, requires human source check. The
  503A bulks-list status is separately sourced below.]

The practical consequence for a member: even the parts of the ipamorelin evidence base that do
exist describe a defined, characterised molecule. A product on the open market is not the same
object as a characterised molecule until someone has documented that it is. See
QUALITY_AND_DOCUMENTATION.md in this folder for the documentation a supplier would need to provide,
and for the honest current state of that documentation at Xenios.

## Safety categories

This section lists categories of concern. It is not a list of side effects, it is not a
reassurance, and the absence of a category here should not be read as absence of risk. Nothing in
this section, or anywhere in this Guide, should be read as a statement that ipamorelin is safe.

**Immunogenicity (immune reaction to an injected peptide).** Per investigative reporting on FDA's
review, FDA cited well-established research that injectable peptides carry immune reaction risks,
ranging from mild rashes to life-threatening anaphylaxis, as part of the basis for placing
ipamorelin in Category 2 in 2023. This is a class-level concern for injectable peptides rather than
an ipamorelin-specific adverse event series.

**Adverse events in reviewed studies, with causation not established.** The same reporting states
that in the ipamorelin studies FDA examined, subjects experienced adverse events including death,
while noting that causation was not definitively proven. Both halves of that sentence carry
weight, and so does the context: the ipamorelin trials enrolled bowel-resection surgical patients,
a population with meaningful baseline mortality and morbidity independent of any study drug. This
is a flag that FDA considered the safety database inadequate. It is not a demonstration that
ipamorelin caused a death. Presenting it without that context would be misleading in either
direction, so we present it with the context attached.

**Insufficient data to resolve known safety concerns.** FDA concluded that available data were
insufficient to address known safety concerns for ipamorelin and recommended against inclusion on
the 503A bulks list, and the advisory committee agreed on 2024-10-29. Insufficient evidence of
safety is a distinct finding from evidence of harm. We state it as what it is.

**Absence of long-term human safety data.** Across every human study located, exposure was short:
a single-administration pharmacology study, an in-hospital post-surgical course, and analytical
detection work. No retrieved study examined repeated use in healthy adults over months or years.
The pattern of use typical of consumer peptide protocols has never been studied in a controlled
human trial we could locate. This is the single largest safety unknown.

**Class-level metabolic considerations for growth hormone axis stimulation.** Growth hormone
secretagogues as a class are generally discussed in relation to glucose metabolism and insulin
sensitivity, and stimulation of the growth hormone axis raises theoretical questions about fluid
retention, joint symptoms, and effects on any undetected malignancy. No source retrieved in our
review documented any of these specifically for ipamorelin.
[UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check. A human reviewer must source or
delete this paragraph before this Guide reaches a member.]

**Sanction risk for tested athletes.** Ipamorelin is prohibited in sport, and the same
peer-reviewed anti-doping work establishes that its metabolites are detectable in human urine by
anti-doping laboratories. The detection capability is not hypothetical. For anyone subject to
testing, this is a concrete consequence rather than a theoretical one.

## Interactions

We located no human interaction studies for ipamorelin. The categories below are therefore
categories requiring review by a qualified professional who knows the individual's full history and
medication list. They are not a complete list, and no combination named here should be understood
as evaluated or cleared.

- **Any other compound acting on the growth hormone axis**, most immediately CJC-1295, with which
  ipamorelin is commonly sold as a blend. Our review located no trial evidence for that combination.
- **Growth hormone itself, or IGF-1 preparations.**
- **Any medication for diabetes or for blood glucose control**, given that the growth hormone axis
  is generally discussed in relation to glucose metabolism and insulin sensitivity. No
  ipamorelin-specific data exists.
  [UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]
- **Any other hormone therapy**, including thyroid, corticosteroid, and sex hormone therapy, where a

2023 FDA placed ipamorelin in Category 2 of the interim 503A bulk drug substances list, the
category for substances FDA identified as presenting significant safety risks for use in
compounding, citing immunogenicity risk for injectable peptides. This is a journalistic account of
the FDA action, and direct FDA source pages could not be retrieved.
Checked 2026-07-19. Source: https://www.propublica.org/article/peptide-safety-fda-compounding-pharmacies

**United States, advisory committee vote.** At the Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee meeting
of 2024-10-29, the committee voted against adding ipamorelin to the 503A bulks list, alongside
L-theanine, ibutamoren mesylate, and kisspeptin-10. FDA had recommended against inclusion.
Individual vote counts were not available in the retrieved source. Ipamorelin has therefore already
been through advisory committee review and was not recommended for compounding eligibility.
Checked 2026-07-19. Source: https://a4pc.org/news/pcac-votes-against-four-nominated-bulk-drug-substances

**United States, April 2026 Category 2 removals.** On 2026-04-15, FDA removed a group of peptides
from Category 2 of the interim 503A bulks list. Per a legal-practice analysis of that action,
ipamorelin was not among the peptides removed. That same analysis stresses that removal from
Category 2 does not authorise compounding, and that substances remain unlawful for compounding
until their formal addition to the Bulks List. Seven peptides (BPC-157, TB-500, KPV, MOTS-C, Semax,
Epitalon, and Emideltide/DSIP) are set for advisory committee review on 2026-07-23 and 2026-07-24
under Federal Register Docket No. FDA-2025-N-6895. Ipamorelin is not among them. Multiple vendor
and clinic pages claim the opposite. See Contradictions.
Checked 2026-07-19. Source: https://www.boesensnowlaw.com/blog/fda-advances-peptide-compounding-review-category-2-removals-announced-and-pcac-hearing-confirmed/

**United States, political context, not a legal change.** Investigative reporting states that as of
April 2026, under HHS Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr., FDA faced pressure to reverse its 2023
position, with stated interest in making roughly 14 peptides more accessible, the specific peptides
unspecified. This is reported policy pressure. It does not by itself change the legal status of
ipamorelin.
Checked 2026-07-19. Source: https://www.propublica.org/article/peptide-safety-fda-compounding-pharmacies

**Sport, World Anti-Doping Agency.** Ipamorelin is prohibited in sport. A peer-reviewed anti-doping
analytical paper studying ipamorelin states that growth hormone releasing peptides stimulate
secretion of endogenous growth hormone and are listed on the WADA Prohibited List, with ipamorelin
among the compounds studied. Growth hormone secretagogues fall under the WADA class covering
peptide hormones, growth factors, related substances and mimetics, which is prohibited at all times,
in and out of competition. The WADA Prohibited List document itself could not be retrieved during
our review, so the exact section number and the verbatim list entry are unconfirmed and are not
published here. Athletes subject to testing should treat ipamorelin as prohibited.
Checked 2026-07-19. Source: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25869809/

**Jurisdictions other than the United States.** No approval was identified anywhere. No
jurisdiction-specific legal analysis outside the United States and the WADA sporting framework was
performed in this review. A member outside the United States should not read the sections above as
describing their local law.

## Unknowns

Stated plainly, because this list is the most useful part of this Guide.

1. Whether ipamorelin produces any clinical benefit in healthy adults, for any outcome. No trial of
   body composition, lean mass, fat mass, strength, recovery, sleep quality, injury healing, skin,
   or aging endpoints was located. The entire consumer use case is unstudied.
2. What the 320-participant Phase 2 trial NCT01280344 found. Completed May 2014, results never
   posted, no publication located.
3. Whether the preclinical selectivity for growth hormone over ACTH, cortisol, and prolactin holds
   in humans, and whether it holds with repeated rather than single exposure.
4. Long-term safety of repeated administration in healthy adults over any meaningful duration.
5. Effects on glucose metabolism and insulin sensitivity in humans, unstudied for ipamorelin in
   anything retrieved here.
6. Whether tolerance or desensitisation of the growth hormone response develops with repeated
   exposure, and whether the hypothalamic-pituitary axis is affected by sustained use.
7. Safety in anyone with a history of malignancy. Completely unstudied for this compound.
8. Interaction with other growth hormone axis compounds, notably the widely sold CJC-1295 blend,
   which has no located trial evidence in combination.
9. Whether products sold as ipamorelin actually contain ipamorelin at labelled identity and purity.
10. Whether ipamorelin's current FDA 503A status changed in 2026, and in which direction. The
    primary FDA record could not be reached during our review.

## Questions for a qualified professional

These are questions a member could reasonably bring to a physician, endocrinologist, pharmacist, or
sports medicine clinician. They are written so that the professional, not the Guide, does the
judging.

1. Given my full medical history and current medications, is there any reason a compound that
   stimulates my growth hormone axis would be a specific concern for me?
2. My understanding is that there are no human trials of this compound for body composition,
   recovery, or sleep. Is that consistent with what you know, and does anything change your view?
3. Does anything in my history (blood glucose, insulin resistance, thyroid, pituitary, any past
   cancer, any family cancer history) make growth hormone axis stimulation a question I should treat
   as closed rather than open?
4. If someone did use a compound like this, what would you want to measure beforehand and monitor
   afterwards, and what would make you tell them to stop?
5. What are the signs of an immune or allergic reaction to an injected peptide that would warrant
   urgent care, and what should someone do if they occur?
6. I am subject to drug testing in my sport. Can you confirm my obligations here, and what
   documentation I would need if a substance appeared on a test?
7. If a product cannot show me a certificate of analysis from an independent laboratory covering
   identity, purity, sterility, and endotoxin, how should I weigh that?
8. Are there approved, evidence-supported options for the outcome I actually care about (recovery,
   sleep, body composition, aging well), and how do those compare on evidence and on risk?
9. Would you want to see the results of the unreported 320-participant trial before forming a view,
   and does its absence change how you read this compound?
10. What would you need to see before you would consider this compound reasonable rather than
    unreasonable for someone in my position?
