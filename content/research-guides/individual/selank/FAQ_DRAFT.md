# Selank: questions members actually ask

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

These answers follow the same rule as the Guide itself. Where the honest answer is that nobody
knows, that is the answer given. Where evidence genuinely exists, it is not talked down either.

---

## 1. Does it work?

Unusually for this series, there is real human evidence to discuss, and the honest answer is that
it is not strong enough to say.

Three clinical trials were located, enrolling 62, 60, and 70 patients with anxiety disorders,
approximately 192 people in total, published in Russian between 2008 and 2015 (verified
2026-07-21). In the 2008 study, the anxiolytic effect was reported as similar to the benzodiazepine
medazepam (PMID 18454096). In the 2014 study, pronounced anxiolytic and mild nootropic effects were
reported against phenazepam, another benzodiazepine (PMID 25176261). In the 2015 study, adding
selank to phenazepam was reported to reduce that medicine's side effects (PMID 26356395).

Four things limit what those results can support, and all four are structural rather than
nitpicking.

- **No placebo-controlled trial of Selank was found anywhere.** In anxiety, symptoms fluctuate,
  often resolve on their own, and improve substantially when a person expects them to. Without a
  placebo arm, none of these trials can separate the compound's effect from either of those.
- **None of the evidence is independent.** All four human records come from an overlapping Russian
  institutional author network, and the researcher credited with originating the compound is a
  co-author on part of it. There is no non-Russian trial and no independent replication.
- **There are zero registered Selank trials on ClinicalTrials.gov.** No pre-specified endpoints
  exist, so selective outcome reporting cannot be excluded.
- **Only abstracts were readable.** All four papers are in Russian, so effect sizes, dropout,
  blinding, and randomisation method could not be checked.

The claim grade for "Selank reliably reduces anxiety in people" is **G (Unverified)**. The grade for
"small non-independent Russian trials reported an anxiolytic effect" is **C (Early human evidence)**.
Nothing here reaches A or B.

## 2. Is it safe?

No human safety dataset was retrieved, and that is the answer that matters most.

None of the four human records yielded adverse event tables, dropout counts, laboratory monitoring,
or long-term follow-up in the abstracts that could be read. No pharmacovigilance database, no FDA
adverse event data, and no post-marketing surveillance data was located. One of the trials has
tolerability in its title and still produced no retrievable tolerability numbers.

That is an absence of retrieved evidence. It is not evidence of safety.

There is a specific claim circulating that must be addressed directly. Peptide vendor and blog pages
assert that across roughly 192 Russian trial subjects there were zero cases of dependence or
withdrawal, and no rebound anxiety, insomnia, or autonomic instability. No retrieved primary source
supports any of that. It is a market claim, not a finding. A blanket safety statement of that shape
cannot be supported by three small abstract-only trials that produced no adverse-event tables at all.

One further point. Selank has been studied alongside benzodiazepines more than separately from them.
One human trial added it to phenazepam, and one rat study combined it with diazepam. Combination
with sedative medicines is a recognised interaction context that has not been characterised outside
those small studies.

Xenios does not describe Selank as safe, as well tolerated, or as free of dependence or withdrawal,
because no evidence supports any of those statements.

## 3. Is it approved in Russia?

This is the question where most of what is written online appears to be out of date, and it is the
single most important correction in this Guide.

Two independent Russian pharmaceutical trade outlets report that on 20 to 21 January 2026 the
Russian Ministry of Health cancelled the marketing registration of 71 medicinal products and
excluded 14 pharmaceutical substances from the State Register of Medicines. The nootropic products
Semax and Selank from the Russian company Peptogen are named among the cancelled registrations, and
selank is named among the excluded substances (checked 2026-07-21,
https://vademec.ru/news/2026/01/21/minzdrav-otmenil-registratsiyu-71-preparata-i-isklyuchil-iz-grls-14-substantsiy/
and https://www.vshouz.ru/news/minzdrav/wcs-20789/).

The reported reason is not a safety withdrawal. All entries were removed at the request of the
registration certificate holders and authorised legal entities, which reads as a commercial or
administrative decision.

So the accurate description is that Selank was previously registered in Russia and that the
registration was cancelled in January 2026. It should not be described as currently approved.

Two honest caveats. The Russian state registry itself could not be read for this Guide, so a human
must confirm this directly before publication. And the commonly cited 2009 approval date and the
brand name Selanc appeared only on vendor pages, so neither is published here as fact. A prior
registration must have existed in order to be cancelled, but its date and certificate number are not
established here.

## 4. Is it legal, and is it FDA approved?

Selank has no FDA approval for any human indication (checked 2026-07-21).

A search restricted to fda.gov surfaced FDA warning letters to two compounding operations whose
search-result summaries indicate Selank was among substances used in compounded products, and state
that Selank and Semax were not nominated for inclusion on the 503A bulk drug substances list. Every
direct fetch to www.fda.gov failed during research, so the verbatim FDA text was not read, and those
statements are flagged for human verification rather than presented as confirmed.

No regulatory position from the European Union, the United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, or anywhere
else was retrieved. Absence there means not checked, not permitted.

Following the January 2026 Russian cancellation, no current national marketing authorisation was
identified in any jurisdiction reached in this research.

"Not approved" is a different question from "illegal to possess", and the second is a legal question
specific to where you live. Whether Selank is legal to sell or possess in any given jurisdiction was
not researched and varies. Xenios does not give legal advice.

## 5. Can I get it from Xenios?

No. Xenios does not sell Selank, does not supply it, and does not tell members where to obtain it or
how to use it.

This Guide exists for a different reason. Members encounter Selank in podcasts, forums, and vendor
marketing, and much of what is written about it repeats a regulatory status that appears to be out of
date and inflates the size of the evidence base. The purpose here is an accurate account of what is
and is not known, including the parts that are unflattering.

---

## 6. Vendors say the trials enrolled more than 800 patients. Is that right?

No, and the same commercial sector contradicts itself on this point.

Systematic retrieval identified three genuine Selank clinical trial records, with 62, 60, and 70
patients, approximately 192 in total, plus one immunological patient study whose sample size was not
stated. One vendor page elsewhere cites roughly 192 subjects, which matches the retrieved total and
contradicts the 800-patient claim coming from the same sector.

There is a second and subtler inflation worth knowing about. PubMed's term matching returns
unrelated records. A search restricted to trial and review publication types returned six records,
of which three concern strength training periodisation, lithium augmentation in depression, and
anaesthesia and circulating blood volume. None of those is about Selank. Anyone quoting a raw PubMed
count is counting papers that have nothing to do with this compound.

---

## 7. Two trials compared it head to head against benzodiazepines. Is that not the strongest kind of test?

It sounds like it, and it is not, for one specific reason.

A head-to-head comparison with no placebo arm tells you how two groups fared relative to each other.
It does not tell you whether either group did better than they would have done on an inactive
substance, or better than they would have done with time and attention alone. In anxiety, both of
those effects are large. That is precisely why placebo arms exist, and no placebo-controlled trial of
Selank was retrieved.

There is a second issue in the 2015 study, which is often cited as the strongest. It did not test
Selank on its own. It compared phenazepam alone against phenazepam plus selank, and reported that the
combination reduced phenazepam's side effects (PMID 26356395). That is a benzodiazepine-sparing
result. It is a reasonable question to ask, and it is not a demonstration that Selank has a standalone
anxiolytic effect.

---

## 8. Does it really work without dependence, like the marketing says?

Nothing retrieved supports that claim in either direction, which is not the same as it being true.

No adverse event data, no dependence assessment, no withdrawal assessment, and no long-term follow-up
was retrievable from any of the three trials. The vendor statements about zero dependence and zero
withdrawal trace to peptide blogs, not to any primary study.

The pattern here is worth naming, because it recurs across this whole subject area. An absence of
reported harm in a small abstract gets converted into a positive safety claim. Those are not the same
thing. When a compound has been studied in roughly 192 people across three small unregistered trials
whose full texts have not been read, there is no system in place that would reliably detect
dependence, withdrawal, or a rare adverse effect.

---

## 9. What about the animal studies? They seem more substantial.

They are, and that inversion is itself worth noticing. The preclinical record for Selank is stronger
than the human record, which is the opposite of what commercial pages imply.

In rats under unpredictable chronic mild stress, selank was reported to enhance the anxiety-reducing
effect of diazepam (PMID 28280289). In DBA/2 mice, it was reported to inhibit ethanol-induced
hyperlocomotion and behavioural sensitisation (PMID 27878720). In rats, it was reported to attenuate
aversive signs of morphine withdrawal (PMID 36322304). In rats under chronic foot-shock stress, its
effect on liver morphology was studied, and the direction of that effect was not retrievable for this
Guide (PMID 31243679). In IMR-32 human neuroblastoma cells in a dish, GABA, selank, and olanzapine
were reported to affect expression of genes involved in GABAergic neurotransmission (PMID 28293190).

Two things follow. The GABA-related mechanism proposal has genuine animal and cell support, which is
more than many compounds in this category can claim. And none of it establishes an effect in a person.
An animal anxiety result is a reason to run a properly controlled human trial, not a substitute for
one.

Note also the shape of the rat result. Selank enhanced another drug's effect. That is not the same as
having an effect of its own.

## 10. Is it banned in sport?

That could not be verified, and this Guide asserts nothing in either direction.

Two attempts to read the WADA Prohibited List failed. The 2026 list PDF returned empty content and
the prohibited-list page returned empty content (checked 2026-07-21,
https://www.wada-ama.org/en/prohibited-list). Selank was not confirmed to appear by name on any WADA
list.

Vendor and supplement blogs assert that it falls under the non-approved substances category,
reasoning from its Russian approval and lack of FDA or European approval. That is an inference rather
than a retrieved finding, and its stated premise, current Russian approval, appears to be false as of
January 2026.

One further point for anyone competing. The non-approved substances category does not list substances
by name, so even reading the list and not finding the word Selank would not settle the question. Any
athlete subject to testing should ask their own anti-doping authority directly.

## 11. What would change your mind?

A specific list, so this position can be tested rather than simply asserted.

- **A placebo-controlled trial.** The single most important missing piece. Randomised, with a placebo
  arm, and pre-registered.
- **An independent replication.** A trial run by a group with no connection to the Russian
  institutional network that developed the compound, and no commercial interest in the result. This
  matters more than a bigger trial from the same network would.
- **A registered trial with pre-specified endpoints**, so that selective outcome reporting can be
  ruled out. Today there is not even a registration.
- **The Russian full texts, read and assessed**, yielding randomisation method, blinding, dropout,
  effect sizes, and adverse events. Everything currently rests on abstracts.
- **Published human safety and pharmacokinetic data**, with systematic adverse event collection over
  a defined observation period, and evidence about whether meaningful exposure is achieved in a
  person at all.
- **A systematic review or meta-analysis**, so there is a synthesis layer above the primary studies.
- **A clear regulatory position from any national regulator**, since none currently oversees this
  compound anywhere identified in this research.
- **Independent analytical testing of commercial Selank**, published by a party with no commercial
  stake, since no such study was located.

If those appeared, this Guide would be updated, the change would be recorded in its correction
history with a date, and the claim grades would move.

---

## 12. What should I do with all this?

Nothing in this Guide is a recommendation, in either direction. It is an account of the evidence.

If Selank came up because anxiety is genuinely affecting you, that is worth taking seriously on its
own terms. Persistent anxiety has treatments with real, independent, placebo-controlled human
evidence behind them, and it can also be a presentation of something else that deserves assessment.
The more useful conversation is about the underlying problem with a clinician who knows your history.
The Guide includes a list of questions written to be taken into that conversation as they stand.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides. This FAQ
is educational. It is not medical advice, it is not a recommendation, and it is not an offer to supply
anything. Xenios does not sell Selank.
