# Selank

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## One-minute summary

Selank is unusual among the compounds covered in this series, because genuine human trial evidence
does exist. Writing that the data is absent would be inaccurate. But the honest description of that
evidence is small, non-independent, and unregistered, and there is a second finding that matters
just as much.

- **Human trials exist, and there are three of them.** They enrolled 62, 60, and 70 patients,
  approximately 192 people in total, plus one further patient study whose sample size was not
  stated. All were published in Russian between 2008 and 2015.
- **The binding weakness is independence, not size.** All four human records come from an
  overlapping Russian institutional author network, and the researcher credited with originating
  the compound is a co-author on part of it. There is no non-Russian trial, no independent
  replication group, and no industry-independent verification.
- **No placebo-controlled trial of Selank was found anywhere.** That matters especially in anxiety,
  where symptoms fluctuate, resolve on their own, and respond strongly to expectation. Without a
  placebo arm the trials cannot separate the compound's effect from either.
- **There are zero registered Selank trials on ClinicalTrials.gov.** A query returned only
  unrelated records matched by fuzzy text. So no pre-specified endpoints exist for any Selank
  trial, and selective outcome reporting cannot be excluded. There is also no systematic review or
  meta-analysis, so nothing sits above the primary studies to weigh them.
- **The strongest defensible reading of the human data** is that the anxiolytic effect was reported
  as similar to the benzodiazepine medazepam in one 62-patient study, and that adding selank to the
  benzodiazepine phenazepam was reported to reduce that medicine's side effects in a 70-patient
  study. The second of those is a benzodiazepine-sparing result, not a demonstration that Selank
  works on its own.
- **The regulatory claim repeated on nearly every commercial page appears to be out of date.** Two
  independent Russian pharmaceutical trade outlets report that on 20 to 21 January 2026 the Russian
  Ministry of Health cancelled the registration of the Peptogen products Semax and Selank and
  excluded selank as a substance from the State Register of Medicines, at the request of the
  registration certificate holders rather than for any reported safety reason. This Guide therefore
  does not describe Selank as a currently approved medicine anywhere (checked 2026-07-21).
- **There is no FDA approval for any human indication**, and no current national marketing
  authorisation was identified in any jurisdiction.
- **On safety, no human safety dataset was retrieved.** No adverse event tables, no dropout counts,
  no laboratory monitoring, and no long-term follow-up were available in the abstracts that could
  be read. That is an absence of retrieved evidence, not evidence of safety.
- **The preclinical record is more substantial than the human record**, which is the opposite of
  what commercial pages imply. It consists of rat models, DBA/2 mice, and a human neuroblastoma
  cell line, and it points at GABAergic mechanisms.

Xenios does not sell Selank, does not supply it, and does not tell members how to obtain or use it.
This Guide exists so that a member reading about Selank elsewhere has an accurate account of what
is and is not known.

**Overall claim grade for "Selank is an effective treatment for anxiety in people": G (Unverified).**
**Overall claim grade for "small non-independent Russian trials reported an anxiolytic effect": C (Early human evidence).**
**Overall claim grade for "Selank affects GABA-related signalling in animal and cell models": D (Preclinical).**

No claim in this Guide is graded A or B.

## What it is

Selank is a synthetic peptide, meaning a short chain of amino acids made in a laboratory rather
than extracted from a living source. It was developed at the Institute of Molecular Genetics of the
Russian Academy of Sciences.

It appears in the FDA Global Substance Registration System, the United States regulator's catalogue
of substances, under two entries: SELANK and SELANK DIACETATE. That listing is what confirms Selank
is a recognised discrete substance rather than a name invented by sellers. Two separate entries also
mean that the single word "Selank" does not by itself establish which exact material is in a given
product. The substance records were located during research but did not render usable content when
fetched, so this identity statement is flagged for human verification.
[UNVERIFIED - GSRS records located but did not render, requires human source check]

Selank is commonly described as derived from tuftsin, a naturally occurring immune peptide, with a
specific amino acid sequence and three additional residues added at one end. That description and
that sequence appeared during this research only on vendor and blog pages. Because the primary
chemical registry records did not render, neither the sequence nor the tuftsin-derivation claim is
independently verified here, and neither is reproduced.
[UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]

One corroborating detail is worth noting without overstating it. The title of a 2022 rat study
published in Bulletin of Experimental Biology and Medicine describes Selank as a peptide analog of
tuftsin (PMID 36322304). That is a peer-reviewed paper title, which is meaningful, but it is not a
chemical registry entry and it does not establish a sequence.

**A compound Selank is commonly confused with.** Semax is a different peptide made by the same
Russian manufacturer, and the two were named together in the same January 2026 regulatory action.
That is a shared regulatory fact, not shared evidence. Findings reported for Semax are not findings
about Selank, and the shared manufacturer makes this substitution easy to miss when checking a
citation.

## Why interest exists

The interest in Selank is straightforward to describe, and it is worth understanding without
endorsing it.

The appeal is the idea of an anxiolytic, meaning a compound that reduces anxiety, that works
without the drawbacks associated with benzodiazepines. Benzodiazepines are effective and widely
used, and they carry well-documented problems: sedation, effects on thinking and memory,
tolerance, dependence, and withdrawal. A compound reported to reduce anxiety without that profile
would be genuinely valuable, which is why the idea travels so far.

Three things drive the enthusiasm specifically.

First, unlike most compounds in this series, human trials really do exist. That is a real
distinction and it should not be flattened. Three trials in patients with anxiety disorders were
published in the peer-reviewed literature.

Second, two of those trials compared Selank directly against benzodiazepines rather than against
nothing, which sounds like the strongest possible test. The head-to-head framing is persuasive.

Third, the compound had a national regulatory registration, which commercial pages present as
official validation.

Each of those three deserves a qualification, and all three qualifications are load-bearing.

The trials are small, they come from an overlapping Russian institutional network that includes the
compound's originators, none was pre-registered, and only their abstracts were readable for this
Guide. A head-to-head comparison against an active medicine, with no placebo arm, cannot separate
the compound's effect from expectation or from the natural resolution of anxiety symptoms, which is
exactly why placebo arms exist. And the registration that is presented as validation was cancelled
in January 2026, so the strongest version of that argument no longer holds.

An anxiolytic rationale is also not an outcome. Interest describes why researchers thought a
compound was worth studying. It does not describe what a properly controlled trial would find,
because no such trial has been run.

## Terminology

Every technical term used in this Guide, in plain English.

- **Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids, the building blocks of proteins. Shorter than a protein.
- **Synthetic peptide.** A peptide made in a laboratory rather than extracted from a living source.
- **Amino acid.** A small molecule that links with others to form peptides and proteins.
- **Tuftsin.** A naturally occurring peptide involved in immune function. Selank is commonly
  described as derived from it, though that description is not verified in this Guide.
- **Anxiolytic.** A substance that reduces anxiety.
- **Nootropic.** A loose term for a substance said to improve thinking, attention, or memory. It is
  a marketing category more than a scientific one.
- **Antiasthenic.** Reducing asthenia, meaning abnormal physical or mental fatigue and weakness.
- **Neurasthenia.** A diagnostic term, used more in some medical traditions than others, for a
  condition of persistent mental and physical fatigue.
- **Generalised anxiety disorder.** A diagnosed condition involving persistent, excessive worry
  across many areas of life.
- **Benzodiazepine.** A class of prescription sedative medicines used for anxiety and related
  conditions. Medazepam, phenazepam, and diazepam are benzodiazepines named in this Guide.
- **Randomised controlled trial (RCT).** A study in which participants are assigned to groups by
  chance, which is what allows a fair comparison between them.
- **Placebo-controlled.** A study in which one group receives an inactive substance, so the effect
  of the compound can be separated from expectation and from natural change over time.
- **Placebo response.** Improvement that occurs because a person expects to improve. It is
  characteristically large in anxiety, which is why placebo arms matter so much in this area.
- **Blinding.** Keeping participants, and ideally the researchers, unaware of who received what.
- **Allocation concealment.** Preventing anyone from knowing in advance which group the next
  participant will be assigned to.
- **Pre-registration.** Publicly recording a trial's design and intended outcomes before it runs,
  so that results cannot be quietly reselected afterwards.
- **Selective outcome reporting.** Publishing the outcomes that looked good and omitting those that
  did not. Pre-registration is the main defence against it.
- **Add-on study.** A study testing a compound added to an existing treatment, rather than on its
  own.
- **Systematic review and meta-analysis.** Structured summaries that gather and weigh all the
  studies on a question. Neither exists for Selank.
- **Preclinical.** Research done before or outside any human study, meaning in animals or in
  cultured cells.
- **In vitro.** Literally "in glass". Research done in cells in a dish, outside a living body.
- **Cell line.** Cells grown indefinitely in a laboratory. IMR-32 is a human neuroblastoma cell
  line, and human-derived cells in a dish are still not people.
- **DBA/2 mice.** A specific inbred mouse strain used in laboratory research.
- **GABA (gamma-aminobutyric acid).** The main calming, or inhibitory, signalling chemical in the
  brain. Benzodiazepines act on the system that responds to it.
- **GABAergic.** Relating to that GABA signalling system.
- **Serotonergic.** Relating to serotonin, another signalling chemical in the brain.
- **Enkephalin.** A naturally occurring peptide in the body's opioid signalling system.
- **Naloxone blockade.** An experimental technique in which a drug that blocks opioid receptors is
  used to test whether an effect depends on the opioid system.
- **Cytokine.** A signalling protein used by immune cells. Interleukin-6 is one.
- **Gene expression.** Whether and how strongly a gene is switched on to produce its product.
  A change in gene expression in a dish is not the same as a change in function in a person.
- **Pharmacokinetics.** What the body does to a substance: absorption, distribution, metabolism,
  and elimination, including how long it lasts.
- **Marketing authorisation, or registration.** Official permission from a national regulator for a
  medicine to be sold in that country.
- **State Register of Medicines (GRLS).** The Russian national register of authorised medicines and
  pharmaceutical substances.
- **Deregistration.** Removal of a medicine or substance from such a register.
- **Compounding.** Preparation of a customised medicine by a pharmacy, as distinct from manufacture
  of an approved drug product.
- **503A bulk drug substances list.** A United States framework governing which raw substances
  pharmacies may use in compounding.
- **Warning letter.** A formal FDA notice to a company stating that the agency considers something
  it is doing to be in violation.
- **Pharmacovigilance.** The ongoing systematic monitoring of a medicine's safety after it is in use.
- **Certificate of analysis (COA).** A document from a testing laboratory stating what a specific
  batch of material was found to contain.
- **Gray market.** Sale outside the regulated and licensed supply chain.

## Proposed mechanism

Everything in this section is **proposed**. All of the mechanistic work retrieved is in rodents or
in a cultured cell line. No study demonstrated that any of these mechanisms operates at clinically
meaningful levels in a living human being. Claim grade for this section: **D (Preclinical)**, with
one item at **G (Unverified)**.

Mechanism is not efficacy. A plausible mechanism explains why researchers thought a compound might
work. It does not establish that it does.

**GABAergic signalling.** In IMR-32 human neuroblastoma cells studied in the laboratory, GABA,
selank, and olanzapine were reported to affect the expression of genes involved in GABAergic
neurotransmission (PMID 28293190, Front Pharmacol, 2017). These are human-derived cells in a dish,
not people, and a change in gene expression is not a demonstrated change in brain function. Grade D.

**Interaction with a benzodiazepine in rats.** In rats under unpredictable chronic mild stress
conditions, selank was reported to enhance the anxiety-reducing effect of diazepam, a medicine that
acts on the GABA system (PMID 28280289, Behav Neurol, 2017). Note the shape of this result. It is
an enhancement of another drug's effect in rats, not a standalone effect, and it is also the reason
that co-administration with sedative medicines is an open safety question rather than a
characterised one. Grade D.

**Enkephalin-related involvement.** The 2008 human study measured serum enkephalin activity
alongside its psychometric assessments (PMID 18454096). Measuring a marker is why enkephalin
involvement is among the proposed mechanisms. It is not itself a demonstration of one, and no
result values for enkephalin activity were retrievable from the abstract. Grade C for the fact that
it was measured, and no grade for any conclusion, because none was retrievable.

**Opioid-system involvement.** A naloxone-blockade study appears in the retrieved PubMed set
(PMID 17415472) and is cited as implicating the opioid system in the anxiolytic effect. That paper
was not individually retrieved, and its species or model was not recorded, so it cannot be written
in line with the rule that every preclinical finding names its model in the same sentence. It is
therefore reported here as an unresolved item and carries no claim.
Grade G. [UNVERIFIED - requires human source check]

**Serotonergic involvement** is frequently asserted commercially. No retrieved source in this
research supports a serotonergic mechanism directly, so it is recorded as a proposal without
retrieved support rather than as a finding.

**What is not known.** Whether any of this operates in a living human. The commonly repeated
commercial framing that Selank "modulates GABAergic and serotonergic pathways" overstates the
certainty of a mechanism that remains largely inferred from rodents and cultured cells.

**Species and models used across the mechanistic literature:** rats, DBA/2 mice, and the IMR-32
human neuroblastoma cell line. No human study demonstrating the mechanism in a living person was
located.

## Human evidence

**Human evidence exists here, and this section says what it is and what it is not.**

Three clinical trials and one further patient study were located. Approximately 192 patients across
the three trials. All four are in Russian, published between 2008 and 2015, and only abstracts were
retrieved for this Guide. Three of the four appeared in the same Russian journal.

Before the table, the finding that governs how to read every row in it: **this evidence is not
independent.** All four records come from an overlapping Russian institutional author network
(Zozulya, Neznamov, Siuniakov, Medvedev, Tereshchenko, and Kost), and Myasoedov, credited with
originating the compound, is a co-author on the immunological patient study and on much of the
surrounding mechanistic literature. There is no non-Russian human trial, no independent replication
group, and no industry-independent verification. Independence, not sample size, is the binding
weakness here.

**How to read that table.**

The 2015 study is the one most often misread. It asks whether adding selank to a benzodiazepine
reduces that benzodiazepine's side effects. The reported answer was yes. That is a
benzodiazepine-sparing result and it is a legitimate question, but it is not a demonstration that
Selank has a standalone anxiolytic effect, and there was no placebo arm.

The 2008 immunological study reports no anxiety outcome at all, and because its sample size was
not stated in the retrieved abstract, it cannot be weighted against anything.

**What is missing from the human record, and why each item matters.**

- **No placebo-controlled trial was retrieved anywhere.** In anxiety, symptoms fluctuate and often
  resolve on their own, and expectation produces large improvements. Without a placebo arm, none of
  these trials can separate the compound's effect from either.
- **Zero registered Selank trials exist on ClinicalTrials.gov.** A query on selank as an
  intervention returned two records and a query on selank as a general term returned ten, and every
  returned record was an unrelated false positive matched by fuzzy text (transcranial magnetic
  stimulation, dyslexia stimulation, brivaracetam in epilepsy, high-altitude hypoxia, and imagery
  rescripting). So no pre-specified endpoints exist for any Selank trial, and selective outcome
  reporting cannot be excluded (verified 2026-07-21).
- **No systematic review or meta-analysis was located.** There is no evidence-synthesis layer above
  the primary studies.
- **No full texts were read.** All four papers are in Russian and only abstracts were retrieved, so
  randomisation method, blinding, allocation concealment, dropout, effect sizes, and confidence
  intervals could not be assessed for any of them.
- **No adverse event or tolerability data was retrievable**, including from the 2014 study whose
  title refers to tolerability.
- **No human pharmacokinetic study was retrieved**, so whether meaningful exposure is achieved in a
  person is not established.

**A note on counting.** A PubMed search restricted to trial and review publication types returned
six records, of which three (strength training periodisation, lithium augmentation in depression,
and anaesthesia and circulating blood volume) have nothing to do with Selank. Only three genuine
Selank trial records exist in that filter. Anyone citing a raw PubMed count is counting papers that
are not about this compound.

Claim grade for any statement of the form "Selank reliably does X in people": **G (Unverified)**.
Claim grade for "small non-independent Russian trials reported an anxiolytic effect": **C (Early
human evidence)**. No claim in this Guide reaches A or B.

## Preclinical evidence

The preclinical record for Selank is more substantial than the human record, which is the reverse
of what commercial material implies. Every finding below carries its species or model in the same
sentence. None of it establishes an effect in a person.

**Rats under unpredictable chronic mild stress.** In rats, selank was reported to enhance the
anxiety-reducing effect of diazepam under unpredictable chronic mild stress conditions
(PMID 28280289, Behav Neurol, 2017). This is the main animal support for a GABA-related proposal,
and it is an enhancement of another drug's effect rather than a standalone action. Grade D.

**DBA/2 mice, alcohol response.** In DBA/2 mice, selank was reported to inhibit ethanol-induced
hyperlocomotion and the manifestation of behavioural sensitisation (PMID 27878720, Bull Exp Biol
Med, 2016). This is a mouse model of alcohol response. It says nothing about drinking in people.
Grade D.

**Rats, morphine withdrawal model.** In rats, selank was reported to attenuate aversive signs of
morphine withdrawal (PMID 36322304, Bull Exp Biol Med, 2022). No human work of this kind was
retrieved, and this result must not be presented as relevant to opioid withdrawal treatment in
people. Grade D.

**Rats, liver morphology under chronic stress.** In rats subjected to chronic foot-shock stress,
selank was studied for its effect on morphological parameters of the liver (PMID 31243679, Bull Exp
Biol Med, 2019). **The direction of effect was not retrievable** from the title and summary
obtained for this Guide, so nothing is claimed here in either direction. It is neither a protective
finding nor a harm signal as far as this Guide can establish. Grade D, direction unknown.

**IMR-32 human neuroblastoma cells, in vitro.** In IMR-32 human neuroblastoma cells in a dish,
GABA, selank, and olanzapine were reported to affect the expression of genes involved in GABAergic
neurotransmission (PMID 28293190, Front Pharmacol, 2017). The cells are human-derived, but they are
cells in a dish, so this carries no implication for a living person. Grade D.

**A general caution about animal anxiety models.** Animal models of anxiety measure behaviour
under laboratory stress. They are a reason to run a human trial, not a substitute for one, and in
Selank's case the human trials that exist are small, unregistered, and not independent.

## Contradictions and disagreement

Where sources genuinely disagree, both positions are stated, along with an assessment of which the
retrieved evidence supports. The full analysis is in CONTRADICTIONS.md.

**1. Whether Selank is currently an approved medicine in Russia. This is the most consequential
disagreement in this Guide.**

- *Position A.* Essentially every vendor, clinic, and blog page retrieved states that Selank is
  currently approved in Russia and available on prescription, commonly citing a 2009 approval and
  the brand name Selanc.
- *Position B.* Two independent Russian pharmaceutical trade outlets report that on 20 to 21
  January 2026 the Russian Ministry of Health cancelled the marketing registration of 71 medicinal
  products and excluded 14 pharmaceutical substances from the State Register of Medicines. The
  Peptogen products Semax and Selank are named among the cancelled registrations, and selank is
  named among the excluded substances. The reported reason is a request from the registration
  certificate holders and authorised legal entities, not a safety withdrawal.
- *Assessment.* Position B should be preferred and Position A treated as stale. The regulatory
  reporting is specific, falsifiable, and corroborated by two independent outlets naming the same
  company, products, and reason, published one day apart. The vendor claim is generic, uncited, and
  repeated near-verbatim across pages. This Guide therefore does not describe Selank as currently
  approved anywhere. A human must confirm directly against the Russian state registry before
  publication, because that registry could not be read for this Guide.

**2. The size of the human evidence base.**

- *Position A.* Vendor pages claim trials enrolling more than 800 patients, describing Selank as
  one of the most clinically documented nootropic peptides available.
- *Position B.* Systematic retrieval identified three genuine trials, with 62, 60, and 70 patients,
  approximately 192 in total, plus one patient study of unstated size.
- *Assessment.* The 800-patient figure is unsupported and is internally inconsistent with the same
  commercial sector, where another vendor page cites roughly 192 subjects, matching the retrieved
  total. Approximately 192 patients across three small trials is a small evidence base.

**3. Whether efficacy is equivalent to benzodiazepines.**

- *Position A.* Vendor pages state that Selank produces benzodiazepine-equivalent calm without
  sedation, cognitive blunting, dependence, or withdrawal, citing head-to-head trials.
- *Position B.* The 2008 study reports the anxiolytic effects of selank and medazepam were similar
  in 62 patients. The 2015 study tested selank as an add-on to phenazepam and reported reduced
  phenazepam side effects in 70 patients. No placebo-controlled trial was retrieved, and no adverse
  event data was retrievable from any trial.
- *Assessment.* Position B. "Similar to medazepam in one 62-patient study by non-independent
  investigators" is defensible. "Benzodiazepine-equivalent without dependence or withdrawal" is
  not, because it turns one small comparison into general equivalence and turns an absence of
  reported adverse events into a positive safety claim.

**4. Anti-doping status.**

- *Position A.* Vendor and supplement blogs assert Selank falls under the WADA non-approved
  substances category, reasoning from its Russian approval and lack of FDA or EMA approval.
- *Position B.* No WADA primary source was successfully read. Both the 2026 list PDF and the
  prohibited-list page returned empty content, and Selank was not confirmed to appear by name on
  any WADA list.
- *Assessment.* Unresolved, and not asserted here in either direction. The vendor reasoning is an
  inference, and it is further undermined because its stated premise, current Russian approval,
  appears to be false as of January 2026. Note also that the non-approved substances category does
  not list substances by name, so absence of the word Selank would not settle it either.

## Limitations

Limitations of the evidence base itself:

- No placebo-controlled trial of Selank was retrieved anywhere in the literature.
- No independent human trial exists. All four human records come from an overlapping Russian
  institutional author network that includes the compound's originators. This is the single largest
  weakness, larger than sample size.
- Zero trial registrations exist on ClinicalTrials.gov, so no pre-specified protocols or endpoints
  exist and selective outcome reporting cannot be excluded.
- No systematic review or meta-analysis exists, so there is no synthesis layer above the primary
  studies.
- Three of the four human records were published in a single Russian journal, and all four are in
  Russian, so they have not been subject to broad international peer scrutiny.
- The trials are small: 62, 60, and 70 patients, with the fourth study's sample size not stated.
- No human pharmacokinetic or bioavailability data was retrieved.
- No human safety or adverse-event dataset was retrieved.
- Trial durations appear short in the abstracts available, and no long-term follow-up data exists.
- The strongest-sounding result, the 2015 study, is an add-on design and answers a
  benzodiazepine-sparing question rather than a standalone efficacy question.

Limitations of this Guide's research process, stated so a reader can judge its reliability:

- Only abstracts were read for all four human studies. The full texts are in Russian and were not
  obtained, so randomisation method, blinding, allocation concealment, dropout, effect sizes, and
  confidence intervals could not be assessed.
- Every direct fetch to www.fda.gov returned HTTP 404 to this retrieval path, so no verbatim FDA
  text was read. The United States statements in this Guide rest on search-result summaries and are
  flagged for human verification.
- Both WADA fetches, the 2026 list PDF and the prohibited-list page, returned empty content. No
  anti-doping statement is made in either direction.
- The FDA Global Substance Registration System records for SELANK and SELANK DIACETATE were located
  but did not render usable content.
- The Russian State Register of Medicines was not read directly. A pharm-portal mirror record
  returned no readable content, so the January 2026 finding rests on two independent trade outlets
  rather than on the registry itself.
- PubMed HTML pages returned a CAPTCHA during research, which was worked around by using the NCBI
  E-utilities application programming interface. Nothing was substituted from memory in place of a
  failed retrieval.
- Only ClinicalTrials.gov was searched. The Russian national registry, the EU Clinical Trials
  Register, ISRCTN, and the WHO ICTRP were not searched, and the Russian trials may be registered
  elsewhere.
- A retrieval control was run. Querying a fabricated PubMed identifier returned "cannot get
  document summary" rather than a fabricated record, which confirms the retrieval path is real.
  That validates the method, not any individual claim.

## Quality and identity

This section is about the material itself, separately from the science.

**There is no current national marketing authorisation identified anywhere.** This is sharper than
the usual situation for an unapproved compound. Following the January 2026 Russian cancellation, no
regulator identified in this research is currently overseeing the identity, content, or sterility
of any Selank product. Whatever product-quality oversight existed under the Russian registration no
longer applies to material obtained outside a regulated supply chain (checked 2026-07-21).

**No Selank-specific quality testing was found.** No study testing material sold as Selank for
identity, purity, sterility, or endotoxin content was located. That is a gap in knowledge, not a
clean result. Nobody appears to have checked.

**United States regulatory concern about compounded preparations exists but could not be read
directly.** Search-result summaries indicate that FDA warning letters to two compounding
operations, Tailor Made Compounding LLC in 2020 and Advanced Nutriceuticals LLC doing business as
The Guyer Institute in 2021, referred to Selank among substances used in compounded products, and
stated that Selank and Semax were not nominated for inclusion on the 503A bulk drug substances
list. **Every direct fetch to www.fda.gov returned HTTP 404 to this retrieval path**, so the
verbatim FDA text was not read and a human must open both letters and the 503A lists before
publication.
[UNVERIFIED - FDA primary text not read, requires human source check]

**Identity is not fully pinned down.** The FDA Global Substance Registration System lists both
SELANK and SELANK DIACETATE, so the single name does not establish which material is in a given
product. The commonly quoted amino acid sequence and the tuftsin-derivation description could not
be confirmed against a primary chemical source, because the registry records did not render, and
neither is reproduced in this Guide.

**Naming confusion with Semax is a practical risk.** Semax is a different compound from the same
manufacturer, named in the same regulatory action. Being named together is a regulatory fact, not
shared evidence, and it is the most likely route by which Semax findings enter Selank claims.

## Safety categories

Nothing in this section is reassurance. These are categories of concern that remain open because no
one has closed them.

**1. No human safety dataset was retrieved. This is the primary safety finding.** None of the four
human records yielded adverse event tables, dropout counts, laboratory monitoring, or long-term
follow-up in the abstracts available. No pharmacovigilance database, no FDA adverse event data, and
no post-marketing surveillance data for Selank was located. **Absence of retrieved evidence is not
evidence of safety.** It is worth noting that one of the trials has tolerability in its title and
still yielded no retrievable tolerability numbers.

**2. Vendor claims of zero dependence and zero withdrawal are unsupported and must not be
believed.** Search-result summaries from peptide vendor and blog pages assert that across roughly
192 Russian trial subjects there were zero cases of dependence or withdrawal and no rebound
anxiety, insomnia, or autonomic instability. No retrieved primary source supports any of that. It
is a grade E market claim only. A blanket safety claim of that shape is not supportable from three
small abstract-only trials that produced no adverse-event tables at all.

**3. Co-administration with sedative medicines is an open question.** One of the three human
trials studied selank added to phenazepam, a benzodiazepine, and one rat study combined selank with
diazepam. Combination with sedative and hypnotic medicines is a recognised interaction context and
it has not been characterised outside these small studies. The compound has been studied alongside
benzodiazepines more than separately from them.

**4. No characterised human pharmacokinetics.** Duration of effect, clearance, and accumulation are
not established, and whether meaningful exposure is achieved in a person is not established either.
Since the mechanism itself is inferred from rodents and cultured cells, downstream and off-target
effects in a living human cannot be predicted from the existing literature.

**5. Product quality as a safety issue in its own right.** Following the January 2026 Russian
deregistration, no current marketing authorisation was identified in any jurisdiction, so material
obtained outside a regulated supply chain has unverified identity, purity, sterility, and endotoxin
status, with no regulator overseeing any of it.

**6. Entirely unstudied populations.** Pregnancy, breastfeeding, children, older adults, and people
with other medical or psychiatric conditions. No data of any kind was located for any of these
groups.

**7. Anxiety is a condition that warrants proper assessment.** Persistent anxiety has effective,
studied treatments and can also be a presentation of something else entirely. The most useful step
for anyone whose interest in Selank came from real symptoms is an assessment by a qualified
clinician, not a compound with no placebo-controlled evidence behind it.

## Interactions

No formal interaction study of Selank was retrieved. Interactions are therefore unstudied, which is
not the same as absent.

One interaction context is not merely theoretical, because it is the setting of the research
itself. Selank was studied added to the benzodiazepine phenazepam in a 70-patient trial
(PMID 26356395), and in combination with diazepam in rats (PMID 28280289). Whatever else that
means, it means the compound has been examined alongside sedative medicines rather than
independently of them, and no characterisation of that combination exists outside those small
studies.

The following categories would require review by a qualified professional, because they intersect
directly with the proposed mechanism:

- **Benzodiazepines and other sedative or hypnotic medicines**, which is the single most direct
  concern given the research context above.
- **Any medicine acting on the GABA system**, including some anti-seizure medicines.
- **Antidepressants and other psychiatric medication**, including anything acting on serotonin.
- **Alcohol**, given that the only mouse study retrieved concerned an alcohol response model and
  given the sedative interaction context.
- **Opioid medicines**, given that enkephalin and opioid-system involvement is among the proposed
  mechanisms and given the rat morphine withdrawal study.
- **Any medicine where sedation, alertness, or driving safety is a consideration.**
- **Any other unapproved peptide or research compound taken at the same time**, which makes both
  benefit and harm impossible to attribute.
- **Pregnancy and breastfeeding**, where no data exists at all.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

## Questions for a qualified professional

If a member wants to discuss Selank with a clinician, these are questions that produce a useful
conversation. They are written to be asked as they stand.

1. I have read that the human trials of Selank are small, come from a single Russian research
   network including the people who developed it, and were never registered. Does that match what
   you can find, and how much weight would you give evidence with that shape?
2. There is no placebo-controlled trial of this compound. For anxiety specifically, how much does a
   missing placebo arm change how you read a positive result?
3. Two of the three trials compared it against a benzodiazepine, and one of those added it to a
   benzodiazepine rather than testing it alone. Does that design tell us what people think it tells
   us?
4. What is actually going on with my anxiety, and are there treatments with proper human evidence
   that we have not tried yet?
5. I take the following medications and have the following conditions. Which of them would make any
   compound acting on sedation or the GABA system a particularly poor idea for me?
6. The compound has mostly been studied alongside benzodiazepines rather than separately from them.
   If someone were taking both, what would you want to watch for?
7. A national registration for this compound was cancelled in January 2026 at the manufacturer's
   request, and no regulator is currently overseeing product quality anywhere I could find. How do
   you think about unverified identity, purity, and sterility for something introduced into the body?
8. I compete in a tested sport. Who is the right authority for me to ask about whether this is
   prohibited, given that I could not verify its status?
9. If I decided to do nothing about this for now, what would you want me to watch for, and when
   should I come back?
10. If better human evidence on Selank appeared, what would you want to see in it before it changed
    your advice?

This Guide does not give medical advice and does not tell anyone what to do. These questions belong
with a clinician who knows your history.
