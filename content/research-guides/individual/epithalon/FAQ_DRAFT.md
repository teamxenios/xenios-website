# Epithalon: questions members actually ask

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

These answers follow the same rule as the Guide itself. Where the honest answer is that nobody
knows, that is the answer given.

---

## 1. Does it work?

Nobody knows, and that is not a hedge.

There is no randomized, blinded, placebo-controlled human trial of this peptide. Direct queries of
ClinicalTrials.gov returned zero registered studies under the spelling "epitalon" and zero under
the spelling "epithalon" (both verified 2026-07-21). Both spellings were checked deliberately,
because the same authors use them interchangeably and searching only one under-retrieves.

One human report of the peptide itself exists: a 2002 non-randomized report in patients with a
degenerative retinal condition, from the institute that developed the compound, reporting a
positive clinical effect in 90 percent of cases (PMID 12195242). It describes no randomization, no
blinding, and no control group composition, and it does not state how many people were enrolled. A
responder percentage from a study with no comparison group does not show that the treatment caused
the improvement.

The rest is laboratory work. In human cell lines studied in a dish, a 2025 group reported telomere
lengthening (PMID 40908429). In human gingival stem cells in a dish, a 2020 group reported
increased expression of neurogenic markers (PMID 32019204). Those are real findings in cells. They
say nothing about what happens in a person.

The honest claim grade for "Epithalon works in people" is **G (Unverified)**. The grade for
"Epitalon lengthened telomeres in human cell lines in the laboratory" is **D (Preclinical)**.

---

## 2. What about the study showing it reduced mortality in elderly people?

**That study was about a different substance.** This is the single most important correction in
this FAQ.

The widely cited report describes 266 elderly and older persons in Russia and Ukraine, observed
over 6 to 8 years, with reported mortality reductions versus controls of 2.0 to 2.1-fold with
Thymalin, 1.6 to 1.8-fold with Epithalamin, and 4.1-fold with combined repeated treatment
(PMID 14523363).

**Epithalamin, also written Epithalamine, is a bovine pineal gland extract. It is not the synthetic
four-amino-acid peptide.** The peptide was designed based on the amino acid composition of that
extract, which is exactly why the two get conflated. The retrieved record does not establish that
the synthetic peptide was administered in that study.

Even setting the substance question aside, the study was non-randomized, with no blinding
described, run by the institutes that developed these preparations, with active administration in
only the first 2 to 3 years of the observation. Mortality effects of that size are extraordinary
claims, and extraordinary claims need independent pre-registered replication. None was found.

So when a page cites those mortality numbers as evidence for Epithalon or Epitalon, it is making a
factual error about which substance was studied, not simplifying.

---

## 3. Is it safe?

Nobody knows that either, and this is the answer that matters most.

There is no human safety dataset, no human pharmacokinetic characterisation (how the body absorbs
and clears it), no immunogenicity assessment (whether the body mounts an immune response against
it), and no interaction data. A 2025 peer-reviewed review states plainly that information about
critical safety issues for this peptide is missing, and recommends short-term and long-term
toxicity study including genotoxicity and carcinogenicity assessment before it could be considered
as an active pharmaceutical ingredient (PMID 40141333).

You will see it said that the retinitis pigmentosa patients reported no side effects. That is
patient-reported data from a non-randomized study run by the originating institute, with no
systematic adverse event ascertainment. When nobody is systematically looking for harms, finding
none is close to uninformative. It cannot support any blanket safety statement.

Xenios does not describe this compound as safe, as well tolerated, or as free of side effects,
because no evidence supports any of those statements.

---

## 4. The telomere thing sounds like the whole point. Is that good news?

It is the benefit story and the safety question at the same time, and that is unusual enough to
be worth sitting with.

The proposed mechanism is telomerase and hTERT upregulation leading to telomere extension.
Telomeres are the protective caps on the ends of chromosomes, and they shorten as cells divide, so
lengthening them maps neatly onto a popular account of aging.

The strongest laboratory study retrieved reports both halves. In normal human cells in a dish it
reports telomere extension via telomerase upregulation. **In the human breast cancer cell lines
21NT and BT474 in a dish, it reports telomere lengthening too, by a telomerase-independent route
called alternative lengthening of telomeres** (PMID 40908429). Extending telomeres in cancer cell
lines is a coherent reason to want carcinogenicity data before anyone uses something in a person,
and that data was not found.

To be precise about what this is and is not: **it is a mechanism-based concern, not a demonstrated
human harm.** Nothing retrieved shows this compound causes cancer in people. Nothing retrieved
shows it does not. A 2002 title-level report describes suppressed breast tumour development in one
strain of transgenic mice, which reads the other way, but that record was not read beyond its
title and a mouse tumour model does not settle a question raised in human cancer cells.

That is the honest state of it. Anyone presenting the telomere story as straightforwardly good
news is telling you half of what the same paper reports.

## 5. Why do I see it spelled two different ways?

Because both spellings are correct, and this genuinely matters for anyone trying to check the
evidence.

Epithalon, Epitalon, and Epithalone all refer to the same AEDG tetrapeptide, and the same authors
use the spellings interchangeably. A search on one spelling alone retrieves an incomplete picture,
so two people searching in good faith can come away with different views of how much evidence
exists.

Every registry and database query behind this Guide was run against both principal spellings,
deliberately and separately. This Guide does not designate one spelling as the correct scientific
label, because that is a decision for a reviewer. Whichever is used for display, both stay
searchable.

Separately, do not let the similar name Epithalamin slide past. That is the bovine pineal extract,
a different substance, and it is the source of most of the confusion described in question 2.

## 6. Vendors point to 142 published studies. Is that not a lot?

The number is real and it is misapplied.

A PubMed search across both spellings returned 142 records at retrieval. That total is dominated
by mechanistic, cell-culture, and animal reports plus reviews from the research network that
developed the compound, and it includes work on related peptides.

**Publication count is not evidence weight.** Volume here reflects the productivity of one research
programme more than accumulated independent confirmation. A hundred laboratory papers from one
network do not add up to one controlled human trial, and a controlled human trial of this peptide
is exactly what could not be found.

---

## 7. Is any of the research independent?

Some is, and the honest answer is more nuanced than either side usually gives.

The critical version, that nearly every study comes from one Russian laboratory, is close to right
but overstated. Work exists without the originating investigator as an author: a 2025 in vitro
study from Brunel University London (PMID 40908429), a 2025 Italian in vitro retinal study
(PMID 40493162), and a 2022 mouse-oocyte study (PMID 35413689).

The promotional version, that findings have been independently replicated, is also wrong. Every
plausibly independent item retrieved is cell-culture or animal work. **No independent human
replication was found.** Some non-Russian publications, such as the 2020 stem cell study
(PMID 32019204), are collaborations that include the originating investigator and therefore are
not independent at all.

One further caution. The Brunel study, the clearest example of apparently independent work,
carries a published Correction (PMID 41240216). The PubMed record has no abstract, so what was
corrected could not be determined. Until somebody reads that correction in the publisher's full
text, this study should not be presented as clean independent confirmation.

The underlying issue is structural rather than personal: for this compound, the developer, the
primary investigator, and much of the review literature come from the same network. That does not
make the findings wrong. It does mean the usual reassurance that comes from independent
confirmation is not available here, and you should be told that rather than left to work it out
from author lists.

## 8. Is it legal? Is it banned in sport?

The accurate answer to the first question is jurisdiction-specific and dated, and the accurate
answer to the second is that **this Guide does not know**.

**United States, checked 2026-07-21.** It is not an FDA-approved drug. As of the retrieval date, a
Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee meeting was scheduled for 23 to 24 July 2026 to consider
several peptides including Epitalon for the 503A bulk drug substances list.

**That regulatory detail could not be verified.** Every attempt to read FDA's own pages returned
HTTP 404, including the meeting calendar page, the 503A page, and two briefing documents. FDA's
own wording was never read, so nothing here is attributed to FDA as its words, and this section is
flagged for human verification before publication. The meeting dates also fall after this Guide's
check date, so no outcome exists here and none is stated. A committee recommendation, when it
comes, is advisory and is not a final FDA determination.
(https://www.fda.gov/advisory-committees/advisory-committee-calendar/july-23-24-2026-meeting-pharmacy-compounding-advisory-committee-07232026,
unverified 2026-07-21)

**Anti-doping, checked 2026-07-21. No status is stated here.** No World Anti-Doping Agency document
could be retrieved, and a fetch of wada-ama.org returned no usable content. Searches surfaced
plenty of pages asserting a classification, but every one was a vendor, supplement-marketing, or
SEO page, and commercial pages are never acceptable regulatory evidence. So this Guide records no
anti-doping status at all. **If you compete in a tested sport, ask your own anti-doping authority
directly.** Do not treat the absence of a statement here as permission.
(https://www.wada-ama.org/en/prohibited-list, not retrieved 2026-07-21)

No regulatory position from the European Union, the United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, Russia, or
anywhere else was retrieved. Absence there means not checked, not permitted.

"Not approved" is a different question from "illegal to possess", and the second is a legal
question specific to where you live. Xenios does not give legal advice.

## 9. Can I get it from Xenios?

No. Xenios does not sell this compound, does not supply it, and does not tell members where to
obtain it or how to use it.

This Guide exists for a different reason. Members encounter Epithalon in podcasts, forums, and
vendor marketing, where laboratory results and results belonging to a different substance are
routinely presented as established human facts. The purpose here is an accurate account of what is
and is not known, including the parts that are unflattering to the compound.

## 10. If I bought some, would I know what was in it?

No, and nothing in the research literature would help you find out.

**No analytical purity, identity, or content-verification data for marketed material was
retrieved.** Nobody appears to have systematically tested what is in material sold under these
names. That is an absence of information, not a favourable result.

The naming problem compounds it. A product might use any of Epithalon, Epitalon, or Epithalone,
and the similar name Epithalamin belongs to a different substance entirely, a bovine pineal
extract. A single name on a label does not establish what is inside.

The full list of quality documentation that would have to exist before any material could be taken
seriously is in QUALITY_AND_DOCUMENTATION.md. Every item on it is currently unmet.

## 11. What would change your mind?

A specific list, so that this position can be tested rather than simply asserted.

- **A registered, completed, randomized controlled human trial of the AEDG tetrapeptide.** With a
  control group, blinding, a pre-specified outcome, and published results. Today there is not even
  a registration under either spelling.
- **A controlled human study that actually measured telomere length before and after.** The
  telomere claim is the whole basis of interest and no retrieved human study measured it.
- **Independent human replication.** Not from the originating network, and not cell culture.
- **Published human safety and pharmacokinetic data**, with systematic adverse event collection
  across a defined observation period.
- **Genotoxicity and carcinogenicity assessment.** The 2025 review says these are needed and were
  not available, and the compound's own proposed mechanism is why they matter.
- **The Correction to the Brunel study, read in full**, so that the clearest apparently independent
  result can be evaluated as published.
- **Independent analytical testing of commercial material**, for identity, purity, and content,
  published by a party with no commercial stake.
- **Primary regulatory records, read directly.** Both the FDA position and the WADA position are
  currently unverified in this Guide because the primary pages could not be read.

If those appeared, this Guide would be updated, the change would be recorded in its correction
history with a date, and the claim grades would move. Until then the grades stay at D for
preclinical findings and G for anything human.

## 12. What should I do with all this?

Nothing in this Guide is a recommendation, in either direction. It is an account of the evidence.

If this compound came up because something is genuinely bothering you, or because aging itself is
on your mind, the more useful conversation is about that with a clinician who knows your history.
The Guide includes a list of questions written to be taken into that conversation as they stand.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides. This
FAQ is educational. It is not medical advice, it is not a recommendation, and it is not an offer to
supply anything. Xenios does not sell this compound.
