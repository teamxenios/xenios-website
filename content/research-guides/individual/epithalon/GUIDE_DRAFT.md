# Epithalon (also spelled Epitalon)

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## One-minute summary

Epithalon is a synthetic peptide made of four amino acids. It is promoted online as an
anti-aging compound that lengthens telomeres, the protective caps on the ends of chromosomes.
The honest position, and the most important thing in this Guide, is that **there is no controlled
human evidence for this compound, and a large part of what circulates as its human evidence is
about a different substance entirely.**

- Direct queries of ClinicalTrials.gov, the main United States registry of clinical studies,
  returned **zero registered studies** under the spelling "epitalon" and **zero** under the
  spelling "epithalon". Both spellings were checked deliberately, because the same authors use
  them interchangeably (verified 2026-07-21).
- **No randomized, blinded, placebo-controlled human trial of this peptide was located.**
- Only one retrieved human report studied the peptide itself: a 2002 non-randomized report in
  patients with a degenerative retinal condition, from the institute that developed the compound,
  reporting a positive effect in 90 percent of cases. It describes no randomization, no blinding,
  and no control group composition, and it reports a responder percentage rather than a comparison
  between groups.
- **The famous mortality findings are about a different substance.** A widely cited report of
  reduced mortality in 266 elderly people studied Epithalamin, a bovine pineal gland extract, and
  Thymalin. It did not establish that the synthetic four-amino-acid peptide was given. The peptide
  was designed from the amino acid composition of that extract, and the two are constantly
  conflated in commercial writing. Treating the extract's results as this peptide's results is a
  factual error.
- There is a real preclinical literature, meaning work in animals and in cultured cells rather
  than in people. In human cell lines studied in the laboratory, one 2025 group reported telomere
  lengthening. That study carries a published correction whose content could not be determined,
  so it should not yet be treated as clean independent confirmation.
- **The mechanism offered as the benefit is the same mechanism that raises the safety question.**
  Telomere extension is the claimed benefit. The strongest retrieved laboratory study also reports
  telomere lengthening in human cancer cell lines. A 2025 peer-reviewed review states that safety
  information for this peptide is missing and that toxicity, genotoxicity, and carcinogenicity
  assessment are needed. That assessment was not found.
- **On safety, the correct statement is not that this compound is safe. It is that nothing is
  established.** There is no human safety dataset, no human pharmacokinetic characterisation (how
  the body absorbs, distributes, and clears a substance), and no immunogenicity assessment
  (whether the body mounts an immune response against it).
- The literature originates overwhelmingly from one research programme, the group that developed
  the compound and that also wrote much of the review literature about it. A search returning 142
  publications sounds like a deep evidence base. It is mostly laboratory and animal work from one
  network, and volume is not the same thing as confirmation.
- **Regulatory statements in this draft could not be verified.** Every attempt to read FDA's own
  pages failed, and no World Anti-Doping Agency document could be retrieved. Those sections are
  flagged for human verification and must not be published as they stand.

Xenios does not sell this compound, does not supply it, and does not tell members how to obtain
or use it. This Guide exists so that a member reading about Epithalon elsewhere has an accurate
account of what is and is not known.

**Overall claim grade for "Epithalon extends human lifespan or slows human aging": G (Unverified).**
**Overall claim grade for "Epithalon lengthens telomeres in a person": G (Unverified).**
**Overall claim grade for "Epitalon lengthened telomeres in human cell lines in the laboratory": D (Preclinical).**

## What it is

Epithalon is a synthetic tetrapeptide, meaning a chain of four amino acids made in a laboratory
rather than extracted from a living source. Its sequence is alanine, glutamic acid, aspartic acid,
glycine, written Ala-Glu-Asp-Gly and abbreviated **AEDG**.

**It appears in the literature under more than one name.** Epithalon, Epitalon, and Epithalone all
refer to the same AEDG tetrapeptide. The same authors use the spellings interchangeably, so a
search using only one of them retrieves an incomplete picture. This Guide deliberately does not
designate one spelling as the correct scientific label. That is a decision for a reviewer, and
whichever is chosen for display, both must remain searchable.

**The identity distinction that matters most.** Epithalon and Epitalon, the synthetic
four-amino-acid peptide, are **not** the same substance as **Epithalamin**, also written
Epithalamine, which is a bovine pineal gland extract. The synthetic peptide was designed based on
the amino acid composition of that extract (Araj et al. 2025, Int J Mol Sci 26(6):2691,
PMID 40141333).

This is not a pedantic point. Much of the human-outcome literature popularly attributed to
Epithalon was conducted with the Epithalamin extract, and the two are frequently conflated in
secondary and commercial writing. An extract is a mixture derived from tissue. A synthetic
tetrapeptide is one defined molecule. Results from one do not transfer to the other without a
study that bridges them, and no such bridging study was retrieved.

A further characterisation point: the 2025 review states that physico-chemical and structural
investigation of this peptide remains **quite limited**. Basic characterisation is thinner than
the publication count suggests.

## Why interest exists

The interest is easy to describe and worth understanding without endorsing it.

The pineal gland produces melatonin and is associated in the research literature with the
regulation of daily and seasonal rhythms. A research programme in St. Petersburg, Russia,
developed a bovine pineal extract, Epithalamin, and then a synthetic peptide based on its amino
acid composition, and studied both as candidate geroprotectors, meaning compounds proposed to
slow aspects of aging.

Three things drive the enthusiasm that followed.

**First, telomeres are an intuitive story.** Telomeres are the protective caps at the ends of
chromosomes, and they shorten as cells divide. A compound described as lengthening them maps
neatly onto a popular account of aging, and that account is easy to sell.

**Second, the compound has a long publication trail.** A PubMed search across both spellings
returned 142 records at retrieval. A number like that reads as a well-established evidence base.

**Third, there are dramatic human-sounding numbers attached to it**, in particular reported
reductions in mortality among elderly people over multi-year observation.

Three things should be held alongside that interest.

The publication count reflects the productivity of one research programme more than accumulated
independent confirmation. The dramatic mortality numbers come from work on the **extract**, not
on the synthetic peptide. And the telomere story, examined closely, does not simplify into good
news, because extending telomeres is also the reason to ask for carcinogenicity data.

An anti-aging rationale is not an outcome. Interest describes why researchers thought a compound
was worth studying. It does not describe what happens when it is given to people in a controlled
study, because as far as any retrievable record shows, that study has not been done.

## Terminology

Every technical term used in this Guide, in plain English.

- **Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids, the building blocks of proteins. Shorter than a
  protein.
- **Tetrapeptide.** A peptide made of exactly four amino acids. Epithalon is one.
- **Amino acid.** A small molecule that links with others to form peptides and proteins. Alanine,
  glutamic acid, aspartic acid, and glycine are the four here.
- **AEDG.** The single-letter shorthand for that four amino acid sequence, Ala-Glu-Asp-Gly.
- **Epithalon, Epitalon, Epithalone.** Three names in the literature for the same AEDG
  tetrapeptide.
- **Epithalamin (Epithalamine).** A bovine pineal gland extract. A **different substance** from
  the synthetic peptide, which was designed from its amino acid composition.
- **Extract.** A mixture of substances drawn out of biological tissue, as opposed to a single
  defined molecule made by synthesis.
- **Pineal gland.** A small gland in the brain that produces melatonin and is associated with the
  regulation of daily rhythms.
- **Telomere.** The protective cap at the end of a chromosome. Telomeres shorten as cells divide.
- **Telomerase.** An enzyme that can add length back to telomeres.
- **hTERT.** The human gene encoding a core component of telomerase. Upregulation means the gene
  is switched on more strongly.
- **ALT (alternative lengthening of telomeres).** A telomerase-independent route by which some
  cells, notably many cancer cells, extend their telomeres.
- **Geroprotector.** A term used in this literature for a compound proposed to slow aspects of
  aging.
- **Preclinical.** Research done before any human study, meaning in animals or in cultured cells.
- **In vitro.** Literally "in glass". Research in cells or tissue in a dish, outside a living body.
- **In vivo.** Research in a living organism.
- **Cell line.** Cells grown indefinitely in a laboratory. Human-derived cell lines are still cells
  in a dish, not people.
- **Fibroblast.** A common cell type in connective tissue, often used in laboratory studies.
- **Mesenchymal stem cell.** A cell type able to develop into several different tissue types,
  studied in the laboratory for that reason.
- **Retinal pigment epithelium.** A cell layer at the back of the eye. Studies of these cells in a
  dish are cell-culture studies, not studies in people.
- **Oocyte.** An egg cell.
- **Randomized.** Participants assigned to groups by chance, so that the groups are comparable.
- **Blinded.** Participants, or investigators, or both, do not know who received what.
- **Placebo-controlled.** Compared against an inactive treatment.
- **Allocation concealment.** Preventing the person enrolling participants from knowing which
  group the next participant will receive.
- **Non-randomized.** Groups formed some other way, which allows differences between groups to
  explain the result instead of the treatment.
- **Responder percentage.** The proportion of treated people described as improving. Without a
  comparison group it does not show that the treatment caused the improvement.
- **Adverse event ascertainment.** The system a study uses to look for and record harms. Without
  one, "no side effects reported" mostly means nobody was systematically asking.
- **Pharmacokinetics.** What the body does to a substance: absorption, distribution, metabolism,
  and elimination, including how long it lasts.
- **Bioavailability.** How much of a substance actually reaches the circulation.
- **Immunogenicity.** Whether a substance provokes an immune response against itself, such as
  antibody formation.
- **Genotoxicity.** The capacity to damage genetic material.
- **Carcinogenicity.** The capacity to cause cancer.
- **Active pharmaceutical ingredient.** The substance in a medicine responsible for its effect,
  held to defined standards.
- **Retinitis pigmentosa.** A group of inherited conditions causing progressive degeneration of the
  retina.
- **Systematic review and meta-analysis.** Structured methods for appraising and combining all
  studies on a question. None specific to this peptide was located.
- **503A bulk drug substances list.** A United States framework governing which raw substances
  pharmacies may use in compounding, meaning preparing customised medicines.
- **Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee.** The FDA advisory committee that reviews substances
  nominated for that list. Its recommendations advise FDA and are not final FDA decisions.
- **WADA.** The World Anti-Doping Agency, which publishes the Prohibited List used in tested sport.
- **Certificate of analysis.** A document from a testing laboratory stating what a specific batch
  of material was found to contain.

## Proposed mechanism

Everything in this section is **proposed**, and all of it derives from cultured cells. No mechanism
has been confirmed in a living human being. Claim grade for this entire section: **D
(Preclinical)**, with the human in vivo question graded **G (Unverified)**.

**Telomerase and hTERT upregulation in normal human cells in the laboratory.** In human cell lines
studied in vitro, including normal human fibroblasts and human mammary epithelial cells, the
authors of a 2025 study report telomere length extension via hTERT and telomerase upregulation
(PMID 40908429, Biogerontology 2025). These are cells in a dish. The tissue is human-derived,
which is not the same as a finding in a person. Grade D.

**Telomere lengthening in human cancer cell lines in the laboratory, by a different route.** In
the same 2025 study, in the human breast cancer cell lines 21NT and BT474 studied in vitro, the
authors report telomere lengthening via alternative lengthening of telomeres, a
telomerase-independent route, which they report was not prominent in the normal cells
(PMID 40908429). **This is the mechanism finding and the safety concern in one sentence.** It is
covered again under safety categories, and neither passage may appear without the other.

**Important qualification on that study.** A formal Correction was published (Biogerontology
2025;27(1):1, PMID 41240216, DOI 10.1007/s10522-025-10326-8). The PubMed record carries no
abstract, so **what was corrected could not be determined** and must be read in the publisher full
text before this study is cited. Until then it should not be presented as clean independent
confirmation. [UNVERIFIED - requires human source check against the publisher full text]

**A proposed epigenetic mechanism, in cultured human stem cells.** In human gingival mesenchymal
stem cells studied in vitro, the AEDG peptide increased mRNA expression of the neurogenic
differentiation markers Nestin, GAP43, beta-Tubulin III, and Doublecortin by 1.6 to 1.8 times, and
the authors propose an epigenetic mechanism involving peptide interaction with histone proteins
(PMID 32019204, Molecules 2020). Authorship includes the originating investigator alongside
Italian collaborators, so this is a collaboration rather than independent work. Grade D.

**What is not known.** The 2025 review states that whether the documented mechanisms are the sole
mechanisms of action is uncertain, and that physico-chemical and structural investigation of the
peptide remains quite limited (PMID 40141333). That is the most honest available summary of the
mechanism question.

**The gap that matters most.** Whether the AEDG tetrapeptide produces any measurable telomere or
telomerase effect in a living adult human is unknown. No retrieved human study measured it.
Activity in cultured cells does not establish activity in adult human tissue in the body. This is
the largest inferential leap in the popular presentation of this compound. Grade G.

**Models used across the mechanistic literature retrieved:** human cell lines in vitro (cancer
lines, fibroblasts, mammary epithelial cells, gingival mesenchymal stem cells), with PC3 and U2OS
cells as positive controls. **No human in vivo mechanism data was located.**

## Human evidence

**There is no controlled human evidence.** Two human records exist in the retrieved set. One
studied the peptide in a design that cannot support a causal claim. The other studied a different
substance.

| Study | Design | Population | Substance actually studied | Outcome as reported |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| PMID 12195242, Neuro Endocrinol Lett 2002 | Described by PubMed as a clinical trial. The abstract reports no randomization, no blinding, no allocation concealment, and no control group composition. Combines an animal component and a human component in one short report. | Patients with degenerative retinal lesions (retinitis pigmentosa), St. Petersburg Institute of Bioregulation and Gerontology, Russia. The abstract does not state the enrolled number. | The synthetic tetrapeptide | A positive clinical effect reported in 90 percent of cases. A 2025 secondary review describes this work as a series of 162 patients aged 18 to 72 compared against conventional methods, non-randomized, reporting improved visual acuity and expanded visual fields. |
| PMID 14523363, Neuro Endocrinol Lett 2003 | Non-randomized clinical observation over 6 to 8 years, with active administration in the first 2 to 3 years. No randomization, blinding, or control-group methodology described in the retrieved record. | 266 elderly and older persons, Russia and Ukraine. | **Epithalamin (extract) and Thymalin, NOT the synthetic tetrapeptide** | Reported mortality reductions versus controls: 2.0 to 2.1-fold with Thymalin, 1.6 to 1.8-fold with Epithalamin, and 4.1-fold with combined repeated treatment over 6 years. |

**What was searched, and what came back:**

- **ClinicalTrials.gov**, queried directly through its public API for the term "epitalon", returned
  an **empty result set**. Zero registered studies (verified 2026-07-21).
- **ClinicalTrials.gov**, queried separately and deliberately for the term "epithalon", also
  returned an **empty result set**. Zero registered studies (verified 2026-07-21). Both spellings
  were checked because a search on one alone under-retrieves.
- **No randomized, blinded, placebo-controlled human trial of the AEDG tetrapeptide was retrieved
  under either spelling.**
- A **PubMed** search for "epitalon OR epithalon" returned 142 records at retrieval. That total is
  dominated by mechanistic, cell-culture, and animal reports plus reviews from the originating
  research network, and includes work on related peptides. It is a map of the literature, not a
  body of clinical results.
- **No independent human replication** of any finding was located. Everything retrieved that is
  plausibly independent of the originating programme is cell-culture or animal work.

**Reading the retinitis pigmentosa report honestly.** It is non-randomized, describes no blinding
or placebo, is single-institution, and was conducted by the group that developed the compound. Its
outcome is framed as a responder percentage rather than as a between-group effect estimate with a
measure of spread. No independent replication of this human result was found. It is early human
evidence in the narrow sense that a human report exists. It is not evidence that the treatment
caused the reported improvement. Grade C, and that grade describes the existence of the report
rather than the reality of the effect.

**Reading the mortality report honestly.** The substances studied were Thymalin and Epithalamin
preparations. The retrieved record does **not** establish that the synthetic AEDG tetrapeptide was
administered. It appears in this Guide only because it is routinely and incorrectly cited as human
evidence for Epithalon and Epitalon. Beyond the substance-identity problem, it is non-randomized
with no blinding described, and mortality effects of this magnitude are extraordinary claims that
would require independent pre-registered replication. All authorship traces to the originating
institutes. It is hypothesis-generating at most, and **it must not be presented as human evidence
for this peptide.** Grade D.

**There is no human pharmacokinetic characterisation, no human immunogenicity assessment, and no
human safety dataset for this compound.**

Claim grade for any statement of the form "Epithalon does X in people": **G (Unverified)**. No
claim in this Guide reaches grade A or B.

## Preclinical evidence

This is where most of the literature lives. Every finding below carries its species or model in
the same sentence. None of it establishes an effect in a person.

**Human cell lines in vitro, 2025, Brunel University London.** In human cell lines studied in a
dish, including the breast cancer lines 21NT and BT474, normal human fibroblasts (IBR.3), and
human mammary epithelial cells (HMEC), with PC3 and U2OS cells as positive controls, the authors
report telomere length extension in the normal cells via hTERT and telomerase upregulation, and
telomere lengthening in the cancer cell lines via alternative lengthening of telomeres, which they
report was not prominent in the normal cells (PMID 40908429, Biogerontology 2025). The work is
from the Centre for Genome Engineering and Maintenance, Brunel University London, United Kingdom,
with the Royal Brompton Hospital, London. This is the clearest retrieved example of work on this
peptide published by a group outside the originating Russian institute. **It carries a published
Correction (PMID 41240216) whose content could not be determined, so it must not yet be treated as
clean independent confirmation.** Grade D.

**Human gingival mesenchymal stem cells in vitro, 2020.** In these cultured human-derived stem
cells, the AEDG peptide increased mRNA expression of the neurogenic differentiation markers
Nestin, GAP43, beta-Tubulin III, and Doublecortin by 1.6 to 1.8 times, with the authors proposing
an epigenetic mechanism involving peptide interaction with histone proteins (PMID 32019204,
Molecules 2020). Authorship includes the originating investigator with Italian collaborators, so
this is a collaboration rather than independent work. Grade D.

**Mouse oocytes in vitro, 2022.** A record titled to report that Epitalon protects against
post-ovulatory aging-related damage of mouse oocytes in vitro (PMID 35413689, Aging (Albany NY)
2022). **Only the title-level record was retrieved.** Effect sizes, controls, and mechanism are
not verified. It appears to be from a group separate from the originating institute, which is why
it matters for the independence question, and that too needs confirming. Grade G until the
abstract is read.

**Human retinal pigment epithelium cells in vitro, 2025.** A record titled to report that the
tetrapeptide Epitalon enhanced delayed wound healing in an in vitro model of diabetic retinopathy
using human retinal pigment epithelium cells (PMID 40493162, Stem Cell Rev Rep 2025). **This is
cell culture, not a study in human participants**, and it is flagged because a naive search
classifies it as a human study. **Only the title-level record was retrieved.** Grade G until the
abstract is read.

**Transgenic HER-2/neu mice, 2002.** A record titled to report that Epithalon decelerates aging
and suppresses development of breast adenocarcinomas in transgenic HER-2/neu mice (PMID 12459848,
Bull Exp Biol Med 2002). **Only the title-level record was retrieved.** Effect sizes, group sizes,
and controls are not verified. Authorship is from the originating Russian research programme.
Note the tension with the cancer cell line finding above: a tumour-suppression report in one mouse
strain does not answer a telomere-lengthening concern raised in human cancer cell lines, and
nothing retrieved resolves the two. Grade G until the abstract is read.

**The model inventory, from a 2025 peer-reviewed review.** The review catalogues the models used
across this literature: cell culture including HeLa cells, human fibroblasts, neurons,
macrophages, oocytes, and stem cells; animals including the fruit fly Drosophila melanogaster,
mice of the SAMP-1, CBA, and HER-2/neu strains, rats of the Wistar, Campbell, and LIO strains, and
rhesus monkeys; the plant Nicotiana tabacum; rabbit tissue; and Xenopus laevis pluripotent cells
(PMID 40141333).

**This is a model-inventory finding, not an efficacy finding.** The preclinical base is broad in
species range, but it is summarized second-hand through a review rather than verified study by
study for this Guide. Breadth of species is a description of where work has been done. It is not a
tally of positive results, and it must never be presented as one. Grade D for the inventory
itself, and no grade at all for any specific result inside it.

**A general caution about preclinical anti-aging research.** A compound that changes a marker in
cultured cells, or extends a measure in a fly or a mouse, has cleared a very low bar relative to
changing an outcome in a person. The history of geroprotection research is largely a history of
results that did not carry across. A preclinical result is a reason to run a controlled human
trial. It is not a substitute for one, and for this compound no such trial was retrieved.

## Contradictions and disagreement

Where sources genuinely disagree, both positions are stated, with an assessment of which the
retrieved evidence supports. Fuller treatment is in CONTRADICTIONS.md.

**1. Whether meaningful human evidence exists at all.**

- *Position A.* Commercial and secondary sources present a substantial human record, typically
  pointing to the retinitis pigmentosa series and to multi-year elderly mortality reductions.
- *Position B.* Direct registry checks found no ClinicalTrials.gov registration under either
  spelling. The retinitis pigmentosa work is a non-randomized single-institution report by the
  developing group. The mortality studies used the Epithalamin extract and Thymalin, not the
  synthetic tetrapeptide.
- *Assessment.* Position B is what the retrieved primary records support. The apparent human
  evidence base is thinner than the citation count suggests, and part of it is about a different
  substance. No randomized, blinded, placebo-controlled human trial of the AEDG tetrapeptide was
  retrieved.

**2. Independent replication outside the originating Russian programme.**

- *Position A.* Critics state that nearly every study comes from one Russian laboratory and that
  independent replication is absent.
- *Position B.* At least one 2025 in vitro study from Brunel University London (PMID 40908429) and
  one 2025 Italian in vitro study (PMID 40493162) exist without the originating investigator as an
  author, and a 2022 mouse-oocyte study (PMID 35413689) appears to be from a separate group.
- *Assessment.* Both are partly right and the distinction matters. Independent work does exist,
  but everything retrieved that is plausibly independent is cell-culture or animal work, and one
  of those papers carries an unexplained published correction. Some non-Russian publications
  (PMID 32019204) are collaborations including the originating investigator and are therefore not
  independent. **No independent human replication was found.**

**3. Telomerase activation as a benefit versus as a risk.**

- *Position A.* Telomerase and hTERT upregulation, and telomere lengthening, are presented as the
  mechanism of geroprotective benefit.
- *Position B.* The same mechanism is a recognised oncological concern, and the strongest
  retrieved in vitro paper reports telomere lengthening in human cancer cell lines via alternative
  lengthening of telomeres.
- *Assessment.* The mechanism claimed as the benefit is the same mechanism generating the safety
  question. This Guide does not present the mechanistic story as favourable, because doing so
  would require dropping half of what the same paper reports. No human carcinogenicity data were
  retrieved either way.

**4. In vitro telomerase activity versus in vivo human effect.**

- *Position A.* Cell-culture telomerase and telomere findings are cited as showing the compound
  works.
- *Position B.* Activity in cultured cells does not establish activity in adult human tissue in
  the body, and no retrieved human study measured telomere length before and after in a controlled
  design.
- *Assessment.* The gap between the dish and the person is unbridged in the retrieved record. This
  is the largest inferential leap in the popular presentation of this compound.

**5. Name-versus-substance confusion.** Not a disagreement between researchers, but the most
consequential error in the popular literature. The synthetic tetrapeptide was designed from the
amino acid composition of the bovine pineal extract Epithalamin. The mortality claims describe the
extract. Any Guide citing those mortality numbers as Epithalon or Epitalon human evidence is
making a substance-identity error.

## Limitations

Limitations of the evidence base itself:

- There is no randomized, blinded, placebo-controlled human trial, so there is no human effect
  size, no controlled responder rate, and no reliable estimate of benefit or harm in people.
- The one human study of the peptide itself is non-randomized, unblinded as far as the record
  shows, single-institution, and conducted by the group that developed the compound.
- The most cited human numbers describe a different substance, a bovine pineal extract, and no
  bridging study connects them to the synthetic peptide.
- The literature originates overwhelmingly from Vladimir Khavinson and colleagues at the
  St. Petersburg Institute of Bioregulation and Gerontology, which developed the compound.
  Developer, primary investigator, and reviewer are substantially the same network. This does not
  make the findings wrong, and it does mean the usual reassurance provided by independent
  confirmation is not available.
- Much of the older primary literature sits in a small number of journals closely associated with
  the originating programme, including Neuro Endocrinology Letters, Bulletin of Experimental
  Biology and Medicine, and Advances in Gerontology.
- Methods reporting in the retrieved abstracts is thin. Randomization, blinding, allocation, and
  adverse-event ascertainment are largely unstated.
- The one clearly non-originating-group human-cell study carries a published Correction whose
  content is unknown.
- No systematic review or meta-analysis specific to the AEDG tetrapeptide was retrieved.
- Physico-chemical and structural investigation of the peptide is described in a 2025 review as
  quite limited.

Limitations of this Guide's research process, stated so a reader can judge its reliability:

- **Every fda.gov retrieval failed with HTTP 404**, including the advisory committee meeting
  calendar page, the 503A bulk drug substances page, and both briefing PDFs. No FDA wording was
  read. Every FDA statement in this Guide is marked unverified.
- **wada-ama.org returned no usable content.** No anti-doping status is stated anywhere in this
  Guide. The only sources asserting a classification were vendor, supplement-marketing, and SEO
  pages, which are never acceptable evidence.
- Three preclinical records (PMID 35413689, PMID 40493162, PMID 12459848) are **title-level only**.
  Their abstracts, group sizes, controls, and effect sizes were not verified.
- A PubMed fetch for PMID 22451889, a 15-year follow-up peptide geroprotector paper, was blocked
  by a reCAPTCHA interstitial, so that record is not characterised here. Which substance it studied
  is unknown.
- The Alzheimer's Drug Discovery Foundation Cognitive Vitality report on Epithalamin and Epithalon
  was fetched but its PDF text did not extract. It is likely the best available independent
  critical appraisal and has been flagged for manual reading.
- **Russian-language primary literature was not retrieved or read in the original.** Since that is
  where the evidence base originates, full appraisal requires someone who can read those primary
  reports. Abstract-level English indexing is not sufficient.
- Only ClinicalTrials.gov was searched. The EU Clinical Trials Register, ISRCTN, the WHO ICTRP,
  the Russian national registry, and the Japanese and Chinese registries were not searched. Given
  where this literature originates, the Russian registry is the most material omission.
- No analytical or third-party purity, identity, or content-verification data for marketed material
  was retrieved.
- A study of 75 women on circadian rhythm and melatonin metabolites is described only inside the
  2025 review. Its primary citation was not retrieved and its design, controls, and provenance are
  unverified, so it is deliberately not cited in this Guide.

**A note on retrieval integrity.** An adversarial control was run during research. A deliberately
fabricated identifier, PMID 99999999, was requested and returned HTTP 404 Not Found, and both
ClinicalTrials.gov queries returned genuinely empty result sets rather than invented records. That
is what allows the two empty registry results to be reported as findings rather than as failures.
Every identifier in this Guide was returned by an actual retrieval, and failed fetches are marked
as failures rather than filled in from memory.

## Quality and identity

This section is about the material itself, separately from the science. It matters because an
unapproved substance is, by definition, obtained outside a regulated supply chain.

**Product identity in the consumer market is unverifiable from the research literature.** Retailer
and forum pages surfaced heavily in every search for this compound and are recorded as market
claims only, never as evidence. **No analytical purity, identity, or content-verification data for
marketed material was retrieved.** That is an absence of information, not a favourable result.
Nobody appears to have systematically checked what is in material sold under these names.

**Three names are a practical identity risk, not only a search problem.** Epithalon, Epitalon, and
Epithalone all refer to the AEDG tetrapeptide, and a product may use any of them. More seriously,
**Epithalamin and Epithalamine name a different substance**, a bovine pineal gland extract, and the
names are similar enough that conflation is easy in both directions. A single name on a label does
not establish which material is inside.

**Publication volume is not a quality signal either.** A PubMed search across both spellings
returned 142 records. That count says nothing about what is in any given vial, and it says less
than it appears to about the science, since the total is dominated by laboratory and animal work

## Safety categories

Nothing in this section is reassurance. These are categories of concern that remain open because
no one has closed them.

**1. Human safety data are absent rather than reassuring. This is the primary safety finding.** A
2025 peer-reviewed review states that information regarding critical issues about this peptide's
safety is missing, and recommends additional short-term and long-term toxicity study including
genotoxicity and carcinogenicity assessment before it could be considered as an active
pharmaceutical ingredient (PMID 40141333). **Absence of reported adverse events in small
non-randomized series is not evidence of safety.**

**2. A theoretical oncological concern arising from the compound's own proposed mechanism.** The
claimed mechanism is telomerase and hTERT upregulation, and the strongest retrieved in vitro work
reports telomere lengthening in human cancer cell lines via alternative lengthening of telomeres
as well as telomerase upregulation in normal cells (PMID 40908429). A mechanism that extends
telomeres in cancer cell lines is a coherent reason to want carcinogenicity data before human use,
and that data was not found. **This is a mechanism-based concern, not a demonstrated human harm.**
It should not be softened into nothing, and it should not be inflated into a claim that this
compound causes cancer in people. Nothing retrieved establishes either.

A title-level 2002 report describes suppression of breast adenocarcinoma development in transgenic
HER-2/neu mice, which reads in the opposite direction. That record is title-level only, its effect
sizes and controls are unverified, and a mouse tumour model does not settle a question raised in
human cancer cell lines. Both are stated here because nothing retrieved resolves them.

**3. Reported no-side-effect claims are weak by design.** The 2025 review reports that none of the
Epitalon-treated patients in the retinitis pigmentosa series reported side effects. That is
patient-reported data from a non-randomized study run by the originating institute without
systematic adverse event ascertainment. **It cannot support any blanket safety statement.** When a
study has no system for looking for harms, finding none is close to uninformative.

**4. Immunogenicity risk not assessed.** Search-result summaries of the July 2026 FDA advisory
committee materials attribute to FDA a concern that human safety data are insufficient including
unassessed immunogenicity risk. **FDA primary documents could not be retrieved. Every fda.gov
fetch returned HTTP 404**, so this basis is unverified and nothing here is attributed to FDA as
its words. [UNVERIFIED - requires human source check against FDA's own text] Independently of that
attribution, no primary source establishing human immunogenicity for this peptide was retrieved
either way.

**5. No characterised human pharmacokinetics.** Nothing on absorption, distribution, metabolism,
or elimination in humans was retrieved. Whether meaningful exposure is achieved at all is unknown.
Since the 2025 review states that whether the documented mechanisms are the sole mechanisms of
action is uncertain, downstream and off-target effects in a living human body cannot be predicted
from the existing literature.

**6. Product quality as a safety issue in its own right.** Separately from the molecule, material
obtained outside a regulated supply chain has unverified identity, purity, sterility, and endotoxin
status, and for this compound no analytical data of any kind was retrieved.

**7. Entirely unstudied populations.** Pregnancy, breastfeeding, children, older adults,
immunocompromised people, and people taking any medication. **People with a current or previous
cancer diagnosis belong on this list specifically**, because of the mechanism-based concern in
category 2. No data of any kind was located for any of these groups.

## Questions for a qualified professional

If a member wants to discuss this compound with a clinician, these are questions that produce a
useful conversation. They are written to be asked as they stand.

1. I have read that there are no registered clinical trials of this peptide under either of its two
   spellings, and no randomized controlled trial. Does that match what you can find, and does it
   change how you would think about it?
2. A lot of what I read about this compound turns out to describe a bovine pineal extract called
   Epithalamin rather than the synthetic peptide. How much weight would you put on results from one
   when someone is talking about the other?
3. The main human study I can find is non-randomized, from the institute that developed the
   compound, and reports that 90 percent of patients improved. How do you read a result like that?
4. The proposed mechanism is telomere lengthening, and the same laboratory study reports telomere
   lengthening in cancer cell lines. Given my personal and family history, how would you think
   about that?
5. I have the following conditions and take the following medications. Which of them would make an
   unstudied compound a particularly poor idea for me?
6. If someone took something with no human safety data at all, what would you want to monitor, and
   what signs would concern you?
7. This substance is not approved anywhere I could find, which means any material comes from
   outside a regulated supply chain, and no purity or identity testing of it appears to exist. How
   do you think about that for something introduced into the body?
8. I compete in a tested sport. Who is the right authority for me to ask about whether this is
   permitted, given that I could not find an official statement either way?
9. What is actually going on with the concern that brought me here, and is there an approved
   option with real human evidence that we have not tried yet?
10. If new human evidence on this peptide appears, what would you want to see in it before it
    changed your advice?

This Guide does not give medical advice and does not tell anyone what to do. These questions
belong with a clinician who knows your history.
