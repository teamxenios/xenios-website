# Gonadorelin

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## One-minute summary

Gonadorelin is unusual in this series. It is not a novel research peptide invented by the online
market. It is a synthetic copy of a hormone the human body already makes, and it genuinely held two
United States Food and Drug Administration (FDA) approvals as a human medicine. Both of those
approvals are now discontinued.

The honest evidence verdict has two halves, and they point in different directions.

- **There is real human clinical evidence for gonadorelin, but it is narrow and it is almost
  certainly not the evidence a member is looking for.** It comes from replacement therapy in people
  with a rare congenital condition in which the body's own version of this hormone is missing, given
  in a hospital by a delivery system designed to reproduce the body's natural pulsed release pattern,
  under specialist supervision. The best single study retrieved for this Guide is a 45-patient
  uncontrolled retrospective series (PMID 39210488, verified 2026-07-19).
- **For the use that actually drives current consumer interest, the human evidence is zero.** A
  PubMed search combining gonadorelin with testosterone therapy, TRT, or fertility preservation
  returned no records at all. A ClinicalTrials.gov query returned no studies with gonadorelin as a
  registered intervention. Not weak evidence. No evidence (verified 2026-07-19).

Everything else follows from those two facts.

- **There is no FDA-approved human gonadorelin product on the United States market.** FACTREL
  (NDA018123, approved 1982) and LUTREPULSE KIT (NDA019687, approved 1989) both show marketing status
  Discontinued in Drugs@FDA (checked 2026-07-19). Anything a consumer obtains today is compounded,
  imported, or sold as a non-drug material, and none of that has been reviewed by FDA for identity,
  potency, purity, sterility, or manufacturing quality.
- **The name "Factrel" today mostly means a cattle drug.** A gonadorelin product is actively marketed
  in the United States for animals (Zoetis, NADA 139-237, labelled for use in cattle only). A member
  searching the brand name will find current, official-looking FDA label material for livestock
  (checked 2026-07-19).
- **The compounding status is genuinely contested and is being misreported online.** Gonadorelin
  acetate does appear in Category 1 of FDA's 503B outsourcing-facility bulk substances list. It does
  **not** appear anywhere on the 503A pharmacy compounding list updated May 2026, despite industry
  and vendor-adjacent pages claiming that it does. This Guide deliberately asserts nothing about
  compounding legality in either direction, and flags it for human regulatory review.
- **Documented harms are sparse but real.** A published case report describes severe anaphylaxis
  (a life-threatening allergic reaction) in a child given gonadorelin acetate during a hospital
  diagnostic test. The 45-patient series reported allergic reactions, breast tissue development in
  men, and hardened lumps at the site where the material entered the body.
- **The absence of adverse event records is not reassurance.** FDA's post-market adverse event
  database returned zero records under the gonadorelin generic name. That reflects an absence of
  surveillance, because the human products were discontinued decades ago, not an absence of harm.
- **Gonadorelin is named on the World Anti-Doping Agency Prohibited List.** Any member subject to
  anti-doping testing faces sanction risk (checked 2026-07-19).

Xenios does not sell gonadorelin, does not supply it, and does not tell members how to obtain or use
it. This Guide exists so that a member reading about gonadorelin elsewhere has an accurate account of
what is and is not known.

**Claim grade for "gonadorelin preserves testicular function, fertility, or natural testosterone
production in men on testosterone therapy": G (Unverified). No human evidence of any kind was
located for this use.**

**Claim grade for "pump-delivered pulsed GnRH therapy was associated with hormonal response and
detectable sperm production in men with congenital hypogonadotropic hypogonadism": C (Early human
evidence). One uncontrolled retrospective series, no comparison group.**

**Claim grade for "gonadorelin is safe": PROHIBITED. This claim may not appear on any member-facing
surface.**

## What it is

Gonadorelin is a synthetic version of gonadotropin-releasing hormone, usually shortened to GnRH. GnRH
is a hormone the human hypothalamus (a control centre at the base of the brain) releases in order to
tell the pituitary gland (a small gland just beneath it) to produce two further hormones, luteinising
hormone (LH) and follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH). Those two hormones in turn act on the testes or
the ovaries.

Gonadorelin is a decapeptide, meaning a chain of ten amino acids, and its sequence is identical to
the hormone the body itself produces.
[UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]

It has appeared in pharmaceutical use as two different salts, which are two different chemical forms
of the same active molecule:

- **Gonadorelin hydrochloride**, marketed for human use in the United States as FACTREL.
- **Gonadorelin acetate**, marketed for human use in the United States as LUTREPULSE KIT. This is
  also the salt named on FDA's 503B bulk substances list.

Common synonyms in the literature are GnRH, LHRH, and LH-RH.

### What gonadorelin is not

Three separate confusions matter here, and each one can lead a reader to attach the wrong body of
evidence to this compound.

**1. It is not a GnRH agonist analogue.** Drugs such as leuprorelin, goserelin, triptorelin,
buserelin, nafarelin, histrelin, and deslorelin are chemically modified, long-acting versions of the
hormone. Because they act continuously rather than in pulses, they eventually shut the system down
rather than stimulating it. Gonadorelin is the native, short-acting sequence and is not one of these.
Their large clinical literature is not gonadorelin's literature.
[UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]

**2. It is not a GnRH antagonist.** Cetrorelix, ganirelix, degarelix, and elagolix are a different
pharmacological class that blocks the receptor rather than stimulating it.
[UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]

**3. "Factrel" today is mostly a cattle drug.** FACTREL (gonadorelin hydrochloride injection) is
currently marketed by Zoetis Inc. under NADA number 139-237 and is labelled "For use in cattle only",
for ovarian follicular cysts and oestrous synchronisation in dairy cows, beef cows, and replacement
heifers (checked 2026-07-19). Searching the brand name today overwhelmingly returns this livestock
label, not a human product. Any statement about "the Factrel label" must specify which one it means,
or it will mislead.

A practical consequence of the sequence identity and the two salt forms is that the single name
"gonadorelin" does not by itself pin down which material is in a given product.

## Why interest exists

The interest in gonadorelin is easy to describe, and it is worth describing accurately without
endorsing it.

The logic runs roughly like this. When a man takes testosterone from an outside source, the body's
own signalling chain from hypothalamus to pituitary to testes quietens down. Because gonadorelin is
the natural top of that chain, the appeal is the idea that supplying it might keep the chain active
and therefore keep the testes working. That is the reasoning behind gonadorelin appearing alongside
testosterone therapy in online discussion and in some commercial offerings.

Four further features drive the enthusiasm.

First, unlike most compounds in this series, gonadorelin really was an approved human medicine, twice.
That history gives it a legitimacy that a novel research peptide does not have, and it is often
presented as though the historical approvals validate the current popular use. They do not. FACTREL
was approved in 1982 and LUTREPULSE KIT in 1989, for different purposes entirely, and both are
discontinued.

Second, it is a copy of a hormone the body already makes, which invites the assumption that it must
therefore be gentle or physiological. That assumption is not evidence.

Third, there is a genuine and successful therapeutic use in a rare disease, described in the human
evidence section below. Its existence is real. Its relevance to a healthy adult is not established.

Fourth, gonadorelin acetate's presence on one FDA compounding list has been read by some commercial
sources as a signal of regulatory acceptance. As set out in the contradictions section, that reading
is contested and the picture is being misreported.

Interest is not evidence. Nothing in this section should be read as support for any use.

## Terminology

Every technical term used in this Guide, glossed in plain English.

- **Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids, the building blocks of proteins.
- **Decapeptide.** A peptide made of ten amino acids.
- **Hypothalamus.** A control centre at the base of the brain that governs several hormone systems.
- **Pituitary gland.** A small gland below the hypothalamus that releases hormones acting on other
  organs.
- **GnRH (gonadotropin-releasing hormone).** The hormone the hypothalamus uses to signal the
  pituitary. Also written LHRH or LH-RH.
- **LH (luteinising hormone) and FSH (follicle-stimulating hormone).** The two pituitary hormones
  released in response to GnRH. In men they act on the testes.
- **Endogenous.** Made by the body itself, as opposed to supplied from outside.
- **Exogenous.** Supplied from outside the body.
- **Agonist.** A substance that activates a receptor.
- **Antagonist.** A substance that blocks a receptor.
- **Analogue.** A chemically modified version of a molecule, similar but not identical.
- **Downregulation.** The response in which continuous stimulation causes a system to become less
  responsive and eventually shut down.
- **Pulsatile.** Released in bursts rather than continuously. The natural pattern of GnRH release.
- **Congenital hypogonadotropic hypogonadism (CHH).** A rare condition present from birth in which
  the signalling chain from brain to gonads does not work, so the body does not produce enough sex
  hormones. The specific relevance here is that the body's own GnRH pulse generator has failed.
- **Cryptorchidism.** A condition in which one or both testicles did not descend normally.
- **Spermatogenesis.** The production of sperm.
- **Gynecomastia.** Development of breast tissue in men.
- **Subcutaneous induration.** A hardened lump under the skin, typically where material entered the
  body.
- **Anaphylaxis.** A severe, rapid, potentially life-threatening allergic reaction affecting the
  whole body.
- **Retrospective study.** A study that looks backwards at records of what already happened, rather
  than planning and controlling the conditions in advance.
- **Uncontrolled study.** A study with no comparison group, so changes cannot be separated from what
  would have happened anyway.
- **Salt form.** A chemical variant of a drug substance, such as a hydrochloride or an acetate.
- **NDA (New Drug Application).** The application through which a human drug is approved in the
  United States.
- **NADA (New Animal Drug Application).** The equivalent for a veterinary drug.
- **Marketing status Discontinued.** FDA's designation meaning the approved product is no longer
  being marketed.
- **Compounding.** Preparation of a medicine by a pharmacy or a licensed facility rather than by a
  drug manufacturer under an approval.
- **Section 503A and section 503B.** Two different parts of United States federal law covering two
  different compounding pathways. 503A covers pharmacy compounding, 503B covers registered
  outsourcing facilities. They have separate lists of bulk substances and must not be conflated.
- **Bulk drug substance.** The raw active ingredient, as distinct from a finished product.
- **FAERS.** The FDA Adverse Event Reporting System, the United States database of post-market safety
  reports.
- **Non-Specified Substance (anti-doping).** A World Anti-Doping Agency classification. Substances in
  this group are treated more strictly in sanctioning.
- **Endotoxin.** A contaminant from bacterial cell walls that can cause fever and serious reactions
  even after the bacteria themselves are gone.

## Proposed mechanism

Everything in this section is **proposed**. It is a description of how the compound is said to work,
not a demonstration that it does work in the way described in the situations members ask about.

The uncontested part is the basic biology of the natural hormone. GnRH released by the hypothalamus
signals the pituitary to release LH and FSH, which then act on the testes or ovaries. Gonadorelin is
described as an identical synthetic copy of that hormone.
[UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]

The proposed extension, which is what drives current consumer interest, is that supplying gonadorelin
would therefore keep the pituitary producing LH and FSH, and keep the testes stimulated, in someone
whose own signalling has been quietened by outside testosterone. **No retrieved source establishes
this.** No human study of gonadorelin in this situation was located at all.

There is a specific and important complication that a member deserves to have stated plainly. The
natural hormone is released in **pulses**, and the therapeutic human evidence that does exist comes
from a hospital delivery system built specifically to reproduce that pulsed pattern in patients whose
own pulse generator has failed. Whether administration that does not reproduce that pattern produces
sustained stimulation, no effect, or the opposite effect of shutting the system down is **not
established by any source retrieved for this Guide**. This is not a minor technical caveat. It is the
central open question about whether the popular use makes pharmacological sense at all.
[UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]

The one directly relevant observation is a comparison. GnRH agonist analogues, which are long-acting
and act continuously rather than in pulses, are known to suppress the system after an initial surge.
That is why they are used clinically to shut hormone production down. This is background context
about a different class of drug and is not a finding about gonadorelin.
[UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]

**Claim grade for the proposed mechanism as applied to the popular consumer use: G (Unverified).**

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

## Human evidence

Human evidence for gonadorelin exists, which distinguishes it from most compounds in this series. It
is narrow, it is old in places, and it is almost entirely from a rare disease setting.

### The single best retrieved human study

| Item | Detail |
|---|---|
| Identifier | PMID 39210488 |
| Journal and year | Zhonghua Nan Ke Xue, 2024 May, volume 30, issue 5, pages 404 to 409. Published in Chinese. |
| Design | Single-centre retrospective observational analysis of clinical records. No control group, no randomisation, no blinding. |
| Population | 45 males with congenital hypogonadotropic hypogonadism treated at one hospital in Wuhan, China, between January 2013 and March 2023. Six of the 45 had cryptorchidism. |
| Follow-up | Mean 19.1 plus or minus 4.3 months. |
| Delivery | Pulsed GnRH therapy using gonadorelin, delivered by pump under specialist supervision. |
| Reported findings | Significant elevation of FSH, LH, and testosterone after treatment compared with baseline (P less than 0.01). Improvement in external genitalia and secondary sexual characteristics in all patients. Sperm detected in semen in 33 of 45 cases (73.3 percent). Eight patients achieved natural fertility and three achieved childbirth through assisted reproductive technology. The six cryptorchidism cases did not reach normal testosterone within the follow-up window. |
| Reported adverse events | Gynecomastia in 8 patients, subcutaneous induration in 6, allergic reaction to the agent in 3. |
| Verification | VERIFIED 2026-07-19. Abstract retrieved. Full text not reviewed. |

**How much this study can carry.** Less than it first appears, and the reasons are worth stating
individually.

- It is retrospective and uncontrolled. With no comparison group, the observed changes cannot be
  separated from natural history, from co-interventions, or from selection of who was treated.
- It is a single centre with 45 patients.
- The population is a rare congenital disease in which the body's own GnRH pulse generator has
  failed. This is the classic replacement-therapy setting. Giving a missing hormone back to someone
  who lacks it is a fundamentally different proposition from giving it to someone whose own system is
  intact.
- The therapy was delivered by pump in a specialist setting, reproducing a natural pulsed pattern.
- The publication is in Chinese and only the abstract was retrieved for this Guide.

**It does not generalise to healthy adults, to men on testosterone therapy, or to any wellness or
optimisation use.** That is not a stylistic hedge. It is the difference between replacing something
that is absent and adding something to a system that already has it.

**Claim grade for the findings within this population: C (Early human evidence).**
**Claim grade for any extension of these findings beyond congenital hypogonadotropic hypogonadism:
G (Unverified).**

### The use members actually ask about

For gonadorelin used alongside testosterone therapy, to preserve testicular function, fertility, or
natural testosterone production, the position is not "thin evidence". It is no evidence.

- A PubMed search for gonadorelin in the title or abstract combined with "testosterone therapy",
  "TRT", or "fertility preservation" returned **zero records** (verified 2026-07-19).
- A ClinicalTrials.gov intervention query for gonadorelin returned **no studies** with gonadorelin as
  a registered intervention. The query returned only trials of GnRH analogues, which are different
  molecules (verified 2026-07-19).

**Claim grade: G (Unverified).**

### The state of the wider human literature

- **No systematic review or meta-analysis of gonadorelin exists in PubMed.** A title-field search
  filtered to systematic reviews returned zero results (verified 2026-07-19).
- **Only four PubMed records match gonadorelin in the title with a clinical trial publication type.**
  On inspection, one is a cattle field trial from 2015, one is a 1987 Croatian paediatric
  cryptorchidism study whose abstract was not available in English, and two are French papers from
  1985 and 1986 that are actually about GnRH agonist analogues rather than gonadorelin itself
  (verified 2026-07-19).

Modern human trial literature on gonadorelin as such is effectively absent. Two peer-reviewed reviews
covering GnRH in hypogonadotropic hypogonadism were retrieved as background context (PMID 30698671
and PMID 33238870, verified 2026-07-19). Reviews are secondary literature and are not primary
evidence, and no specific finding is attributed to them in this Guide.

## Preclinical evidence

There is no meaningful modern preclinical programme supporting the popular human use of gonadorelin.
What the searchable literature contains instead is veterinary reproduction work, which is a finding
about the shape of the evidence base rather than a finding about humans.

**In Holstein dairy cattle (n = 3938), in a timed artificial insemination programme, the specific
gonadorelin salt used (hydrochloride versus diacetate tetrahydrate) had no effect on pregnancies per
insemination (38.4 plus or minus 1.2 versus 35.7 plus or minus 1.3)** (PMID 25979657, Theriogenology,
2015, verified 2026-07-19).

This is a livestock reproduction field trial. It is not a human-relevant preclinical model, and it is
included here for two reasons only. It is the single largest controlled trial with gonadorelin in its
title retrievable in PubMed, and it illustrates that the modern gonadorelin literature is dominated by
cattle reproduction rather than human research. **Nothing about cattle fertility transfers to a
person.**

**Claim grade for the cattle finding, within cattle: D (Preclinical).**
**Claim grade for any human inference drawn from it: PROHIBITED.**

No rodent, primate, or cultured-cell study relevant to the popular human use was located during
research for this Guide. That is recorded as a gap, not as a negative result.

## Contradictions and disagreement

Three genuine conflicts exist in the public record. All three are resolved here in favour of the
primary document, and the unresolved one is flagged rather than papered over.

### 1. Whether gonadorelin acetate is on FDA's 503A Category 1 list

**Position A.** Several secondary sources, including a compounding-industry blog and a telehealth
blog surfaced in search, state that gonadorelin acetate has been added to the 503A Category 1 list,
describing it as a peptide previously approved as Lutrepulse.

**Position B.** The primary FDA document, "Bulk Drug Substances Nominated for Use in Compounding
Under Section 503A of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act", updated May 14, 2026, does not
contain gonadorelin in Category 1, Category 2, or Category 3. The full text of all three categories
was extracted and searched (verified 2026-07-19).

**Assessment.** The primary FDA document governs. Gonadorelin is not on the 503A nominations list as
of the May 14, 2026 version. It **is** on the 503B Category 1 list (updated March 21, 2025), flagged
with a double asterisk that the document defines as designating bulk drug substances that are
components of FDA approved drugs. That is a different list, under a different section of the law,
covering a different type of facility. The secondary sources appear to be conflating the two.

There is a further possibility that this Guide deliberately does not assert. Section 503A may permit
compounding from bulk substances that are components of approved drug products without requiring a
bulks-list nomination, which would make nomination unnecessary and would explain the absence
innocently. **That pathway could not be verified from a retrieved source and is therefore not stated
as fact here.**

**This Guide asserts nothing about gonadorelin's 503A compounding status in either direction. It is
flagged for human regulatory review before publication.**

### 2. Whether GnRH or gonadorelin is on FDA's Category 2 significant-safety-risk list

**Position A.** One search result summary asserted that gonadorelin remains permissible for
compounding despite GnRH being Category 2.

**Position B.** The FDA 503A Category 2 list, updated May 14, 2026, contains exactly six entries:
cesium chloride, domperidone, germanium sesquioxide, ibutamoren mesylate, kisspeptin-10, and
quinacrine hydrochloride for intrauterine administration. Neither GnRH nor gonadorelin appears
(verified 2026-07-19).

**Assessment.** The claim is false. Worth noting alongside it: kisspeptin-10, a related peptide
acting upstream in the same reproductive signalling system, **is** on Category 2. That adjacency may
be the source of the confusion, and it is itself relevant when members ask about neighbouring
compounds.

### 3. What "Factrel" refers to

**Position A.** A historical human product, gonadorelin hydrochloride injection, NDA018123, approved
1982, now discontinued.

**Position B.** A currently marketed veterinary product, gonadorelin hydrochloride injection, Zoetis,
NADA 139-237, labelled for use in cattle only.

**Assessment.** Both are true and refer to different products sharing a brand name. The human one is
gone. The cattle one is current. Any reference to "the Factrel label" that does not specify which one
it means is misleading, and search results today return the cattle label far more readily than
anything about the historical human product.

## Limitations

Limitations of the evidence base itself:

- The only substantive human study retrieved is retrospective and uncontrolled, with 45 patients at a
  single centre, so effects cannot be separated from natural history or co-interventions.
- That study's population has a rare congenital disease in which the relevant hormone is absent, which
  is the setting where replacement therapy is most likely to show an effect and least likely to
  generalise.
- The therapy in that study was delivered by pump in a specialist hospital setting, reproducing a
  natural pulsed pattern. Whether any other delivery situation behaves the same way is unestablished.
- The study was published in Chinese and only the abstract was retrieved for this Guide.
- There is no systematic review or meta-analysis of gonadorelin in PubMed.
- Modern human trial literature on gonadorelin as such is effectively absent. The largest controlled
  trial with the compound in its title is a dairy cattle study.
- For the popular use, there is no evidence of any design, so there is no effect size, no responder
  rate, no duration of effect, and no safety profile to report.
- The safety signals that do exist come from a single case report and from uncontrolled observational
  reporting, both of which are weak designs for establishing causation or rates.

Limitations of this Guide's research process, stated so a reader can judge its reliability:

- **The original FDA-approved human prescribing information could not be retrieved** for either
  FACTREL (NDA018123) or LUTREPULSE KIT (NDA019687). Both predate the era of routinely posted PDFs in
  Drugs@FDA, and the DailyMed record for "Factrel" resolves to the Zoetis cattle label instead. This
  means the original human contraindications, warnings, and adverse reaction profile could not be
  sourced. **This Guide therefore says nothing about what the historical human label contained.**
- **The World Anti-Doping Agency Prohibited List PDF could not be retrieved directly.** The site
  returned an empty response to direct PDF requests, which appears to be automated-access mitigation.
  The gonadorelin entry was extracted from the live list page instead, and the specific list year
  could not be confirmed from the retrieved page.
- **No ClinicalTrials.gov identifiers are recorded anywhere in this document set.** A web search
  suggested two possible gonadorelin registrations, but neither record could be retrieved from
  ClinicalTrials.gov at source. Because they could not be verified, no identifier has been written
  down. **They must not be added unless a human retrieves them directly.**
- State pharmacy board actions, drug scheduling status, and import alert records were not searched.
- The vendor and retailer market was deliberately not characterised. Compounding pharmacy and peptide
  vendor pages appeared in search results and were not used as sources, because vendor claims are not
  evidence.
- Only PubMed and ClinicalTrials.gov were searched for evidence. Other registries and non-English
  databases were not.

## Quality and identity

This section is about the material itself, separately from the science. A fuller checklist is in the
accompanying `QUALITY_AND_DOCUMENTATION.md`.

**There is no FDA-approved human gonadorelin product on the United States market.** Both human
applications show marketing status Discontinued in Drugs@FDA (checked 2026-07-19). It follows that
anything a United States consumer obtains today is compounded, imported, or sold as a non-drug
material. None of those routes has been reviewed by FDA for identity, potency, purity, sterility, or
manufacturing quality.

**No published analytical study of compounded, gray-market, or non-pharmaceutical gonadorelin product
purity, identity, or label accuracy could be located.** A PubMed search combining gonadorelin with
purity, counterfeit, content, or quality returned 25 records, and on inspection of the top-ranked
results none was a product-quality analysis of gonadorelin. They were reviews of endometriosis,
menorrhagia, and prostate cancer therapy (verified 2026-07-19). The honest statement is that
gonadorelin-specific product quality data does not exist in the indexed literature. That is an
absence of information, not a favourable result.

**The brand-name collision with a livestock product is a practical supply risk.** A gonadorelin
product is currently marketed for cattle under the same brand name that a discontinued human product
carried. Veterinary material is a known gray-market supply route for compounds whose human product
has been withdrawn. A member should understand that current, official-looking, genuinely FDA-related
label material for "Factrel" describes a drug for cows.

**Identity is not fixed by the name alone.** Gonadorelin has appeared as a hydrochloride and as an
acetate. The single word "gonadorelin" on a label does not establish which material is present, nor
in what form.

**The regulatory picture circulating online is partly wrong**, as set out in the contradictions
section. Members, and in some cases the people advising them, may be relying on an inaccurate
description of what FDA lists gonadorelin on. That is a documentation problem as much as a science
problem.

**Claim grade for any statement about the identity, purity, or sterility of commercially available
gonadorelin material: G (Unverified). Nothing has been tested and nothing has been confirmed.**

## Safety categories

Nothing in this section is reassurance. These are categories of concern, some of which are open
because they have been documented and some of which are open because nobody has closed them.

**1. Severe allergic reaction, documented in a human case report.** A published case describes an
8-year-old girl with central precocious puberty, already receiving triptorelin, who underwent a
pituitary stimulation test using gonadorelin acetate. Within minutes she lost consciousness and
developed tonic seizures in her hands and feet. She was treated with epinephrine, diphenhydramine,
and fluids, and her vital signs recovered within 30 minutes. The authors describe life-threatening
reactions to gonadorelin acetate as extremely rare and this as the first reported paediatric case,
and conclude that administration should occur in a setting equipped to manage systemic reactions
(PMID 26197466, verified 2026-07-19). A single case report is the weakest design for establishing
causation, but it is a real documented signal, and the authors' conclusion about the necessary
setting is the operative point.
**Claim grade: C (Early human evidence) for the occurrence of a severe reaction in a human.**

**2. Allergic reaction, gynecomastia, and hardened lumps under the skin during therapy.** In the
45-patient congenital hypogonadotropic hypogonadism series, reported adverse events were gynecomastia
in 8 patients, subcutaneous induration in 6, and allergic reaction to the agent in 3 (PMID 39210488,
verified 2026-07-19). This is uncontrolled observational reporting with no comparison group, so
background rates are unknown and these counts should not be converted into a risk estimate.
**Claim grade: C (Early human evidence).**

**3. There is no post-market safety surveillance to consult.** A query of FDA's adverse event
database (FAERS) under the gonadorelin generic name returned no records (retrieved 2026-07-19). This
is exactly what would be expected given that both human applications have been discontinued for
decades and that compounded products are not systematically captured in FAERS. **The absence of FAERS
records must not be read as evidence of safety. It reflects an absence of surveillance, not an absence
of harm.**

**4. There is no safety profile at all for the popular use.** No human study of gonadorelin alongside
testosterone therapy exists, so there is no adverse event dataset, no dose-response information, no
exposure-duration data, and no long-term safety information for that situation. Nothing can be said
about it in either direction.

**5. The historical human safety language could not be retrieved.** The original approved human
prescribing information for both discontinued products was not retrievable during research. Whatever
contraindications and warnings those labels carried, this Guide does not know them and does not
characterise them. A human reviewer must retrieve those documents before anything is said about them.

**6. Deliberate manipulation of a hormone control system.** Gonadorelin sits at the top of the
signalling chain that governs reproductive hormones. Any agent proposed to act there raises questions
about downstream effects on that whole system, about what happens when it is stopped, and about
whether the effect is stimulation or suppression depending on the pattern of exposure. None of these
questions has been answered for the popular use by any source retrieved for this Guide.

**7. Entirely unstudied populations.** Women outside the historical hypothalamic amenorrhoea and
diagnostic-testing settings, pregnancy, breastfeeding, children, older adults, people with pituitary
or hypothalamic disease, people with hormone-sensitive cancers, and people already taking any
hormonal medication. No data of any kind was located for any of these groups in connection with the
uses members ask about.

**8. Product quality as a safety issue in its own right.** Separately from the molecule, material
obtained outside a regulated supply chain has unverified identity, purity, sterility, and endotoxin
status. No gonadorelin-specific testing study exists.

**9. Anti-doping sanction risk.** This is a consequence risk rather than a toxicity risk, and it is
covered in the regulatory section below.

Xenios does not describe gonadorelin as safe, as well tolerated, or as free of side effects. No
evidence supports any of those statements.

## Interactions

No interaction study of gonadorelin with any medication or supplement was located in any retrieved
source. **Unstudied is not the same as absent.**

The following categories intersect directly with what gonadorelin is proposed to do, and would require
review by a qualified professional before anyone considers anything in this area:

- **Testosterone and any other exogenous androgen or anabolic agent.** This is the exact combination
  that drives current interest, and it is precisely where the human evidence is zero.
- **GnRH agonist analogues** (leuprorelin, goserelin, triptorelin, buserelin, nafarelin, histrelin).
  Note that the published anaphylaxis case involved a patient already receiving one of these.
- **GnRH antagonists** (cetrorelix, ganirelix, degarelix, elagolix).
- **Fertility medication of any kind**, including gonadotropins and agents used in assisted
  reproduction.
- **Any treatment for a hormone-sensitive cancer**, including prostate and breast cancer therapy,
  where the reproductive hormone axis is being deliberately manipulated.
- **Hormonal contraception and hormone replacement therapy.**
- **Medication for pituitary or hypothalamic disease**, including anything affecting prolactin.
- **Corticosteroids and other agents affecting hormone regulation.**
- **Any medication where an allergic reaction history exists**, given the documented hypersensitivity
  signal.
- **Any other unapproved peptide or research compound taken at the same time**, which makes both
  benefit and harm impossible to attribute.
- **Pregnancy and breastfeeding**, where no data exists at all.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

All statements below were checked on **2026-07-19**. Regulatory status changes, and anything read

## Unknowns

An explicit list of what is not known.

- **Whether gonadorelin has any effect at all on the outcomes for which it is currently promoted to
  consumers**, specifically preservation of testicular function, fertility, or natural testosterone
  production in men taking testosterone from an outside source. A targeted PubMed search returned zero
  records. There is no retrieved human evidence for this use whatsoever.
- **Whether the pharmacology even supports the popular use.** The human therapeutic evidence that
  exists comes from delivery designed to reproduce the body's natural pulsed release pattern in
  patients whose own pulse generator has failed. Whether administration that does not reproduce that
  pattern produces sustained stimulation, no effect, or suppression is not established by any source
  retrieved here.
  [UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]
- Any dose-response relationship, exposure duration, or long-term safety profile in healthy adults.
  No such study was located.
- Effects in women outside the historical hypothalamic amenorrhoea and diagnostic-testing settings.
- The real-world potency, identity, and contamination profile of compounded and gray-market material.
- Whether FDA has taken, or intends to take, any specific enforcement position on gonadorelin
  compounding. No gonadorelin-specific FDA communication was located.
- What the original approved human labels contained by way of contraindications, warnings, and adverse
  reactions. Neither could be retrieved.
- The correct interpretation of gonadorelin's absence from the 503A nominations list, which is
  contested and unresolved.
- Whether gonadorelin behaves differently in people whose own signalling system is intact compared
  with people in whom it has failed. This is the single most consequential unknown for a member.
- The rate at which severe allergic reactions occur. One case report and three reported allergic
  reactions in an uncontrolled series do not establish a rate.

## Questions for a qualified professional

If a member wants to discuss gonadorelin with a clinician, these questions are written to be asked as
they stand.

1. I have read that the only real human evidence for gonadorelin comes from a rare congenital
   condition where the body's own version of this hormone is missing, and that it was given in
   hospital by a pump reproducing a natural pulsed pattern. Does that match what you can find, and
   does it change how you would think about it for someone whose own system works?
2. There appear to be no human studies at all of gonadorelin alongside testosterone therapy. If that
   is right, how would you weigh a proposed use with no human evidence behind it?
3. My actual concern is (fertility, testicular function, natural testosterone production, or another
   specific concern). What approved options with real human evidence exist for that, and what have we
   not tried?
4. If my concern is fertility, what tests would tell us where I actually stand before considering
   anything at all?
5. There is a published case of a severe allergic reaction to this compound during a hospital test.
   Given my allergy history, how would you think about that risk?
6. Both human products containing this compound were discontinued decades ago. Do you know why they
   were withdrawn, and can you access the original prescribing information?
7. The regulatory picture for compounding this substance appears to be described inconsistently
   online. Can you or your pharmacist tell me what the current position actually is?
8. If something is not an approved product, it comes from outside a regulated supply chain. How do you
   think about unverified identity, purity, and sterility for a material introduced into the body?
9. I compete in a tested sport. This compound is named on the anti-doping prohibited list. Who is the
   right authority for me to ask directly?
10. If I decide to do nothing here for now, what should I watch for, and when should I come back?
11. What would you need to see published before your advice on this would change?

This Guide does not give medical advice and does not tell anyone what to do. These questions belong
with a clinician who knows your history.
