# KPV Contradictions and Divergences

Where the sources disagree, and what the Guide should say when they do. Source ids refer to
SOURCE_REGISTRY.md. Claim ids refer to CLAIM_TABLE.md.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

There are genuine contradictions here, and they matter. Two of them concern claims a member is
likely to encounter before reading the Guide.

## C-01. Whether human data on KPV exists at all

**Position A.** Vendor, clinic, and peptide marketing pages retrieved this session assert that
limited clinical data indicate good tolerability in humans with preliminary evidence of benefit
in inflammatory bowel disease and in skin conditions, and refer to a body of more than fifty
peer-reviewed publications establishing efficacy.

**Position B.** The trial registry returns zero studies for KPV (S01). A bibliographic search
restricted to the clinical trial publication type returns zero records (S02). The reported
rationale for the regulator's 2023 restriction of KPV was an absence of human exposure data
(S07). Two peer-reviewed reviews discuss KPV only as a candidate suitable for possible future
treatment of inflammatory conditions (S05).

**Assessment.** Position B is correct and Position A is unsupported. The publication volume
figure conflates the entire alpha-MSH literature, much of it concerning the full hormone and
the separate compound KdPT, with KPV-specific evidence, and conflates preclinical publications
with clinical ones. The verified KPV and alpha-MSH record set is seventeen items, none indexed
as a clinical trial (S03). The claim of demonstrated human tolerability is not merely unproven.
It runs against the regulator's own reported finding that human exposure data are absent.

**What the Guide says.** No human trial of KPV has been conducted. See KPV-H-01, KPV-H-02, and
the prohibitions at KPV-X-02 and KPV-X-03.

## C-02. The status of the July 2026 advisory committee review

**Position A.** Multiple search-engine summaries and vendor status-tracker pages state in the
past tense that the Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee discussed KPV on July 23, 2026, and
that the agency proposed KPV not be added to the 503A bulk drug substances list, citing poor
characterisation, little or no human efficacy evidence, and unassessed immunogenicity.

**Position B.** The meeting is scheduled for July 23 to 24, 2026, and had not taken place as of
the research date. The two contemporaneous trade sources, both dated 2026-04-16, describe the
meeting in the future tense (S07, S08). The briefing document that would establish any
pre-meeting agency position could not be retrieved.

**Assessment.** Position B. Position A is either speculative pre-meeting commentary or an
artifact of date handling in the summarising system. No outcome was verifiable. This is worth
naming as a pattern rather than a one-off: a confident past-tense summary of an event that has
not occurred is exactly the failure mode that makes secondary summaries unusable as a source
for regulatory facts.

**What the Guide says.** The review is pending and no outcome is stated. See KPV-X-10 and
REGULATORY_STATUS.md. Note additionally that an advisory committee recommendation, whenever it
arrives, is advisory and is not a final agency determination.

## C-03. Whether KPV acts through melanocortin receptors

**Position A.** KPV derives from alpha-MSH, the classical melanocortin hormone, which invites
the assumption that it acts as a melanocortin agonist and that inferences may be drawn from
melanotan-type compounds.

**Position B.** A 2010 review states that KPV lacks the entire sequence motif required to bind
any of the known melanocortin receptors (S05). In mouse colitis work the reported effects
appeared partially independent of melanocortin-1 receptor signalling (S04). In human
keratinocyte cell lines, calcium responses occurred without a rise in cyclic AMP, which is not
the classical melanocortin-1 receptor pathway (S04). In a human bronchial epithelial cell line,
a different peptide acted through a melanocortin receptor while KPV did not (S04).

**Assessment.** Position B is supported across four independent retrieved reports. KPV is not
understood to act as a melanocortin receptor agonist. The same review that makes this point also
concedes that the actual mechanism is unknown, so the honest description is that the mechanism
is unresolved, not that it is well characterised through some alternative pathway.

**What the Guide says.** See KPV-I-02, KPV-M-02, and the prohibitions at KPV-X-07 and KPV-X-08.
This matters in two directions. It blocks inference from melanocortin agonist compounds, and it
blocks confident mechanism copy.

## C-04. Where mechanism plausibility outruns the evidence

This is not a disagreement between sources. It is a gap between how the mechanism is described
in marketing material and what the peer-reviewed record supports, and it is the most common way
a reader is misled about this compound.

- Suppression of NF-kappaB signalling was reported in cultured cells (S04). That is a finding
  about cells in a dish, not about inflammation in a person.
- The intestinal peptide transporter (PepT1) uptake route is repeated constantly in marketing
  material, and no primary paper establishing it was retrieved this session. It is graded G and
  excluded from member-facing text (KPV-M-04).
- A 2010 review states plainly that the signalling mechanism is unknown (S05). A confident
  mechanism narrative therefore contradicts the most recent retrieved review of the subject.

**What the Guide says.** Mechanism is presented as proposed, preclinical, and unresolved, with
the model named in the same sentence as every finding.

## C-05. Where the preclinical result does not mean what it appears to mean

The strongest-looking animal result is weaker than it reads. In the 2017 mouse ulcerative
colitis study, the tested article was KPV carried in an engineered hyaluronic-acid-functionalised
nanoparticle, and the authors' own framing attributes the advantage substantially to the
targeting vehicle rather than to the peptide alone (S04). A reader who sees this cited as a KPV
result will draw a conclusion the study does not support.

Separately, the mouse colitis literature as a whole carries a well-known translation problem.
Rodent colitis models have a long history of failing to predict human inflammatory bowel disease
outcomes.

**What the Guide says.** See KPV-P-02 with its delivery caveat, and KPV-U-07 and KPV-U-08.
Neither result may be presented without its caveat travelling alongside it.

## C-06. Where the supply-channel evidence is about the channel, not the compound

The only retrieved analytical evidence of product failure concerns a different peptide (S06).
It demonstrates how the unregulated online channel fails, and it does not say anything about
what is in a vial sold as KPV, because no study testing KPV material was located.

There is a temptation to close that gap with the circulating gray-market statistics. Those
figures trace only to vendor blogs and similar pages, with no locatable primary source (S12),
and are prohibited by name at KPV-X-09.

**What the Guide says.** The channel evidence is attributed to the compound that was actually
tested, with an explicit statement that KPV was not tested. The absence of KPV-specific product
testing is reported as a gap, not as an absence of problems.

## C-07. Jurisdictional differences

No genuine conflict between jurisdictions was found, because only one jurisdiction produced
retrievable statements. The United States position is described in REGULATORY_STATUS.md. The
anti-doping position could not be established, because the Prohibited List could not be read
directly (S10), and no statement about KPV's status under it appears anywhere in this folder.
No other national regulator was consulted.

The absence of a conflict here reflects the absence of retrievable information from other
jurisdictions, not agreement among them.

## C-08. A note on the integrity of the regulatory record

Every regulatory statement in this folder rests on secondary trade reporting, because the
regulator's own pages returned HTTP 404 and HTTP 403 responses consistent with bot blocking, and
the Federal Register notice could not be read in full. That is not a contradiction between
sources, but it is a weakness in the chain that a reviewer must close before publication by
opening those pages directly. It is recorded here so that it is not lost in the gaps list.
