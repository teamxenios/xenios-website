# KPV (Lys-Pro-Val)

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## One-minute summary

KPV is a very short peptide (a chain of just three amino acids) that appears widely in online
discussion as an anti-inflammatory compound. The honest position, and the single most important
thing in this Guide, is that there is no human evidence for KPV at all. This is not a case of
thin or mixed data. It is a case of absent data.

- A direct query of ClinicalTrials.gov, the main United States registry of clinical studies,
  returned zero studies for KPV. There is no registered trial, ongoing, completed, or terminated
  (verified 2026-07-19).
- A search of PubMed, the main biomedical literature database, restricted to the clinical trial
  publication type, returned zero records for KPV (verified 2026-07-19).
- When the United States Food and Drug Administration (FDA) placed KPV in a category for
  substances raising significant safety concerns pending further evaluation, the stated reason
  included "an absence of human exposure data for KPV", according to trade reporting of an FDA
  September 2023 statement. That is the regulator describing the human data as absent, not thin.
- There is a real preclinical literature, meaning research done in animals and in cultured cells
  rather than in people. In two mouse models of inflammatory bowel disease, KPV was reported to
  produce earlier recovery and reduced inflammatory changes. In cultured human airway cells,
  KPV was reported to suppress inflammatory signalling. None of this establishes anything about
  a living person.
- Even the mechanism is unsettled. A 2010 peer-reviewed review states plainly that the exact
  signalling mechanism used by KPV "currently is unknown". Marketing material that describes a
  confident mechanism is ahead of the published science.
- On safety, the correct statement is not that KPV is safe. It is that nothing is known. There is
  no adverse event dataset, no human pharmacokinetic data (how the body absorbs, distributes,
  and clears a substance), and no immunogenicity assessment (whether the body mounts an immune
  response against it). Absence of reported harm here reflects absence of study.
- A separate and concrete risk attaches to the supply channel rather than to the molecule. Because
  KPV is unapproved, material is obtained outside any regulated supply chain, where identity,
  purity, sterility, and contamination are unverified.
- FDA is currently reconsidering KPV's classification. As of the date of this Guide that review is
  pending and no outcome exists. Some websites already describe an outcome in the past tense. That
  could not be verified and is not stated here.

Xenios does not sell KPV, does not supply it, and does not tell members how to obtain or use it.
This Guide exists so that a member reading about KPV elsewhere has an accurate account of what is
and is not known.

**Overall claim grade for "KPV is effective for any human condition": G (Unverified).**
**Overall claim grade for "KPV has anti-inflammatory activity in rodent and cell models": D (Preclinical).**

## What it is

KPV is a tripeptide, meaning a chain of three amino acids. The three are lysine, proline, and
valine, whose single-letter codes are K, P, and V, which is where the name comes from.

KPV corresponds to the tail end (the C-terminal fragment) of a larger hormone called
alpha-melanocyte-stimulating hormone, usually shortened to alpha-MSH, which is 13 amino acids long.
In the scientific literature KPV is therefore written as alpha-MSH(11-13), meaning amino acids 11
through 13 of that hormone.

Two peer-reviewed reviews retrieved for this Guide describe KPV as the C-terminal tripeptide of
alpha-MSH, and state that it lacks the entire sequence motif required for binding to any of the
known melanocortin receptors (the family of cell-surface receptors that the full alpha-MSH hormone
acts through), while reportedly retaining anti-inflammatory activity without the pigmentation
effect of the full hormone (PMID 18612139, PMID 21222263).

**Compounds KPV is commonly confused with.** Their evidence is not KPV's evidence and must not be
merged with it.

- **KdPT**, a derivative of KPV corresponding to amino acids 193 to 195 of interleukin-1 beta, an
  immune signalling protein. It is named as a separate emerging tripeptide in both retrieved
  reviews. It is not KPV.
- **(CKPV)2**, a dimeric peptide (two linked copies) studied separately (PMID 16413580). It is not KPV.
- **Full-length alpha-MSH**, which has broad effects on pigmentation, appetite, and immune function
  that KPV is specifically described as lacking.
- **Melanocortin receptor agonists**, such as afamelanotide or melanotan-type compounds. Because
  KPV is described as not binding the known melanocortin receptors, it is mechanistically distinct
  from these despite sharing a parent hormone. Inferences drawn from those compounds do not
  transfer to KPV.

A further identity point: regulatory filings distinguish KPV free base from KPV acetate as separate
bulk substances. In practice, the single name "KPV" does not by itself pin down exactly which
material is in a given product.

Commonly quoted chemical registry identifiers (a CAS number and a molecular weight near 342 g/mol)
appeared during research only on vendor and marketing pages and were not confirmed against a primary
chemical registry, so they are deliberately not reproduced here.

## Why interest exists

The interest in KPV is easy to describe and worth understanding without endorsing it.

Alpha-MSH, the parent hormone, has a long-studied anti-inflammatory profile. A 2008 review in
Endocrine Reviews states that the anti-inflammatory effects of alpha-MSH were validated in animal
models of fever, contact dermatitis, vasculitis, fibrosis, ocular, gastrointestinal, brain, and
airway inflammation, and arthritis (PMID 18612139). The appeal of KPV is the idea of keeping that
anti-inflammatory character while leaving behind the pigmentary and other broad hormonal effects of
the full hormone, in a molecule small enough to be simple to make.

Two further features drive the enthusiasm. KPV is extremely small, which invites the assumption
that it is easy for the body to absorb. And the animal work is concentrated in gut inflammation,
which is an area where people living with inflammatory bowel disease have real unmet need.

Three things should be held alongside that interest.

First, both retrieved reviews frame KPV in the future tense. The 2008 review positions it as an
alternative for anti-inflammatory therapy "suitable for the future treatment" of such conditions.
The 2010 review calls these peptides "interesting candidates" for future treatment. Forward-looking
phrasing from review authors is itself evidence that nothing had been demonstrated in people at the
time of writing.

Second, an anti-inflammatory rationale is not an outcome. Interest describes why researchers thought
a compound was worth studying. It does not describe what happened when it was given to people,
because as far as any retrievable record shows, it has not been systematically given to people.

Third, some of the enthusiasm in commercial material is built on evidence belonging to other
molecules, particularly full-length alpha-MSH and KdPT. That is discussed in the contradictions
section below.

## Terminology

Every technical term used in this Guide, in plain English.

- **Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids, the building blocks of proteins. Shorter than a protein.
- **Tripeptide.** A peptide made of exactly three amino acids. KPV is one.
- **Amino acid.** A small molecule that links with others to form peptides and proteins. Lysine,
  proline, and valine are three of them.
- **C-terminal fragment.** A piece taken from one specific end of a larger peptide or protein chain.
- **Alpha-MSH (alpha-melanocyte-stimulating hormone).** A 13-amino-acid hormone in the body with
  effects on pigmentation, appetite, and immune function. KPV is its final three amino acids.
- **Melanocortin receptor (MC-R).** A family of receptors on cell surfaces that alpha-MSH acts
  through. Individual members are numbered, for example MC1R and MC3R.
- **Receptor.** A structure on or in a cell that a signalling molecule binds to in order to produce
  an effect.
- **Agonist.** A substance that activates a receptor.
- **Preclinical.** Research done before any human study, meaning in animals or in cultured cells.
- **In vitro.** Literally "in glass". Research done in cells or tissue in a dish, outside a living body.
- **In vivo.** Research done in a living organism. In this Guide, in vivo work means animals only.
- **Cell line.** Cells grown indefinitely in a laboratory. Human-derived cell lines are still cells
  in a dish, not people.
- **Murine.** Relating to mice.
- **NF-kappaB.** A protein complex inside cells that switches on many inflammation-related genes.
  Suppressing it is the most commonly proposed way KPV is said to reduce inflammation.
- **Cytokine.** A signalling protein used by immune cells. Interleukin-8 (IL-8), eotaxin, and tumour
  necrosis factor alpha (TNF-alpha) are cytokines mentioned in this Guide.
- **Matrix metalloproteinase-9 (MMP-9).** An enzyme that breaks down structural material between
  cells and is involved in tissue remodelling and inflammation.
- **Cyclic AMP (cAMP).** A common internal messenger molecule inside cells. Its absence in a
  response is used by researchers to argue that a different signalling route is involved.
- **PepT1.** A transporter protein in the gut lining that carries very short peptides into cells.
- **Pharmacokinetics.** What the body does to a substance: absorption, distribution, metabolism,
  and elimination, including how long it lasts.
- **Immunogenicity.** Whether a substance provokes an immune response against itself, such as
  antibody formation.
- **Endotoxin.** A contaminant from bacterial cell walls. It can cause fever and serious reactions
  if present in an injectable product, even when no live bacteria remain.
- **Sterility.** Freedom from living micro-organisms.
- **Colitis.** Inflammation of the colon. DSS-colitis and transfer colitis are two standard ways of
  producing colitis in laboratory mice.
- **Inflammatory bowel disease (IBD).** A group of conditions involving chronic gut inflammation,
  including ulcerative colitis and Crohn's disease.
- **Nanoparticle delivery system.** An engineered carrier designed to take a drug to a specific
  tissue. The carrier itself can be responsible for an observed effect.
- **503A bulk drug substances list.** A United States framework governing which raw substances
  pharmacies may use in compounding (preparing customised medicines).
- **Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee (PCAC).** The FDA advisory committee that reviews
  substances nominated for that list. Its recommendations advise FDA and are not final FDA decisions.
- **Compounding.** Preparation of a customised medicine by a pharmacy, as distinct from manufacture
  of an approved drug product.
- **Certificate of analysis (COA).** A document from a testing laboratory stating what a specific
  batch of material was found to contain.
- **Gray market.** Sale outside the regulated and licensed supply chain.

## Proposed mechanism

Everything in this section is **proposed**, and all of it derives from cultured cells and animals.
No mechanism has been confirmed in a living human being. Claim grade for this entire section: **D
(Preclinical)**, and in one case **G (Unverified)**.

**Suppression of NF-kappaB signalling.** In an immortalised human bronchial epithelial cell line
(16HBE14o-, cells in a dish, not people), KPV was reported to inhibit NF-kappaB, matrix
metalloproteinase-9 activity, and secretion of the cytokines interleukin-8 and eotaxin, with the
authors proposing that it works by blocking movement of a NF-kappaB component (p65RelA) into the
cell nucleus (PMID 22837805). In the same cultured-cell paper, a different peptide (gamma-MSH) acted
through the MC3R receptor while KPV did not, which the authors read as support for a route that does
not involve melanocortin receptors. Grade D.

**PepT1-mediated uptake.** PepT1 is a transporter in the gut lining that carries very short peptides
into intestinal cells, and it is described as being increased in inflamed intestinal tissue. This is
the single most repeated mechanistic claim in KPV marketing material. During research for this Guide
it appeared repeatedly on vendor and marketing pages, and no primary paper establishing it for KPV
was retrieved. It is therefore reported here as an unverified claim, not as a mechanism.
Grade G. [UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]

**Calcium signalling without cyclic AMP.** In human keratinocyte cell lines (HaCaT cells and normal
human keratinocytes, cells in a dish), alpha-MSH, KPV, and ACTH produced rapid internal calcium
responses without raising cyclic AMP, which the authors interpreted as signalling distinct from the
classical MC-1R and cAMP pathway (PMID 15102092). This was a signalling characterisation study in
cultured cells, not an efficacy study and not a human study. Grade D.

**What is not known.** A 2010 peer-reviewed review states directly that "the exact signalling
mechanism utilized by KPV and related peptides currently is unknown" (PMID 21222263). That admission
from a review author is the most honest available summary. A 2005 paper described the three
dimensional structure of the alpha-MSH-derived peptide (PMID 15946192), but structure is not
mechanism.

**Species used across the mechanistic literature:** mouse (colitis models), plus human and hamster
cell lines. No human in vivo mechanism data was located.

## Human evidence

**There is none.**

This is the central finding of this Guide, and it is stated as plainly as possible.

| Study | Design | Population | Outcome |

| *(no records)* | | | |

The evidence table is empty, and the empty table is the result.

What was searched, and what came back:

- **ClinicalTrials.gov**, queried directly through its public API for the term KPV, returned **zero
  studies**. There is no registered clinical trial of KPV, ongoing, completed, or terminated. A
  secondary query on "lysine-proline-valine" returned only unrelated amino-acid studies matched on
  keyword (tryptophan depletion, hepatic encephalopathy, prandial metabolism), none involving KPV.
  (Verified 2026-07-19.)
- **PubMed**, searched with the clinical trial publication type filter, returned **zero records**
  for KPV. (Verified 2026-07-19.)
- A broader PubMed search for KPV together with alpha-MSH returned 17 records in total. Their titles
  were verified. **None is indexed as a clinical trial.** They span laboratory and delivery-system
  work, structural chemistry, reviews, and studies of related but distinct compounds.
- **FDA**, in the rationale reported for placing KPV in Category 2 in 2023, cited "an absence of
  human exposure data for KPV".
- The **Partnership for Safe Medicines**, in a public comment dated 2026-07-01, described the group
  of peptides under consideration, KPV among them, as being evaluated "despite a disqualifying
  absence of adequate human clinical trials to establish their safety or efficacy". This is an
  advocacy submission rather than a regulator's finding and is attributed as such, but it
  independently corroborates the absence of human trials.

Claim grade for any statement of the form "KPV does X in people": **G (Unverified)**. No claim in
this Guide reaches grade A, B, or C, because no human study of any design was located.

## Preclinical evidence

This is where the real KPV literature lives. It is genuine research, and it is entirely in animals
and in cultured cells. Every finding below carries its model in the same sentence. None of it
establishes an effect in a person.

**Mice, two models of inflammatory bowel disease (DSS-colitis and transfer colitis).** In mice, KPV
treatment was reported to produce earlier recovery and stronger regain of body weight in DSS-colitis,
and in transfer colitis to produce recovery, body weight regain, and histologically reduced
inflammatory changes, with effects appearing partly independent of MC1R signalling
(PMID 18092346, Inflamm Bowel Dis, 2008). This mouse result is the most frequently cited animal
finding behind the gut-inflammation rationale. Grade D.

**Mouse model of ulcerative colitis, plus colonic epithelial cell and macrophage cultures.** In mice
and in cultured cells, KPV encapsulated in hyaluronic-acid-functionalised nanoparticles was reported
to prevent mucosal damage, to reduce TNF-alpha, and to alleviate experimental colitis more
effectively than non-targeted formulations (PMID 28143741, Mol Ther, 2017). **Important
interpretive caveat:** the thing tested was an engineered targeted delivery system, not plain KPV,
and the paper's own framing is that the targeting vehicle drove the advantage. This mouse result
does not transfer to unformulated KPV. Grade D, with the delivery-system caveat attached.

**Human bronchial epithelial cell line (16HBE14o-), in a dish.** In cultured human airway cells,
KPV was reported to inhibit NF-kappaB, matrix metalloproteinase-9 activity, and secretion of
interleukin-8 and eotaxin, proposed to work by blocking p65RelA entry into the nucleus
(PMID 22837805, Int J Physiol Pathophysiol Pharmacol, 2012). The tissue is human-derived, but these
are immortalised cells in a dish, so the finding carries no implication for a living person. Grade D.

**Human keratinocyte cell lines (HaCaT, normal human keratinocytes) and Chinese hamster ovary cells,
in a dish.** In these cultured cells, alpha-MSH, KPV, and ACTH produced rapid internal calcium
responses under specific conditions without raising cyclic AMP, suggesting signalling distinct from
the classical MC-1R and cAMP pathway (PMID 15102092, J Invest Dermatol, 2004). This was a signalling
study, not an efficacy study. Grade D.

**Narrative review of animal and in vitro literature (2008).** A review in Endocrine Reviews states
that the anti-inflammatory effects of alpha-MSH were validated in **animal** models of fever, contact
dermatitis, vasculitis, fibrosis, ocular, gastrointestinal, brain, and airway inflammation, and
arthritis, and positions KPV as an alternative for anti-inflammatory therapy "suitable for the future
treatment" of such conditions (PMID 18612139). Note that much of the validated animal work described
concerns the full alpha-MSH hormone rather than KPV itself. Grade D.

**Narrative review of animal and in vitro literature (2010).** A review in Advances in Experimental
Medicine and Biology describes KPV as retaining much of alpha-MSH's anti-inflammatory capacity
without pigmentary action, while explicitly conceding that the exact signalling mechanism "currently
is unknown", and calls these peptides "interesting candidates" for future treatment (PMID 21222263).
Grade D.

**Further records identified but not individually read.** A PubMed search for KPV plus alpha-MSH
returned 17 records in total. Titles were verified. They include a self-cross-linked hydrogel
delivery system (PMID 34547895, 2021), reductive structural modification of KPV (PMID 29953505,
2018), a receptor-targeted fluorescent probe (PMID 28349696, 2017), basophil activity modulation
(PMID 22178636, 2012), an anti-inflammatory peptide class review (PMID 17934097, 2007),
corneal and ocular effects of alpha-MSH(11-13) (PMID 16965771, 2006), the distinct dimer (CKPV)2 in
endotoxin-induced injury (PMID 16413580, 2006), a three-dimensional structure paper (PMID 15946192,
2005), a functions review (PMID 12851308, 2003), HIV-1 inhibition by alpha-MSH peptides
(PMID 11073109, 2000), and antimicrobial effects of alpha-MSH peptides (PMID 10670585, 2000).
**None is indexed as a clinical trial.** Their individual findings were not independently verified,
so they are listed here to map the literature, not as substantiated results. Grade G for any
specific finding within them until a human reads them.

**A general caution about preclinical gut research.** Rodent colitis models have a long history of
failing to predict human inflammatory bowel disease outcomes. A benefit in a mouse colitis model is
a reason to run a human trial, not a substitute for one.

## Contradictions and disagreement

Where sources genuinely disagree, both positions are stated, along with an assessment of which the
retrieved evidence supports.

**1. Whether human clinical data on KPV exist at all.**

- *Position A.* Vendor, clinic, and peptide-marketing pages assert that limited clinical data suggest
  good tolerability in humans with preliminary evidence of therapeutic benefit in inflammatory bowel
  disease and dermatological applications, and reference more than 50 peer-reviewed publications
  establishing efficacy.
- *Position B.* ClinicalTrials.gov returns zero studies for KPV. PubMed returns zero records indexed
  as a clinical trial. FDA's cited rationale for Category 2 placement was an absence of human exposure
  data. The 2008 and 2010 peer-reviewed reviews describe KPV only as a candidate suitable for future
  treatment.
- *Assessment.* Position B is correct and Position A is unsupported. The publication count conflates
  the entire alpha-MSH literature, much of it on the full hormone and on the separate compound KdPT,
  with KPV-specific evidence, and conflates preclinical publications with clinical ones. The claim of
  demonstrated human tolerability is not merely unproven, it is contradicted by the regulator's own
  stated finding that human exposure data are absent. No human trial of KPV has been conducted.

**2. Status of the July 2026 FDA advisory committee review.**

- *Position A.* Multiple search-engine summaries and vendor "FDA status tracker" pages state in the
  past tense that the committee discussed KPV on 23 July 2026 and that FDA proposed KPV not be added
  to the 503A bulks list.
- *Position B.* The meeting is scheduled for 23 to 24 July 2026 and, as of the date of this Guide,
  has not taken place. The two contemporaneous trade sources (RAPS and BioSpace, both dated
  2026-04-16) describe it in the future tense. The FDA briefing document could not be retrieved, so no
  FDA pre-meeting position could be confirmed.
- *Assessment.* Position B. Position A is either speculative pre-meeting commentary or a date-handling
  artefact. This Guide describes the review as pending and asserts no outcome. Note also that even a
  committee recommendation is advisory and is not a final FDA determination.

**3. Whether KPV acts through melanocortin receptors.**

- *Position A.* KPV comes from alpha-MSH, the classical melanocortin hormone, which invites the
  assumption that it acts as a melanocortin agonist.
- *Position B.* The 2010 review states that KPV lacks the entire sequence motif required for binding
  to any of the known melanocortin receptors. The mouse colitis work found effects partly independent
  of MC1R signalling (PMID 18092346). The cultured human keratinocyte study found calcium responses
  without cyclic AMP elevation, meaning not the classical MC-1R route (PMID 15102092). The cultured
  human bronchial cell study found that gamma-MSH acted through MC3R while KPV did not
  (PMID 22837805).
- *Assessment.* Position B is supported by the retrieved literature. This matters twice over. It
  undercuts any inference drawn from melanocortin-agonist compounds such as melanotan-type peptides,
  and it means the honest description of KPV's mechanism is that it is unresolved, since the same
  2010 review concedes the actual mechanism is unknown. Material describing a confident mechanism
  overstates the published science.

## Limitations

Limitations of the evidence base itself:

- There is no human evidence of any design, so there is no human effect size, no responder rate, no
  dose-response relationship, and no duration of effect to report.
- The animal work is concentrated in a narrow area (rodent colitis models) that has historically been
  a poor predictor of human inflammatory bowel disease outcomes.
- The strongest single animal result used an engineered nanoparticle delivery vehicle, and the
  authors attributed the advantage substantially to that vehicle rather than to KPV alone.
- The cultured-cell studies used immortalised cell lines. A response in a cell line does not
  establish a response in intact tissue, let alone in a person.
- Two of the six core preclinical sources are narrative reviews rather than primary experiments, and
  narrative reviews are selective by nature.
- Much of the anti-inflammatory work cited in support of KPV was actually performed on the full
  alpha-MSH hormone or on the separate compound KdPT.

Limitations of this Guide's research process, stated so a reader can judge its reliability:

- FDA's own web pages could not be retrieved during research. Direct requests returned HTTP 404 and
  403 responses that appear to be automated-access blocking rather than genuine absence. Every FDA
  fact in this Guide therefore rests on reputable secondary trade reporting (RAPS and BioSpace)
  rather than on FDA's own pages. A human must confirm these directly before publication.
- The FDA briefing document for the July 2026 advisory committee meeting could not be retrieved, so
  claims circulating about FDA's pre-meeting position on KPV are not attributed to FDA here.
- The Federal Register notice (document 2026-07361, dated 2026-04-16) could not be read in full. The
  site redirected to an anti-automation page and the public-inspection PDF was unparseable. The
  verbatim list of substances on the committee agenda was therefore never read from the primary
  notice.
- The World Anti-Doping Agency Prohibited List PDF could not be parsed on two attempts, so this Guide
  cannot confirm by direct reading whether KPV appears on it.
- Individual abstracts for 11 of the 17 KPV and alpha-MSH PubMed records were not retrieved. Only
  their titles were verified. None is indexed as a clinical trial, so a fuller reading is unlikely to
  change the central finding.
- Only ClinicalTrials.gov was searched. The EU Clinical Trials Register, ISRCTN, the WHO ICTRP, and
  the Japanese and Chinese registries were not searched. Given a zero signal everywhere else, these
  are unlikely to change the conclusion, but they were not checked.

## Quality and identity

This section is about the material itself, separately from the science. It matters because an
unapproved substance is, by definition, obtained outside a regulated supply chain.

**No KPV-specific quality testing was found.** A search for independent analytical testing of
gray-market peptides located no peer-reviewed study that tested KPV specifically. This is a genuine
gap and not a clean bill of health. Nobody appears to have systematically checked what is in vials
sold as KPV.

**The supply channel itself has documented failures, from a peer-reviewed source.** A 2024
market-surveillance study published in the Journal of Medical Internet Research identified 317 online
pharmacy links, of which 134 were illegal. Test purchases from six illegal pharmacies yielded three
injection vials, all considered probable substandard or falsified products. Visual inspection showed
non-compliance on 59 to 63 percent of criteria. Measured active content exceeded the label by 28.56
to 38.69 percent, while purity was only 7.7 to 14.37 percent against a claimed 99 percent, and
endotoxin was detected in every sample (PMID 39509151).

That study tested **semaglutide, not KPV**. It is evidence about the channel through which unapproved
peptides are typically obtained, and it must be read that way. It is included because the channel is
the same, and because it demonstrates the specific failure modes (wrong content, very low purity,
contamination) that no one has checked for in KPV. Claim grade: **B for the semaglutide supply-channel
finding itself, G for any inference about KPV product quality.**

**Naming and identity confusion is a practical quality risk.** KPV must not be conflated with KdPT,
with (CKPV)2, or with full-length alpha-MSH or melanocortin receptor agonists. Preclinical results
for those compounds are routinely presented in commercial material as though they were KPV results.
Regulatory filings also treat KPV free base and KPV acetate as separate substances, so the single
name does not fix the identity of the material.

**A note on circulating statistics.** Research for this Guide surfaced a set of striking gray-market
failure figures, including claimed rates of incorrect amino acid sequences, heavy metal contamination,
residual solvent, and mass lab-report audit failure rates. Every one traced to vendor blogs, Substack
posts, or supplement-marketing pages, and no primary source could be located for any of them. They
are deliberately excluded from this Guide. Precise-sounding numbers with no traceable source do more
harm than good in health content.

## Safety categories

Nothing in this section is reassurance. These are categories of concern that remain open because no
one has closed them.

**1. There is no human safety data. This is the primary safety finding.** FDA's stated rationale for
Category 2 placement cited an absence of human exposure data for KPV. Zero registered trials and zero
PubMed records indexed as clinical trials mean there is no systematic safety monitoring, no adverse
event dataset, and no characterised safety profile in people. **Absence of reported harm here reflects
absence of study, not evidence of safety.**

**2. Unassessed immunogenicity for injectable routes.** An uncharacterised peptide introduced by
injection carries an unassessed risk that the body mounts an immune response against it. This is
standard regulatory reasoning and it has not been assessed for KPV. During research, search summaries
attributed a specific concern of this kind to FDA briefing material, but the underlying FDA document
could not be retrieved, so nothing here is attributed to FDA as its words.
[UNVERIFIED - requires human source check against the FDA briefing document once published]

**3. No characterised human pharmacokinetics.** Duration of effect, clearance, and accumulation are
unknown. Whether meaningful exposure is achieved at all by any route is unknown. Since the 2010 review
concedes that even the basic signalling mechanism is unknown, downstream and off-target effects in a
living human body cannot be predicted from the existing literature.

**4. Broad immune modulation with uncharacterised consequences.** The retrieved preclinical literature
centres on suppression of NF-kappaB and of pro-inflammatory cytokines in rodent and cell models
(PMID 22837805, PMID 18092346). Inflammation is a normal protective response. Any agent proposed to
suppress it broadly raises questions about infection risk, masking of symptoms that would otherwise
prompt investigation, and interaction with existing immune or inflammatory conditions. None of these
questions has been studied in humans for KPV, so no reassurance can be offered in either direction.

**5. Product quality as a safety issue in its own right.** Separately from the molecule, material
obtained outside a regulated supply chain has unverified identity, purity, sterility, and endotoxin
status. The peer-reviewed channel evidence above shows what that failure looks like in practice for a
different peptide.

**6. Entirely unstudied populations.** Pregnancy, breastfeeding, children, older adults,
immunocompromised people, people with autoimmune or inflammatory disease, and people taking
immune-modulating medication. No data of any kind was located for any of these groups.

## Interactions

No interaction data for KPV was located in any retrieved source. No study of KPV alongside any
medication or supplement was found. This means interactions are unstudied, which is not the same as
absent.

The following categories would require review by a qualified professional before anyone considers
anything in this area, because they intersect directly with the proposed anti-inflammatory action:

- **Immunosuppressant and immune-modulating medication**, including anything taken after a transplant.
- **Biologic therapies for inflammatory bowel disease, rheumatological, or dermatological conditions**,
  which frequently act on the same inflammatory signalling described in the preclinical literature.
- **Corticosteroids** and other anti-inflammatory medication.
- **Any medication for an active infection**, or use during an active infection, given that
  suppressing inflammation could affect both the response and the visible signs of it.
- **Cancer treatment of any kind**, including immunotherapy.
- **Any condition where inflammation is being deliberately monitored as a marker of disease
  activity**, since suppression could obscure the picture a clinician relies on.
- **Any other unapproved peptide or research compound taken at the same time**, which makes both
  benefit and harm impossible to attribute.
- **Pregnancy and breastfeeding**, where no data exists at all.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

## Unknowns

An explicit list of what is not known. This is longer than the evidence section, which is itself the
most accurate summary of the state of knowledge.

- Whether KPV has any effect whatsoever in a living human being. No human study of any design was
  located.
- The human safety profile, adverse event rate, and dose-limiting toxicity. Entirely uncharacterised.
  FDA itself cited an absence of human exposure data.
- Human pharmacokinetics: absorption, distribution, metabolism, elimination, half-life, and whether
  meaningful systemic exposure is achieved by any route at all.
- The actual molecular target and signalling mechanism. A peer-reviewed review states this is unknown.
- Whether the PepT1 uptake mechanism, repeated constantly in marketing material, is supported by any
  primary literature.
- Immunogenicity, including whether repeated exposure provokes an antibody response.
- Whether any of the mouse colitis benefit would translate to humans, given the poor historical
  predictive record of rodent colitis models.
- Whether unformulated KPV does anything comparable to the engineered targeting nanoparticle used in
  the 2017 mouse study, whose benefit the authors attributed substantially to the delivery vehicle.
- Long-term consequences of sustained NF-kappaB suppression in humans, including infection risk and
  symptom masking.
- Behaviour in specific populations: pregnancy, breastfeeding, children, older adults,
  immunocompromised people, people with autoimmune or inflammatory disease, and people taking
  immune-modulating medication.
- Interactions with any medication or supplement. No interaction data was located.
- The actual identity, purity, sterility, and endotoxin content of material sold as KPV. Never
  systematically tested in any retrieved study.
- Whether KPV is specifically addressed by any anti-doping authority, as opposed to falling under a
  catch-all category by inference.
- The outcome of the pending FDA advisory committee review.

## Questions for a qualified professional

If a member wants to discuss KPV with a clinician, these are questions that produce a useful
conversation. They are written to be asked as they stand.

1. I have read that there are no registered clinical trials and no human studies of KPV. Does that
   match what you can find, and does that change how you would think about it?
2. My interest came from research in mice with colitis and in cells in a dish. How much weight do you
   put on that kind of evidence when there is no human data behind it?
3. What is actually going on with my symptoms, and is there an approved treatment with real human
   evidence that we have not tried yet?
4. I take the following medications and have the following conditions. Which of them would make any
   anti-inflammatory compound a particularly poor idea for me?
5. If something suppressed inflammation broadly in my body, what would you want to monitor, and what
   signs would concern you?
6. Are there markers you currently use to track my condition that could be masked or made harder to
   read by an anti-inflammatory compound?
7. This substance is not approved anywhere I could find, which means any material comes from outside a
   regulated supply chain. How do you think about unverified identity, purity, and sterility for
   something introduced into the body?
8. I compete in a tested sport. Who is the right authority for me to ask about whether this falls
   under the non-approved substances category?
9. If I decided to do nothing about this for now, what would you want me to watch for, and when should
   I come back?
10. If new human evidence on KPV appears, what would you want to see in it before it changed your
    advice?

This Guide does not give medical advice and does not tell anyone what to do. These questions belong
with a clinician who knows your history.
