# SS-31 (elamipretide)

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## One-minute summary

SS-31 is one of the few compounds in this Guide series with a large, genuine human trial
programme behind it. That makes it more interesting than most, and the honest reading of that
programme is **mixed to negative** outside one ultra-rare genetic disease.

The three most informative controlled results are failures.

- **MMPOWER-3** (NCT03323749), a phase 3 trial in 218 people with primary mitochondrial
  myopathy, **missed both co-primary endpoints**, six-minute walk distance and total fatigue. The
  registry lists it as Terminated and records that the double-blind portion did not achieve its
  primary endpoints (verified 2026-07-21).
- The randomized crossover phase of **TAZPOWER** (NCT03098797), in 12 people with Barth
  syndrome, **missed both co-primary endpoints**, again walk distance and fatigue (verified
  2026-07-21).
- **ReCLAIM-2** (NCT03891875), a phase 2 trial in 176 people with geographic atrophy, **did not
  beat placebo** on its primary endpoints. The sponsor then carried a secondary imaging endpoint
  into phase 3 (verified 2026-07-21).

The positive human signals come from an uncontrolled open-label extension, from a subgroup plus
a post-hoc analysis after a failed phase 3, and from a surrogate strength measure. Those are the
weakest forms of human evidence, not the strongest.

There is also a real regulatory milestone, and it must be stated exactly. On **19 September
2025** the FDA granted **accelerated** approval to FORZINITY (elamipretide hydrochloride)
injection, to improve muscle strength in adult and pediatric patients with Barth syndrome
meeting the label's criteria. The approval was based on improvement in knee extensor muscle
strength, which the label describes as an **intermediate clinical endpoint, not a demonstrated
clinical benefit**, and the label states that continued approval may be contingent on
verification in confirmatory trials (verified 2026-07-21). This is the first FDA-approved
mitochondria-targeted therapeutic. It is also a conditional approval in an ultra-rare disease
affecting very few people.

**It is emphatically not evidence for use in healthy adults for energy, fatigue, performance,
recovery, or longevity.** For that use, the human evidence is absent. The one relevant trial
(NCT07275424, healthy aging) was recruiting at the time of retrieval and has no results.

What does exist for the popular use case is preclinical work, and in aged **mice** it is strong:
reversed decline in mitochondrial ATP production and increased treadmill exercise tolerance. A
separate mouse study, as titled, reports improved muscle function **without** detectable change
in epigenetic or transcriptomic measures of biological age, which cuts directly against
reverses-aging marketing.

One more thing matters for anyone reading about this compound online. **The FDA-approved product
and the grey-market material sold as "SS-31" are not established to be the same material.** Only
the approved product has been through FDA quality review. The molecule appears under at least
five names, and a name is not a quality assurance.

Xenios does not sell SS-31 or elamipretide, does not supply either, and does not tell members how
to obtain or use them. This Guide exists so that a member reading about SS-31 elsewhere has an
accurate account of what is and is not known.

**Overall claim grade for "SS-31 improves energy, performance, or longevity in healthy adults":
G (Unverified).**
**Overall claim grade for "elamipretide failed its primary endpoints in three controlled human
trials": B (Supported human evidence).**
**Overall claim grade for "SS-31 improved mitochondrial function and exercise tolerance in aged
mice": D (Preclinical).**

## What it is

SS-31 is a synthetic **tetrapeptide**, meaning a chain of four amino acids, designed to target
mitochondria (the structures inside cells that produce most of the cell's usable energy). It was
developed clinically under the name **elamipretide** by Stealth BioTherapeutics Inc.

Per the FDA-approved US label, it acts as a mitochondrial cardiolipin binder that localizes to
the inner mitochondrial membrane and improves mitochondrial morphology and function (verified
2026-07-21).

It is marketed in the United States as **FORZINITY (elamipretide hydrochloride) injection**.

**The names matter more than usual here.** The same molecule appears in the literature and in
commerce under at least five names:

- **SS-31**, the research designation and the name used on the grey market.
- **elamipretide**, the clinical development name and the international non-proprietary name.
- **MTP-131** and **Bendavia**, earlier development codes. Trials from the earlier programme are
  registered under these.
- **FORZINITY**, the US brand name of the approved product.

This matters for a specific reason. Guide copy, and marketing copy, must not let the
approved-drug name lend borrowed credibility to an unapproved research chemical bought under a
different name.

**Critically for a member Guide: the FDA-approved product and the grey-market "SS-31" sold as a
research chemical are not established to be the same material.** FDA approval covers a specific
manufactured product with a defined formulation that has been through quality review. Material
sold online as a research chemical has had none of that review, and its identity, purity, and
content are unverified.

## Why interest exists

The interest in SS-31 is easy to describe and worth understanding without endorsing it.

Mitochondrial decline is one of the more credible stories in aging biology. Mitochondria produce
most of the energy a cell can use, their function declines measurably with age, and a compound
that could restore that function has an obvious and appealing narrative attached to it: more
energy, better endurance, slower aging.

Three things then amplified the interest.

First, the **preclinical data in aged mice are genuinely strong**, not marginal. That is
unusual, and it is a reasonable basis for scientific enthusiasm.

Second, a large pharmaceutical programme was actually built on it, across mitochondrial
myopathy, Barth syndrome, heart failure, macular degeneration, and aging muscle. Serious money
and serious investigators took the hypothesis seriously.

Third, in September 2025 an approval arrived. That is a real milestone, and it is the first
FDA-approved mitochondria-targeted therapeutic.

Four things should be held alongside that interest.

**The programme largely failed.** The three most informative controlled results, across three
different diseases, missed their primary endpoints. That is the central fact and it usually goes
missing in summaries.

**The approval is narrow and conditional.** It covers one ultra-rare genetic disease, on an
intermediate endpoint, under a pathway explicitly designed to permit earlier access while the
evidence is completed afterwards.

**The endpoints that failed are exactly the ones a healthy adult would care about.** Walk
distance and fatigue. Those were the co-primary endpoints in both MMPOWER-3 and TAZPOWER, in
people who had a diagnosed mitochondrial disorder and therefore the most room to improve. They
were not met.

**A mechanism is not an outcome.** "Mitochondria decline with age, this targets mitochondria" is
a rationale for running a trial. Trials were run. The results are what count.

## Terminology

Every technical term used in this Guide, in plain English.

- **Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids, the building blocks of proteins. Shorter than a
  protein.
- **Tetrapeptide.** A peptide made of exactly four amino acids. SS-31 is one.
- **Mitochondria.** Structures inside cells that produce most of the cell's usable energy. Their
  function declines measurably with age.
- **Inner mitochondrial membrane.** The inner of the two membranes surrounding a mitochondrion,
  where the energy-producing machinery sits.
- **Cristae.** The folds of the inner mitochondrial membrane. Their structure affects how
  efficiently energy production works.
- **Cardiolipin.** A fat molecule found in the inner mitochondrial membrane. SS-31 is described
  as binding it.
- **Electron transport.** The chain of chemical steps in the mitochondrion that ultimately
  produces usable energy.
- **ATP.** Adenosine triphosphate, the molecule cells use as their immediate energy currency.
- **ATPmax.** A measured biochemical value: the maximum rate at which a tissue can make ATP. It
  is a laboratory measure, not a measure of how someone feels or performs.
- **Oxidative phosphorylation.** The main energy-producing process in mitochondria. "Coupling"
  refers to how efficiently it converts fuel into ATP rather than wasting it as heat.
- **Redox homeostasis.** The balance between oxidising and reducing chemical conditions inside a
  cell. It is disturbed in aging tissue.
- **Preclinical.** Research done before any human study, meaning in animals or in cultured cells.
- **Randomized controlled trial (RCT).** A study in which participants are randomly assigned to
  the treatment or to a comparison group, usually placebo. It is the design best able to
  separate a real effect from wishful thinking.
- **Placebo-controlled.** Including a comparison group receiving an inactive substitute.
- **Double-blind or double-masked.** Neither participants nor investigators know who received
  what, which prevents expectation from shaping the result.
- **Crossover trial.** A design where each participant receives both treatment and placebo in
  sequence, acting as their own comparison.
- **Open-label extension.** A continuation phase after the blinded part ends, in which everyone
  knows they are receiving the treatment and there is usually no comparison group. It is the
  weakest form of trial evidence.
- **Primary endpoint.** The main outcome a trial commits in advance to measuring. It is chosen
  before the data are seen, which is what makes it meaningful.
- **Co-primary endpoints.** Two primary endpoints, both of which must be met.
- **Missed the primary endpoint.** The trial did not show the effect it set out to show. This is
  a negative result, not an inconclusive one.
- **Subgroup analysis.** Looking at results within a slice of the participants. Even when planned
  in advance, it is much weaker than the overall result.
- **Post-hoc analysis.** An analysis decided after the data were seen. Hypothesis-generating
  only.
- **Surrogate endpoint** or **intermediate endpoint.** A measurement used as a stand-in for the
  outcome that actually matters to a patient. Muscle strength on a specific test, for example,
  standing in for the ability to live better.
- **Accelerated approval.** A United States regulatory pathway permitting marketing for a serious
  condition with unmet need on the basis of a surrogate or intermediate endpoint, with the
  clinical benefit to be confirmed afterwards in further trials.
- **Confirmatory trial.** The later trial required to verify that the surrogate translated into
  real benefit.
- **Six-minute walk test.** A standard measure of functional capacity: how far someone can walk
  in six minutes.
- **Regression to the mean.** The statistical tendency for an unusually bad measurement to be
  followed by a better one purely by chance. It is one reason uncontrolled studies mislead.
- **Learning effect.** Improvement on a repeated test simply from having done it before, which
  can look like a treatment effect on something like a walk test.
- **NT-proBNP.** A blood marker associated with heart strain. A biomarker, not a clinical
  outcome such as survival or symptoms.
- **Geographic atrophy.** An advanced form of dry age-related macular degeneration involving
  loss of retinal tissue.
- **Ellipsoid zone.** A layer visible on retinal imaging, used as a structural measure in eye
  trials.
- **Barth syndrome.** A progressive, life-limiting, ultra-rare X-linked genetic disorder of
  mitochondrial cardiolipin metabolism.
- **Primary mitochondrial myopathy.** A group of genetic conditions in which mitochondrial
  dysfunction causes muscle weakness and fatigue.
- **Orphan drug.** A designation for a therapy targeting a rare disease.
- **Excipient.** An inactive ingredient in a formulation. Excipients carry their own risks.
- **Benzyl alcohol.** A preservative used in some injectable formulations, with a specific and
  serious risk in newborns.
- **Eosinophilia.** An increase in a type of white blood cell, detected on a blood test.
- **Contraindication.** A circumstance in which a product must not be used at all.
- **Grey market.** Sale outside the regulated and licensed supply chain.
- **Research chemical.** Material sold with a research-use label, outside any medicines
  regulation. The label does not mean the material has been characterised.

## Proposed mechanism

Everything in this section is **proposed**. Unusually for this Guide series, the mechanism
wording below comes from an FDA-approved label rather than from marketing material, which makes
it better sourced than most. It still does not establish an outcome, and the label itself says
so.

**Cardiolipin binding at the inner mitochondrial membrane.** Per the FDA-approved US label,
elamipretide acts as a mitochondrial cardiolipin binder that localizes to the inner mitochondrial
membrane and improves mitochondrial morphology and function (verified 2026-07-21). Grade B, as a
label mechanism statement.

**Proposed downstream consequence.** Binding cardiolipin is proposed to stabilise cristae
structure, the folded architecture of the inner membrane, and thereby to improve electron
transport efficiency and mitochondrial function. This wording is taken from the label's
Mechanism of Action section. Grade C.

**What the label itself says about benefit.** The FDA-approved label frames clinical benefit as
**still requiring confirmation**. This sentence must travel with any description of the
mechanism. A plausible mechanism inside an approved label is still not a demonstrated benefit,
and in this case the agency explicitly declined to conclude that benefit had been shown. Grade A.

**Why a good mechanism is not enough here.** This compound is a useful illustration of a general
point. The mechanistic story for SS-31 is coherent, targeted, and supported by strong animal
data. Trials were then run in people who had exactly the mitochondrial dysfunction the mechanism
addresses. The trials mostly failed. That sequence is common in drug development and it is the
reason mechanism alone is graded low everywhere in this series.

**Species and populations across the mechanistic and preclinical literature:** mice, plus the
human disease populations in the clinical programme. No mechanism has been confirmed to produce
a functional benefit in a healthy human being.

## Human evidence

**Substantial human evidence exists, and the honest reading of it is mixed to negative.**

This is not a compound with an empty evidence table. Stealth BioTherapeutics ran a large
programme across five areas. What follows is every human study retrieved for this Guide, with
the negative results first, because that is the order the evidence supports.

### The controlled trials that failed

| Study | Design | Population | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| **MMPOWER-3** (NCT03323749), phase 3 | Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, with open-label extension. Sponsor: Stealth BioTherapeutics Inc. Registry status: **Terminated**. | 218 participants with primary mitochondrial myopathy, ages 16 to 80, randomized approximately 109 to elamipretide and 109 to placebo. | **NEGATIVE. Failed both co-primary endpoints** at week 24: change in six-minute walk distance, and total fatigue on the Primary Mitochondrial Myopathy Symptom Assessment. The registry records that the double-blind portion did not achieve its primary endpoints. |
| **TAZPOWER** (NCT03098797), phase 2/3 | Randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled crossover, 28-week blinded phase, followed by a 168-week open-label extension. Sponsor: Stealth BioTherapeutics Inc. | 12 participants with Barth syndrome, an ultra-rare X-linked genetic disorder of mitochondrial cardiolipin metabolism. Not a healthy or athletic population. | **NEGATIVE on its randomized phase.** Neither co-primary endpoint was met in the blinded crossover phase: six-minute walk test distance, and the Barth Syndrome Symptom Assessment total fatigue score. |
| **ReCLAIM-2** (NCT03891875), phase 2 | Randomized, double-masked, placebo-controlled parallel-group, 48 weeks. Sponsor: Stealth BioTherapeutics Inc. Registry status: Completed. | 176 participants with dry age-related macular degeneration with geographic atrophy. | **NEGATIVE on primary endpoints.** Did not beat placebo on low-luminance best-corrected visual acuity change, or on geographic atrophy lesion area. |

**MMPOWER-3 is the most important row.** It is the largest randomized trial in the programme, it
was run in exactly the population the mechanism predicts should benefit, and it did not work.
The reported least-squares mean difference on the six-minute walk test was -3.2 metres (95% CI
-18.7 to 12.3; p = 0.69), numerically favouring placebo and clearly non-significant.
**[UNVERIFIED - these specific figures came from secondary reporting. The Neurology full text
was not retrievable this session and they require human verification against the published
article. The failure itself, and its direction, are verified from the trial registry.]** Grade B
for the failure, G for the numbers.

**Note the pattern across two of the three.** The same two endpoint types, walk distance and
fatigue, failed in both MMPOWER-3 and TAZPOWER, in two different diseases. Those are precisely
the outcomes a healthy adult hoping for more energy and endurance would care about.

### The positive human signals, and what they are worth

Each of these is real and each is a weak form of evidence. They are presented after the failures
because that is where they belong.

**The TAZPOWER open-label extension.** After the blinded phase, an uncontrolled 168-week
open-label extension reported improvement in walking distance, fatigue scores, and cardiac
volumes in the 10 participants who continued and the 8 who reached week 168 (PMID 38602181,
Genetics in Medicine, 2024). **The limitations are severe and the authors state them
themselves:** an extremely small sample, and no control group in the extension. Without a
placebo group, regression to the mean, learning effects on a repeated walk test, and expectancy
cannot be excluded. The findings are specific to a rare genetic disease and do not transfer to
healthy adults. Grade C, the weakest human evidence in this folder.

**The MMPOWER-3 subgroup.** The benefit most frequently quoted from MMPOWER-3 comes from a
prespecified subgroup of participants with nuclear DNA pathogenic variants, plus a later
post-hoc genotype analysis. **The trial failed.** Subgroup rescue after a missed primary endpoint
is hypothesis-generating at best, and is one of the most common sources of false positives in
clinical research. Grade C, and it must never be presented before the failure it follows.

**The ReCLAIM-2 imaging signal.** After the trial missed its primary endpoints, secondary and
exploratory signals on ellipsoid zone preservation were used by the sponsor to select the primary
endpoint for the subsequent phase 3 programme (ReNEW, NCT06373731, and ReGAIN). Choosing the
endpoint that worked, after seeing which one worked, substantially weakens what a later positive
result on it would mean. Grade C. No numeric result from ReCLAIM-2 appears in this Guide, because
the primary publication returned HTTP 403 and only registry-level facts are verified.

### The trials whose results are not known to this review

|---|---|---|---|

Status: Completed. | 308 participants hospitalized with congestion due to heart failure. | **NOT KNOWN to this review.** The registry record confirms the design, enrollment, and the primary outcome measure, change in NT-proBNP between baseline and day 8 or early discharge. NCT02245620 is the trial closest to the popular anti-aging and mitochondrial-performance
framing, which is exactly why its unknown outcome is stated rather than omitted. Note also that
its primary endpoint is a biochemical measure rather than strength, endurance, or any lived
outcome. Even a positive ATPmax result would not establish a functional or longevity benefit.
Grade G for both rows. An unknown result is not a hint in either direction.

### Healthy adults

**No completed randomized trial in a healthy population was retrieved.**

The question of whether elamipretide does anything for a healthy adult is currently under study
and is **not yet answered**. NCT07275424, a phase 2 investigator-sponsored study (David
Marcinek) of healthy aging with a planned enrollment of 30, was **Recruiting** at the time of
retrieval and has **no results**.

Nothing about efficacy in healthy adults can be claimed from a recruiting trial. Claim grade for
any statement of the form "SS-31 does X for energy, fatigue, performance, recovery, or longevity
in a healthy person": **G (Unverified)**.

## Preclinical evidence

This is where the popular case for SS-31 actually lives, and it is animal work. Every finding
below carries its model in the same sentence. None of it establishes an effect in a person, and
the human trials described above are the direct test of whether it transferred.

**Aged mice, treated for 8 weeks.** In female C57BL/6Nia **mice**, aged at 26 months and
compared with young mice at 5 months, SS-31 was reported to reverse the age-related decline in
maximum mitochondrial ATP production, improve coupling of oxidative phosphorylation, restore
redox homeostasis, and increase treadmill exercise tolerance, without an increase in
mitochondrial content (Campbell MD, Duan J, Samuelson AT, et al., Free Radical Biology and
Medicine, PMID 30597195). This is a **mouse** result only. It has not been reproduced as a
functional benefit in a completed randomized trial in healthy humans. Grade D.

This is the strongest result in the entire record for the anti-aging framing, and it should be
described accurately rather than dismissed. It is also the clearest illustration of the gap this
Guide exists to describe: strong rodent data, and human trials in the same functional domain
that were negative or have not been run.

**Aging mice, cardiac and skeletal muscle.** A separate **mouse** study, as titled, reports that
elamipretide improved cardiac and skeletal muscle function during aging **without detectable
changes in tissue epigenetic or transcriptomic measures of biological age** (PMCID PMC12151887).
This is noted because it directly undercuts marketing claims that SS-31 reverses biological
aging. **[UNVERIFIED - the full text was not retrieved this session. This finding is recorded
from the title as listed in a search result and requires human verification.]** Grade D, flagged.

The flag on that second study is deliberate and worth explaining. That finding is convenient to
this Guide's position. A packet that verifies inconvenient claims strictly and convenient ones
loosely is not honest, it is just biased in a different direction. So it carries the same
verification requirement as anything else.

**A general caution about strong animal data.** A benefit in aged mice is a reason to run a human
trial, not a substitute for one. In this case the human trials were run, in people with
diagnosed mitochondrial dysfunction who had the most room to improve, and the walk-distance and
fatigue endpoints were not met.

## Contradictions and disagreement

Where sources genuinely disagree, both positions are stated, along with an assessment of which
the retrieved evidence supports. The full treatment is in CONTRADICTIONS.md.

**1. Does elamipretide work in Barth syndrome?**

- *Position A.* The randomized, placebo-controlled crossover phase of TAZPOWER missed both
  co-primary endpoints in 12 participants.
- *Position B.* The FDA granted accelerated approval in September 2025 on the basis of
  improvement in knee extensor muscle strength, an intermediate endpoint, and the uncontrolled
  168-week open-label extension reported sustained functional and cardiac improvement.
- *Assessment.* Both are true, and the tension is the single most important thing to communicate
  honestly. The controlled evidence did not hit its prespecified targets. The approval rests on
  an intermediate surrogate endpoint under a pathway designed for serious rare diseases with
  unmet need, and the label itself states that continued approval may depend on confirmatory
  trials. Regulatory approval here is a conditional bet, not a settled demonstration of clinical
  benefit. It also says nothing about healthy adults. Worth adding for fairness: accelerated
  approval is not a standard applied carelessly. It is a deliberate policy choice to let people
  with a serious, ultra-rare, life-limiting disease and no alternative access a therapy earlier.
  That is defensible for that population. It is not a transferable endorsement.

**2. Does elamipretide improve mitochondrial myopathy?**

- *Position A.* MMPOWER-3, the largest trial at 218 participants, failed both co-primary
  endpoints, the registry status is Terminated, and the reported walk-test difference numerically
  favoured placebo.
- *Position B.* A prespecified subgroup with nuclear DNA pathogenic variants, and a later
  post-hoc genotype analysis, reported improvement.
- *Assessment.* The trial is a failure. Subgroup rescue after a missed primary endpoint is
  hypothesis-generating at best. Any text must lead with the failure, not the subgroup.

**3. Mouse rejuvenation versus human function.**

- *Position A.* Aged **mice** show reversed decline in mitochondrial ATP production, restored
  redox balance, and increased treadmill endurance.
- *Position B.* No completed randomized trial retrieved shows a functional performance, fatigue,
  or longevity benefit in healthy humans, and the relevant healthy-aging trial is still
  recruiting. A separate mouse study title reports improved muscle function without detectable
  change in epigenetic or transcriptomic age.
- *Assessment.* The mouse-to-human gap is the whole story for the consumer positioning of this
  compound. Strong rodent data plus repeated human trial failures in the exact functional
  endpoints argues for caution, not enthusiasm.

**4. WADA status.**

- *Position A.* Vendor and affiliate blogs assert that elamipretide moved off WADA class S0 for
  2026 because FDA approval removed its unapproved-substance status.
- *Position B.* The official WADA Prohibited List page could not be retrieved this session, and
  no official source confirms this.
- *Assessment.* Unresolved. No WADA claim is published in this Guide. Vendor blogs have a
  commercial interest in an it-is-not-banned conclusion, and the consequence of being wrong falls
  entirely on the athlete.

## Limitations

Limitations of the evidence base itself:

- The three most informative controlled trials missed their primary endpoints. The programme's
  positive human signals all come from weaker designs: an uncontrolled extension, a subgroup, a
  post-hoc analysis, and a surrogate endpoint.
- The TAZPOWER open-label extension had no control group and involved 8 to 10 participants. In
  that design, regression to the mean, learning effects on the walk test, and expectancy cannot
  be excluded.
- TAZPOWER's randomized phase involved 12 participants. Findings there are specific to an
  ultra-rare genetic disease and do not transfer to healthy adults.
- In ReCLAIM-2, the endpoint carried into phase 3 was selected after the primary endpoints had
  failed and the data had been seen.
- The two trials most relevant to a general-population reader, NCT02914665 in heart failure and
  NCT02245620 in elderly skeletal muscle, have outcomes that are unknown to this review.
- Both of those trials used biomarker or biochemical primary endpoints, NT-proBNP and ATPmax
  respectively, rather than strength, endurance, symptoms, survival, or any lived outcome.
- The entire clinical programme was sponsored by Stealth BioTherapeutics Inc., the company
  commercialising the compound. That is normal in drug development and it is a standard reason
  to weight independent replication highly. Note that the one healthy-aging trial is
  investigator-sponsored.
- All human evidence is in diagnosed disease populations under clinical supervision. There is no
  completed randomized trial in healthy adults.
- No systematic review or meta-analysis of elamipretide was retrieved, so no independent
  synthesis across the programme informs this Guide.

Limitations of this Guide's research process, stated so a reader can judge its reliability:

- The FDA approval letter and the Integrated Review PDFs on accessdata.fda.gov both returned HTTP
  404 on direct fetch. The label content was recovered through DailyMed, an authoritative NIH
  mirror of FDA labelling, but the FDA review documents, which contain the agency's own critique
  of the evidence, were not read. That is a significant gap for a Guide whose central point
  concerns the strength of the evidence behind a conditional approval.
- The primary Neurology publication of MMPOWER-3 was not retrievable in full. The numeric effect
  sizes quoted for that trial came from secondary reporting and are flagged for human
  verification.
- The Ophthalmology Science publication of ReCLAIM-2 returned HTTP 403. Only registry-level facts
  about that trial are verified, and no numeric result from it appears here.
- The official WADA Prohibited List page returned empty content, so no anti-doping status is
  stated.
- The ClinicalTrials.gov results sections were not queried field by field. Posted results may
  exist for several completed trials and would strengthen or correct this record.
- The full text of the mouse study on epigenetic and transcriptomic age was not retrieved. Its
  finding is recorded from the title as listed.
- The regulatory history items, the Complete Response Letter of 15 May 2025 and the various FDA
  designations, rest on a sponsor press release and were not verified against FDA primary
  sources.
- No analytical chemistry study of grey-market SS-31 product identity or purity was retrieved.
- Only ClinicalTrials.gov was searched. The EU Clinical Trials Register, ISRCTN, the WHO ICTRP,
  and the Japanese and Chinese registries were not searched.

## Quality and identity

This section is about the material itself, separately from the science. For this compound it is
unusually important, because a genuine FDA approval exists and can be misread as covering
something it does not.

**The approved product and the research chemical are not established to be the same material.**
FDA approval covers a specific manufactured product, FORZINITY (elamipretide hydrochloride)
injection, with a defined formulation that has been through FDA quality review. Material sold
online as a research chemical labelled "SS-31" has had none of that review, and its identity,
purity, and content are unverified. Nothing about the approved product's quality transfers to a
vial bought elsewhere.

**The multiple-names problem is a practical quality risk.** Elamipretide appears under at least
five names: SS-31, MTP-131, Bendavia, elamipretide, and FORZINITY. A shared molecule name is not
a shared quality assurance. The specific failure mode is that an approval granted to FORZINITY
becomes, through the shared name, an implied endorsement of a vial labelled SS-31.

**No SS-31-specific quality testing was found.** No analytical study testing material sold as
grey-market SS-31 for identity or purity was located this session. That is a genuine gap and not
a clean bill of health. Nobody appears to have systematically checked what is in these vials.
Grade G.

**A market-risk claim that is recorded but not published as fact.** Secondary reporting retrieved
this session describes FDA warning letters to online peptide sellers in April 2026 over
research-use-only labelling combined with consumer-health marketing, and cites independent
laboratory findings that a substantial share of grey-market peptide samples fail identity,
purity, or quantity checks. **These descriptions came from law-firm, trade, and press blogs, not
from a retrieved FDA primary document. No specific failure percentage is reproduced here, and
nothing here is attributed to FDA.** Grade E, market claim only.
**[UNVERIFIED - requires human verification against FDA warning-letter records directly.]**

**Formulation carries risk of its own.** The approved label contains a warning driven not by the
peptide but by an excipient, benzyl alcohol, with a contraindication in neonates. That is a
useful reminder in this context: what a product contains besides the active molecule can matter
as much as the molecule, and for unverified material nobody knows what else is in there.

## Safety categories

Nothing in this section is reassurance. Two framings must be held apart throughout.

The first four items below come from the **FDA-approved US prescribing information** for a
specific manufactured product, used under clinical supervision, in studied disease populations.
That is a real safety database and it deserves to be reported accurately.

The fifth item is the one that applies to the use most members are asking about, and it is an
absence.

**1. Injection site reactions.** The most common adverse reactions in the FDA-approved US label
are injection site reactions, including erythema, pain, induration, pruritus, bruising, and
urticaria (Adverse Reactions section, retrieved 2026-07-21). Grade A.

**2. Hypersensitivity, including serious reactions.** The label describes hypersensitivity
reactions, including serious reactions requiring urgent medical care and permanent
discontinuation. Serious hypersensitivity to elamipretide or any excipient is a
**contraindication** (Contraindications and Warnings and Precautions sections, retrieved
2026-07-21). A contraindication is among the strongest statements a label makes. Grade A.

**3. Benzyl alcohol toxicity, and a contraindication in neonates.** The product must not be used
in neonates. The label describes fatal reactions, metabolic acidosis progressing to
neurotoxicity, and gasping syndrome in low-birth-weight and preterm neonates exposed to
benzyl-alcohol-containing drugs (Warnings and Precautions, retrieved 2026-07-21). This is an
excipient-driven warning, and a reminder that the formulation, not just the peptide, carries
risk. Grade A.

**4. Eosinophilia with longer exposure.** Increased eosinophilia, an increase in a type of white
blood cell, was observed with more prolonged exposure, described in the label as resolving after
discontinuation (Adverse Reactions, retrieved 2026-07-21). The label attaches a specific exposure
duration to this observation, which is deliberately not reproduced here: a duration figure in a
safety line is readily misread as a threshold below which use is fine. Grade A.

**5. There is no long-term safety data for healthy adults. This is the primary safety finding for
the use members are actually asking about.** No completed randomized trial in a healthy
population was retrieved, and the healthy-aging trial NCT07275424 is still recruiting. The entire
safety database supporting approval comes from an ultra-rare disease population and from patients
with mitochondrial myopathy, heart failure, and macular degeneration, all under clinical
supervision. **Absence of reported harm in healthy adults reflects absence of study, not evidence
of safety.** Grade B.

**6. The label is not a safety profile for a different material.** Everything in items 1 through
4 describes a specific FDA-reviewed manufactured product. It does not describe material sold as a
research chemical, whose identity, purity, and content are unverified. This cuts both ways: the
label is not reassurance about grey-market material, and it is also not a characterisation of
grey-market material's specific risks. It simply does not describe it.

**7. Entirely unstudied contexts.** Use outside clinical supervision. Use by healthy adults for
performance, fatigue, or longevity. Long-term use beyond the studied disease populations. No data
of any kind was located for any of these.

## Interactions

No interaction data for elamipretide or SS-31 was located in any retrieved source this session,
and no interaction section content was extracted from the label into this Guide. This means
interactions are **not characterised in this research record**, which is not the same as absent.
The FDA-approved label is the appropriate source for interaction information about the approved
product, and a clinician has access to it.

The following would require review by a qualified professional, because they intersect with the
populations in which this compound has actually been studied or with the risks its label
describes:

- **Any known hypersensitivity to elamipretide or to any excipient in a formulation**, which the
  approved label treats as a contraindication where the reaction was serious.
- **Neonates**, for whom the approved product is contraindicated because of benzyl alcohol.
- **Existing cardiac conditions or heart failure**, given that one trial population was
  hospitalized heart failure patients and cardiac measures appear across the programme.
- **Diagnosed mitochondrial disease or myopathy**, where a person is likely already under
  specialist care and where the controlled trial evidence is negative.
- **Retinal or macular disease**, where an ongoing phase 3 programme exists and self-directed use
  could interfere with proper assessment.
- **Pregnancy and breastfeeding**, where no data was located in this research record.
- **Any other unapproved peptide or research compound used at the same time**, which makes both
  benefit and harm impossible to attribute.
- **Any situation where blood counts are being monitored**, given the eosinophilia observation in
  the label.

## Questions for a qualified professional

If a member wants to discuss SS-31 or elamipretide with a clinician, these are questions that
produce a useful conversation. They are written to be asked as they stand.

1. I have read that the FDA approved this compound in 2025, and also that its three largest
   controlled trials missed their primary endpoints. Can you help me understand how both of those
   are true at once?
2. The approval is described as accelerated and based on an intermediate endpoint. What does that
   pathway actually mean about how confident anyone can be that it works?
3. The trials that failed were in people with diagnosed mitochondrial disorders, measuring walk
   distance and fatigue. I am a healthy adult interested in energy and endurance. What should I
   take from those results?
4. There is strong data in aged mice. How much weight do you put on strong animal data when the
   human trials in the same functional area came out negative?
5. The approved product is a specific manufactured medicine. If something is sold under a
   different name as a research chemical, what do you assume about whether it is the same
   material?
6. I take the following medications and have the following conditions. Which of them would make
   any unstudied compound a particularly poor idea for me?
7. The approved label lists hypersensitivity reactions, some serious, as a contraindication. How
   would I know if I were at risk of that, and what would you want me to do about it?
8. I compete in a tested sport. Who is the right authority for me to ask about this compound's
   anti-doping status, given that I should not rely on what a vendor says?
9. What is actually going on with my energy or fatigue, and is there something with real human
   evidence that we have not investigated yet?
10. If new human evidence in healthy adults appears, what would you want to see in it before it
    changed your advice?

This Guide does not give medical advice and does not tell anyone what to do. These questions
belong with a clinician who knows your history.
