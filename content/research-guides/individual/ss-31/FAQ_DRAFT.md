# SS-31: questions members actually ask

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

These answers follow the same rule as the Guide itself. Where the honest answer is that the
trials failed, that is the answer given. Where the honest answer is that nobody knows yet, that
is the answer given too.

---

## 1. Does it work?

For healthy adults wanting more energy, less fatigue, better performance, or a longer life:
**nobody knows, and there is no completed human trial to draw on.** The one study asking that
question (NCT07275424, healthy aging) was recruiting at the time of retrieval and has no results
(verified 2026-07-21).

For the diseases it was actually tested in, the answer is harder and more interesting, because
this compound does have a real trial programme. The honest summary is that the controlled results
mostly failed.

- **MMPOWER-3** (NCT03323749), a phase 3 trial in 218 people with primary mitochondrial myopathy,
  **missed both co-primary endpoints**: six-minute walk distance and total fatigue. The registry
  lists it as Terminated and records that the double-blind portion did not achieve its primary
  endpoints.
- The randomized crossover phase of **TAZPOWER** (NCT03098797), in 12 people with Barth syndrome,
  **missed both co-primary endpoints**, again walk distance and fatigue.
- **ReCLAIM-2** (NCT03891875), a phase 2 trial in 176 people with geographic atrophy, **did not
  beat placebo** on its primary endpoints.

There is a genuine exception, and it is narrow. In September 2025 the FDA granted accelerated
approval for improving muscle strength in Barth syndrome, an ultra-rare genetic disease, based on
a measure of knee extensor strength that the label calls an intermediate endpoint rather than a
demonstrated clinical benefit.

Notice which endpoints failed: walk distance and fatigue, in two different diseases, in people
who had diagnosed mitochondrial dysfunction and therefore the most room to improve. Those are
exactly the outcomes a healthy person hoping for more energy would care about.

Claim grade for "SS-31 improves energy, performance, or longevity in healthy adults": **G
(Unverified)**. Claim grade for "elamipretide failed its primary endpoints in three controlled
human trials": **B (Supported human evidence)**.

## 2. But it was FDA approved. Doesn't that settle it?

No, and this is the most important thing in the whole Guide to get right.

The approval is real. FORZINITY (elamipretide hydrochloride) injection was approved on 19
September 2025, and it is the first FDA-approved mitochondria-targeted therapeutic. That is a
genuine milestone.

Four qualifiers change what it means, and all four come from the FDA-approved label itself
(verified 2026-07-21).

- **It is accelerated approval.** That is a pathway allowing marketing for a serious condition
  with unmet need before clinical benefit has been demonstrated.
- **It is based on an intermediate endpoint.** Improvement in knee extensor muscle strength. The
  label describes this as an intermediate clinical endpoint and **not a demonstrated clinical
  benefit**.
- **It is conditional.** The label states that continued approval may be contingent upon
  verification and description of clinical benefit in confirmatory trials. A phase 4 Barth trial,
  NCT07531251, was recruiting.
- **It is narrow.** It covers Barth syndrome, a progressive, life-limiting, ultra-rare genetic
  disease, in patients meeting the label's criteria. It is **not approved for aging, performance,
  fatigue, longevity, cognition, or general mitochondrial support**.

One thing worth saying in fairness to the approval. Accelerated approval is not a lower standard
applied carelessly. It is a deliberate policy choice to let people with a serious, life-limiting,
ultra-rare disease and no alternative get access earlier, while the evidence is completed
afterwards. That is a defensible decision for those patients. It is not a transferable
endorsement for anyone else.

## 3. Is it safe?

Two different questions are hiding in that one, and they have different answers.

**For the approved product, used under clinical supervision in the studied populations**, there
is a real safety database, and the FDA-approved label reports it. The most common adverse
reactions are injection site reactions, including erythema, pain, induration, pruritus, bruising,
and urticaria. The label describes hypersensitivity reactions, including serious reactions
requiring urgent medical care and permanent discontinuation, and serious hypersensitivity to
elamipretide or any excipient is a contraindication. The product must not be used in neonates
because of benzyl alcohol, with the label describing fatal reactions, metabolic acidosis
progressing to neurotoxicity, and gasping syndrome in low-birth-weight and preterm neonates
exposed to benzyl-alcohol-containing drugs. Increased eosinophilia, an increase in a type of white
blood cell, was observed with more prolonged exposure, described as resolving after
discontinuation (all verified 2026-07-21).

**For a healthy adult using material bought as a research chemical**, the answer is that nobody
knows. There is no long-term safety data for healthy adults using this for performance, fatigue,
or longevity. The entire safety database supporting approval comes from an ultra-rare disease
population and from patients with mitochondrial myopathy, heart failure, and macular
degeneration, all under clinical supervision.

The critical point: a real drug label is not reassurance about a different material used for a
different purpose by a different population. Absence of reported harm in healthy adults reflects
absence of study.

Xenios does not describe SS-31 as safe, as well tolerated, or as free of side effects for the use
members are asking about, because no evidence supports any of those statements.

## 4. Is the SS-31 sold online the same thing as the approved drug?

**Not established, and that is the honest answer.**

FDA approval covers a specific manufactured product with a defined formulation that has been
through FDA quality review. Material sold online as a research chemical labelled "SS-31" has had
none of that review, and its identity, purity, and content are unverified. No analytical study
testing grey-market SS-31 specifically was located this session, so nothing is known about it in
either direction. That is a gap, not a clean result.

The name situation makes this worse rather than better. The same molecule appears as SS-31,
elamipretide, MTP-131, Bendavia, and FORZINITY. A shared molecule name is not a shared quality
assurance. The specific way people get misled is that an approval granted to FORZINITY becomes,
through the shared name, an implied endorsement of a vial labelled SS-31.

One detail from the approved label is worth carrying over here. Its most serious warning, the
neonatal contraindication, is driven not by the peptide but by an excipient, benzyl alcohol. What
is in a product besides the active molecule matters, and for unverified material nobody knows
what else is in there.

## 5. Can I get it from Xenios?

No. Xenios does not sell SS-31 or elamipretide, does not supply either, and does not tell members
where to obtain them or how to use them.

This Guide exists for a different reason. SS-31 is discussed widely in longevity and performance
circles, usually as a mitochondrial energy compound, and the 2025 FDA approval has been widely
cited as proof that it works. The purpose here is to give an accurate account, including the
parts that are unflattering to the compound and the parts that are genuinely favourable.

## 6. The mouse data looked amazing. What happened?

The mouse data are genuinely good, and they should not be dismissed.

In aged female C57BL/6Nia **mice**, at 26 months compared with young mice at 5 months and treated
for 8 weeks, SS-31 was reported to reverse the age-related decline in maximum mitochondrial ATP
production, improve coupling of oxidative phosphorylation, restore redox homeostasis, and
increase treadmill exercise tolerance, without an increase in mitochondrial content (PMID
30597195). That is a strong result in a well-conducted animal study.

What happened is the thing that happens often in drug development. A strong animal result led to
human trials, and the human trials in the same functional area, walk distance and fatigue, came
out negative.

That sequence is the honest answer to "what happened". Not that the mouse work was bad, but that
mice are not people, and this is a case where the translation did not carry.

There is one more mouse study worth knowing about, because it cuts against the marketing rather
than for it. As titled, it reports that elamipretide improved cardiac and skeletal muscle
function during aging in **mice** **without detectable changes in tissue epigenetic or
transcriptomic age**. In other words, function improved and the biological-age measures did not
move. **[UNVERIFIED - the full text was not retrieved this session and this is recorded from the
title as listed. It requires human verification, and it is flagged precisely because it is
convenient to this Guide's position.]**

## 7. I read that a subgroup in the big trial did benefit. Is that not evidence?

It is a reason to run another trial. It is not a result.

MMPOWER-3 failed both co-primary endpoints in 218 participants. The benefit that circulates comes
from a prespecified subgroup of participants with nuclear DNA pathogenic variants, plus a later
post-hoc genotype analysis.

Here is why that carries so little weight, and the reason is structural rather than anyone acting
in bad faith. Once a trial has missed its primary endpoint, there are many ways to divide the
participants, and some divisions will look favourable purely by chance. A subgroup planned in
advance is better than one chosen afterwards, and neither is a substitute for the overall result.
Subgroup rescue after a failed primary endpoint is one of the most common sources of false
positives in clinical research.

The same pattern appears elsewhere in this programme. In ReCLAIM-2, after the primary endpoints
failed, a secondary imaging signal was used to select the primary endpoint for the next phase 3
programme. Choosing the endpoint that worked, after seeing which one worked, substantially
weakens what a later positive result on it would mean.

## 8. Two of the trials have no results listed. What does that mean?

It means the outcome is unknown to this review, and it is stated that way rather than guessed at.

**NCT02914665**, a phase 2 trial in 308 people hospitalized with heart failure, is Completed. Its
primary outcome measure was change in NT-proBNP, a blood marker, between baseline and day 8 or
early discharge. The registry record was retrieved. The outcome was not.

**NCT02245620**, a phase 2 trial in 41 elderly people with documented skeletal muscle
mitochondrial dysfunction, is Completed. Its primary outcome measure was change from baseline in
ATPmax, a biochemical measure of the maximal rate of ATP production. The registry record was
retrieved. The outcome was not.

The second one is the trial closest to the popular anti-aging and mitochondrial-performance
framing, which is exactly why leaving it out would have been convenient and dishonest.

Two things are worth noticing even without the results. Both trials used a laboratory measure as
their primary endpoint, not strength, endurance, symptoms, or survival. Even a positive ATPmax
result would not establish a functional or longevity benefit, and NT-proBNP is a biomarker rather
than a clinical outcome. And an unknown result is not a hint. It does not lean positive because
the compound is interesting, and it does not lean negative because the rest of the programme
struggled. It is simply not known, and querying the registry results sections is a named task for
a human reviewer.

## 9. I compete in a tested sport. Is it banned?

**This Guide does not know, and it will not guess.**

The official WADA Prohibited List page returned empty content when it was fetched for this
research (2026-07-21), so no anti-doping classification for elamipretide or SS-31 is asserted
anywhere in this folder.

There is a claim circulating that elamipretide moved off WADA class S0 for 2026, on the basis
that the September 2025 FDA approval removed its unapproved-substance status. Every source making
that claim in this research was a peptide-vendor or affiliate blog. Those are market claims, not
evidence, and the people making them have a direct commercial interest in an it-is-not-banned
conclusion.

Check the official WADA Prohibited List directly, and check with your own national anti-doping
organisation. Do not rely on this Guide, and certainly do not rely on a vendor, because the
consequence of being wrong falls entirely on you and a sanctioning panel will not accept a
website as a defence.

## 10. What would change your mind?

A specific list, so that this position can be tested rather than simply asserted.

- **A completed randomized controlled trial in healthy adults**, with a functional outcome such
  as endurance, strength, or fatigue, and published results. NCT07275424 is recruiting. Its
  results, when they exist, are the single most relevant future evidence for this Guide's
  audience.
- **A positive confirmatory trial in Barth syndrome.** The accelerated approval depends on it.
  NCT07531251 was recruiting. A confirmed clinical benefit would convert a conditional approval
  into a demonstrated one for that indication.
- **A successful phase 3 in geographic atrophy** (ReNEW, NCT06373731, or ReGAIN), read in light
  of the fact that its primary endpoint was selected after ReCLAIM-2's primary endpoints failed.
- **The posted results of NCT02245620 and NCT02914665.** Both are currently unknown to this
  review and either could move the picture.
- **A systematic review or meta-analysis** synthesising the programme independently of the
  sponsor. None was retrieved.
- **Independent analytical testing of material sold as SS-31**, for identity and purity, published
  by a party with no commercial stake. Nothing of the kind was located.
- **The FDA review documents.** The approval letter and Integrated Review PDFs returned HTTP 404
  during research, so the agency's own critique of this evidence base has not been read.

If those appeared, this Guide would be updated, the change would be recorded in its correction
history with a date, and the claim grades would move. Note that the grades here are not uniformly
low: the negative trial findings are already graded B, and the regulatory facts are graded A. It
is the healthy-adult benefit claim that sits at G, and that is where new evidence would matter
most.

## 11. What should I do with all this?

Nothing in this Guide is a recommendation, in either direction. It is an account of the evidence.

If SS-31 came up because you are tired, or because your training has stalled, or because you are
thinking seriously about aging, those are real questions and they deserve a real conversation.
The more useful version of that conversation is about the underlying issue, with a clinician who
knows your history. The Guide includes a list of questions written to be taken into that
conversation as they stand.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides. This
FAQ is educational. It is not medical advice, it is not a recommendation, and it is not an offer
to supply anything. Xenios does not sell SS-31 or elamipretide.
