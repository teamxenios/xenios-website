# SS-31 Contradictions and Divergences

Where the sources disagree, and what the Guide should say when they do. Source ids refer to
SOURCE_REGISTRY.md. Claim ids refer to CLAIM_TABLE.md.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

The contradictions here are unusually consequential. This compound has a real FDA approval
sitting on top of a trial programme whose controlled results largely failed. A reader who sees
only the approval, and a reader who sees only the failures, both come away with a wrong picture.
Holding both at once is the whole task.

## C-01. Does elamipretide work in Barth syndrome?

**Position A.** The randomized, placebo-controlled crossover phase of TAZPOWER (NCT03098797)
**missed both co-primary endpoints**, six-minute walk distance and the Barth Syndrome Symptom
Assessment total fatigue score, in 12 participants (S03, S04).

**Position B.** The FDA granted accelerated approval in September 2025 on the basis of
improvement in knee extensor muscle strength, an intermediate endpoint, and the uncontrolled
168-week open-label extension reported sustained functional and cardiac improvement (S01, S04).

**Assessment.** Both are true, and the tension between them is the single most important thing
to communicate honestly in this Guide. The controlled evidence did not hit its prespecified
targets. The approval rests on an intermediate surrogate endpoint under a pathway designed for
serious rare diseases with unmet need, and the label itself states that continued approval may
depend on confirmatory trials. Regulatory approval here is a conditional bet, not a settled
demonstration of clinical benefit. It also says nothing whatsoever about healthy adults.

There is a second point worth making to a member. Accelerated approval is not a lower standard
applied carelessly. It is a deliberate policy choice to let people with a serious, ultra-rare,
life-limiting disease and no alternative access a therapy earlier, while the evidence is
completed afterwards. That is a defensible decision for that population. It is not a
transferable endorsement.

**What the Guide says.** Present the failure and the approval together, in that order, with all
four qualifiers on the approval. See SS31-H-04, SS31-H-05, and the prohibitions at SS31-X-02 and
SS31-X-03.

## C-02. Does elamipretide improve mitochondrial myopathy?

**Position A.** MMPOWER-3 (NCT03323749), the largest trial in the programme at 218 participants,
**failed both co-primary endpoints**. The registry status is Terminated, with the registry
recording that the double-blind portion did not achieve its primary endpoints, and the reported
six-minute walk difference numerically favoured placebo (S02, S05).

**Position B.** A prespecified subgroup with nuclear DNA pathogenic variants, and a later
post-hoc genotype analysis, reported improvement (S02, S05).

**Assessment.** The trial is a failure. Subgroup rescue after a missed primary endpoint is
hypothesis-generating at best and is one of the most common sources of false positives in
clinical research. The reason is structural rather than a matter of anyone's good faith: once a
trial has missed its primary endpoint, there are many ways to slice the data, and some slices
will look favourable by chance alone. A prespecified subgroup is better than a post-hoc one, and
neither is a result.

Any Guide text must lead with the failure, not the subgroup.

**Verification note.** The specific numeric effect size and confidence interval came from
secondary reporting. The Neurology full text was not retrievable this session, so those numbers
are flagged and require human verification (S05, SS31-H-02). The direction and the failure
itself are verified from the registry (S02) and do not depend on the unverified numbers.

**What the Guide says.** See SS31-H-01, SS31-H-02, SS31-H-03, and the prohibition at SS31-X-04.

## C-03. Mouse rejuvenation versus human function

**Position A.** Aged **mice** show reversed decline in mitochondrial ATP production, improved
coupling of oxidative phosphorylation, restored redox balance, and increased treadmill exercise
tolerance, without an increase in mitochondrial content (S10).

**Position B.** No completed randomized trial retrieved this session shows a functional
performance, fatigue, or longevity benefit in healthy humans. The relevant healthy-aging trial
is still recruiting and has no results (S09). Separately, a mouse study title reports improved
muscle function **without** detectable change in epigenetic or transcriptomic age (S11).

**Assessment.** The mouse-to-human gap is the whole story for the consumer positioning of this
compound. Strong rodent data plus repeated human trial failures in the exact functional
endpoints that matter, walk distance and fatigue, argues for caution rather than enthusiasm.

This is worth naming as a general pattern and not just a fact about SS-31. When rodent data are
strong and human trials in the same functional domain are negative, the honest inference is that
the rodent model did not predict the human result. Marketing tends to run the inference the
other way, treating the human failures as noise around a mechanism that "obviously" works.

**A note on the convenient source.** S11 undercuts reverses-aging marketing, which makes it
useful to this Guide's position. It is also the source recorded from a title listing rather than
a retrieved full text. It is flagged as requiring human verification precisely because it is
convenient. A packet that verifies inconvenient claims strictly and convenient ones loosely is
not honest, it is just differently biased.

**What the Guide says.** See SS31-P-01, SS31-P-02, SS31-H-10, and the prohibitions at SS31-X-06
and SS31-X-07.

## C-04. WADA status

**Position A.** Vendor and affiliate blogs assert that elamipretide moved off WADA class S0 for
2026, on the basis that the September 2025 FDA approval removed its unapproved-substance status.

**Position B.** The official WADA Prohibited List page returned empty content on fetch this
session, and no official source confirms this (S13).

**Assessment.** Unresolved, and it must stay unresolved on the page rather than being closed by
inference. No WADA claim may be published in the Guide until an official WADA source is checked.

Two reasons for the strictness. First, the sources making the claim are peptide vendors and
affiliates, who have a direct commercial interest in an it-is-not-banned conclusion. Second, the
consequence of being wrong falls entirely on the athlete, who does not get to cite a Guide at a
hearing. Any athlete-facing statement must be checked against the official WADA list and against
the athlete's own national anti-doping organisation before publication.

**What the Guide says.** No status is asserted. Athletes are referred to the official list and
to their national anti-doping organisation. See SS31-U-04 and the prohibition at SS31-X-09.

## C-05. Where the approved drug and the research chemical are treated as the same thing

This is not a disagreement between sources. It is a gap between what a name implies and what a
product is, and it is the most consequential way a member could be misled about this compound.

Elamipretide appears under multiple names across the literature: SS-31, MTP-131, Bendavia,
elamipretide, and FORZINITY (S01, S02). The FDA approved a specific manufactured product with a
defined formulation that has been through quality review. Material sold online as a research
chemical has had none of that review, and its identity, purity, and content are unverified
(S01). No analytical study testing grey-market SS-31 specifically was retrieved, so nothing is
known about it in either direction.

The failure mode is precise: an approval granted to FORZINITY becomes, through the shared
molecule name, an implied endorsement of a vial labelled SS-31. Guide copy must not let the
approved-drug name lend borrowed credibility to an unapproved research chemical bought under a
different name.

**What the Guide says.** See SS31-I-02, SS31-I-03, SS31-Q-01, SS31-Q-04, and the prohibition at
SS31-X-08.

## C-06. Where a positive signal was selected after the data were seen

In ReCLAIM-2 (NCT03891875, 176 participants with geographic atrophy), the trial did not beat
placebo on its primary endpoints of low-luminance best-corrected visual acuity change and
geographic atrophy lesion area. Secondary and exploratory signals on ellipsoid zone preservation
were then used by the sponsor to select the primary endpoint for the subsequent phase 3
programme, ReNEW (NCT06373731) and ReGAIN (S06).

This is not a contradiction between sources so much as a pattern a reader needs named. Choosing
the endpoint that worked, after seeing which one worked, substantially weakens what a later
positive result on that endpoint would mean. It is not misconduct, and moving a programme
forward on an exploratory signal is a normal commercial decision. But it changes the
interpretation, and a member reading a future ReNEW or ReGAIN headline should know where that
endpoint came from.

**Verification note.** Enrollment and status are verified from the registry. The primary
Ophthalmology Science publication returned HTTP 403, so no numeric secondary result from this
trial appears anywhere in this folder.

**What the Guide says.** See SS31-H-06, SS31-H-07, and the prohibition at SS31-X-05.

## C-07. Where the record is simply silent, and silence is being read as a result

Two completed trials sit in this record with unknown outcomes: NCT02914665 (308 participants
hospitalized with heart failure, primary outcome change in NT-proBNP) and NCT02245620 (41
elderly participants with documented skeletal muscle mitochondrial dysfunction, primary outcome
change in ATPmax). Registry records were retrieved. Results were not (S07, S08).

NCT02245620 is the trial closest to the popular anti-aging and mitochondrial-performance
framing, which makes the temptation to fill its silence in one direction or the other strongest
exactly where the evidence is weakest. It is recorded as unknown. Note also that its primary
endpoint is a biochemical measure rather than strength, endurance, or any lived outcome, so even
a positive result would not establish a functional or longevity benefit. The same applies to
NT-proBNP in the heart failure trial, which is a biomarker rather than a clinical outcome such
as survival, hospitalisation, or symptoms.

**What the Guide says.** Both are reported as unknown to this review, with their endpoints named
so a reader can see what the endpoint would and would not have shown. See SS31-H-08,
SS31-H-09, SS31-U-03, and the prohibition at SS31-X-14.

## C-08. Jurisdictional differences

No genuine conflict between jurisdictions was found, because only one jurisdiction produced
retrievable statements. The United States position is described in REGULATORY_STATUS.md. The
anti-doping position could not be established (S13, C-04 above). No other national regulator was
consulted.

The absence of a conflict here reflects the absence of retrievable information from other
jurisdictions, not agreement among them.

## C-09. A note on the integrity of the regulatory record

The FDA approval letter and the Integrated Review PDFs on accessdata.fda.gov both returned HTTP
404 on direct fetch. The label content was recovered through DailyMed, an authoritative NIH
mirror of FDA labelling, which is why the label-based statements in this folder are graded
highly. But the FDA review documents, which would contain the agency's own critique of the
evidence base, were never read.

That matters more than usual here. This Guide's central position is that a conditional approval
sits on top of a programme of failed controlled trials. The agency's own assessment of exactly
that question exists, in a document this research could not open. A reviewer must retrieve it
before publication.

Separately, the regulatory history items (the Complete Response Letter of 15 May 2025, and the
Orphan Drug, Fast Track, Priority Review, and Rare Pediatric Disease designations with a voucher
granted at approval) rest on the sponsor press release only (S12). They are attributed to the
sponsor and flagged, never stated as FDA record. See the prohibition at SS31-X-11.
