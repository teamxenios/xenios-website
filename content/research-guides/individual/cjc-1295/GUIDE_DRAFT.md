# CJC-1295

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## One-minute summary

CJC-1295 is a laboratory-made peptide (a short chain of amino acids) designed to prompt the pituitary
gland to release more of the body's own growth hormone. The single most important fact in this Guide is
not about effect or safety. It is about identity.

- **Two different molecules are sold under the one name "CJC-1295".** One carries an added chemical
  group called DAC that makes it long-acting. The other, more accurately called modified GRF(1-29), does
  not. They behave very differently in the body.
- **Essentially all of the human research is on the DAC version.** Three human studies of CJC-1295 exist
  in the PubMed-indexed literature, published between 2006 and 2009. All were in healthy volunteers, all
  came from the same investigator group linked to the original developer, and all studied the DAC form.
- **Those studies measured hormones, not outcomes.** They reported that the DAC compound raised growth
  hormone and IGF-1 for days after administration. No retrieved human study measured body composition,
  strength, recovery, sleep, injury healing, cognition, or any longevity endpoint. Raising a hormone
  level is not the same as producing a benefit, and the benefit was never tested.
- **The version without DAC has no controlled human studies at all.** A 2026 peer-reviewed review states
  it is uncharacterised in the peer-reviewed human literature and that no controlled clinical studies
  have directly evaluated it in humans.
- **The only trial in people with a condition to treat produced nothing public.** A Phase 2 study in 120
  participants with HIV-associated visceral obesity was terminated, and no results were ever posted.
- **On safety, the honest statement is that long-term safety data do not exist for either version.** The
  longest human exposure identified ran 49 days. The concerns that exist are mechanism-based rather than
  observed.
- **No approved product containing CJC-1295 in any form was identified in any country.**
- **It is prohibited in sport at all times**, not only in competition.
- **What is actually inside a product sold under this name is unknown.** CJC-1295 has been identified by
  laboratory analysis in an unlabelled seized preparation, and the peer-reviewed literature describes an
  unregulated market for these peptides with compositional variability.

A reader can encounter confident half-life and safety figures for a product that were measured on a
different molecule. That misattribution is the practical hazard this Guide exists to name.

**Claim grade for "CJC-1295 produces any clinical benefit in humans": G (Unverified).**
**Claim grade for "CJC-1295 with DAC raises growth hormone and IGF-1 in healthy adults": C (Early human
evidence), from three small studies by one investigator group.**
**Claim grade for any statement about modified GRF(1-29) in humans: G (Unverified), because no controlled
human study of it was found.**

This Guide is educational. It is not medical advice, it is not a recommendation, and it is not an offer
to supply anything.

## What it is

CJC-1295 is a synthetic analog of the first 29 amino acids of growth hormone-releasing hormone, written
GHRH(1-29). An analog is a molecule deliberately altered from a natural one. It was developed by
ConjuChem Biotechnologies.

Its class is GHRH receptor agonist, also described as a growth hormone secretagogue. That means it is
intended to act on the pituitary gland so that the body releases more of its own growth hormone, rather
than supplying growth hormone directly from outside.

### The two molecules that share one name

**1. CJC-1295 with DAC** (also written CJC-1295 DAC, or DAC:GRF). DAC stands for drug affinity complex,
a maleimidopropionyl-lysine group added to the peptide. That group binds covalently to albumin
circulating in the blood, which greatly extends how long the compound persists. This is the ConjuChem
clinical drug candidate. In healthy adults its estimated half-life was 5.8 to 8.1 days (Teichman 2006).
Essentially all published human data on "CJC-1295" is data on this form.

**2. CJC-1295 without DAC**, more accurately called modified GRF(1-29), mod GRF 1-29, or
tetrasubstituted GRF(1-29). This has the amino acid substitutions but not the albumin-binding DAC group,
so it is short-acting. A 2026 peer-reviewed review in Frontiers in Endocrinology states plainly that this
compound is uncharacterised in the peer-reviewed human literature, and that no controlled clinical
studies have directly evaluated it in humans.

The consequence is direct. Human pharmacokinetic and safety findings for the DAC form do not transfer to
the no-DAC form, and the reverse is equally true. A page that cites the 2006 human trial next to a "no
DAC" product is misattributing evidence from a different molecule. The two are also marketed for
different purposes (sustained elevation versus a short pulse), which makes the mislabelling consequential
rather than pedantic.

The specific amino acid positions commonly cited for the substitutions in modified GRF(1-29) appeared
only in vendor marketing material during source gathering and were not confirmed against any
peer-reviewed or regulatory source. They are therefore excluded from this Guide.

### Compounds a reader may confuse it with

- **Sermorelin**, which is unmodified GHRH(1-29).
- **Tesamorelin**, an FDA-approved GHRH analog, and a different molecule.
- **CJC-1293**, a related developmental compound.
- **The GHRP or ghrelin-mimetic class**, including ipamorelin, GHRP-2, GHRP-6 and MK-677, which act on a
  different receptor entirely.

Evidence about any of these does not carry across to CJC-1295.

## Why interest exists

Describing why people are interested is not an endorsement of the reasoning.

Interest in CJC-1295 comes from a simple chain of thought. Growth hormone declines with age. Growth
hormone and IGF-1 are involved in tissue repair and body composition. A compound that raises those
hormones using the body's own pituitary, rather than supplying growth hormone from outside, is presented as a
more natural or more controlled way to reach the same end.

The 2006 human data gave that chain a real anchor at its first link. The DAC compound did raise growth
hormone and IGF-1 in healthy adults, and did so with the natural pulsing pattern of hormone release
preserved in one small study. That is a genuine finding.

The chain breaks at its second link. No retrieved human study tested whether raising those hormones with
this compound produced any change a person would notice or value. The compound is discussed online in
connection with body composition, recovery, sleep and healing, and none of those endpoints was measured
in any human study identified. Interest has run far ahead of what was tested.

A further driver of interest is availability. The compound circulates in an unregulated market where
confident-sounding figures are attached to products, and where, as described below, the product name
itself does not establish which molecule is present.

## Terminology

Every technical term used in this Guide, in plain English.

- **Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids, the building blocks that make up proteins.
- **Amino acid.** One of the individual chemical units that link together to form peptides and proteins.
- **Analog.** A molecule deliberately modified from a natural one, so that it is similar but not
  identical.
- **Growth hormone (GH).** A hormone released by the pituitary gland, involved in growth, tissue repair
  and metabolism.
- **Growth hormone-releasing hormone (GHRH).** The body's own signalling hormone that tells the pituitary
  to release growth hormone. [UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check] GHRH is
  produced in the hypothalamus, a region of the brain.
- **GHRH(1-29).** The first 29 amino acids of that hormone, which is the active portion the compound is
  modelled on.
- **Pituitary gland.** A small gland at the base of the brain that releases growth hormone and other
  hormones.
- **Receptor agonist.** A substance that binds to a cell receptor and switches it on, imitating the
  natural signal.
- **Secretagogue.** A substance that prompts a gland to secrete something, here growth hormone.
- **IGF-1 (insulin-like growth factor 1).** A hormone produced largely in response to growth hormone, and
  the marker usually measured to judge growth hormone activity.
- **DAC (drug affinity complex).** A chemical group added to the peptide so that it attaches to albumin
  in the blood and stays in the body far longer.
- **Albumin.** [UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check] The most abundant protein
  in blood plasma, used here as a carrier the DAC group binds to.
- **Half-life.** The time it takes for half of a substance to be cleared from the body. A long half-life
  means the substance persists.
- **Pharmacokinetics.** What the body does to a substance over time, including how long it lasts.
- **Pharmacodynamics.** What a substance does to the body, such as changing a hormone level.
- **Phase 1 trial.** An early human study, usually small and in healthy volunteers, focused on safety and
  on how the substance behaves in the body rather than on whether it helps anyone.
- **Phase 2 trial.** A later study in people who actually have the condition being targeted, designed to
  look for an effect.
- **Randomised, placebo-controlled, double-blind.** The standard design for a fair test. Participants are
  assigned by chance to the substance or to an inactive placebo, and neither participants nor
  investigators know who received which.
- **Terminated (trial status).** A registry status meaning the study was stopped before completion.
- **Preclinical.** Research done in animals or in cells in a dish, not in people.
- **In vitro.** In a dish or test tube, outside a living body.
- **Narrative review.** An article in which authors summarise a literature in their own framing. It is a
  lower grade of evidence than a systematic review, which follows a pre-declared search and appraisal
  method.
- **Proteomics.** The study of the whole set of proteins present in a sample, here blood serum.
- **Mitogenic.** Tending to stimulate cells to divide. The relevance is that anything encouraging cell
  division raises a theoretical question about tumour growth.
- **Tachyphylaxis.** A diminishing response to a substance with repeated use.
- **Pulsatility.** The natural pattern in which growth hormone is released in bursts rather than
  steadily.
- **Bulk drug substance.** The active ingredient itself, before it is made into a finished medicine.
- **503A compounding.** A United States framework under which a pharmacy prepares a medicine for an
  individual patient. Which bulk substances may lawfully be used is governed by an FDA list.
- **Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee (PCAC).** The FDA advisory committee that considers which
  bulk substances belong on that list.
- **Federal Register.** The official daily journal of the United States government, where such meetings
  and decisions are formally announced.
- **WADA.** The World Anti-Doping Agency, which publishes the list of substances prohibited in sport.

## Proposed mechanism

Everything in this section is a **proposed** mechanism. It describes what the compound was designed to
do and what was measured, not a settled account of what happens in a person who takes it.

The intended mechanism is that the compound acts as an agonist at the GHRH receptor on the pituitary
gland, prompting release of the body's own growth hormone, which in turn raises IGF-1. Because the
signal is applied at the pituitary rather than by supplying growth hormone directly, the design intent is
that the body's own regulation remains involved.

The DAC modification is the second half of the design. By binding albumin in the blood, it was intended
to keep the compound present for days rather than minutes, producing sustained rather than brief
stimulation.

What has actually been demonstrated for this pathway, and in what:

- **In rats**, human GRF(1-29)-albumin bioconjugates activated the GRF receptor on the anterior
  pituitary, and this work identified CJC-1295 as a long-lasting GRF analog (Jetté 2005, rat study). This
  is the originating characterisation of the albumin-bioconjugate design and is a rodent finding.
- **In humans**, the DAC form was reported to raise growth hormone and IGF-1 for days after
  administration in healthy adults (Teichman 2006), and in a separate small study in healthy men the
  natural pulsing pattern of growth hormone release was reported to be preserved while overall levels
  rose (Ionescu 2006).

Two limits belong in the same breath as that mechanism.

First, the mechanism is only established as far as hormone levels. No retrieved human study followed the
chain further, to whether the raised hormones changed anything about the person.

Second, and more importantly, this mechanistic account is drawn from work on the DAC form. It is
routinely applied to the no-DAC form in commercial material, and a 2026 peer-reviewed review states that
the no-DAC compound has no controlled human studies at all.

Claim grade for the proposed mechanism as applied to the DAC form in humans, at the level of hormone
measurement: **C (Early human evidence)**.
Claim grade for the mechanism as applied to modified GRF(1-29) in humans: **G (Unverified)**.

## Human evidence

Three human studies of CJC-1295 exist in the PubMed-indexed literature. All three were published between
2006 and 2009, all were in healthy volunteers, all came from the same investigator group linked to the
original developer, and all studied the DAC form. There is also one registered trial that produced no
public results.

### 1. Teichman 2006, the principal human study

**Design.** Two randomised, placebo-controlled, double-blind, ascending-dose Phase 1 trials, of 28 and 49
days, at two investigational sites. Teichman SL, Neale A, Lawrence B, Gagnon C, Castaigne JP, Frohman LA.
J Clin Endocrinol Metab. 2006 Mar;91(3):799-805. PMID 16352683.

**Population.** Healthy adults aged 21 to 61 years. The exact enrollment number is not stated in the
retrieved abstract, and the full text was not obtained.

**Reported findings.** Dose-dependent increases in mean plasma growth hormone (2- to 10-fold, sustained
6 days or more) and in mean plasma IGF-1 (1.5- to 3-fold, sustained 9 to 11 days) after administration.
Estimated half-life 5.8 to 8.1 days. The authors concluded the compound was safe and relatively well
tolerated in that setting.

**How to read the authors' safety conclusion.** It applies to a small, short, healthy-volunteer study of
the DAC form, with no long-term follow-up. Xenios does not repeat it as a general statement that the
compound is safe. The correct general statement about safety appears in the Safety categories section
below.

**Limitations.** This is a Phase 1 pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic study. It measured hormone levels,
not clinical outcomes. No body composition, strength, sleep, recovery, injury-healing or longevity
endpoint was tested. It was short (weeks), small, industry-sponsored (ConjuChem authorship), and had no
long-term safety follow-up. It establishes that the DAC compound raises growth hormone and IGF-1. It does
not establish that doing so benefits anyone. It applies to the DAC form only.

Claim grade: **C (Early human evidence)** for the hormone finding. **G (Unverified)** for any benefit.

### 2. Ionescu 2006, pulsatility

**Design.** Double-blinded clinical trial with 12-hour overnight blood sampling at 20-minute intervals,
before and one week after a single administration. Ionescu M, Frohman LA. J Clin Endocrinol Metab.
2006;91(12):4792-7. PMID 17018654.

**Population.** Healthy men aged 20 to 40 years. Exact enrollment is not stated in the retrieved
abstract.

**Reported findings.** Growth hormone secretion increased with pulsatility preserved. Basal growth
hormone rose 7.5-fold, mean growth hormone rose 46 percent, and IGF-1 rose 45 percent, while the
frequency and magnitude of secretory pulses were unaltered. The authors concluded that long-acting GHRH
preparations may have clinical utility in people whose pituitary retains the capacity to secrete growth
hormone.

**Limitations.** Very small, healthy young men only, a single administration, and one week of
observation. This is an endocrine-physiology study, not an outcomes study. It shares an author with the
principal trial. It applies to the DAC form only.

Claim grade: **C (Early human evidence)** for the hormone and pulsatility finding.

### 3. Sackmann-Sala 2009, exploratory proteomics

**Design.** A proteomic substudy. Serum collected before and one week after a single administration,
analysed by two-dimensional gel electrophoresis and mass spectrometry. Sackmann-Sala L, Ding J,
Frohman LA, Kopchick JJ. Growth Horm IGF Res. 2009 Dec;19(6):471-7. PMID 19386527.

**Population.** 11 healthy young adult males.

**Reported findings.** The serum protein profile changed after administration. Two spots decreased (an
apolipoprotein A1 isoform and a transthyretin isoform) and three increased (beta-hemoglobin, a
C-terminal albumin fragment, and a mix of immunoglobulin and albumin fragments). A linear relationship
was observed between one spot and IGF-1 levels.

**Limitations.** Exploratory biomarker discovery in 11 men, with no clinical endpoint of any kind. The
authors state that the molecular mechanisms linking these proteins to growth hormone and IGF-1
biological activity remain to be clarified, so the findings are hypothesis-generating and of uncertain
meaning. It applies to the DAC form only.

Claim grade: **D to G**. Exploratory, of unclear significance, and not a claim about benefit.

### 4. NCT00267527, the Phase 2 trial that produced nothing

**Design.** Multicenter, randomised, placebo-controlled, double-blind Phase 2 study of CJC-1295 over 12
weeks. Sponsor ConjuChem (industry). Started December 2005, completion September 2006. Enrollment 120
participants.

**Population.** People with HIV infection and HIV-associated visceral obesity. This is the only
registration identified in a patient population rather than healthy volunteers.

**Findings.** None are available. The study status is Terminated, and no results have been posted to
ClinicalTrials.gov. The retrieved registry record gives no reason for termination.

**Why it appears here.** It is listed as a registration record and an evidence gap, not as evidence of
effect. No efficacy or safety conclusion of any kind can be drawn from it. It is the only registered
clinical trial of CJC-1295 found on ClinicalTrials.gov, and the compound's only test in people with a
condition to treat yielded nothing public.

### Human evidence on modified GRF(1-29), the no-DAC version

There is none that was identified. A 2026 peer-reviewed narrative review states that this compound is
uncharacterised in the peer-reviewed human literature and that no controlled clinical studies have
directly evaluated it in humans. An empty evidence table here is the accurate result, not an omission.

Claim grade for every human claim about modified GRF(1-29): **G (Unverified)**.

## Preclinical evidence

Preclinical means animal or laboratory research. Findings below are labelled with the species or model
they came from. None of them establishes anything about people.

- **In rats**, human GRF(1-29)-albumin bioconjugates activated the GRF receptor on the anterior
  pituitary, and the study identified CJC-1295 as a long-lasting GRF analog. Jetté L, et al.
  Endocrinology. 2005. PMID 15817669. This is the originating characterisation of the albumin
  bioconjugate (DAC) design. Grade **D (Preclinical)**.
- **In GHRH knockout mice** (mice genetically engineered to lack GHRH), repeated administration of
  CJC-1295 normalised growth. Alba M, et al. Am J Physiol Endocrinol Metab. 2006. PMID 16822960. A
  genetically GHRH-deficient mouse is a model of a specific deficiency and is not a model of a healthy
  adult human. Grade **D (Preclinical)**.
- **In horses**, analytical methods were developed to confirm CJC-1295 in equine plasma by LC-MS/MS and
  to screen for CJC-1295 and other GHRH analogs by immuno-PCR. Timms M, et al. Drug Test Anal. 2019.
  PMID 30938069 and PMID 30489688. These are detection methodology papers, not efficacy or safety
  studies. They are listed only to document that veterinary and equine misuse prompted assay
  development. Grade **D (Preclinical, methodology)**.

No preclinical finding here should be read forward into a human effect. The rodent work supports that the
molecule engages the intended receptor and lasts a long time. It says nothing about whether a healthy
adult benefits.

## Contradictions and disagreement

Where sources disagree, the disagreement is shown rather than resolved for the reader's convenience.

### 1. Current FDA compounding status. UNRESOLVED.

**Position A.** The 2026 Frontiers in Endocrinology peer-reviewed review states that FDA listed CJC-1295
on Category 2 of the Bulk Drug Substances List, meaning a substance that may present significant safety
risks.

**Position B.** Web search snippets and vendor or clinic marketing pages assert that CJC-1295 was removed
from Category 2 in September 2024 after its nomination was withdrawn, and separately assert removal in
April 2026, and describe it as awaiting review with no lawful compounding basis in the interim.

**Assessment.** Unresolved. Every fda.gov URL attempted during source gathering returned HTTP 404, and
downloads.regulations.gov returned HTTP 403, so no primary FDA document could be read. The two removal
dates offered by vendor pages (September 2024 and April 2026) contradict each other, which is itself a
reason to distrust them. What is verified from govinfo.gov is narrower: five CJC-1295 forms were formally
considered at the December 4, 2024 PCAC meeting, and a January 2025 FDA final guidance ended the Category
1, 2 and 3 framework for substances nominated on or after that guidance's publication date. **No current
FDA compounding status is stated in this Guide until a human opens fda.gov directly.**

### 2. Whether CJC-1295 is on the July 2026 PCAC agenda. RESOLVED AGAINST THE VENDOR CLAIM.

**Position A.** Vendor and clinic pages assert that CJC-1295 is pending a July 2026 Pharmacy Compounding
Advisory Committee review.

**Position B.** The Federal Register notice announcing the July 23-24, 2026 PCAC meeting (docket
FDA-2025-N-6895, published April 16, 2026) lists peptide substances including BPC-157, KPV, TB-500,
MOTS-C, DSIP, Semax and Epitalon. CJC-1295 does not appear.

**Assessment.** Resolved against the vendor claim on a primary government source. Treat the vendor claim
as unsupported.

### 3. What "CJC-1295" means, and what half-life it has. NOT A SCIENTIFIC DISPUTE, BUT THE CENTRAL POINT.

**Position A.** The peer-reviewed human literature (Teichman 2006) reports a half-life of 5.8 to 8.1
days, measured on the DAC form.

**Position B.** Vendor pages describe "CJC-1295 (no DAC)" as having a very short half-life on the order
of minutes, while citing the DAC-form human literature as its evidence base.

**Assessment.** These are two different molecules sharing one street name. The peer-reviewed half-life
belongs to the DAC compound. Short half-life figures for the no-DAC compound appeared only in vendor
marketing during source gathering and were not confirmed by any peer-reviewed or regulatory source, so
they are excluded from this Guide. The 2026 review states the no-DAC compound is uncharacterised in the
peer-reviewed human literature.

### 4. Whether development stopped for a safety reason. UNRESOLVED.

**Position A.** Wikipedia, a tertiary encyclopedia source, states that Phase II development was
discontinued following the death of a trial subject, that the attending physician attributed the death to
pre-existing coronary artery disease unrelated to treatment, and that research was terminated as a
precaution.

**Position B.** The ClinicalTrials.gov record for NCT00267527 shows the Phase 2 study as Terminated but
gives no reason, and no results were ever posted.

**Assessment.** Unresolved. The registry confirms termination but not its cause. No primary source for
the death account was retrieved. It must not be presented as established fact, and equally must not be
presented as debunked. **[UNVERIFIED - requires human source check.]** It is flagged for verification
against the trial sponsor record, FDA correspondence, or contemporaneous reporting before it could appear
on any member-facing surface.

## Limitations

The limitations of this Guide, stated as plainly as the limitations of the evidence.

- **Volume.** Three human studies exist in the indexed literature, all more than 15 years old.
- **Independence.** All three come from the same investigator group, connected to the developer, and the
  principal trial was industry-authored. No independent group replicated the findings in the retrieved
  record.
- **Endpoints.** Every human study measured hormone levels or serum proteins. None measured an outcome a
  person would notice.
- **Duration.** The longest human exposure identified was 49 days.
- **Population.** Two of the three human studies enrolled men only. The third enrolled adults aged 21 to
  61 without a sex breakdown in the retrieved abstract. Effects in women are unstudied in this record.
- **The one patient trial produced nothing.** 120 participants, terminated, no results posted, no reason
  given.
- **Full texts were not obtained.** Only PubMed abstracts were retrieved for the three human studies, so
  exact enrollment numbers, adverse event tables and dropout data are unverified.
- **No systematic review or meta-analysis specific to CJC-1295 was found.** The 2026 sources identified
  are narrative reviews, which is a lower evidence grade.
- **FDA sources were unreachable during source gathering.** Every fda.gov URL returned HTTP 404. This is
  the single largest gap in this record, and it means FDA's own stated position on this compound is not
  represented here.
- **Several 2026 reviews were identified but not read.** PubMed began returning challenge pages partway
  through source gathering, so the content of several recently published narrative reviews was not
  retrieved. Their existence is verified; their content is not.
- **Everything in this Guide is an AI draft with no human review.** No citation here has been
  independently verified by a person. See `EDITORIAL_REVIEW_CHECKLIST.md`.

## Quality and identity

This section describes what is known about the material that actually circulates. It is not a route to
obtaining anything.

**The name does not identify the substance.** A product labelled "CJC-1295" may be the albumin-binding
DAC compound or the short-acting modified GRF(1-29). These are pharmacologically very different. A
purchaser cannot determine which molecule is present from the common product name alone. Every vendor
page encountered during source gathering used the two names interchangeably or as parenthetical
synonyms. This is an identity defect, not a labelling nicety.

**The compound has been found in unlabelled illicit preparations.** CJC-1295 was identified by mass
spectrometry in an unlabelled pharmaceutical preparation of unknown origin submitted by Norwegian police
and customs authorities. The product's contents were unknown until analysed. Henninge J, Pepaj M,
Hullstein I, Hemmersbach P. Drug Test Anal. 2010;2(11-12):647-50. PMID 21204297. That analysis is now 16
years old and describes a seizure, not the current market.

**The supply chain is unregulated and variable.** The 2026 Frontiers in Endocrinology review states that
real-world use is complicated by quality-control uncertainty and compositional variability characteristic
of unregulated peptide supply chains, and notes this is especially acute for agents lacking any human
validation, which includes the no-DAC variant.

**A black market in these peptides is documented in the anti-doping literature.** Illicit synthesis of
analogue peptide hormones and a growing GHRH black market are described in the peer-reviewed
anti-doping analytical literature, which cites CJC-1295's identification in confiscated products.
Knoop A, Thomas A, Fichant E, Delahaut P, Schanzer W, Thevis M. Anal Bioanal Chem. 2016. PMID 26879649.

**Commercial pages cite evidence from the wrong molecule.** Vendor and retailer pages encountered during
source gathering presented specific pharmacokinetic figures for the no-DAC variant while citing the
DAC-form human trials as supporting evidence. Manufacturer and supplier claims grade **E** and are never
evidence. In this case they are also a misattribution of data across two dissimilar compounds.

**No independent product testing was found.** No purity or identity analysis of currently marketed
"CJC-1295" products was identified in the retrieved record.

Xenios has confirmed none of the quality documentation that would characterise a material containing this
compound. Every item is currently unmet. See `QUALITY_AND_DOCUMENTATION.md`.

## Safety categories

This section lists categories of concern. It is not a reassurance, and the absence of documented harm in
a short study is not evidence of safety.

**The single most important safety statement in this record: no long-term safety data exist for either
version.** The 2026 Frontiers in Endocrinology review states plainly that no long-term safety data are
available. That is consistent with what was retrieved directly. The longest human trial identified ran 49
days, and the only Phase 2 trial was terminated with no results posted. There is no post-marketing
surveillance, because there is no approved product.

**Category 1. Theoretical oncologic concern from mitogenic signalling.** Growth hormone and IGF-1
signalling encourages cell division, which raises a theoretical question about tumour growth. The 2026
review flags this while explicitly stating there is no established clinical carcinogenic signal. This is
a mechanism-based concern, not an observed harm. Both versions share it. Neither alarm nor reassurance is
supported by data. Grade **G (Unverified)**.

**Category 2. Glucose metabolism and fluid retention.** The 2026 review describes these as theoretically
plausible through somatotropic activation, and states explicitly that they lack direct peer-reviewed
documentation specifically for CJC-1295 products. They are listed here so the Guide neither hides them
nor overstates them as observed effects. Grade **G (Unverified)**.

**Category 3. Reported cardiovascular reactions. UNVERIFIED AT PRIMARY SOURCE.** The 2026 review
attributes to an FDA Category 2 listing rationale a mention of vasodilatory reactions and tachycardia
(faster heart rate). The underlying FDA document could not be retrieved during source gathering, so
neither the listing nor the stated rationale is independently confirmed here, and its currency is
unknown. Treat as a secondhand report requiring primary confirmation. Grade **G (Unverified)**.

**Category 4. Reported death of a trial subject during Phase II development. TERTIARY SOURCE ONLY, NOT
VERIFIED.** Described in the Contradictions section above. It is consistent with, but not established by,
the verified fact that NCT00267527 is Terminated with no posted results and no stated reason.
**[UNVERIFIED - requires human source check.]**

**Category 5. Evidence-transfer hazard between the two versions.** Because the no-DAC variant has no
controlled human studies, any safety reassurance drawn from the DAC Phase 1 trials is being applied to a
compound it was never tested on. This misattribution is itself a safety issue, not merely a labelling
one.

**Category 6. Unknown product identity and purity.** What is in any given product sold under this name,
including which of the two molecules it contains and at what purity, is unknown independent of every
consideration above.

**What is not known about safety.** Whether tachyphylaxis occurs with continued use, what happens beyond
seven weeks of exposure, what happens in women, what happens in people with existing conditions, and what
the 120 participants in the terminated Phase 2 trial experienced. None of these was addressed in any
retrieved source.

## Interactions

No interaction studies of CJC-1295 with any medicine, supplement or condition were identified in the
retrieved record. Nothing below is a warning derived from data, and nothing below is advice. These are
categories that a qualified clinician would need to review, precisely because the data to settle them do
not exist. Claim grade for every line in this section: **G (Unverified)**.

- **Diabetes and blood glucose management.** The 2026 review describes glucose intolerance as
  theoretically plausible through somatotropic activation while stating it is not directly documented for
  CJC-1295 products. Anyone whose glucose is medically managed is in a category where an unstudied effect
  on glucose matters.
- **Cancer history, active cancer, or cancer surveillance.** The mitogenic GH and IGF-1 concern described
  above is mechanism-based and unresolved, with no established clinical carcinogenic signal reported.
- **Growth hormone therapy, other growth hormone secretagogues, and other GHRH analogs.** No study of
  combined use was identified. Compounds acting on the same axis or on the related ghrelin receptor
  (ipamorelin, GHRP-2, GHRP-6, MK-677) were not studied in combination with CJC-1295 in any retrieved
  source.
- **Pituitary, thyroid or adrenal conditions, and any condition affecting hormone regulation.** The
  compound is intended to act on the pituitary, and the Ionescu authors framed potential utility around
  people whose pituitary retains secretory capacity.
- **Cardiovascular history.** Listed because of the unverified vasodilatory and tachycardia report and
  the unverified account of a trial death attributed by the attending physician to pre-existing coronary
  artery disease, both flagged above as requiring primary confirmation.
- **Pregnancy, attempting pregnancy, and breastfeeding.** No data of any kind were identified.
- **Competitive sport.** GHRH analogues including CJC-1295 are prohibited in sport at all times. See
  Regulatory status.
- **Any current prescription medicine.** With no interaction data available at all, no medicine can be
  described as compatible or incompatible on the basis of evidence.

## Regulatory status

Every statement below carries a jurisdiction, a date checked and a source. Regulatory status changes.
These were checked on **2026-07-19** and must be re-verified before publication.

### United States, FDA approval status

No FDA-approved drug product containing CJC-1295 in any form was identified. The only registered clinical
trial found (NCT00267527, ConjuChem, Phase 2) was terminated with no results posted. The 2026 Frontiers
in Endocrinology review states that neither the DAC nor the no-DAC variant holds FDA approval for
performance or body-composition indications.

Date checked 2026-07-19. Source: https://clinicaltrials.gov/api/v2/studies/NCT00267527 (verified).

### United States, FDA 503A compounding, December 2024 PCAC meeting

A Federal Register notice (docket FDA-2024-N-4777) announced a Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee
meeting on December 4, 2024, at which five CJC-1295 forms were being considered for inclusion on the
Section 503A Bulk Drug Substances List: CJC-1295 (free base), CJC-1295 acetate, CJC-1295 DAC (free base),
CJC-1295 DAC acetate, and CJC-1295 DAC trifluoroacetate. The notice states only that they were being
considered. It does not state FDA's recommendation. **The outcome of that meeting could not be verified
during source gathering.**

Date checked 2026-07-19. Jurisdiction United States.
Source: https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/FR-2024-10-25/html/2024-24828.htm (verified).

### United States, FDA, the Category 1, 2 and 3 framework

An FDA final guidance announced January 7, 2025 (docket FDA-2015-D-3517) ends the categorisation of bulk
drug substances into Categories 1, 2 or 3 for substances nominated on or after that guidance's
publication date. That notice does not mention CJC-1295 or peptides by name, so it does not by itself
establish CJC-1295's current status. A reader encountering "Category 2" language about this compound
should treat it as possibly describing a superseded framework.

Date checked 2026-07-19. Jurisdiction United States.
Source: https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/FR-2025-01-07/html/2024-31546.htm (verified).

### United States, the July 2026 PCAC meeting

The Federal Register notice announcing the July 23-24, 2026 Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee
meeting (docket FDA-2025-N-6895, published April 16, 2026) lists peptide substances including BPC-157,
KPV, TB-500, MOTS-C, DSIP, Semax and Epitalon. **CJC-1295 is not mentioned in that notice.** This
directly contradicts vendor pages claiming CJC-1295 is pending July 2026 PCAC review.

Date checked 2026-07-19. Jurisdiction United States.
Source: https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/FR-2026-04-16/html/2026-07361.htm (verified).

### United States, reported Category 2 listing. SECONDARY CLAIM, NOT VERIFIED AT SOURCE

The 2026 Frontiers in Endocrinology narrative review states that FDA listed CJC-1295 as prohibited on
Category 2 of the Bulk Drug Substances List, attributing the rationale to reports of vasodilatory
reactions and tachycardia. The underlying FDA document could not be retrieved during source gathering, as
fda.gov returned HTTP 404 to every attempt. Neither the listing nor the stated safety rationale is
independently confirmed here, and its currency is unknown.

Date checked 2026-07-19. Jurisdiction United States. Status **NOT VERIFIED**.
Source of the secondary claim:
https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2026.1822475/full

### World Anti-Doping Agency

GHRH analogues including CJC-1295 and CJC-1293 are prohibited in sport at all times, not in competition
only. This was confirmed from a peer-reviewed anti-doping analytical paper referencing the WADA
Prohibited List, whose reference is to the 2015 Prohibited List. The current-year WADA Prohibited List
page could not be parsed directly (it is JavaScript-rendered), so the exact current section number and
wording were not verified during source gathering.

Date checked 2026-07-19. Jurisdiction international, sport.
Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4830873/ (verified).

### Jurisdictions not checked

No regulatory position was retrieved for the European Union, the United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, or
any other jurisdiction. Silence here means not checked, not permitted.

## Unknowns

Stated plainly, because these are the honest content of this Guide.

1. Whether raising growth hormone and IGF-1 with CJC-1295 produces any clinical benefit in healthy
   adults. No retrieved human study measured body composition, strength, sleep quality, recovery, injury
   healing, cognition, or any longevity endpoint.
2. Anything at all about modified GRF(1-29), the no-DAC version, in humans. Pharmacokinetics, safety and
   effect are all unknown for that variant.
3. Long-term safety of either version. The longest human exposure identified was 49 days. There is no
   chronic-use safety data and no post-marketing surveillance, because there is no approved product.
4. Whether sustained elevation of IGF-1 by this mechanism carries real oncologic risk in humans. The
   concern is mechanism-based, and the 2026 review states no established clinical carcinogenic signal
   exists. Neither reassurance nor alarm is supported.
5. Effects on glucose metabolism and insulin sensitivity in humans taking this compound.
6. Why the ConjuChem Phase 2 trial (NCT00267527) was terminated, and what its 120 participants
   experienced. No results were ever posted.
7. Whether tachyphylaxis (a diminishing response over time) occurs with continued use. Not addressed in
   any human study retrieved.
8. What is actually in any given product sold as "CJC-1295" in the unregulated market, including which of
   the two molecules it contains and at what purity.
9. The current, definitive FDA compounding status as of July 2026.
10. Effects in women. Two of the three human studies enrolled men only, and the third reported no sex
    breakdown in the retrieved abstract.
11. The exact amino acid substitution positions in modified GRF(1-29). Those figures appeared only in
    vendor material and are excluded from this Guide.

## Questions for a qualified professional

Questions a member could reasonably take to a clinician who knows their history. Xenios does not answer
these, and this Guide is not a substitute for that conversation.

1. Given what is on this page, is there any clinical reason for me to consider a compound in this class
   at all, or is the honest answer no?
2. There are two different molecules sold under this one name. If someone brought you a product, how
   would you determine which one it actually is, and would you rely on the label?
3. The human data are three small studies in healthy volunteers that measured hormone levels only. Does
   raising growth hormone and IGF-1, on its own, mean anything clinically for someone like me?
4. My personal and family history includes the following. Does the theoretical concern about growth
   hormone and IGF-1 signalling and cell division apply differently to me?
5. I have (or am at risk of) a condition involving blood glucose. What would you want to monitor if
   somebody in my position were exposed to a compound that acts on this hormone axis?
6. Is there an approved, studied treatment for what I actually want to address, and how does its evidence
   compare with what is on this page?
7. If I were to have hormone levels checked out of curiosity, what would the results actually tell you,
   and what would they not tell you?
8. I compete in a sport with anti-doping rules. Can you help me confirm the current status of GHRH
   analogues on the applicable prohibited list?
9. The Phase 2 trial in this compound was terminated and never reported results. How much weight do you
   put on an unexplained termination when judging an unapproved compound?
10. What would you want to see published before you considered the evidence for this compound adequate?
