# Contradictions and Disagreements: CJC-1295

Where sources disagree, where preclinical and human findings diverge, where mechanism
plausibility outruns evidence, and where commercial claims conflict with the literature.
Source ids refer to SOURCE_REGISTRY.md. Session date 2026-07-19.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

## Summary of what is actually in dispute

There is very little genuine scientific disagreement about this compound, for a simple
reason: there is very little science. Three human studies exist in the indexed literature,
all from one investigator group, all in healthy volunteers, all on the same variant, and
they agree with each other. Nothing has contradicted them because nothing has tested them.

The real conflicts are of four other kinds, and each is more consequential than a scientific
dispute would be:

1. A naming conflict that causes evidence to be attributed to a molecule it was never
   measured on.
2. A regulatory conflict that this session could not resolve because the primary source was
   unreachable.
3. A conflict between commercial claims and a primary government document, which the
   government document wins outright.
4. A gap between mechanism plausibility and measured effect, which is not a contradiction at
   all but is the thing most likely to be read as one.

## C-01. What "CJC-1295" refers to, and what half-life it has

**Position A.** The peer-reviewed human literature reports an estimated half-life of 5.8 to
8.1 days, measured in healthy adults on the DAC (albumin-binding) form (S1).

**Position B.** Commercial pages describe a product sold as CJC-1295 without DAC as very
short acting, while citing the same DAC form human literature as its evidence base.

**Assessment: not a genuine scientific contradiction, and the single most important point in
this Guide.** These are two different molecules sharing one street name. The published
half-life belongs to the DAC compound. The short duration figures for the no-DAC compound
appeared only in marketing material this session and were not confirmed by any peer-reviewed
or regulatory source retrieved. A 2026 peer-reviewed review states plainly that the no-DAC
compound is uncharacterised in the peer-reviewed human literature, with no controlled
clinical studies in humans (S6).

**How the Guide resolves it.** The variant is named in the same sentence as any
pharmacokinetic or hormonal finding. The half-life figure is never printed without the DAC
label. No duration figure is printed for the no-DAC form at all. See CLAIM_TABLE.md rows
PH-02, ND-02 and P-19.

**Why this is a safety matter rather than a labelling nicety.** The two products are
marketed on opposite premises, sustained elevation versus a short pulse. A reader comparing
them is being shown confident numbers that were measured on the other molecule. The review
treats this misattribution as a safety issue in its own right (S6).

## C-02. Current FDA compounding status

**Position A.** The 2026 peer-reviewed review states that FDA listed CJC-1295 on Category 2
of the bulk drug substances list, that is, a substance that may present significant safety
risks, attributing this to vasodilatatory reactions and tachycardia reports (S6).

**Position B.** Web search snippets and commercial pages assert that CJC-1295 was removed
from Category 2 in September 2024 following withdrawal of its nomination, and separately
assert removal in April 2026, and describe it as awaiting review with no lawful compounding
basis in the interim.

**Assessment: UNRESOLVED, and not resolvable from this session's record.** Every fda.gov URL
returned HTTP 404 to automated fetch, and downloads.regulations.gov returned HTTP 403, so no
primary FDA document could be read. The two removal dates offered commercially (September
2024 and April 2026) are mutually inconsistent, which is itself a reason to distrust both.

What is verified from primary government sources: five CJC-1295 forms were listed as being
considered for inclusion on the section 503A bulk drug substances list at the December 4
2024 Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee meeting (S10), and a January 2025 FDA final
guidance ends the Category 1, 2 and 3 framework for substances nominated on or after that
guidance's publication date (S11). The second point matters here: a reader encountering
Category 2 language may be reading about a framework that has since changed, which means
Position A and Position B may not even be describing the same regime.

**How the Guide resolves it.** It does not. No current United States compounding status
appears in the Guide until a human opens fda.gov directly. See CLAIM_TABLE.md rows RG-03 and
P-10.

## C-03. Whether CJC-1295 is on the July 2026 advisory committee agenda

**Position A.** Commercial and clinic pages assert that CJC-1295 is pending a July 2026
Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee review.

**Position B.** The Federal Register notice announcing the July 23 to 24 2026 meeting
(docket FDA-2025-N-6895, published April 16 2026) lists peptide substances including
BPC-157, KPV, TB-500, MOTS-C, DSIP, Semax and Epitalon. CJC-1295 does not appear (S12).

**Assessment: RESOLVED AGAINST THE COMMERCIAL CLAIM, on a primary government source.** The
notice announcing the meeting is the authoritative statement of what that meeting will
consider. Treat the commercial claim as false, or at the most generous reading, unsupported.

**How the Guide resolves it.** RG-04 states the verified fact. P-11 prohibits the claim. This
row is also useful as a general calibration: it demonstrates that the commercial regulatory
narrative around this compound contains at least one claim that a primary document
contradicts outright, which is a reason to distrust the rest of that narrative including the
removal dates in C-02.

## C-04. Whether development stopped for a safety reason

**Position A.** A tertiary encyclopedia source states that Phase II development was
discontinued following a trial subject's death, that the attending physician attributed the
death to pre-existing coronary artery disease unrelated to treatment, and that research was
terminated as a precaution (S13).

**Position B.** The ClinicalTrials.gov record for the Phase 2 study shows it as Terminated,
gives no reason for termination, and shows that no results were ever posted (S4).

**Assessment: UNRESOLVED.** The registry confirms termination but not its cause. No primary
source for the death account was retrieved this session. The two positions are not in
conflict, they simply do not reach each other: one is an unsourced narrative, the other is a
record with a blank where the reason would be.

**How the Guide resolves it.** Neither asserted nor dismissed. Publishing it as fact would
put an unverified death claim in front of members. Publishing a denial would be equally
unsupported, and would risk suppressing a real safety signal. It is flagged for human
verification against the sponsor record, FDA correspondence and contemporaneous reporting.
See CLAIM_TABLE.md rows SF-06, P-08 and P-17.

## C-05. Preclinical findings versus human findings

There is no direct contradiction between the animal work and the human work, because they
measured different things in different organisms. There is, however, a divergence in what
they permit anyone to say.

| Preclinical finding | Human record on the same question |
|---|---|
| In a mouse genetically lacking growth hormone-releasing hormone, repeated administration normalized growth (S15) | No human study retrieved measured growth, body composition, or any anthropometric outcome. The population studied was healthy adults with intact pituitary function, not people with a deficiency (S1, S2, S3, S5, S9) |
| In rats, the albumin bioconjugate activated the growth hormone-releasing factor receptor on the anterior pituitary (S14) | Consistent with the human hormonal findings in direction, but the human studies stop at hormone levels. Receptor activation in rats does not establish any downstream human effect |

**Assessment: divergence, not contradiction.** The risk is one directional. A reader shown a
knockout mouse that grew normally will infer a growth or body composition effect in healthy
adults, and nothing in the human record supports that inference. A GHRH deficient animal is
not a model of a healthy adult human, and normalizing a deficiency is a different claim from
enhancing a normal state.

**How the Guide resolves it.** Species or model appears inside every preclinical sentence,
never after it. See CLAIM_TABLE.md rows PC-01 to PC-03 and P-13.

## C-06. Mechanism plausibility outrunning evidence

Three claims sit in the gap between what a mechanism suggests and what has been measured.
Each is a place where the Guide must be careful in both directions.

**Benefit.** The DAC form raises growth hormone and IGF-1 for days, with pulsatility
preserved (S1, S2). It is mechanistically coherent to expect downstream effects on tissue.
No retrieved human study measured any. The mechanism is not in doubt. The benefit is
entirely unmeasured. Stating the first without the second is the most likely way this Guide
could mislead.

**Oncologic risk.** Sustained growth hormone and IGF-1 signalling is mitogenic, and the 2026
review flags this as an oncologic concern while stating explicitly that there is no
established clinical carcinogenic signal (S6). Both halves are load bearing. Printing the
concern alone implies an observed harm that does not exist in the record. Printing the
absence of a signal alone implies a safety finding, when in fact no study of adequate
duration or size to detect such a signal has been performed. The honest statement is that
neither reassurance nor alarm is supported.

**Glucose intolerance and fluid retention.** The same review describes these as
theoretically plausible through somatotropic activation, while stating they lack direct
peer-reviewed documentation specifically for CJC-1295 products (S6). They are recorded in
this Guide precisely so they are not overstated as observed effects.

**Assessment: none of these is a contradiction between sources.** All three are the same
structural situation: a plausible mechanism with an empty outcome table. That is the honest
state of the evidence and the Guide should say so rather than resolving the tension in
either direction.

## C-07. Jurisdictional differences

**United States.** No approved product was identified. The compounding status is unresolved
(C-02). See REGULATORY_STATUS.md.

**Sport, World Anti-Doping Agency.** Growth hormone-releasing hormone analogues including
CJC-1295 are prohibited at all times, not in-competition only, confirmed through a
peer-reviewed anti-doping paper referencing the 2015 Prohibited List (S7). The current year
list page could not be parsed, so the current section number and exact wording are
unverified.

**Everywhere else.** Nothing was searched and nothing is known. No European, United Kingdom,
Australian, Canadian or other national regulator was consulted this session.

**Assessment: not a contradiction between jurisdictions, an absence of coverage.** The
important editorial point is the one that holds regardless: a status in one jurisdiction is
never a status in another, and the absence of a prohibition in a jurisdiction that was never
searched is not permission. See CLAIM_TABLE.md rows RG-06, RG-07 and P-12.

## C-08. Commercial claims versus the literature, as a pattern

Taken together, the commercial material encountered this session conflicts with the
retrieved record in four distinct ways. This is recorded as a pattern because a member is
far more likely to encounter that material than to encounter the literature.

1. **Misattributed pharmacokinetics.** Human trial figures from the DAC form presented as
   properties of the no-DAC form (C-01).
2. **A regulatory claim contradicted by a primary government notice.** The July 2026
   advisory committee agenda claim (C-03).
3. **Mutually inconsistent regulatory narratives.** Two different removal dates offered for
   the same event (C-02).
4. **Specific figures for a compound with no controlled human studies.** Confident numbers
   for a variant that a peer-reviewed review describes as uncharacterised in humans (S6).

**Assessment.** Commercial and retailer pages are disqualified as evidence for this Guide by
policy, so none of this changes any grade. It is documented because the pattern itself is
informative: the confident, specific, well presented figures a member will find are the ones
with the weakest basis, and the honest answer to most questions about this compound is that
nobody has measured it.

## What is not in dispute

For balance, the following are consistent across every source retrieved and are contradicted
by nothing in the record:

- CJC-1295 is a synthetic analog of the first 29 amino acids of growth hormone-releasing
  hormone, acting at the GHRH receptor (S1, S6, S14).
- The DAC form raised growth hormone and IGF-I in healthy adults in the studies that
  measured it (S1, S2).
- Only three human studies of this compound exist in the indexed literature, and one
  registered trial exists in the registry (S5, S9).
- That registered trial was terminated with no results posted (S4).
- No long-term safety data exist for either variant (S6, S1).
- The compound circulates in unlabeled and unregulated form (S7, S8, S6).
