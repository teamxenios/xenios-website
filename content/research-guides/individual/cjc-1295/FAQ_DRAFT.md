# CJC-1295: questions members actually ask

These answers are honest rather than encouraging. Where the answer is that nobody knows, that is what is
written.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## 1. Does it work?

That depends entirely on what "work" means, and the honest answer splits into two parts.

If "work" means raising growth hormone and IGF-1 levels, then for the version with DAC, yes, that has
been measured in humans. Three small studies in healthy volunteers reported that the DAC compound raised
both hormones for days after administration, with the natural pulsing pattern of release preserved in one
of them.

If "work" means producing a result you would notice or value, that has never been tested. No retrieved
human study measured body composition, strength, recovery, sleep, injury healing, cognition or any
longevity endpoint. Every human study measured hormone levels or blood proteins. A hormone number moving
is not an outcome.

The one trial designed to look for a real-world effect, a Phase 2 study in 120 participants with
HIV-associated visceral obesity, was terminated and never posted results. So the compound's only test in
people with a condition to treat produced nothing public.

Claim grade for "raises growth hormone and IGF-1 in healthy adults, DAC version": **C (Early human
evidence)**.
Claim grade for "produces any clinical benefit in humans": **G (Unverified)**.

Sources: PMID 16352683 https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16352683/ ; PMID 17018654
https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17018654/ ; NCT00267527
https://clinicaltrials.gov/api/v2/studies/NCT00267527 (all verified 2026-07-19)

## 2. What is the difference between "CJC-1295" and "CJC-1295 no DAC", and does it matter?

It matters more than anything else on this page.

They are two different molecules sold under one name. The DAC version carries an added chemical group
that binds to albumin in the blood and keeps the compound present for days. In healthy adults its
estimated half-life was 5.8 to 8.1 days. The version without DAC, more accurately called modified
GRF(1-29), lacks that group and is short-acting.

Essentially all published human research on "CJC-1295" was done on the DAC version. A 2026 peer-reviewed
review states that the no-DAC version is uncharacterised in the peer-reviewed human literature, and that
no controlled clinical studies have directly evaluated it in humans.

The practical consequence is that a reader can encounter a confident half-life figure, a safety
reassurance, or a citation to a real 2006 clinical trial attached to a product that is not the molecule
that trial studied. During source gathering, every vendor page encountered used the two names
interchangeably or as parenthetical synonyms. A purchaser cannot tell which molecule they have from the
common product name alone.

Claim grade for any human claim about the no-DAC version: **G (Unverified)**.

Source: Frontiers in Endocrinology 2026 review
https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/endocrinology/articles/10.3389/fendo.2026.1822475/full (verified
2026-07-19)

## 3. Is it safe?

The correct answer is not that it is safe, and not that it is dangerous. It is that no one knows, and
that the studies needed to find out have not been done.

The 2026 peer-reviewed review states plainly that no long-term safety data are available for either
version. That matches what was retrieved directly. The longest human trial identified ran 49 days. The
only Phase 2 trial was terminated with no results posted. There is no post-marketing surveillance,
because there is no approved product anywhere.

The authors of the 2006 Phase 1 study concluded the compound was safe and relatively well tolerated in
that study. That conclusion applies to a small, short, healthy-volunteer study of the DAC version with no
long-term follow-up. It is not a general statement that the compound is safe, and Xenios does not repeat
it as one.

The concerns that do exist are mechanism-based rather than observed. Growth hormone and IGF-1 signalling
encourages cell division, which raises a theoretical question about tumour growth, and the 2026 review
flags this while explicitly stating there is no established clinical carcinogenic signal. Glucose
intolerance and fluid retention are described in that review as theoretically plausible but lacking
direct peer-reviewed documentation specifically for CJC-1295 products.

There is also a structural safety problem specific to this compound. Because the no-DAC version has no
human studies, any reassurance drawn from the DAC trials is being applied to a compound it was never
tested on.

Claim grade for every safety line above: **G (Unverified)**.

## 4. Is it legal?

The honest answer is that the regulatory picture could not be established at primary source, and this
Guide declines to state a current status rather than guess.

What is verified, as of 2026-07-19:

- No FDA-approved drug product containing CJC-1295 in any form was identified in any country.
- Five CJC-1295 forms were formally considered at the December 4, 2024 Pharmacy Compounding Advisory
  Committee meeting, per a Federal Register notice (docket FDA-2024-N-4777). The notice states only that
  they were being considered. The outcome of that meeting could not be verified.
- A January 2025 FDA final guidance ended the Category 1, 2 and 3 framework for bulk substances nominated
  on or after its publication date. So "Category 2" language about this compound may describe a
  superseded framework.
- The Federal Register notice for the July 23-24, 2026 PCAC meeting does not list CJC-1295. Vendor claims
  that it is pending that review are unsupported.
- GHRH analogues including CJC-1295 are prohibited in sport at all times, not in competition only.

What could not be verified: FDA's actual position. Every fda.gov URL returned an error during source
gathering, so no primary FDA document could be read. Vendor pages offered two mutually inconsistent
removal dates (September 2024 and April 2026), which is itself a reason to distrust them.

No regulatory position was checked for the European Union, the United Kingdom, Canada, Australia or any
other jurisdiction. Silence here means not checked, not permitted.

Sources: https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/FR-2024-10-25/html/2024-24828.htm ;

## 6. Why is the evidence so thin if this has been around since 2005?

Because development stopped, and nobody restarted it.

Only three human studies of CJC-1295 exist in the PubMed-indexed literature. They were published in 2006,
2006 and 2009. All three were in healthy volunteers, all came from the same investigator group connected
to the original developer, and all studied the DAC version. No independent group replicated them in the
retrieved record.

The single Phase 2 trial, the one that would have tested whether the compound helped anyone, was
terminated and never reported results. After that, the commercial development path ended. There is no
approved product, so there is no company running the large trials that would normally follow.

What filled the gap was not more research. It was a market. The compound now circulates in an unregulated
supply chain, and commercial pages cite the small 2006 studies as though they settle questions those
studies never asked, sometimes about a molecule those studies never used.

No systematic review or meta-analysis specific to CJC-1295 was found. The recent sources are narrative
reviews, which is a lower grade of evidence.

## 7. If it raises growth hormone, isn't that enough to expect a benefit?

That reasoning is the reason interest exists, and it is also exactly where the evidence stops.

Raising a hormone level is a measurement, not a result. Whether raising growth hormone and IGF-1 in a
healthy adult with this compound changes body composition, recovery, sleep, healing or anything else was
not tested in any retrieved human study. The step from "the number moved" to "the person is better off"
has to be demonstrated, and it has not been.

There is a second reason not to assume the benefit follows. The same signalling that makes the mechanism
attractive, encouragement of cell growth and division, is the source of the theoretical oncologic concern
described above. A mechanism cuts both ways until outcomes are actually measured.

## 8. Is it detectable, and what happens in competitive sport?

GHRH analogues including CJC-1295 and CJC-1293 are prohibited in sport at all times, not only in
competition. That is confirmed from a peer-reviewed anti-doping analytical paper referencing the WADA
Prohibited List. The current-year list page could not be parsed during source gathering, so the exact
current section number and wording were not verified, and an athlete should confirm against the current
list directly.

On detection, the peer-reviewed literature describes methods developed specifically to identify these
compounds. Analytical methods have been published for confirming CJC-1295 in human plasma, and, in
horses, for confirming it in equine plasma and screening for GHRH analogs. The existence of that
literature is a reasonable indication that detection is an active field.

Source: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4830873/ (verified 2026-07-19)

## 9. I read that someone died in a trial. Is that true?

It is reported, it is not verified, and this Guide will not tell you it is either established or
debunked.

The account appears in Wikipedia, a tertiary encyclopedia source. It states that Phase II development was
discontinued following the death of a trial subject, that the attending physician attributed the death to
pre-existing coronary artery disease unrelated to treatment, and that research was terminated as a
precaution. No primary source for that account was retrieved during source gathering.

What is verified is narrower and does not settle it. The ClinicalTrials.gov record for NCT00267527 shows
the Phase 2 study as Terminated, with no reason given and no results ever posted. The registry confirms
termination, not its cause. The two facts are consistent with each other, which is not the same as one
proving the other.

## 10. What would change your mind?

Naming this in advance is how a Guide stays honest rather than merely cautious.

The position stated here would change if any of the following were published and independently verified.

- **A controlled human trial with a clinical endpoint.** Randomised, placebo-controlled, adequately
  powered, measuring something a person experiences rather than a hormone level. A positive result would
  move the relevant claim toward grade B or C. A well-run negative result would move it toward a clear
  statement that it does not work for that endpoint, which would be equally useful.
- **Any controlled human study of modified GRF(1-29) at all.** Right now every human claim about the
  no-DAC version grades G because the studies do not exist. One good study would change the entire shape
  of this Guide.
- **Long-term safety data.** Exposure well beyond 49 days, with monitoring, in a population large enough
  to detect uncommon effects. That is the gap that currently makes any safety statement impossible.
- **Independent replication.** All three existing human studies come from one investigator group linked
  to the developer. Replication by an unconnected group would strengthen even the hormone-level finding.
- **Publication or release of the NCT00267527 results,** and a stated reason for termination. That single
  disclosure would resolve two open items on this page at once.
- **Primary FDA documentation.** The current compounding status here is unresolved only because fda.gov
  could not be reached. A human opening those documents would settle it either way.
- **Independent product testing.** Analysis of currently marketed products showing which molecule they
  contain and at what purity would address the identity problem, which no amount of clinical research
  addresses on its own.

If any of these appears, the change is recorded in the correction history table in `GUIDE_DRAFT.md`, with
the date, what changed, and who verified it.
