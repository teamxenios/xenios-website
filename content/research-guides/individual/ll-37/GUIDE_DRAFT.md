# LL-37 (human cathelicidin antimicrobial peptide)

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## One-minute summary

LL-37 is a peptide (a short chain of amino acids) that the human body already makes on its own. It
is part of the immune system's first line of defence. Because of that, it is often described online
as a natural immune-support compound, and it is sold by unregulated vendors on that basis.

The honest verdict is more specific, and more negative, than the way it is usually promoted.

- Real human randomised trials of LL-37 do exist. That already makes LL-37 unusual in this
  category. But there are only three or four of them, they are small, and they point in a mixed
  direction.
- Every human trial retrieved for this Guide studied LL-37 applied to the surface of a chronic
  wound, or injected directly into a skin tumour. No retrieved human trial gave LL-37 in a way
  that would circulate through the body generally. That is exactly the way it is most often sold.
- The largest and most rigorous human trial, in 148 people with long-standing leg ulcers, did not
  meet its main pre-planned goal. Complete wound closure was statistically indistinguishable from
  placebo (26.5 percent and 24.7 percent in the two active groups, versus 25.3 percent for
  placebo).
- A widely circulated figure of "28.1 percent versus 8.1 percent" comes from that same trial. It
  is a real number, but it comes from an analysis done after the main result had already failed,
  restricted to a subgroup of people with larger wounds. Vendor pages tend to quote it without
  saying that the main result failed. That omission is the single most important correction in
  this Guide.
- An earlier, promising 34-person trial reported much better healing than placebo. It did not
  replicate when tested properly at larger size. It should be read as superseded, not as a second
  positive study.
- In a separate trial in diabetic foot ulcers, LL-37 was linked with more granulation tissue
  (new repair tissue in a wound), but it did not significantly reduce inflammatory markers, and it
  did not significantly reduce bacterial colonisation compared with placebo. For a compound
  described as an antimicrobial peptide, that last point matters.
- No retrieved human trial enrolled healthy people. Every one enrolled people with a specific
  disease.
- On safety, the correct statement is not that LL-37 is safe. The topical wound trials reported no
  safety concerns that the investigators attributed to treatment, which is genuinely reassuring
  for those specific short-term uses, and says essentially nothing about long-term use, use
  throughout the body, or use in healthy people.
- The peer-reviewed literature also documents a serious theoretical concern in the other
  direction. LL-37 is recognised as an autoantigen (a molecule the immune system can mistakenly
  attack, or use as a trigger) in psoriasis, and is implicated in lupus biology. This is the part
  of the picture that promotional material generally omits.

Xenios does not sell LL-37, does not supply it, and does not tell members how to obtain or use it.
This Guide exists so that a member reading about LL-37 elsewhere has an accurate account of what is
and is not known.

**Overall claim grade for "LL-37 improves health outcomes in healthy adults": G (Unverified).**
**Claim grade for "topical LL-37 improves closure of hard-to-heal venous leg ulcers": C, and the
largest trial testing it was negative on its primary endpoint.**

## What it is

LL-37 is a peptide made of 37 amino acids. It is the only cathelicidin (a family of antimicrobial
defence peptides) found in humans. The body produces it from a larger precursor protein called
hCAP-18, which is made from instructions in the CAMP gene. Epithelial cells (the cells lining
skin, gut and airways) and immune cells, particularly neutrophils (a type of white blood cell),
produce it.

It has recognised roles in innate immunity (the fast, general-purpose part of the immune system
you are born with), in wound healing, and in tuning immune signalling.

Names and related terms you may encounter: cathelicidin, cathelicidin LL-37, hCAP-18/LL-37, CAMP
gene product, CAP-18.

### The most important distinction in this Guide

LL-37 is unusually easy to misread in the literature, because the same name covers two completely
different things.

1. **Your own LL-37, measured as a marker.** Most published studies indexed under "LL-37" measure
   a person's naturally produced LL-37 in saliva, gum fluid, blood or skin, as an outcome or a
   biomarker. These are not studies of giving LL-37 to a person. In a search of the
   ClinicalTrials.gov registry that returned 50 records for the term "LL-37" (retrieved
   2026-07-19), only two studies actually administered LL-37 as the intervention. Nearly all the
   rest were observational, or were vitamin D or probiotic trials using LL-37 as a readout.
2. **LL-37 given as a product.** This is the small evidence base described below.

Two further sources of confusion:

- **Vitamin D trials.** Many "LL-37" trials are vitamin D studies asking whether supplementation
  raises the body's own LL-37. Raising your own LL-37 is a different intervention from taking
  LL-37.
- **Engineered analogues.** Much of the laboratory literature concerns modified versions of LL-37
  (chemically altered, shortened, or ring-closed), built specifically because the natural peptide
  is unstable. Findings on those modified versions do not transfer to the natural peptide.

## Why interest exists

The interest is easy to understand, and it is not irrational.

LL-37 sits at an attractive intersection. It is naturally occurring in humans rather than
synthetic, it belongs to the immune system rather than to a hormonal pathway, and its described
biology covers three things people want at once: fighting microbes, healing wounds, and modulating
inflammation. A molecule the body already trusts is a persuasive story.

Vendors and online communities build on that story in several ways: as broad immune support, as an
antimicrobial for infections that conventional treatment has not resolved, as a wound and skin
repair aid, and occasionally, on the strength of one four-person cancer trial, as an anti-tumour
agent.

Describing that interest is not the same as endorsing it. The gap between the story and the
retrieved evidence is wide, and it runs in a consistent direction: the appeal rests on general
biology, while the actual human trials are narrow, route-specific, and mostly negative on their
pre-planned goals. Where a mechanism is genuinely dual-natured, as this one is, an attractive story
is not a reason to assume the favourable half applies.

## Terminology

Terms are glossed here and at first use in the text.

- **Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids. Shorter than a protein.
- **Cathelicidin.** A family of antimicrobial peptides involved in innate immune defence. LL-37 is
  the only one humans make.
- **Endogenous.** Produced by the body itself, as opposed to given as a product.
- **Innate immunity.** The fast, general-purpose immune defence present from birth, as distinct
  from the learned, targeted adaptive immune response.
- **Neutrophil.** A common type of white blood cell, among the first responders to infection.
- **Cationic and amphipathic.** Positively charged, and having both water-attracting and
  fat-attracting regions. These properties allow a peptide to interact with cell membranes.
- **In vitro.** In laboratory glassware or culture, using cells or biochemistry outside a living
  organism.
- **Preclinical.** Research done in animals or in cultured cells, not in people.
- **Randomised controlled trial (RCT).** A study where participants are assigned by chance to
  treatment or comparison, which is what allows a result to be attributed to the treatment.
- **Placebo-controlled and double-blind.** The comparison group receives an inactive treatment,
  and neither participants nor investigators know who received which.
- **Primary endpoint.** The single main outcome a trial commits to in advance. A trial that misses
  its primary endpoint is a negative trial, regardless of what else was found.
- **Post-hoc analysis.** An analysis decided on after the results were seen. It can generate
  hypotheses. It cannot confirm them.
- **Subgroup analysis.** An analysis restricted to part of the enrolled population.
- **Surrogate endpoint.** An intermediate measure used as a stand-in for an outcome that matters
  to a patient. Granulation index is a surrogate for wound healing; complete wound closure is the
  outcome that matters.
- **Granulation tissue.** The new tissue that forms in a healing wound.
- **Venous leg ulcer.** A chronic open wound on the lower leg caused by poor vein function.
- **Diabetic foot ulcer.** A chronic open wound on the foot occurring as a complication of
  diabetes.
- **Compression therapy.** Standard care for venous leg ulcers, using bandaging or stockings.
- **Cytotoxicity.** The capacity to damage or kill cells.
- **Erythrocyte, lymphocyte, fibroblast.** A red blood cell, a type of immune cell, and a
  connective tissue cell that builds structural repair material.
- **Haemolysis.** Rupture of red blood cells.
- **Proteolytic degradation.** Being broken apart by the body's protein-cutting enzymes.
- **Autoantigen.** A molecule belonging to the body that the immune system treats as a target or
  trigger, as happens in autoimmune disease.
- **Plasmacytoid dendritic cell.** An immune cell specialised in producing interferon.
- **Toll-like receptor (TLR7, TLR9).** Immune sensors that detect patterns associated with
  infection, including nucleic acids.
- **Type I interferon.** A family of immune signalling molecules central to antiviral defence and
  to several autoimmune diseases.
- **Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE, lupus).** An autoimmune disease that can affect many organs.
- **Psoriasis.** An immune-driven inflammatory skin disease.
- **Pleiotropic.** Having many different effects.
- **Angiogenesis.** Formation of new blood vessels.
- **Re-epithelialisation.** Regrowth of the surface cell layer over a wound.

## Proposed mechanism

Everything in this section is **proposed**. Almost all of it is characterised in cultured cells or
in animal systems, not demonstrated as the operating mechanism in a person given LL-37. The species
or model is stated with each finding. Claim grade for the mechanism as a whole: **D (Preclinical).**

**Direct action on microbes.** LL-37 is a cationic amphipathic peptide, and the proposed primary
mechanism is direct disruption of microbial membranes, with reported activity against
gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria, some fungi and some viruses, characterised in
laboratory systems (PMC12386566, retrieved 2026-07-19).

**Immune modulation and wound repair.** Beyond direct antimicrobial action, LL-37 is described as
pleiotropic, influencing chemotaxis (the recruitment of immune cells), inflammatory signalling,
angiogenesis and re-epithelialisation. This is the stated rationale for the wound-healing trials
rather than a demonstrated human mechanism.

**The same property is the toxicity.** This is the point most often left out. A 2025 review reports
that in cultured human cells, LL-37 can damage the membranes of erythrocytes, lymphocytes and
fibroblasts at concentrations similar to those required for antimicrobial activity (PMC12386566,
retrieved 2026-07-19). On this account the separation between antimicrobial effect and damage to
the body's own cells is narrow by mechanism, not by accident.

**A second, opposite mechanism: driving autoimmunity.** LL-37 binds the body's own DNA and other
nucleic acids, and in cultured human immune cells these complexes are reported to activate
plasmacytoid dendritic cells through TLR9 and TLR7, driving a type I interferon response. This is
proposed as a driver of psoriatic inflammation and as a contributor to lupus biology (PMC7388365,
retrieved 2026-07-19). The same molecule is therefore proposed as host defence and as an
autoimmune driver, depending on context and level.

**Stability argues against systemic activity.** The 2025 review reports, from biochemical and
in vitro work, that the natural peptide is susceptible to being cut apart by enzymes, is unstable
at high salt concentrations, and shows low stability in biological fluids (PMC12386566). This is a
mechanistic argument against the natural peptide having durable activity through the body, and it
is the stated reason the research field has moved towards modified versions.

## Human evidence

Human interventional evidence for LL-37 exists, which is genuinely uncommon in this category. It
comprises three published randomised trials and one very small registry trial. All of it concerns
application to the surface of a chronic wound, or direct injection into a skin tumour. None of it
involved healthy people. None of it involved giving LL-37 in a way that would circulate generally
through the body.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides. Study
design is described only to make the strength and scope of the evidence assessable.

### 1. Phase IIb trial in hard-to-heal venous leg ulcers (the largest trial, primary endpoint missed)

- **Reference.** Mahlapuu M, et al., *Wound Repair and Regeneration*, 2021. PMID 34687253, PMCID
  PMC9298190, DOI 10.1111/wrr.12977. VERIFIED 2026-07-19.
- **Design.** Multicentre, prospective, double-blind, randomised, placebo-controlled, across 15
  sites in Poland and Sweden. Three groups (two active, one placebo). A 3-week open run-in, a
  13-week randomised treatment period, and 16 weeks of follow-up. Applied to the wound alongside
  standard compression therapy.
- **Population.** 149 randomised, 148 treated, with hard-to-heal venous leg ulcers. Mean age about
  67.6 years, median ulcer duration about 20.3 months, mean wound size about 11.6 cm2 at
  randomisation.
- **Result.** The primary endpoint was complete wound closure sustained at two weeks after closure
  was confirmed. It was **not met**. Confirmed complete closure occurred in 26.5 percent and 24.7
  percent of the two active groups, versus 25.3 percent of placebo. The differences were not
  statistically significant.
- **The subgroup finding.** A post-hoc analysis restricted to people with larger wounds (10 cm2 or
  above) showed a statistically significant benefit in one active group: 28.1 percent closure
  versus 8.1 percent for placebo, P = 0.0458. No benefit was detected in smaller wounds.
- **Limitations.** The subgroup figure was generated after the primary endpoint had already
  failed. Post-hoc subgroup findings are hypothesis-generating, not confirmatory, and are a
  well-recognised source of false positives. The trial was topical, in a specific chronic wound
  population, under compression therapy, in an elderly group. It says nothing about use throughout
  the body, general immune support, or use in healthy adults. No independent replication of the
  subgroup finding was retrieved.
- **Claim grade.** C for the primary question (early human evidence, and negative on the
  pre-planned outcome). G for the large-wound subgroup benefit (unverified, unreplicated).

### 2. First-in-man dose-response trial in venous leg ulcers (superseded)

- **Reference.** Gronberg A, Mahlapuu M, Stahle M, Whately-Smith C, Rollman O, *Wound Repair and
  Regeneration*, 2014 Sep-Oct. PMID 25041740. VERIFIED 2026-07-19.
- **Design.** Randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled dose-response trial. A 3-week open
  run-in on placebo, a 4-week randomised double-blind treatment phase, and 4 weeks of follow-up.
  Applied to the wound.
- **Population.** 34 participants with hard-to-heal venous leg ulcers.
- **Result.** The two lower-strength groups showed improved healing compared with placebo. The
  lowest-strength group showed approximately sixfold higher healing rates (p = 0.003), with mean
  ulcer area decreasing by 68 percent in that group and 50 percent in the next. On safety, the
  authors reported that there were no safety concerns regarding local or systemic adverse events.
  Notably, the response did not increase consistently with increasing strength.
- **Limitations.** Very small (n = 34), a short treatment period, and explicitly a first-in-man
  dose-finding study rather than a confirmatory efficacy trial. Its signal was subsequently not
  confirmed on the primary endpoint of the much larger trial above, by overlapping authors, in the
  same condition. It should be read as a superseded early signal, not as standing positive
  evidence, and not as a second supporting trial.
- **Claim grade.** C, downgraded in practice by non-replication.

### 3. Randomised trial in diabetic foot ulcers (mixed, mostly negative on biology)

- **Reference.** *Archives of Dermatological Research*, 2023 Nov. PMID 37480520. VERIFIED
  2026-07-19 (abstract retrieved; full text not retrieved).
- **Design.** Randomised, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial of a topical LL-37 cream versus
  placebo cream, with wound measurement at several timepoints over four weeks, plus measurement of
  inflammatory markers and bacterial colonisation from wound fluid.
- **Population.** People with diabetic foot ulcers with mild infection. The exact enrolled number
  was not stated in the retrieved abstract. A plausible corresponding registration exists
  (NCT04098562, Phase 2, listed enrolment 40, status "Unknown"), but this Guide did not confirm
  that the registration and the publication are the same study, so the link is treated as
  unverified.
- **Result.** Mixed, and mostly negative on the biological endpoints. The increase in granulation
  index was consistently greater in the LL-37 group across measurement points. However, treatment
  did **not** significantly reduce inflammatory cytokines (IL-1 alpha and TNF-alpha), and did
  **not** significantly reduce bacterial colonisation compared with placebo.
- **Limitations.** Small and single-region. Granulation index is a surrogate measure of wound
  progress, not confirmed wound closure. The pre-specified antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory
  endpoints were not met. No adverse events were reported in the retrieved abstract, which is not
  the same as an affirmative statement that none occurred. Exact enrolment, randomisation detail
  and safety reporting were not verified.
- **Claim grade.** C for the granulation surrogate. The antimicrobial and anti-inflammatory claims
  were tested here and were not supported.

### 4. Phase 1/2 melanoma trial (too small to inform anything about efficacy)

- **Reference.** NCT02225366, M.D. Anderson Cancer Center. Results posted 9 December 2021.
  VERIFIED 2026-07-19.
- **Design.** Interventional Phase 1/Phase 2 trial of LL-37 injected directly into cutaneous
  tumours, with tumour photography and measurement.
- **Population.** 4 participants (actual enrolment) with melanoma with skin metastases. Three of
  the four completed; one withdrew for lack of efficacy. The study started 8 July 2015 with
  primary completion 24 November 2020, meaning more than five years to enrol four people.
- **Result.** Adverse events were reported as generally mild, with no serious adverse events
  reported. No efficacy conclusion is possible.
- **Limitations.** Four participants is far too few to support any efficacy conclusion, and one of
  the four withdrew for lack of efficacy. Direct injection into a skin tumour does not generalise
  to any other use. The very slow enrolment and small final number suggest a trial that did not
  proceed as planned. Vendor claims that LL-37 has shown significant potency against cancer on the
  basis of this trial are not supportable from this record. The efficacy results tables posted on
  the registry were not read directly for this Guide, and are logged as a gap.
- **Claim grade.** G (Unverified) for any anti-cancer claim.

### What is not in this table, and why

- An oral LL-37 study relating to COVID-19 surfaced as a preprint (a manuscript posted before peer
  review), described as small-scale, single-arm and exploratory. The document could not be
  retrieved. Being a preprint without a control group, it would be low-grade evidence even if
  retrieved. It is deliberately excluded rather than included on the strength of a search snippet.
- No systematic review or meta-analysis of LL-37 human trials was retrieved. It is possible none
  exists, given how few interventional human studies there are, but this was not confirmed.

## Preclinical evidence

Preclinical means animal or laboratory-dish work. It does not establish what happens in a person.
The species or model appears in the same sentence as every finding below. Claim grade for this
section: **D (Preclinical).**

- **Damage to human cells in culture.** A 2025 review reports that in cultured human cells, LL-37
  can damage the plasma membranes of erythrocytes, lymphocytes and fibroblasts at concentrations
  similar to those required for antimicrobial activity. The same review notes that elevated LL-37
  levels have been reported in people with ulcerative colitis, psoriasis, rosacea and chronic
  periodontitis, and discusses these in the context of host cell damage at inflamed sites. This is
  recorded here as safety-relevant, not as evidence of benefit.
  (PMC12386566, VERIFIED 2026-07-19.)
- **Instability.** The same review reports, from biochemical and in vitro characterisation, that
  the natural peptide is susceptible to enzymatic cleavage, is unstable at high salt
  concentrations, shows low stability in biological fluids, and breaks down rapidly in biological
  environments. It lists low stability, cytotoxicity and high production cost as the principal
  barriers to clinical application, and describes chemical modification strategies developed to
  overcome them. (PMC12386566, VERIFIED 2026-07-19.)
- **Autoimmune signalling.** A review of LL-37 in lupus reports that, in cultured human
  plasmacytoid dendritic cells, LL-37 complexed with the body's own DNA activates those cells
  through TLR9 to trigger type I interferon production. In human blood measurements, the same
  review states that people with lupus have higher blood levels of LL-37 than healthy individuals,
  and reports antibodies against LL-37 in patients that correlate positively with anti-DNA
  antibodies, suggesting neutrophil-derived antimicrobial peptides may act as triggers for the
  antibody-producing arm of the immune system. The review itself acknowledges inconsistency,
  noting that some studies show no association between LL-37 and disease activity or organ
  involvement. (PMC7388365, VERIFIED 2026-07-19.)

One further preclinical article was surfaced by title in a search listing and was **not retrieved
or read** for this Guide. Its content is therefore not stated here in any form, and it is logged in
the Limitations section as an item requiring human retrieval before any use.

## Contradictions and disagreement

Four genuine disagreements matter here. The first is the most consequential.

**1. Whether the largest trial was a positive result.**
Vendor and affiliate pages present the Phase IIb leg ulcer trial as supportive, quoting "28.1
percent on LL-37 versus 8.1 percent on placebo" as the headline. The published trial reports that
the primary endpoint was not met: confirmed complete wound closure was 26.5 percent and 24.7
percent in the active groups versus 25.3 percent in placebo, not statistically significant.
*Assessment:* the primary source decisively contradicts the vendor framing. This was a negative
trial on its pre-planned primary endpoint. The quoted numbers are real, but they are presented
stripped of the fact that they are post-hoc, restricted to a subgroup, and produced after the main
result failed. This is the single most important correction in this Guide.

**2. Whether the early positive signal replicated.**
The 2014 first-in-man trial (n = 34) reported approximately sixfold higher healing rates versus
placebo. The 2021 Phase IIb trial (n = 148), by overlapping authors in the same condition, failed
to confirm this on its primary endpoint.
*Assessment:* this is a textbook case of a promising small early signal failing to replicate at
adequate sample size. The 2014 result is superseded, not additive. Presenting the two trials
together as "two positive trials" would be misleading.

**3. Whether LL-37 is protective or harmful.**
One framing describes a host-defence peptide with antimicrobial, wound-healing and
immune-modulating benefit. The other describes a recognised autoantigen in psoriasis, implicated
in lupus biology through complexes with the body's own DNA and type I interferon, elevated in
several inflammatory diseases, and damaging to human cells in culture at concentrations near its
antimicrobial range.
*Assessment:* both are supported in the retrieved peer-reviewed literature. This is a genuinely
dual-natured, context-dependent and concentration-dependent molecule, not a case of one side being
wrong. Any account presenting only the host-defence half is materially incomplete.

**4. An antimicrobial peptide that did not reduce bacteria.**
LL-37 is characterised mechanistically as a broad-spectrum antimicrobial peptide. In the diabetic
foot ulcer trial, the one retrieved human trial that directly measured it, topical LL-37 did not
significantly reduce bacterial colonisation compared with placebo.
*Assessment:* a notable gap between mechanism and measured human outcome. Mechanistic plausibility
did not translate.

## Limitations

Limitations of the evidence itself:

- **Route.** All retrieved human evidence is topical wound application or direct injection into a
  skin tumour. Nothing is known from these trials about giving LL-37 in a way that circulates
  through the body, which is how it is typically promoted.
- **Population.** Every retrieved human trial enrolled people with a specific disease: chronic
  venous ulcers, diabetic foot ulcers, or metastatic melanoma. No retrieved trial enrolled healthy
  people.
- **Size.** The largest trial treated 148 people. The others treated 34, an unstated number
  (probably around 40), and 4.
- **Duration.** The longest retrieved randomised treatment period was 13 weeks.
- **Direction.** The largest trial was negative on its primary endpoint. The most-quoted positive
  number is post-hoc. The most mechanistically central claim, antimicrobial effect, was tested in
  one human trial and was not supported.

Limitations of this Guide's research process, stated so a reader can judge its reliability:

- Only the abstract of the diabetic foot ulcer trial was retrieved. Exact enrolment, randomisation
  detail and the adverse event section are unverified.
- The efficacy results tables for the melanoma registry record were not read directly. Only
  enrolment, participant disposition and a summary safety statement were retrieved.
- One preclinical article was surfaced by title only in a search listing and was not retrieved or
  read. Its content is not stated anywhere in this Guide. A human reviewer must retrieve and read
  the full record before it is used or dismissed.
- Regulatory confirmation failed on several attempts, detailed in the Regulatory status section.
- No non-United States regulators were searched. No statement about status in the European Union,
  United Kingdom, Australia or Canada should be made.
- Whether the diabetic foot ulcer publication corresponds to registration NCT04098562 was not
  confirmed.

## Quality and identity

LL-37 sold outside a regulated pharmaceutical supply chain has no independent guarantee that the
contents match the label. This section separates what is documented from what is inferred, because
that distinction matters.

**What could not be verified.** A search for documented purity, counterfeiting and mislabelling
problems in the unregulated peptide market returned only vendor, affiliate and testing-service
marketing pages. Under the source rules used for these Guides, those are not acceptable evidence.
Specific figures circulating on such pages, including claims about the proportion of grey-market
products with incorrect sequences, the proportion falling below a stated purity, discrepancy rates
in certificates of analysis, contamination claims, and a reported enforcement action against a
vendor, are **not independently verified and are not reproduced here as fact.** A human reviewer
should source any purity or adulteration claim from regulator warning letters, regulator import
alerts, or peer-reviewed analytical chemistry literature.

**What can be reasoned from the peer-reviewed literature.** Two identity hazards follow directly
from published characterisation of the molecule, rather than from a market survey.

1. **Degradation.** The peer-reviewed literature establishes that the natural peptide is
   proteolytically unstable and degrades rapidly in biological fluids (PMC12386566, VERIFIED
   2026-07-19). A product that is genuinely LL-37 at manufacture may not still be intact LL-37
   after storage and shipping, and an unregulated supplier has no obligation to demonstrate
   otherwise.
2. **Substitution by analogue.** Much of the research field works with modified versions of LL-37
   rather than the natural peptide, precisely because of that instability. A product labelled
   "LL-37" could be a fragment or an engineered analogue whose evidence base is not the evidence
   base described in this Guide.

Both points are inferences drawn from published stability and modification findings. Neither is a
documented finding about a specific marketed product.

The practical consequence: for an unregulated product, a member cannot assume that what is in the
container is LL-37, that it is intact, or that it is the same substance studied in the trials above.
See `QUALITY_AND_DOCUMENTATION.md` for the documentation a supplier would need to provide, and the
current state of that documentation, which is that Xenios has confirmed none of it.

## Safety categories

This section lists **categories of concern**. It is not a safety assessment, and nothing in it
should be read as reassurance. The honest summary is that short-term topical use in chronic wounds
was reported without treatment-attributed safety concerns, and that this tells a member almost
nothing about the way LL-37 is generally sold.

**1. Damage to the body's own cells (preclinical).**
In cultured human cells, LL-37 damages the membranes of red blood cells, lymphocytes and
fibroblasts at concentrations similar to those needed for antimicrobial activity (PMC12386566).
The implication is that the separation between antimicrobial effect and damage to human cells is
narrow by mechanism. This has not been established as a clinical event rate in people.

**2. Autoimmune and interferon-driven inflammation (mixed preclinical and human observational).**
This is the most substantive theoretical concern in the record. LL-37 combined with the body's own
DNA activates plasmacytoid dendritic cells through TLR9 in cultured human cells, driving type I
interferon. LL-37 is described as a key autoantigen in psoriasis and is implicated in lupus
biology, with higher blood levels reported in people with lupus than in healthy individuals, and
antibodies against LL-37 correlating with anti-DNA antibodies (PMC7388365). This is an association
and a mechanism in people who already have autoimmune disease. It is **not** a demonstrated
consequence of giving LL-37 to a person, because no retrieved trial studied that. It is
nonetheless the concern most relevant to anyone with, or predisposed to, psoriasis or lupus. The
source review itself notes that some studies find no association with disease activity.

**3. Elevated LL-37 accompanies several inflammatory diseases (human observational).**
Elevated LL-37 has been reported in ulcerative colitis, psoriasis, rosacea and chronic
periodontitis. Direction of causation is not established by those observations; LL-37 may be a
marker of inflammation rather than a driver. It is recorded because it complicates any framing in
which more LL-37 is straightforwardly better.

**4. Effects of substances of unverified identity or purity.**
Because the unregulated market cannot be assumed to supply intact, correctly identified LL-37, the
risks of an unregulated product include the risks of whatever else is in it. Those risks cannot be
characterised from any retrieved source.

**5. Everything not studied.**
No retrieved human trial studied use throughout the body, use beyond 13 weeks, repeated long-term
exposure, or use in healthy people. Absence of reported harm in settings that were never studied is
not evidence of absence of harm.

**What the trials did report, stated precisely.** In the Phase IIb leg ulcer trial (n = 148),
local reactions including redness, swelling and raised temperature were mostly mild to moderate
with no consistent differences between groups; serious adverse events occurred in 12 patients (7.4
percent) but were assessed by the investigators as unrelated to treatment. The 2014 trial (n = 34)
reported no safety concerns for local or systemic adverse events. The four-person melanoma trial
reported generally mild adverse events and no serious adverse events. That record applies to
topical wound application over weeks, and to direct tumour injection in four people. It should not
be generalised.

## Interactions

No interaction studies for LL-37 were retrieved. The following are categories that would require
review by a qualified professional, derived from the mechanisms documented above rather than from
any interaction study.

- **Autoimmune disease and immune-modulating treatment.** Anyone with psoriasis, lupus, or another
  interferon-driven autoimmune condition, and anyone taking immunosuppressive, biologic or
  interferon-modulating treatment, falls squarely inside the documented mechanistic concern.
- **Active infection and antimicrobial treatment.** A compound described as antimicrobial that did
  not reduce bacterial colonisation in the one human trial measuring it should not be assumed to
  contribute to, or to be neutral alongside, treatment of an infection.
- **Wound care and chronic wounds under active management.** The human trials were conducted
  alongside standard care, including compression therapy for venous ulcers. Wound management is a
  clinical matter, and self-directed changes to it carry real consequences.
- **Diabetes with foot complications.** Diabetic foot ulceration is a condition where delay in
  proper care leads to serious outcomes.
- **Cancer and oncology treatment.** The only relevant trial involved four people and direct
  tumour injection, and supports no conclusion.
- **Anticoagulation, bleeding disorders, and conditions affecting red blood cells.** Given
  documented membrane damage to red blood cells in cultured human cells, this is a category for
  professional review rather than a documented interaction.
- **Pregnancy and breastfeeding.** No retrieved study addressed either.
- **Competitive sport.** See Regulatory status. Anti-doping status could not be verified for this
  Guide, and an athlete should not assume anything from its absence here.

## Unknowns

These are the things nobody knows, stated plainly. This list is not a gap in the Guide. It is the
result.

1. Whether giving LL-37 in a way that circulates through the body is safe or effective in humans
   for any purpose. No retrieved human trial used that route. This is the largest single unknown,
   and it is directly relevant to how LL-37 is marketed.
2. Whether the large-wound subgroup benefit in the Phase IIb trial is real. It has not been tested
   in advance in any retrieved study, and has not been replicated.
3. Whether giving LL-37 can trigger or worsen psoriasis, lupus, or other interferon-driven
   autoimmune disease in susceptible people. The mechanism is documented. The clinical consequence
   of administration has not been studied in any retrieved trial.
4. Whether the damage to red blood cells, lymphocytes and fibroblasts seen in cultured human cells
   translates into any real cell damage at exposures achieved in a person.
5. Long-term safety of repeated exposure. The longest retrieved randomised treatment period was 13
   weeks, applied topically.
6. Whether the natural peptide survives in the body long enough to do anything at all beyond the
   site it is applied to, given documented rapid breakdown and low stability in biological fluids.
7. Any effect in healthy adults. Every retrieved human trial enrolled people with a specific
   disease.
8. The efficacy outcome of the four-person melanoma trial. Results are posted on the registry, but
   only enrolment, disposition and a summary safety statement were retrieved for this Guide.
9. Whether the diabetic foot ulcer publication corresponds to the registration NCT04098562.
10. Whether any documented purity, adulteration or counterfeiting data exists for LL-37 products
    from a source that meets the evidence standard for these Guides.

## Questions for a qualified professional

These are questions a member could genuinely take to a clinician. They are framed to get useful
answers, not to seek permission.

1. I have read that the largest randomised trial of LL-37 did not meet its main endpoint. Does
   that match your reading of the evidence, and does anything in the published literature change
   your view?
2. Every human trial I can find applied LL-37 to a wound or injected it into a tumour. Is there any
   evidence you are aware of for other uses in people?
3. I have (or have a family history of) psoriasis or lupus. LL-37 is described in the literature as
   an autoantigen in psoriasis and is implicated in lupus biology. What would that mean for me?
4. Published work reports that LL-37 damages human red blood cells and other cells in culture at
   concentrations near those needed for antimicrobial activity. How should I weigh a laboratory
   finding like that?
5. If I were to take an unregulated product, what testing would you want to see before you were
   willing to advise me at all, and what would you monitor?
6. Are there interactions with anything I currently take, particularly immune-modulating or
   anticoagulant treatment?
7. I have a wound that is not healing. What treatments actually have good evidence for this
   specific problem, and where would that place something like LL-37?
8. If I compete in a sport with anti-doping rules, how do I confirm the current status of this
   substance, and who is accountable if a product is mislabelled?
9. What symptoms or changes would make you want to see me immediately if I were using an
   unregulated peptide product?
10. What would you need to see published before you would consider the evidence for this compound
    adequate?
