# LL-37, questions members actually ask

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

These answers reflect the research record dated 2026-07-19. Where the honest answer is "nobody
knows", that is what is written. See `GUIDE_DRAFT.md` for the full evidence account and references.

---

## 1. Does it work?

For the thing most people are asking about, the honest answer is that nobody knows, and the
evidence that does exist points the wrong way more often than it points the right way.

The specific picture:

- The largest and most rigorous human trial, in 148 people with long-standing leg ulcers, tested
  LL-37 applied to the wound. It did **not** meet its main pre-planned goal. Complete wound
  closure was statistically indistinguishable from placebo.
- The often-quoted "28.1 percent versus 8.1 percent" figure comes from that same trial, from an
  analysis run after the main result had failed, restricted to people with larger wounds. Findings
  produced that way generate hypotheses. They do not confirm them, and this one has never been
  tested in advance or replicated.
- An earlier 34-person trial did report much better healing than placebo. That signal did not hold
  up when the same researchers tested it properly at larger size. It is superseded, not additional
  supporting evidence.
- In a diabetic foot ulcer trial, LL-37 was linked with more granulation tissue (the new tissue
  that fills a healing wound), but it did not significantly reduce inflammatory markers, and it
  did not significantly reduce bacteria in the wound compared with placebo.
- The melanoma trial enrolled four people over more than five years, one of whom withdrew for lack
  of effect. It supports no conclusion about anything.

So: some early human evidence in specific chronic wounds, negative on the outcome that matters
most, and nothing at all for the general uses it is sold for.

## 2. It is a natural human peptide. Doesn't that make it safe?

No. Being made by the body is not a safety argument, and in this case the literature specifically
argues against that intuition.

Two documented points, both from peer-reviewed sources:

- In cultured human cells, LL-37 damages the membranes of red blood cells, immune cells and
  connective tissue cells at concentrations similar to those needed for its antimicrobial effect.
  On that account, the gap between the useful effect and damage to your own cells is narrow by
  design, not by accident. This is a laboratory finding, and whether it happens in a person at any
  real exposure is unknown.
- LL-37 is recognised as an autoantigen in psoriasis, meaning the immune system can treat it as a
  trigger, and it is implicated in lupus biology through complexes it forms with the body's own
  DNA. People with lupus have been reported to have higher blood levels of LL-37 than healthy
  people.

Elevated LL-37 has also been reported in ulcerative colitis, psoriasis, rosacea and chronic
periodontitis. Whether it is driving those conditions or simply marking inflammation is not
established, but it does complicate any idea that more is better.

The research literature itself describes this molecule as having two faces. Any account that gives
you only the host-defence half is incomplete.

## 3. Is it safe?

The correct answer is: not established, and the reassuring parts of the record do not cover the way
it is actually sold.

What the trials reported, stated precisely. In the 148-person leg ulcer trial, local reactions were
mostly mild to moderate with no consistent difference between treatment and placebo, and the
serious adverse events that occurred (in 12 patients, 7.4 percent) were assessed by the
investigators as unrelated to treatment. The 34-person trial reported no safety concerns for local
or general adverse events. The four-person melanoma trial reported generally mild adverse events
and none serious.

That is a genuinely reassuring short-term record. It applies to LL-37 applied to a chronic wound
for a matter of weeks, and to direct tumour injection in four people. It carries essentially no
information about:

- use in a way that circulates through the body, which no retrieved human trial studied
- use for longer than 13 weeks, which no retrieved human trial studied
- use in healthy people, which no retrieved human trial studied
- an unregulated product whose contents have not been verified

Absence of reported harm in situations nobody studied is not evidence of absence of harm.

## 4. Is it legal?

Regulatory status could not be confirmed to the standard this Guide requires, and this is one of
the places where the honest answer is uncomfortable.

As of **2026-07-19**:

- **United States (FDA), approval.** No FDA-approved LL-37 drug product for any indication was
  identified. This is recorded as a negative search result, not as an affirmative statement from
  the FDA.
- **United States (FDA), compounding status.** Secondary legal and trade sources report that
  cathelicidin (LL-37) is scheduled for review by the FDA's Pharmacy Compounding Advisory
  Committee before the end of February 2027. This could not be confirmed from an FDA page or the
  Federal Register, both of which failed to load. It is flagged as unconfirmed and requires direct
  human verification.
- **Anti-doping.** The World Anti-Doping Agency Prohibited List could not be retrieved. Claims that
  LL-37 is not prohibited appeared **only** on vendor pages, which do not meet the evidence
  standard for these Guides. If you compete under anti-doping rules, do not rely on this Guide, on
  a vendor page, or on the absence of a statement here. Check the current Prohibited List directly
  and speak to your sport's anti-doping authority.
- **Other countries.** Not searched. Nothing here should be taken as a statement about status in
  the European Union, the United Kingdom, Australia or Canada.

Being sold does not mean being approved, and being unapproved is not the same as being illegal to
possess. Those are different questions with different answers in different countries, and this
Guide does not answer them for your jurisdiction.

## 5. Can I get it from Xenios?

No. Xenios does not sell LL-37, does not supply it, and does not tell members where to obtain it or
how to use it.

This Guide exists for a different reason. Members encounter LL-37 in podcasts, forums and vendor
marketing, and much of that material is inaccurate in a specific and checkable way. Publishing an
accurate account is useful whether or not anyone is selling anything.

If you have decided to use it regardless, the most useful thing this Guide can offer is the list of
questions in the Guide's "Questions for a qualified professional" section, and the documentation
checklist in `QUALITY_AND_DOCUMENTATION.md`. Both are designed to be taken to a clinician.

## 6. Why is the evidence so thin when this compound has actual human trials?

This is a fair challenge, because LL-37 does have real randomised trials, which puts it ahead of
most compounds discussed in the same conversations. The thinness is about shape, not just about
quantity.

Four reasons:

1. **The name covers two different things.** Most published research indexed under "LL-37"
   measures a person's own naturally produced LL-37 as a marker. Out of 50 registry records
   returned for the term, only two studies actually gave LL-37 as a treatment. Many of the rest
   are vitamin D studies asking whether supplementation raises your own LL-37, which is a
   completely different intervention.
2. **The route studied is not the route sold.** Every retrieved human trial applied it to a wound
   surface or injected it into a tumour. The general uses it is promoted for have not been studied
   in people at all.
3. **The molecule is unstable.** Published work reports that the natural peptide is broken down
   rapidly by the body's enzymes and is unstable in biological fluids. That is precisely why the
   research field has moved towards chemically modified versions, and it is a mechanistic argument
   against the natural peptide doing much of anything once it is past the site where it is placed.
4. **The trials that were run mostly did not work.** The field's largest attempt missed its main
   goal. The most mechanistically central claim, killing microbes, was tested once in a human
   trial and was not supported.

An honest reading is not that the research is immature and will fill in. It is that a serious
attempt was made in one specific condition, and the result was largely negative.

## 7. Vendors quote a trial showing 28.1 percent versus 8.1 percent. Is that made up?

The numbers are real. The framing is not honest.

They come from the 148-person Phase IIb leg ulcer trial. That trial committed in advance to a
single main outcome, complete wound closure sustained after confirmation. On that outcome the
result was 26.5 percent and 24.7 percent for the two active groups versus 25.3 percent for placebo,
with no statistically significant difference. The trial failed.

Afterwards, the researchers looked at subsets of the data and found that among people whose wounds
were larger, one active group had 28.1 percent closure versus 8.1 percent for placebo.

Why this matters: when you go looking through subgroups after a result has failed, you will
sometimes find a positive one by chance alone. This is one of the best-documented ways false
findings enter medicine. Such a result is worth testing in a new trial. It is not a result you can
rely on. It has never been tested in advance, and it has not been replicated.

Quoting the subgroup number without saying the main result failed converts a negative trial into a
positive-sounding one. That is the single most important correction in this Guide.

## 8. If it is an antimicrobial peptide, shouldn't it help with infections?

That is the expectation, and it was tested. It did not hold.

In the diabetic foot ulcer trial, researchers measured bacterial colonisation in the wound
directly. Topical LL-37 did not significantly reduce it compared with placebo. The same trial found
no significant reduction in the inflammatory markers it measured.

The antimicrobial description comes from laboratory work, where LL-37 disrupts microbial membranes
in a dish. Laboratory conditions differ from a wound or a body in ways that matter, including salt
concentration and the presence of enzymes that break the peptide apart. In this instance a
plausible mechanism did not translate into a measurable human result.

## 9. Should I be worried about it if I have psoriasis or an autoimmune condition?

This is the concern the research record supports most clearly, and it is the one promotional
material generally leaves out. It is also a question for your clinician, not for a Guide.

What is documented: LL-37 is described as a key autoantigen in psoriasis. Combined with the body's
own DNA, it activates a type of immune cell to produce type I interferon, a signalling molecule
central to several autoimmune diseases. It is implicated in lupus biology, people with lupus have
been reported to have higher blood levels of it than healthy people, and antibodies against LL-37
correlate with anti-DNA antibodies.

What is **not** documented: that giving someone LL-37 triggers or worsens these conditions. No
retrieved trial studied that, so the clinical consequence is genuinely unknown. The source review
also notes that some studies find no association with disease activity.

That combination, a well-described mechanism and no study of the actual question, is exactly the
situation where a specialist's judgement about your particular case matters and a general article
cannot help you.

## 10. What would change your mind?

Specific things, and stating them in advance is the point.

The assessment in this Guide would move towards positive if:

- A properly powered randomised trial tested the large-wound hypothesis in advance, rather than
  after the fact, and confirmed it. That is the single most direct test available.
- Any randomised human trial studied LL-37 given in a way that circulates through the body, with
  safety measured, and reported a result. This would address the largest single unknown.
- A human trial enrolled healthy people and reported outcomes, since none has.
- Human pharmacokinetic data showed that the natural peptide survives in the body long enough to
  act, which would answer the stability objection directly.
- A study examined whether giving LL-37 affects autoimmune disease activity in susceptible people,
  which would convert the main safety concern from theoretical to measured, in either direction.

The assessment would move further towards negative if:

- The unreplicated subgroup finding were tested prospectively and failed.
- Case reports or trial data linked administration to autoimmune flares.
- Independent analytical work documented that marketed products do not contain intact LL-37.

Two things that would **not** change it: more laboratory or animal work supporting mechanism, since
the mechanism is not the weak point, and more vendor pages citing the same trial.
