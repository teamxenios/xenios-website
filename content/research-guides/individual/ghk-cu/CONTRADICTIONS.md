# Contradictions and divergences: GHK-Cu (copper tripeptide-1)

Where the sources disagree, and how the disagreement resolves on the evidence retrieved this
session. Source IDs refer to SOURCE_REGISTRY.md.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

## 1. Whether GHK-Cu has demonstrated efficacy in controlled human trials

**Position A.** Consumer and vendor sources present GHK-Cu as clinically proven for skin, hair
and scar outcomes, and cite specific trial statistics in support (S-23).

**Position B.** The two identified randomized, controlled, blinded human trials of a copper
tripeptide complex were both null on objective endpoints. In 86 evaluable patients with venous
stasis ulcers, the copper complex arm did not differ meaningfully from inert vehicle placebo,
while a comparator did produce a statistically significant reduction (S-04). In 13 completers
after CO2 laser skin resurfacing, there was no significant improvement in erythema, wrinkles or
overall skin quality on blinded and computer-based assessment (S-05).

**Assessment.** The controlled evidence resolves against the marketing claim. The
best-designed human data retrieved are negative on objective measures. The positive human
reports come from uncontrolled, unblinded, manufacturer-sponsored studies of multi-ingredient
products (S-06, S-07), plus statistics that could not be located at all (see section 5). Where
trial quality and reported effect size run in opposite directions, that pattern is itself
informative. The Guide should lead with the controlled negative trials rather than placing
them after the positive-sounding material.

## 2. Objective effect versus subjective satisfaction

**Position A.** Patients using a topical GHK-Cu regimen after CO2 laser resurfacing were
significantly more satisfied (P = .04) (S-05).

**Position B.** The same trial found no significant improvement in erythema, wrinkles or
overall skin quality on blinded and computer-based assessment (S-05).

**Assessment.** This is not a conflict between sources. It is a clean dissociation inside a
single study, and it is the most useful finding in the whole record for a member audience. It
is consistent with a real subjective experience without a measurable tissue-level effect, and
it explains how enthusiastic testimonials can coexist with flat trial data. The Guide should
present it as an explanation of the testimonial pattern, and must not present higher
satisfaction as evidence of efficacy.

## 3. Preclinical findings versus human findings

**Position A.** Controlled animal experiments in rabbits, rats, mice and pigs have reported
wound-healing benefits, including increased collagen production and angiogenesis, and further
work exists in rodent inflammation and fibrosis models, a nematode lifespan model, zebrafish
larvae, and cultured fibroblasts with ex vivo human skin (S-10 to S-15, S-19).

**Position B.** The best-controlled human wound trial retrieved was null for the copper
complex against inert vehicle placebo (S-04), and the controlled human skin trial retrieved
was null on objective endpoints (S-05).

**Assessment.** This is the central divergence for this compound. Animal wound-healing results
have not translated into human wound outcomes in the controlled trial that tested exactly that
question. Several of the preclinical records were retrieved at title level only, so their
findings cannot even be stated in detail yet. The Guide must name the species or model in the
same sentence as any preclinical finding, and must never allow a bare mechanism or effect
sentence to read as a human fact. A nematode lifespan result and a non-peer-reviewed mouse
preprint are particularly distant from any human claim.

## 4. Mechanism plausibility versus demonstrated effect

**Position A.** There is a coherent, decades-old mechanistic account: GHK-Cu is proposed to
modulate copper uptake into cells, with complexation described as silencing copper's redox
activity so the metal can be delivered in a non-toxic form, and GHK has been hypothesized to
act as an emergency response molecule released from damaged extracellular matrix after injury
(S-19).

**Position B.** That account has been characterized largely in animal and cell systems, and
the controlled human trials that exist have been largely null (S-04, S-05).

**Assessment.** Mechanism plausibility clearly outruns demonstrated clinical effect here, and
the gap has persisted for decades rather than closing. A plausible mechanism is not evidence of
a clinical effect. This should be stated directly rather than left for the reader to infer,
because the mechanistic story is the most persuasive material a member will encounter about
this compound.

## 5. Vendor-claimed trials that could not be located

**Position A.** Peptide vendor and affiliate pages described specific human trials with
confident numbers, including a 2022 randomized controlled trial in androgenetic alopecia
patients reporting a hair count increase versus minoxidil, a 2023 phase II trial in
post-surgical patients reporting a scar volume reduction versus silicone, and a human trial
reporting a wrinkle volume reduction versus a named cosmetic control (S-23).

**Position B.** None of these could be located in PubMed searches or in the ClinicalTrials.gov
registry this session, and none of the sites making the claims offered a PMID, NCT number, or
any citation (S-01, S-08, S-09).

**Assessment.** These claims are excluded from the evidence tables and are prohibited from
member-facing surfaces (CLAIM_TABLE.md, P-04). It could not be established whether they exist
under different indexing or do not exist at all, so the Guide should report the search finding
and the absence of citation rather than asserting fabrication. The reviewer's practical rule
should be that an uncited trial statistic on a peptide retail page is not evidence, whatever
its precision suggests.

## 6. Regulatory posture, topical versus injectable

**Position A.** GHK-Cu is an established cosmetic ingredient carrying the INCI name Copper
Tripeptide-1, and a CIR Expert Panel safety assessment reportedly covers the metal salts of
tripeptide-1 (S-19, S-21).

**Position B.** Secondary sources consistently report that FDA has treated injectable GHK-Cu
differently from non-injectable use, citing immunogenicity from aggregation and peptide-related
impurities (S-20).

**Assessment.** These are not actually in conflict. They are statements about two different
products. The apparent contradiction arises only when a cosmetic-ingredient safety review is
misapplied to an injectable product, which is exactly what happens in the consumer market. Two
verification caveats are mandatory. The CIR conclusion could not be primary-verified (the PDF
was unparseable, the journal page returned HTTP 403, PubMed returned zero results), and every
fda.gov page attempted returned HTTP 404 to automated retrieval, so the specific FDA category
status and its current date are unverified. Neither statement may be published until a human
confirms it. See REGULATORY_STATUS.md.

## 7. Reported absence of adverse effects versus the strength of the evidence

**Position A.** The topical studies retrieved reported no local or systemic adverse effects
(S-05, S-06, S-07), and a CIR assessment covering the relevant ingredient class reportedly
concluded safety for cosmetic use (S-21, unverified).

**Position B.** Those studies were small, short, and mostly uncontrolled, two of them were
manufacturer-sponsored studies of multi-ingredient products, and the CIR conclusion could not
be primary-verified. Separately, no human trial of systemic administration was located at all,
so the injectable route has no human safety characterization (S-01, S-08, S-09).

**Assessment.** Absence of a reported signal in weak studies is weak reassurance, not a safety
finding. The Guide may state what was reported and in what population, but may never state
that GHK-Cu is safe, well tolerated, or free of side effects, and must never carry a topical
safety impression across to the injectable route.

## 8. Where there is no genuine contradiction

Two apparent tensions are worth naming as non-contradictions so a reviewer does not spend time
on them.

- The gap between positive preclinical work and negative human trials is a translation
  failure, not a disagreement between sources. Both bodies of work can be accurate about their
  own systems.
- The gap between cosmetic and injectable regulatory treatment is a difference of product and
  route, not a regulatory inconsistency.

The one true unresolved question in this record is not a contradiction between sources at all.
It is an absence: whether topical GHK-Cu, isolated from other actives, produces measurable
objective changes in a properly controlled trial. Every positive human study retrieved tested a
multi-ingredient formulation. The registered vehicle-controlled Phase 2 topical trial (S-02)
is designed to answer exactly that question and has no posted results.
