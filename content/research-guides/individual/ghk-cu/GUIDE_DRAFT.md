# GHK-Cu (copper tripeptide-1)

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

This Guide describes what is known, what is claimed, and what is not known. It is not medical
advice, it is not a recommendation to use anything, and it is not a product listing.

---

## One-minute summary

GHK-Cu is a small naturally occurring peptide bound to copper. It has been studied since the
1970s and it has a large body of laboratory and animal research behind it. That is the part
most people encounter first, and it is genuine.

The honest verdict is less flattering than the marketing. When we looked for controlled human
trials, we found two. Both were on the skin surface, and both were negative on their objective
measures. In a 1992 trial in 86 patients with leg ulcers, a copper tripeptide cream did not
outperform an inert cream (PMID 1495150). In a 2006 trial in 13 patients recovering from a
laser skin procedure, blinded evaluation and computer analysis found no measurable improvement
in redness, wrinkles, or skin quality (PMID 16847171). Notably, in that second trial patients
using GHK-Cu were significantly more satisfied than the comparison group even though nothing
measurable had changed. That single finding explains a great deal about the gap between
enthusiastic reviews and flat trial data.

The recent positive human studies we found are weaker still. They are open-label (nobody was
blinded), they had no control group, they were short, they tested multi-ingredient commercial
products rather than the peptide by itself, and they came from one author group funded by the
company that sells the products.

For injected or otherwise systemically administered GHK-Cu, the finding is simpler and more
important. We found no human trial at all, in either the ClinicalTrials.gov registry or PubMed.
The human evidence base for injectable GHK-Cu is not thin. It is absent. Its safety by that
route is uncharacterized by trial data.

One genuinely well designed skin study is currently recruiting (NCT07437586, a Phase 2,
vehicle-controlled trial). It has no posted results. It is the study worth waiting for.

What a member should take away: the cosmetic and the injectable versions of this compound share
a name and share almost nothing else, the strongest evidence anyone cites for GHK-Cu is
cosmetic and topical, and the controlled part of that evidence is negative.

## What it is

GHK-Cu is the copper(II) complex of a tripeptide, meaning a chain of three amino acids, called
glycyl-L-histidyl-L-lysine. A copper atom is held by the peptide.

It occurs naturally in human plasma (the liquid part of blood), saliva, and urine. Levels in
plasma decline with age. It was isolated from human plasma albumin by Loren Pickart in 1973 and
identified as glycyl-L-histidyl-L-lysine by 1977. That history is drawn from a tertiary
reference (the Wikipedia entry, which cites primary literature we did not individually
retrieve), so it is background rather than primary-verified fact.

In cosmetics it carries the INCI name Copper Tripeptide-1. INCI is the standardized naming
system used on cosmetic ingredient labels.

The single most useful thing to understand about this compound is that "GHK-Cu" refers to two
very different things in practice:

1. A reviewed cosmetic ingredient applied to the skin surface, sold in regulated consumer
   products.
2. An injectable grey-market product sold outside the pharmaceutical system.

Consumer marketing treats these as the same substance. They have completely different evidence
and completely different regulatory footing. This Guide keeps them separate throughout, and a
reader should too.

## Why interest exists

Interest in GHK-Cu is real and it has understandable roots. Describing those roots is not an
endorsement of the compound.

- It is a naturally occurring human molecule whose levels fall with age. That fact alone makes
  it an attractive story for anyone interested in aging.
- It has a long and coherent mechanistic literature going back decades, which makes it feel
  well understood.
- It has a substantial animal and cell-culture literature reporting wound healing effects,
  including increased collagen production and new blood vessel formation in rodent and other
  animal models.
- It is inexpensive to manufacture and widely available as a cosmetic ingredient, so it appears
  in a very large number of skincare products, which creates familiarity.
- Personal reports from users are often positive.

That last point deserves a note rather than dismissal. The 2006 controlled trial found
significantly higher patient satisfaction alongside no measurable change (PMID 16847171). People
can genuinely feel better about a result that instruments cannot detect. Interest in GHK-Cu is
not irrational. It is simply not the same thing as demonstrated effect.

## Terminology

- **Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids. A tripeptide is a chain of three.
- **GHK.** The peptide glycyl-L-histidyl-L-lysine without copper attached. GHK and GHK-Cu are
  different entities and should not be treated as interchangeable.
- **Copper tripeptide-1 / Copper Tripeptide-1.** The cosmetic labelling name (INCI) for the
  copper complex.
- **INCI.** International Nomenclature of Cosmetic Ingredients, the standardized ingredient
  naming system used on cosmetic labels.
- **Topical.** Applied to the surface of the skin.
- **Systemic.** Reaching the whole body through the bloodstream rather than acting at a surface.
- **Randomized controlled trial (RCT).** A study in which participants are assigned by chance to
  the test product or a comparison, so that differences are less likely to be caused by who
  chose what.
- **Blinded / evaluator-blinded.** The person judging the result does not know which participant
  got which product, which reduces wishful assessment.
- **Vehicle or placebo.** The inactive base cream or gel without the ingredient being tested. If
  a product does not beat its own vehicle, the ingredient did not do the work.
- **Open-label.** Everyone knows who is getting what. Expectation effects are uncontrolled.
- **In vitro.** In glassware, meaning in cultured cells or in a dish, not in a living body.
- **Ex vivo.** In tissue that has been removed from a body, for example a donated skin sample.
  Not a living human study.
- **Preclinical.** Laboratory or animal research done before, or instead of, human research.
- **PMID.** The identifier for a record in PubMed, the US National Library of Medicine index.
- **NCT number.** The identifier for a study registered on ClinicalTrials.gov.
- **CIR.** Cosmetic Ingredient Review, a US expert panel that assesses cosmetic ingredient safety.
- **Immunogenicity.** The tendency of a substance to provoke an immune response against itself.
- **Endotoxin.** A bacterial breakdown product that can cause fever and other reactions if it
  contaminates an injected product.
- **Compounding.** Preparation of a drug product by a pharmacy rather than by a licensed
  manufacturer under a drug approval.
- **Angiogenesis.** The formation of new blood vessels.
- **Regression to the mean.** The tendency of a condition measured when it is at its worst to
  look better at the next measurement, with or without treatment. It is one reason uncontrolled
  studies mislead.

## Proposed mechanism

Everything in this section is proposed, not established, and it was largely worked out in animal
and cell systems rather than in humans.

GHK-Cu is thought to act by carrying copper into cells. The glycine part of the peptide is
described as playing the major role in binding copper, and forming the complex is described as
quieting copper's chemical reactivity so that the metal can be delivered in a form that is less
damaging than free copper. GHK has been hypothesized to act as an emergency response molecule
released from damaged connective tissue after injury, which would make it a wound signalling
molecule. Downstream effects reported in animal and cell models include increased collagen
production and angiogenesis.

This framing comes from a tertiary background source (the Wikipedia entry on the compound, which
cites primary literature that we did not individually retrieve). It should be read as a summary
of a proposed mechanism, not as verified primary evidence.

Broader claims circulating in the consumer market about wide-ranging effects on gene expression
were not verified against primary literature in our review and are not asserted here.

One point deserves emphasis, because it is the recurring failure mode in this subject area. A
plausible mechanism is not evidence of a clinical effect. The mechanistic story for GHK-Cu has
been developing for roughly fifty years, and the controlled human trials that exist have been
largely negative. Both of those things are true at once, and the second one is the one that
matters for a person deciding what to do.

**Claim grade for the proposed mechanism: D (preclinical).**

## Human evidence

All human studies identified were topical, meaning applied to the skin surface. We found no
human trial of injected or otherwise systemically administered GHK-Cu in either ClinicalTrials.gov
or PubMed. For that route the human evidence table is empty.

### Controlled, blinded human trials

**Venous stasis ulcers, 1992. Negative for the copper complex.**
A prospective randomized, evaluator-blinded, three-arm trial in 86 evaluable patients with
venous stasis ulcers (leg ulcers caused by poor vein circulation). Silver sulfadiazine produced
a statistically significant reduction in ulcer size compared with both the tripeptide-copper
complex cream and placebo. There was no meaningful difference between the copper complex and the
inert vehicle. Bishop JB et al. J Vasc Surg. 1992 Aug;16(2):251-257. PMID 1495150.
Limitations: this is an older trial, it was topical only, and the indication was wound care
rather than cosmetic or systemic use. It is nonetheless one of the better controlled human
trials of a copper tripeptide complex that exists, and its result for the copper arm was null.
VERIFIED 2026-07-19. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1495150/
*Claim grade for topical GHK-Cu improving venous ulcer healing: PROHIBITED. The controlled
evidence is negative, so this claim may not appear on any member-facing surface.*

**Skin after CO2 laser resurfacing, 2006. Negative on objective endpoints.**
A randomized controlled trial with blinded evaluators, comparing a post-procedure topical
regimen with or without GHK-Cu in 13 patients who completed the study following circumoral CO2
laser skin resurfacing. There was no significant reduction or resolution of post-treatment
redness by computer analysis or by blinded evaluators, and no significant improvement in
wrinkles or overall skin quality. Patient satisfaction was significantly higher in the GHK-Cu
group (P = .04) despite the absence of objective benefit. Miller TR et al. Arch Facial Plast
Surg. 2006 Jul-Aug. PMID 16847171.
Limitations: very small (13 completers), single setting, and a post-procedural population rather
than general cosmetic use. The divergence between satisfaction and objective measurement is
itself informative and is discussed under Contradictions.
VERIFIED 2026-07-19. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16847171/
*Claim grade for topical GHK-Cu improving post-laser redness, wrinkles, or skin quality:
PROHIBITED. Objective endpoints were negative.*

### Uncontrolled human studies

**Scalp flaking and seborrheic dermatitis, 2024.**
A prospective, interventional, open-label, single-center study in 29 adults with mild to
moderate dandruff or seborrheic dermatitis. No randomization, no control group, no blinding.
Reported improvements in scalp flaking, hydration, hair density and thickness over a short
period, with all subjects reporting satisfaction. Patel MN et al. Cureus. 2024. PMID 39449909.
Limitations are severe. The study tested a two-product regimen (a hydroxy-acid scrub plus a
copper tripeptide serum), so no effect can be attributed to GHK-Cu specifically. Being
uncontrolled and unblinded, regression to the mean and expectation effects are uncontrolled. The
duration was very short. It was sponsored by the manufacturer of the tested products (Anveya
Living Private Limited), and three of four authors disclosed employment or financial
relationships with the manufacturer or the research company.
VERIFIED 2026-07-19. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39449909/
*Claim grade: E (manufacturer or supplier reported), and it cannot be attributed to GHK-Cu.*

**Acne scarring, 2025.**
An open-label, prospective real-world evidence study in females with acne scarring. Exact
enrollment was not stated in the abstract we retrieved. No control group. Reported reductions in
scar visibility and improvements in skin smoothness at the study endpoint, with no local or
systemic adverse effects reported. Patel MN et al. Cureus. 2025. PMID 41001334.
Limitations are severe. It tested a proprietary multi-ingredient branded gel rather than GHK-Cu
in isolation, so nothing can be attributed to the copper peptide. There was no control group and
no blinding. It was manufacturer-sponsored (Anveya Living Private Limited) with author
employment relationships disclosed. One reported effect size in the abstract exceeds 100 percent,
which suggests a denominator or reporting problem and further reduces confidence. Same author
group and sponsor as the 2024 study above.
VERIFIED 2026-07-19. https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41001334/
*Claim grade: E (manufacturer or supplier reported), and it cannot be attributed to GHK-Cu.*

### Registered but unreported

**NCT07437586.** Topical GHK-Cu gel for acute skin wound healing. Sponsor Hudson Biotech. Phase
2, recruiting, planned enrollment 60, punch-biopsy wounds, GHK-Cu gel compared against vehicle
placebo. No results posted. This is the design that could actually answer the question for
topical use, because it isolates the peptide against its own vehicle. It has not reported.
VERIFIED 2026-07-19. https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT07437586
*Claim grade: no claim. A registered trial without results is not evidence of anything.*

**NCT07706361.** A two-part study of the effects of a commercial patch (the X39 patch, LifeWave
Inc) on circulating GHK and GHK-Cu levels in healthy adults. Not Applicable phase, not yet
recruiting, planned enrollment 100. No results posted. Note that this studies a device's effect
on the body's own GHK levels rather than administration of GHK-Cu, so it does not speak to the
compound's effects.
VERIFIED 2026-07-19. https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT07706361

A filtered PubMed search restricted to clinical trials, randomized controlled trials, and

## Preclinical evidence

Preclinical means laboratory and animal work. None of the findings below are human findings, and
none of them should be read as predicting a human result. For this specific compound that
caution is not theoretical: animal wound healing results have repeatedly failed to translate
into human wound outcomes, as the 1992 human ulcer trial shows.

Note also that for most of the records below we retrieved the bibliographic record and title
only. Abstract-level detail was not retrieved, because PubMed began returning automated-access
challenges during the search. Each entry states what was actually retrieved.

- **In rats,** a wound healing study reported 64.5 percent wound size reduction with GHK-Cu
  compared with 28.2 percent in controls. **In rabbits, rats, mice and pigs,** multiple
  controlled animal experiments have reported wound healing benefits including increased
  collagen production and angiogenesis. These findings reached us through a tertiary source (the
  Wikipedia entry, which cites primary literature) and the underlying primary papers were not
  individually retrieved, so treat them as background rather than primary-verified.
  NOT PRIMARY-VERIFIED. https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper_peptide_GHK-Cu
  *Claim grade: D (preclinical).*

- **In rabbits,** an evaluation of topical tripeptide-copper complex and zinc oxide on open-wound
  healing. Vet Dermatol. 2006. PMID 17083573. The record was confirmed to exist; abstract-level
  findings were not retrieved, so the direction and size of the result are not stated here.
  VERIFIED 2026-07-19 (record only). https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17083573/
  *Claim grade: D (preclinical).*

- **In middle-aged mice,** a record title indicates behavioral rescue following GHK-Cu peptide
  treatment. Res Sq 2026. PMID 42245779. This is a preprint, meaning it has not been peer
  reviewed. Only the title was retrieved from a PubMed results listing; the abstract was not
  retrieved.
  VERIFIED 2026-07-19 (title only).
  *Claim grade: D (preclinical), and further discounted as an unreviewed preprint.*

- **In the nematode worm Caenorhabditis elegans,** a record title indicates that GHK-Cu delays
  aging. Biogerontology 2026. PMID 42084774. Nematode lifespan models are extremely distant from
  human aging outcomes. Only the title was retrieved; the abstract was not retrieved.
  VERIFIED 2026-07-19 (title only).
  *Claim grade: D (preclinical).*

- **In zebrafish larvae,** a record title indicates that GHK-Cu attenuates copper sulfate or LPS
  induced inflammation. Eur J Pharmacol 2026. PMID 41997403. Only the title was retrieved.
  VERIFIED 2026-07-19 (title only).
  *Claim grade: D (preclinical).*

- **In a mouse model of silicosis,** a record title indicates that GHK-Cu attenuates lung
  inflammation and fibrosis. Redox Biol 2024. PMID 38879894. Only the title was retrieved.
  VERIFIED 2026-07-19 (title only).
  *Claim grade: D (preclinical).*

- **In cultured fibroblasts and in ex vivo human skin,** a record title indicates a study of
  synergy between GHK-Cu and hyaluronic acid on collagen IV upregulation. J Cosmet Dermatol
  2023. PMID 37062921. This is in vitro and ex vivo work, not a study in living human subjects,
  and it must not be presented as human evidence. Only the title was retrieved.
  VERIFIED 2026-07-19 (title only).
  *Claim grade: D (preclinical).*

A dated PubMed search for "copper tripeptide" or "GHK-Cu" returned 92 total publications,
overwhelmingly preclinical. VERIFIED 2026-07-19.
https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/?term=%22copper+tripeptide%22+OR+%22GHK-Cu%22&sort=date&size=50

## Contradictions and disagreement

**Does GHK-Cu work in controlled human trials?**
One position, common on vendor and consumer sites, is that GHK-Cu is clinically proven for skin,
hair and scar outcomes, often accompanied by specific trial statistics. The opposing position is
that the two identified randomized, controlled, blinded human trials of a copper tripeptide
complex were both null on objective endpoints: the copper arm did not beat inert vehicle in the
1992 ulcer trial (n=86, PMID 1495150), and there was no objective skin improvement in the 2006
laser trial (n=13, PMID 16847171).

Our assessment is that the controlled evidence resolves against the marketing claim. The best
designed human data available are negative on objective measures. The positive human reports
come from uncontrolled, unblinded, manufacturer-sponsored studies of multi-ingredient products,
plus trial statistics that we could not locate at all (see Quality and identity). Where study
quality and reported effect size run in opposite directions, that pattern is itself diagnostic.
This Guide leads with the negative controlled trials rather than burying them.

**Objective effect versus subjective satisfaction.**
In the 2006 trial, patients using GHK-Cu after laser resurfacing were significantly more
satisfied (P = .04), while the same trial found no significant improvement in redness, wrinkles,
or overall skin quality on blinded and computer-based assessment. This is a clean dissociation
inside a single study and it is genuinely informative. It is consistent with a real subjective
experience without a measurable tissue-level effect. It also explains how favorable testimonials
can coexist with null trial data, which is worth understanding before reading any review section.

**Topical versus injectable regulatory posture.**
One position notes that GHK-Cu is an established, reviewed cosmetic ingredient with a CIR safety
assessment covering its ingredient class. Another notes that secondary sources consistently
report that FDA has treated injectable GHK-Cu differently from non-injectable, citing
immunogenicity and impurity concerns for the injectable form. These are not actually in
conflict. They are statements about two different products. The apparent contradiction only
appears when a cosmetic-ingredient safety review is misapplied to an injectable product. We must
flag that we could not verify the specific FDA category status from a primary source (see
Regulatory status).

## Limitations

- The controlled human literature consists of two trials, one from 1992 and one from 2006, and
  both were negative on objective endpoints.
- The larger of those two trials enrolled 86 evaluable patients. The smaller had 13 completers.
  Small trials can miss real effects as well as fail to find false ones, so the correct reading
  is that benefit was not demonstrated, not that benefit was disproved for all uses.
- Every positive human study we found tested a multi-ingredient product, so no effect in those
  studies can be attributed to GHK-Cu itself.
- All human studies we found were short. Durability of any effect is unknown.
- All human studies we found were topical. Nothing here speaks to systemic use.
- For most preclinical records we retrieved only the bibliographic record or title, not the
  abstract, because PubMed began returning automated-access challenges. Effect directions and
  sizes for those entries are therefore not stated.
- The animal wound-healing summary reached us through a tertiary source rather than the primary
  papers.
- Two likely relevant review articles could not be read: PMID 41966639 (Sports Med 2026, a
  review of approved and unapproved peptide therapies) and PMID 39963574 (Bioimpacts, a review
  of topically applied GHK as an anti-wrinkle peptide). Both should be read by a human reviewer
  before publication.
- Three regulatory sources could not be retrieved at all. See Regulatory status.

## Quality and identity

This section is about the market, not the molecule. It is the part most likely to affect a real
person.

**Grey-market injectable peptide products sit outside pharmaceutical quality control.** The ECRI
and ISMP white paper on compounded peptide products (May 7 2026) documents contamination risk,
mislabeling, and lack of standardization relative to FDA-approved products, and references the
2012 contaminated-compounding meningitis outbreak as precedent for real-world harm. That paper
does not name GHK-Cu, so this is a category-level finding about compounded and grey-market
peptides rather than a compound-specific one.
VERIFIED 2026-07-19.
https://assets.ecri.org/PDF/ECRI%20and%20ISMP%20White%20Paper%20on%20Compounded%20Peptide%20Products.pdf

**Identity confusion is built into this market.** The strongest-sounding evidence for GHK-Cu is
cosmetic and topical. Much of the consumer demand is for injectable use. Vendors routinely cite
the topical literature, and the cosmetic safety review, in support of injectable products. Those
citations do not transfer. A safety assessment of a cream ingredient says nothing about what
happens when a substance enters the bloodstream. This is the single most common way a reader
will be misled about this compound.

**Some trial statistics circulating in the consumer market could not be located at all.** During
our review, peptide vendor and affiliate sites confidently described specific human trials,
including a 2022 randomized controlled trial in 50 patients with androgenetic alopecia reporting
a 22 percent hair count increase versus minoxidil at p=0.01, a 2023 phase II trial in 40
post-surgical patients reporting 35 percent scar volume reduction versus silicone, and a human
trial reporting 31.6 percent wrinkle volume reduction against a comparison product. None of
these could be located in PubMed searches or in the ClinicalTrials.gov registry, and none of the
sites making the claims offered a PMID, an NCT number, or any citation. We record them here only
as a warning about the information environment. They are excluded from the evidence tables, they
must not be repeated as evidence, and their apparent non-existence should raise suspicion of any
uncited trial statistic found on a peptide retail site.

**Concentration of the positive literature.** Multiple recent positive human studies on copper
tripeptide topicals come from the same author group and the same manufacturer sponsor (Anveya
Living Private Limited), are open-label and uncontrolled, and test multi-ingredient proprietary
formulations. Their design cannot attribute any effect to the copper peptide.

**Purity, mislabeling, and counterfeiting.** For any product sold outside a regulated
manufacturing chain, identity (is the contents actually the stated compound), purity (what else
is in there), sterility, and endotoxin content are unverified by default. Xenios has not
confirmed any of these for any product containing this compound. See
QUALITY_AND_DOCUMENTATION.md in this folder for the full checklist and its current state.

## Safety categories

This section lists categories of concern. It is not a reassurance, and the absence of a category
here does not mean an absence of risk.

- **Systemic use is uncharacterized in humans.** No human trial of injected or otherwise
  systemically administered GHK-Cu was found in ClinicalTrials.gov or PubMed. Systemic safety,
  immunogenicity, and the consequences of delivering additional copper are therefore not
  characterized by trial data.
  VERIFIED 2026-07-19.
  https://clinicaltrials.gov/api/v2/studies?query.term=GHK-Cu%20OR%20%22copper%20tripeptide%22

- **Immunogenicity concern for compounded injectable peptides.** Multiple secondary sources
  attribute to FDA a concern that compounded injectable peptides, including GHK-Cu, may raise
  immunogenicity risk through aggregation and peptide-related impurities. We could not retrieve
  the primary FDA source (fda.gov pages returned HTTP 404 to automated retrieval), so this is
  reported as an unverified secondary attribution and requires human verification before it is
  stated as an FDA position.
  NOT VERIFIED 2026-07-19.
  https://www.fda.gov/drugs/human-drug-compounding/certain-bulk-drug-substances-use-compounding-may-present-significant-safety-risks

- **Copper is a physiologically active metal with an upper tolerance, so a copper-carrying
  compound entering the body is not pharmacologically inert.**
  [UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]. This is general toxicology
  background rather than a retrieved finding about GHK-Cu. It is listed because it is the
  mechanistically obvious risk axis and a Guide should not omit it, but it needs a proper source
  before publication.

- **Contamination, wrong-sequence product, heavy metals, and endotoxin in grey-market injectable
  peptide products generally.** Documented at the category level by the ECRI and ISMP white
  paper, which does not name GHK-Cu. Specific failure-rate percentages circulating online were
  sourced only to retailer and affiliate blogs and are deliberately excluded.
  VERIFIED 2026-07-19.
  https://assets.ecri.org/PDF/ECRI%20and%20ISMP%20White%20Paper%20on%20Compounded%20Peptide%20Products.pdf

- **Topical use.** The topical human studies we retrieved reported no local or systemic adverse
  effects, and a CIR assessment covering metal salts of tripeptide-1 reportedly concluded safety
  for cosmetic use. Confidence in this is limited. The studies were small, short, and mostly
  uncontrolled, and the CIR conclusion could not be primary-verified. Absence of a reported
  signal in weak studies is weak reassurance and is not a statement that the compound is safe.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

## Interactions

We did not retrieve any interaction study for GHK-Cu in this review, so no specific interaction
is asserted. The following are categories that would require review by a qualified professional
before any use is considered. Listing a category is not a claim that an interaction exists.

- Any condition affecting copper handling in the body, including Wilson disease and other
  disorders of copper metabolism.
  [UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]
- Copper-containing supplements, multivitamins, or other copper-carrying products used at the
  same time.
  [UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]
- Liver or kidney conditions, given that these organs handle metals and clearance.
  [UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]
- Pregnancy and breastfeeding. No relevant data were retrieved.
- Active skin disease, open wounds, or recent skin procedures, for any product applied to skin.
- Immune-mediated conditions or immunosuppressive treatment, given the immunogenicity concern
  raised for compounded injectable peptides above.
- Competitive athletes subject to anti-doping rules. See Regulatory status.

## Regulatory status

All statements below are current as of the date checked and are jurisdiction-specific. Three of
the four could not be verified from a primary source, and each says so explicitly. Regulatory
status changes, and a reader should not rely on this section without re-checking.

**United States (FDA), drug approval status. Checked 2026-07-19.**
We identified no FDA-approved drug product containing GHK-Cu for any medical indication by any
route. GHK-Cu appears in the US market as a cosmetic ingredient (INCI: Copper Tripeptide-1),
not as an approved drug. This is a negative finding based on the absence of any approval record
in the sources we retrieved, not on a retrieved FDA statement of non-approval.
NOT PRIMARY-VERIFIED. Source consulted:
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper_peptide_GHK-Cu

**United States (FDA), compounding bulk drug substance lists (503A and 503B). Checked
2026-07-19. COULD NOT VERIFY.**
Multiple secondary sources assert that FDA has handled GHK-Cu differently by route, with
injectable GHK-Cu treated as raising safety concerns (immunogenicity from aggregation and
peptide-related impurities) while non-injectable use was treated more permissively, and that
category status changed during 2023 to 2026. Direct fetches of the FDA 503A bulks page, the 503B
bulks page, the "Certain Bulk Drug Substances That May Present Significant Safety Risks" page,
and the FDA nominated-bulks PDF all returned HTTP 404 to automated retrieval. The route-based
distinction is directionally consistent across independent secondary sources, but the specific
category assignment and its current date are NOT verified and must be confirmed against fda.gov
by a human before publication.
NOT VERIFIED.
https://www.fda.gov/drugs/human-drug-compounding/certain-bulk-drug-substances-use-compounding-may-present-significant-safety-risks

**Cosmetic Ingredient Review (CIR), United States. Checked 2026-07-19. COULD NOT PRIMARY-VERIFY.**
A CIR Expert Panel safety assessment titled "Safety Assessment of Tripeptide-1, Hexapeptide-12,
Their Metal Salts and Fatty Acyl Derivatives, and Palmitoyl Tetrapeptide-7 as Used in Cosmetics"
exists, published in the International Journal of Toxicology in 2018, and its ingredient scope
includes the metal salts of tripeptide-1, the category into which Copper Tripeptide-1 falls. Per
secondary retrieval, the panel concluded that these ingredients are safe in cosmetics under the
practices of use described in the assessment. Primary verification failed: the CIR PDF returned
unparseable compressed streams, the journal page returned HTTP 403, and a PubMed query for the
assessment returned zero results. The exact conclusion wording and the explicit inclusion of

## Unknowns

These are the questions we could not answer. They are listed because they are the real state of
knowledge, not because the list is expected to shrink soon.

1. Whether injected or otherwise systemically administered GHK-Cu produces any clinical benefit
   in humans. No human trial of systemic administration was found.
2. Whether injected GHK-Cu is safe in humans over any period. Immunogenicity, copper
   accumulation, and organ effects are uncharacterized by human trial data.
3. Whether topical GHK-Cu, isolated from other active ingredients, produces measurable objective
   changes in skin or hair in a controlled trial. Every positive human study retrieved tested a
   multi-ingredient formulation. NCT07437586 is designed to answer this and has not reported.
4. Whether meaningful delivery of the intact complex through the skin occurs at cosmetic use
   levels in a living person. Several retrieved records concern liposomal and nanocarrier
   delivery systems, which implies that plain delivery is a recognized problem, but this was not
   verified in a human study.
5. Whether the mechanistic and gene-expression claims made in the consumer market have any
   primary-literature basis. Not verified in this review.
6. GHK-Cu's current WADA prohibited-list status for competitive athletes.
7. GHK-Cu's current FDA compounding category status and any route-based restriction.
8. The durability of any effect. The retrieved topical studies were short.
9. Whether the vendor-cited trials (a 2022 alopecia RCT, a 2023 phase II scar trial, a 31.6
   percent wrinkle volume result) exist at all. We could not locate them in any registry or
   index.

written so that the professional, not this Guide, supplies the answer.

1. Given my medical history, is there any reason a copper-carrying compound would be a
   particular concern for me?

   copper exposure?

   objective measures. Does that change your view of whether it is worth considering at all?

   about a product with no human safety data?
6. If a product is prepared by a compounding pharmacy rather than a licensed manufacturer, what
   quality documentation should I ask to see, and what would make you refuse it?

8. Is there an approved, evidence-supported option for the outcome I actually care about, and
   how does its evidence compare with what I have just read here?

   contact you?
