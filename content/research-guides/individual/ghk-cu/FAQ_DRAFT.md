# GHK-Cu: questions members actually ask

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.
Nothing here is medical advice. Read this alongside GUIDE_DRAFT.md in this folder, which carries
the full evidence and the citations.

---

## 1. Does it work?

For the outcomes people usually have in mind, the honest answer is that it has not been shown to.

Two randomized, controlled, blinded human trials of a copper tripeptide complex exist in the
literature we searched, and both were negative on their objective measures. In the larger one,
86 patients with leg ulcers, the copper cream did not outperform an inert cream (PMID 1495150,
1992). In the smaller one, 13 patients recovering from a laser skin procedure, blinded evaluators
and computer analysis found no significant improvement in redness, wrinkles, or skin quality
(PMID 16847171, 2006).

The positive human studies we found were open-label with no control group, were short, tested
multi-ingredient commercial products rather than the peptide alone, and were funded by the
company selling the products. Those designs cannot tell you what the copper peptide did.

There is a great deal of positive animal and cell-culture work. That is real research, but for
this specific compound it has repeatedly failed to translate into human results.

---

## 2. Why do so many people say it worked for them?

There is a specific and unusually clean answer to this, and it comes from inside one of the
trials.

In the 2006 laser resurfacing trial, patients using GHK-Cu were significantly more satisfied than
the comparison group (P = .04). The same trial found no measurable improvement in their skin on
blinded and computer-based assessment. Satisfaction went up. The skin did not measurably change.

That is not a claim that people are lying or imagining things. Feeling better about a result is a
real experience. It is simply not the same as a measurable tissue effect, and it explains how
glowing reviews and flat trial data can both be genuine at the same time.

Uncontrolled studies have the same problem at a larger scale. People typically start a product
when a condition is at its worst, and conditions at their worst tend to look better at the next
measurement whether or not anything was done. That effect alone can produce a page of impressive
before-and-after data.

## 3. Is it safe?

We cannot tell you that, and we will not say it.

Here is what we can tell you. For products applied to the skin, the human studies we retrieved
did not report adverse effects, and a cosmetic expert panel assessment reportedly concluded that
the relevant ingredient class is safe in cosmetics. Our confidence in that reassurance is limited,
because the studies were small, short, and mostly uncontrolled, and because we could not verify
the panel's conclusion from the primary document. Absence of a reported problem in weak studies
is weak reassurance.

For injected or otherwise systemic use, the position is different and more serious. We found no
human trial of GHK-Cu given by injection in either ClinicalTrials.gov or PubMed. Nothing has been
characterized: not immunogenicity, not what happens to the additional copper, not organ effects.
It is not that the safety data are thin. There are none.

Separately, secondary sources report that FDA has raised immunogenicity concerns about compounded
injectable peptides including GHK-Cu, related to aggregation and peptide impurities. We could not
retrieve the primary FDA page to confirm this, so we report it as unverified rather than as an
FDA position.

## 4. Is it legal?

That depends entirely on the jurisdiction and on the form of the product, and one of the key
answers is one we could not verify.

In the United States, we identified no FDA-approved drug product containing GHK-Cu for any
medical indication by any route. It appears in the US market as a cosmetic ingredient under the
label name Copper Tripeptide-1. A cosmetic ingredient and an approved drug are not the same
thing, and a cosmetic ingredient listing is not permission to inject something.

Multiple secondary sources report that FDA has treated injectable GHK-Cu differently from
non-injectable forms in its compounding bulk substance categories, and that the category status
changed between 2023 and 2026. We could not confirm this. Every relevant fda.gov page returned an
error to our retrieval attempts. So we are telling you what the secondary sources say and
explicitly flagging that we did not verify it. Anyone who needs a real answer must check fda.gov
directly or ask a professional who can.

Status checked 2026-07-19. Regulatory status changes.

## 7. Why is the evidence so thin when this compound has been around since the 1970s?

Because almost all of the research effort went into laboratory and animal work, and very little
went into controlled human trials.

A dated PubMed search for this compound returned 92 publications, overwhelmingly preclinical.
When we filtered the same search to clinical trials, randomized controlled trials, and systematic
reviews, three records came back in total.

There are ordinary reasons for that pattern. The molecule is inexpensive and difficult to
protect commercially, which weakens the incentive to fund large trials. It is already sold
freely as a cosmetic ingredient, so there is no commercial need to prove anything to a regulator.
And a compelling mechanistic story is often enough to sell a product, which removes the pressure
that would otherwise drive a company to run the expensive, blinded, vehicle-controlled study that
could settle the question.

The result is a compound with fifty years of mechanism and almost no controlled human outcome
data.

## 8. Some sites cite specific trials with specific numbers. Are those real?

Several of them, we could not find at all.

During our review, peptide vendor and affiliate sites described a 2022 randomized controlled
trial in 50 patients with hair loss reporting a 22 percent hair count increase against minoxidil
at p=0.01, a 2023 phase II trial in 40 post-surgical patients reporting 35 percent scar volume
reduction against silicone, and a human trial reporting a 31.6 percent wrinkle volume reduction
against a comparison product.

We searched PubMed and the ClinicalTrials.gov registry and could not locate any of them. None of
the sites making those claims offered a PMID, an NCT number, or any citation at all.

We are not in a position to say those studies definitely do not exist. We can say we could not
find them, that we have excluded them from our evidence tables, and that a confident trial
statistic with no identifier attached, on a page that also sells the product, deserves your
suspicion by default. That is a useful habit well beyond this one compound.

## 9. The skin research and the injectable products are the same molecule, so why does the distinction matter?

Because almost everything positive that gets cited for this compound comes from the skin side,
and almost all of the consumer demand is on the injectable side.

A cosmetic safety review examines what happens when an ingredient sits on the surface of the
skin in a finished consumer product. It says nothing about what happens when a substance enters
the bloodstream. A topical trial measures a cream against a cream. It does not measure an
injection against anything.

Vendors routinely cite the cosmetic literature and the cosmetic safety conclusion in support of
injectable products. That substitution is, in our assessment, the single most common way a
reader gets misled about GHK-Cu. When you see evidence cited for this compound, the first
question is always which product the evidence was actually about.

## 10. What would change your mind?

A specific and already-identifiable thing.

There is a Phase 2 trial currently recruiting, NCT07437586, testing a topical GHK-Cu gel against
a vehicle placebo in acute skin wounds, with a planned enrollment of 60. That design isolates the
peptide against its own base, which is exactly what the existing positive literature fails to do.
It has no posted results yet.

If that trial reports a clear positive result on objective endpoints, we would revise this Guide
and say so plainly. If it reports null, that would strengthen the current reading considerably.

For injectable use, the bar is simply the existence of human data. A published human trial with
real safety monitoring would change the conversation from "uncharacterized" to something that can
actually be discussed.

We would also revise this Guide if a human reviewer verifies the regulatory items we could not
retrieve, or if the two review articles we could not read (PMID 41966639 and PMID 39963574) turn
out to contain human evidence we missed. Any such change will be recorded in the correction
history at the end of the Guide.
