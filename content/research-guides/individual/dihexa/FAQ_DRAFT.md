# Dihexa: questions members actually ask

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

These answers follow the same rule as the Guide itself. Where the honest answer is that nobody knows,
that is the answer given.

---

## 1. Does dihexa work?

Nobody knows, and that is not a hedge. There is no human evidence of any kind.

A ClinicalTrials.gov query for dihexa returned zero registered studies, interventional or
observational (checked 2026-07-21). A PubMed search restricted to clinical trial publication types
returned zero records (checked 2026-07-21). No human study of any design was located.

That zero was tested rather than assumed. In the same session and against the same registry, a
deliberately fake study identifier returned a not-found error, and a control search on a well-studied
drug returned hundreds of real studies. So the zero reflects genuine absence, not a broken search.

What exists is research in rats, in rat brain tissue, and in cells in a dish. In rat hippocampal
neurons and rat slice cultures, dihexa induced the formation of new connections between nerve cells,
and in male Sprague-Dawley rats it produced a procognitive effect in a maze task (PMID 25187433). A
separate 2024 study in male Wistar rats was negative: dihexa did not protect against a chemically
induced model of Huntington's-like symptoms (PMID 38489193). Those are real findings in rats and in
cells. They say nothing about what happens in a person.

The honest claim grade for "dihexa works for any human condition" is **G (Unverified)**. The grade for
"dihexa induced synaptogenesis in rat neuronal preparations" is **D (Preclinical)**.

## 2. But I found clinical trials for it. What are those?

They are for a different compound, and this is the most important question on this page.

The trials people find belong to **fosgonimeton**, also called **ATH-1017** and formerly
**NDX-1017**. It is a distinct brain-penetrant HGF and MET activating clinical candidate developed by
Athira Pharma, formerly M3 Biotechnology, a company that had commercialised dihexa. The shared
commercial lineage is why the two get conflated. ALZFORUM, a curated therapeutics database, describes
fosgonimeton only as one that *may be related* to dihexa. It does not say they are the same chemical.

Fosgonimeton's registered studies retrieved during this research were NCT04488419 (Phase 2/3,
completed), NCT04491006 (Phase 2, completed), NCT04886063 (Phase 2/3, terminated), NCT04831281 (Phase
2, in Parkinson's disease dementia and Lewy body dementia, terminated), and NCT05511558 (Phase 1 ADME,
completed).

There is a second half to this answer that rarely travels with the first. **That programme did not
succeed.** Per ALZFORUM, the ACT-AD Phase 2 trial failed its primary endpoint, and the LIFT-AD Phase
2/3 trial failed both its primary and its key secondary endpoints.

So borrowing credibility from those trials for dihexa is wrong twice over. It attributes another
compound's programme to dihexa, and it implies a favourable clinical signal that the programme did not
produce.

## 3. Is it safe?

Nobody knows that either, and there is a specific reason for concern that most content about this
compound leaves out.

There is no human safety profile of any kind. No adverse event profile, no known contraindication set,
no interaction data, no long-term exposure data, and no organ-system toxicity characterisation in
humans. Absence of trials means absence of systematically collected human safety data. It is not
evidence of safety.

The specific concern follows directly from how dihexa is proposed to work. Dihexa is proposed to
amplify signalling through the hepatocyte growth factor (HGF) and c-Met system (PMID 25187433). c-MET
is the product of a proto-oncogene, and HGF and Met signalling is an established contributor to
oncogenesis and tumour progression, promoting proliferation, angiogenesis, invasion,
epithelial-mesenchymal transition, and metastasis across multiple tumour types. It is an active target
for cancer-inhibiting drugs (PMID 22530990, PMID 33123991).

Put plainly: a compound intended to amplify a pathway that the pharmaceutical industry spends its
effort designing drugs to block. That is an inherent theoretical risk, and it has never been
characterised in people, because no human data exists.

Two things have to be held together here. This is not a speculative worry invented to fill a safety
section: it is mechanistic reasoning grounded in two peer-reviewed oncology reviews. And it is not a
demonstrated harm: no adverse event has been observed in a person, because no person has been
systematically studied. The word "theoretical" describes the state of the evidence. It is not
reassurance.

An independent expert review of dihexa by the Alzheimer's Drug Discovery Foundation reported no human
clinical trials and flagged this same tumour-progression concern. That document could not be read
directly during this research, so this is a paraphrase and nothing from it is quoted.

Xenios does not describe dihexa as safe, as well tolerated, or as free of side effects, because no
evidence supports any of those statements.

## 4. Is it legal? Is it banned in sport?

The honest answer to both is that this research could not establish it, and saying so is more useful
than guessing.

All checks were made on 2026-07-21.

**FDA.** Secondary and vendor-adjacent pages claim dihexa was placed on, and later affected by changes
to, the FDA 503A bulk drug substances Category 2 list. Two attempts to retrieve the relevant FDA.gov
page returned HTTP 404, so no FDA-sourced statement about dihexa could be confirmed. **This Guide
therefore states no FDA position.** A page that states one confidently is not sourced to the
regulator.

**WADA.** Dihexa is not known to be named explicitly on the WADA Prohibited List. Aggregator pages
assert that it falls under the S0 non-approved substances catch-all because it lacks regulatory
approval for human therapeutic use. Attempts to retrieve the official list failed: the page returned
empty content and two mirror PDFs returned HTTP 403 and unparseable binary. **The S0 characterisation
is unconfirmed and is not stated here as fact.**

If you compete in a tested sport, ask your own anti-doping authority directly. Do not rely on an
inference, including one made here.

What can be said is narrower: dihexa is an investigational research compound with no marketing
approval in any jurisdiction identified during this research, and ClinicalTrials.gov holds zero
registered studies of it. No regulatory position from the European Union, the United Kingdom, Canada,
Australia, or elsewhere was retrieved. Absence there means not checked, not permitted.

"Not approved" is a different question from "illegal to possess", and the second is a legal question
specific to where you live. Xenios does not give legal advice.

## 5. Can I get it from Xenios?

No. Xenios does not sell dihexa, does not supply it, and does not tell members where to obtain it or
how to use it.

This Guide exists for a different reason. Dihexa is sold widely by research-chemical and consumer
peptide vendors as a nootropic, and vendor pages surfaced prominently in every search performed during
this research. Much of what is written about it presents rat findings, unverified numbers, and another
compound's clinical programme as though they were established human facts. The purpose here is to give
an accurate account of what is and is not known, including the parts that are unflattering to the
compound.

## 6. Why is there no human research if the animal work looked good?

The animal work is genuinely interesting, and the honest answer is that the record simply stops before
humans.

Dihexa came out of academic work at Washington State University on analogs of angiotensin IV. The
foundational 2014 paper is good mechanistic science: it showed the effect in rat neuronal preparations
and then showed it disappeared when HGF signalling or c-Met was blocked, which is stronger evidence of
mechanism than most compounds in this space have (PMID 25187433).

What did not follow was a human programme for dihexa itself. Taking a molecule from rodent work into
human trials requires a sponsor prepared to fund toxicology, manufacturing to a pharmaceutical
standard, and phased trials. What appears to have happened instead is that the commercial effort moved
to a different molecule, fosgonimeton, which is the one that entered trials. And no systematic
toxicology or carcinogenicity study of dihexa in any species was located during this research either,
which is a notable gap given the mechanism.

Meanwhile the compound is sold as a consumer product. Compounds sold without approval do not need a
trial in order to be sold, so the incentive to run one is weak.

## 7. I have seen a claim that it is thousands of times more potent than a natural growth factor. Is that true?

That figure is deliberately not reproduced in this Guide, for two reasons.

First, its primary source was not retrieved and verified during this research, so it cannot be stated
as fact here.

Second, and more importantly, even if verified it would be an in-vitro relative-potency figure from a
specific laboratory assay in a non-human system. It is not a clinical effect size. To a reader it
reads as a claim of superhuman efficacy, and it is exactly the sort of precise-sounding number that
survives being copied while its qualifications fall away.

The same applies to a specific HGF binding affinity figure that circulates. Its primary source was not
opened during this research, so no number appears here either.

If a claim about this compound arrives as a striking multiplier with no study attached, treat that as
information about the page you are reading rather than about the molecule.

## 8. What about the rat studies? Those looked convincing.

They are real, and two things materially change how they should be read.

First, the positive findings are in rats and rat tissue. In male Sprague-Dawley rats, dihexa produced
a procognitive effect in a maze task, and in rat hippocampal neurons and rat slice cultures it induced
the formation of new nerve cell connections (PMID 25187433). A rodent result is a reason to run a human
trial. It is not a substitute for one, and in dihexa's case no human trial has been run.

Second, the rat record is not uniformly positive. In male Wistar rats given 3-nitropropionic acid to
model Huntington's-disease-like symptoms, dihexa did not protect against the induced deficits, despite
its reported activity in other rodent neurodegeneration models (PMID 38489193).

Those two results are not in scientific conflict. They used different rat strains, different models,
and different insults, and a compound can act in one and not another. They are in conflict as
impressions, and content that reports only the first leaves a false picture of a reliably
neuroprotective compound.

You may also encounter a 2018 systematic review reporting cognitive benefits. That review covers
angiotensin IV generally rather than dihexa, it screened 450 articles down to 32 experimental studies,
and it contained **no human studies at all** (PMID 29733881). It explains why researchers found this
class interesting. It is not evidence about dihexa.

## 9. If the molecule itself is uncertain, what about the product?

This is a separate risk, and in practical terms it may be the more immediate one.

No analytical study testing material sold as dihexa was located. Nobody appears to have systematically
checked what is in these vials. That is a gap in knowledge, not a clean result.

There is also nothing official to check a product against. There is no pharmacopoeial monograph for
dihexa and no approved reference standard, so even a well-intentioned supplier has no published
specification to test against and no authenticated sample to compare to. Vendor-supplied material has
no regulatory identity, purity, or content verification behind it, and vendor claims are a grade E or G
market claim at best.

One further identity point, which is the practical version of question 2. The names matter. "Dihexa",
"PNB-0408", and "N-hexanoic-Tyr-Ile-(6)-aminohexanoic amide" are the same compound. "Fosgonimeton",
"ATH-1017", and "NDX-1017" are a different one. A product or an article that blurs those is not being
careful with the thing that matters most.

See QUALITY_AND_DOCUMENTATION.md for the full standard a supplier would have to meet, every item of
which is currently unmet for this compound.

## 10. What would change your mind?

A specific list, so that this position can be tested rather than simply asserted.

- **A registered, completed human trial of dihexa itself.** A properly registered study with a control
  group, a pre-specified outcome, and published results. Today there is not even a registration.
- **Published human safety and pharmacokinetic data.** Evidence about how a person absorbs, distributes,
  and clears it, and what happens across a defined observation period with systematic adverse event
  collection.
- **Carcinogenicity and toxicology data.** Given that the central concern follows from the mechanism,
  systematic toxicology in animals and eventually carcinogenicity assessment would be the most
  informative single addition to the record. None in any species was located.
- **Evidence on the oncologic question specifically.** Anything characterising what growth-factor
  pathway potentiation does over time in a living body, at exposures a person would encounter.
- **A verified primary source for the circulating potency and binding affinity figures**, framed
  honestly as in-vitro assay comparisons in non-human systems.
- **Independent analytical testing of commercial dihexa.** Batch testing across multiple suppliers for
  identity, purity, and content, published by a party with no commercial stake.
- **A retrievable regulator statement**, from FDA, WADA, or any other authority, that names dihexa
  specifically. Both attempted checks failed during this research.

If those appeared, this Guide would be updated, the change would be recorded in its correction history
with a date, and the claim grades would move. Until then the grades stay at D for preclinical findings
and G for anything human.

## 11. What should I do with all this?

Nothing in this Guide is a recommendation, in either direction. It is an account of the evidence.

If dihexa came up because something is genuinely bothering you, whether that is memory, focus, or
worry about cognitive decline, the more useful conversation is about that underlying concern with a
clinician who knows your history. The Guide includes a list of questions written to be taken into that
conversation as they stand, including one specifically about the growth-factor pathway question and
your own history.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides. This FAQ
is educational. It is not medical advice, it is not a recommendation, and it is not an offer to supply
anything. Xenios does not sell dihexa.
