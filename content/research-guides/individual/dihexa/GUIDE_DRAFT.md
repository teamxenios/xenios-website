# Dihexa

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## One-minute summary

Dihexa is a synthetic peptide studied for its effects on nerve cell connections. The honest
position, and the single most important thing in this Guide, is that there is no human evidence for
dihexa at all. This is not a case of thin or mixed data. It is a case of absent data.

- A query of ClinicalTrials.gov, the main United States registry of clinical studies, returned
  **zero registered studies** of dihexa, interventional or observational (checked 2026-07-21).
- A PubMed search restricted to clinical trial publication types returned **zero records** for
  dihexa (checked 2026-07-21).
- That zero was tested rather than assumed. In the same session and against the same registry, a
  deliberately fake study identifier returned a not-found error, and a control search on a
  well-studied drug returned hundreds of real studies. So the zero reflects genuine absence rather
  than a broken search.
- **The trials people find when they search are for a different compound.** Athira Pharma's
  Alzheimer's programme is for fosgonimeton, also called ATH-1017 and formerly NDX-1017. It is a
  distinct clinical candidate from the same commercial lineage, and the curated therapeutics
  database ALZFORUM describes it only as one that *may be related* to dihexa. Its results are not
  dihexa's results. This is the most common error made about this compound.
- **And that programme did not succeed.** Per ALZFORUM, fosgonimeton's ACT-AD Phase 2 trial failed
  its primary endpoint, and its LIFT-AD Phase 2/3 trial failed both its primary and its key
  secondary endpoints. So the nearest clinical relative of dihexa did not demonstrate efficacy,
  which weakens rather than supports any borrowed inference.
- There is a real preclinical literature, meaning research done in animals and in cultured cells
  rather than in people. In rat hippocampal neurons and rat slice cultures, dihexa induced the
  formation of new connections between nerve cells, and in male Sprague-Dawley rats it produced a
  procognitive effect in a maze task. A separate 2024 study in male Wistar rats was **negative**:
  dihexa did not protect against a chemically induced model of Huntington's-like symptoms.
- **The central safety concern comes from the mechanism itself.** Dihexa is proposed to work by
  amplifying signalling through the hepatocyte growth factor (HGF) and c-Met system. c-MET is the
  product of a proto-oncogene, and this pathway is an established contributor to tumour growth and
  spread, to the point that cancer drugs are designed to block it. A compound intended to amplify
  the pathway that oncology drugs are designed to block carries an inherent theoretical risk. No
  human data of any kind exists, so that risk has never been characterised in people.
- On safety generally, the correct statement is not that dihexa is safe. It is that nothing is
  known. There is no adverse event profile, no contraindication set, no interaction data, no
  long-term exposure data, and no organ-system toxicity characterisation in humans.
- Two regulatory checks could not be completed. FDA pages returned errors and WADA sources could not
  be read. **This Guide therefore states no FDA position and no anti-doping status for dihexa.**
  Pages that state either confidently are not sourced to the regulator.

Xenios does not sell dihexa, does not supply it, and does not tell members how to obtain or use it.
This Guide exists so that a member reading about dihexa elsewhere has an accurate account of what is
and is not known.

**Overall claim grade for "dihexa is effective for any human condition": G (Unverified).**
**Overall claim grade for "dihexa induced synaptogenesis in rat neuronal preparations": D (Preclinical).**

## What it is

Dihexa, which also carries the development code PNB-0408, is a synthetic oligopeptide. An
oligopeptide is a short chain of amino acids, the building blocks of proteins. It is an analog of
angiotensin IV, meaning a laboratory-made molecule built to resemble a naturally occurring peptide
called angiotensin IV. Its chemical description is N-hexanoic-Tyr-Ile-(6)-aminohexanoic amide.

It was developed by researchers at Washington State University and has been studied for neurotrophic
and synaptogenic activity, meaning effects on the growth and survival of nerve cells and on the
formation of connections between them.

Its proposed mechanism runs through the hepatocyte growth factor (HGF) and c-Met receptor system
rather than through the classical angiotensin receptors that its parent molecule acts on. That
distinction matters and is covered in the mechanism section below.

Dihexa is an investigational research compound. No marketing approval in any jurisdiction was
identified in this research. PubMed indexes it under the supplementary concept
"n-hexanoic-tyr-ile-(6) aminohexanoic amide", which is useful for anyone who wants to run the
literature search themselves.

**The compound dihexa is most commonly confused with.** This is the most important paragraph in this
section.

- **Fosgonimeton**, also known as **ATH-1017** and formerly **NDX-1017**, is a **different
  compound**. It is a brain-penetrant HGF and MET activating clinical candidate developed by Athira
  Pharma, formerly M3 Biotechnology, a company that had commercialised dihexa. It has registered
  human clinical trials. Dihexa does not. ALZFORUM, a curated therapeutics database, describes
  fosgonimeton only as one that may be related to dihexa and does not assert that they are the same
  chemical. Findings, trials, and safety data belonging to fosgonimeton are not dihexa's, in either
  direction.

A commonly quoted chemical registry (CAS) number for dihexa appeared during research only on vendor
listings and was not confirmed against a regulatory or authoritative chemical registry, so it is
deliberately not reproduced here.

There is also no pharmacopoeial monograph and no approved reference standard for dihexa, which means
there is no official published specification that any material could be tested against.

## Why interest exists

The interest in dihexa is easy to describe and worth understanding without endorsing it.

The starting point is a genuine and interesting line of academic research. The naturally occurring
peptide angiotensin IV had been reported to affect learning and memory tasks in animals, and a 2018
systematic review of that literature, which screened 450 articles down to 32 experimental studies
and was explicitly limited to non-human models, reported that angiotensin IV showed beneficial
effects on passive and conditioned avoidance and object recognition tasks in normal animals, and
improved spatial working memory in cognitive-deficit models. That review included no human studies
at all.

Dihexa came out of the effort to build a more active analog of that peptide. The foundational work,
published in the Journal of Pharmacology and Experimental Therapeutics in 2014, reported that dihexa
binds HGF and potentiates its signalling at the c-Met receptor, and that in rat hippocampal neurons
and rat slice cultures it induced the formation of new connection points between nerve cells. The
authors then showed that these effects disappeared when HGF signalling or c-Met was blocked, which
is unusually direct mechanistic evidence for a compound of this kind.

Three things should be held alongside that interest.

First, the scientific interest is in a mechanism, and a mechanism is not an outcome. Interest
describes why researchers thought a compound was worth studying. It does not describe what happened
when it was given to people, because as far as any retrievable record shows, it has not been
systematically given to people.

Second, the same mechanism that makes dihexa interesting is the source of the principal safety
concern. The HGF and c-Met pathway is an established contributor to tumour growth and spread. This
is covered in full in the safety section and it should not be read as a footnote to the enthusiasm.

Third, a substantial part of the perceived credibility of dihexa is borrowed from the clinical
programme of a different compound, fosgonimeton, and that programme did not meet its endpoints. That
is discussed in the contradictions section below.

## Terminology

Every technical term used in this Guide, in plain English.

- **Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids, the building blocks of proteins. Shorter than a protein.
- **Oligopeptide.** A peptide made of a small number of amino acids.
- **Analog.** A laboratory-made molecule built to resemble a naturally occurring one, usually with
  the intention of making it more stable or more active.
- **Angiotensin IV.** A naturally occurring peptide, part of the body's renin-angiotensin system,
  which has been studied in animals for effects on learning and memory. Dihexa is an analog of it.
- **Renin-angiotensin system.** A hormone system best known for regulating blood pressure and fluid
  balance, which also has activity in the brain.
- **Neurotrophic.** Supporting the growth, survival, or maintenance of nerve cells.
- **Synaptogenesis.** The formation of synapses, the connection points through which nerve cells
  signal to one another.
- **Spinogenesis.** The formation of dendritic spines, the small protrusions on a nerve cell where
  many synapses form.
- **Hepatocyte growth factor (HGF).** A naturally occurring signalling protein that acts on cells to
  drive growth, movement, and survival.
- **c-Met.** The receptor that HGF acts on. It sits on the cell surface and passes the signal
  inward.
- **Proto-oncogene.** A normal gene which, when altered or overactive, can contribute to cancer.
  c-MET is the product of a proto-oncogene.
- **Receptor.** A structure on or in a cell that a signalling molecule binds to in order to produce
  an effect.
- **Phosphorylation.** The attachment of a phosphate group to a protein. For a receptor such as
  c-Met, it is a standard laboratory signal that the receptor has been activated.
- **Subthreshold concentration.** An amount of a signalling molecule too low to produce an effect on
  its own. Producing an effect at such a level is what "potentiation" means here.
- **Potentiation.** Making an existing signal stronger, as distinct from creating a new one.
- **Antagonist.** A substance that blocks a receptor or a signalling molecule.
- **Short hairpin RNA (shRNA).** A laboratory tool used to switch off production of a specific
  protein, so researchers can test whether an effect depends on it.
- **Preclinical.** Research done before any human study, meaning in animals or in cultured cells.
- **In vitro.** Literally "in glass". Research done in cells or tissue in a dish, outside a living
  body.
- **In vivo.** Research done in a living organism. In this Guide, in vivo work means rats only.
- **Cell line.** Cells grown indefinitely in a laboratory. Human-derived cell lines are still cells
  in a dish, not people.
- **HEK-293 cells.** A widely used human embryonic kidney cell line. A laboratory tool, not a person.
- **Madin-Darby canine kidney (MDCK) cells.** A dog kidney cell line used to study how cells move
  and separate from one another.
- **Organotypic slice culture.** A thin slice of living tissue, here rat brain tissue, kept alive in
  a dish so its structure is preserved.
- **Morris water maze.** A standard rodent test of spatial learning and memory.
- **Sprague-Dawley and Wistar rats.** Two commonly used laboratory rat strains.
- **3-nitropropionic acid.** A toxin used in rats to produce damage resembling some features of
  Huntington's disease, as a disease model.
- **Angiogenesis.** The growth of new blood vessels. Tumours depend on it.
- **Epithelial-mesenchymal transition.** A change in cell state that makes cells more mobile and is
  associated with cancer spread.
- **Metastasis.** The spread of cancer from its original site to elsewhere in the body.
- **Pharmacokinetics.** What the body does to a substance: absorption, distribution, metabolism, and
  elimination, including how long it lasts.
- **Blood-brain barrier.** The lining that restricts what can pass from the bloodstream into the
  brain.
- **Acetylcholinesterase inhibitor.** A class of medicine used in Alzheimer's disease.
- **Primary endpoint.** The main pre-specified result a clinical trial is designed to measure.
  Failing it means the trial did not show what it set out to show.
- **Post-hoc analysis.** An analysis performed after a trial has finished, looking for patterns that
  were not pre-specified. It generates questions rather than answers.
- **Pharmacopoeial monograph.** An official published specification for a substance, stating what it
  must contain and how it must be tested.
- **Reference standard.** An authenticated sample of a substance used to check that other material
  is what it claims to be.
- **Investigational compound.** A substance under study that has not been approved for medical use.

## Proposed mechanism

Everything in this section is **proposed**, and all of it derives from cultured cells, rat neuronal
preparations, and rats. No mechanism has been confirmed in a living human being. Claim grade for
this entire section: **D (Preclinical)**.

**Potentiation of HGF signalling at c-Met.** Dihexa is reported to bind hepatocyte growth factor
(HGF) with high affinity and to potentiate HGF signalling at the c-Met receptor, inducing c-Met
phosphorylation in the presence of subthreshold HGF concentrations, meaning at HGF levels too low to
act on their own (PMID 25187433). The specific binding affinity figure reported for dihexa was not
verified against the primary paper during this research and is therefore not reproduced here.
[UNVERIFIED - primary source not opened, requires human source check]

**Not the classical angiotensin route.** Although dihexa is an analog of angiotensin IV, its proposed
mechanism runs through the HGF and c-Met system rather than through classical angiotensin receptors
(PMID 25187433). Inferences drawn from blood-pressure or classical angiotensin biology therefore do
not carry over.

**New connections in rat nerve tissue.** In dissociated postnatal rat hippocampal neurons and in rat
organotypic hippocampal slice cultures, dihexa induced spinogenesis and synaptogenesis, meaning the
formation of new connection points between nerve cells (PMID 25187433). Grade D.

**How the c-Met dependence was established.** This is the strongest part of the mechanistic case and
deserves to be described precisely. In those rat neuronal preparations, the effects were abolished by
an HGF antagonist and by short hairpin RNA directed at c-Met, which is what shows the effect depends
on c-Met rather than merely coinciding with it. In male Sprague-Dawley rats, the procognitive effect
of dihexa was blocked when an HGF antagonist was delivered directly into the brain (PMID 25187433).
Grade D.

**Supporting cell-culture findings.** In human HEK-293 cells and Madin-Darby canine kidney cells,
both laboratory cell lines rather than people, dihexa and its parent compound induced c-Met
phosphorylation and augmented HGF-dependent cell scattering (PMID 25187433). Human-derived cells in a
dish are still preclinical. Grade D.

**Why the mechanism is also the risk.** This is a growth-factor-pathway potentiation mechanism, and
that is the same mechanism that generates the principal theoretical safety concern for this compound.
The mechanism section and the safety section are not separable here. See the safety categories below.

**Species and systems used across the mechanistic literature:** male Sprague-Dawley rats,
dissociated postnatal rat hippocampal neurons, rat organotypic hippocampal slice cultures, human
HEK-293 cells, and Madin-Darby canine kidney cells. No human in vivo mechanism data was located.

## Human evidence

**There is none.**

This is the central finding of this Guide, and it is stated as plainly as possible.

| Study | Design | Population | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| *(no records)* | | | |

The evidence table is empty, and the empty table is the result.

What was searched, and what came back:

- **ClinicalTrials.gov**, queried directly through its public API for the term Dihexa, returned
  **totalCount 0**. No registered interventional or observational study of dihexa exists in the
  registry. This is a registry-absence finding, not an approval statement. (Checked 2026-07-21.)
- **PubMed**, searched with a filter restricting results to clinical trial and randomized controlled
  trial publication types, returned **zero records** for dihexa. (Checked 2026-07-21.)
- A broader PubMed search for the term dihexa returned **18 records in total, none of a clinical
  trial publication type**. (Checked 2026-07-21.)

**The null result was tested, not assumed.** A search that returns nothing looks identical to a
search that is broken, so the retrieval path was checked adversarially in both directions in the same
session and against the same registry API.

- A deliberately fake record identifier, NCT99999999, returned **HTTP 404 Not Found**. The tool does
  not invent records.
- A positive control query on semaglutide, a well-studied drug, returned **totalCount 744** with real
  study identifiers. The tool does return records when records exist.

Between those two controls, the zero for dihexa reflects genuine absence in the registry rather than
a failed query.

**The trials that exist belong to a different compound.** Searching for dihexa will surface Athira
Pharma's Alzheimer's programme. That programme is for **fosgonimeton (ATH-1017, formerly NDX-1017)**,
a distinct brain-penetrant HGF and MET activating clinical candidate from the same commercial
lineage. Its registered studies retrieved during this research were NCT04488419 (Phase 2/3,
completed), NCT04491006 (Phase 2, completed), NCT04886063 (Phase 2/3, terminated), NCT04831281
(Phase 2, in Parkinson's disease dementia and Lewy body dementia, terminated), and NCT05511558

ALZFORUM describes fosgonimeton only as one that may be related to dihexa and does not assert
chemical identity. Second, per ALZFORUM, the **ACT-AD Phase 2 trial failed its primary endpoint** and
the **LIFT-AD Phase 2/3 trial failed both its primary and key secondary endpoints**. So the nearest
clinical relative of dihexa did not demonstrate efficacy. Borrowing credibility from that programme
for dihexa would be misleading twice over.

Claim grade for any statement of the form "dihexa does X in people": **G (Unverified)**. No claim in
this Guide reaches grade A, B, or C, because no human study of dihexa of any design was located.

## Preclinical evidence

This is where the real dihexa literature lives. It is genuine research, and it is entirely in rats,
rat tissue, and cultured cells. Every finding below carries its model in the same sentence. None of
it establishes an effect in a person.

**Male Sprague-Dawley rats, Morris water maze; rat hippocampal neurons; rat organotypic slice
cultures; human HEK-293 cells; Madin-Darby canine kidney cells.** In this foundational study, dihexa
and its parent compound Nle1-AngIV bound HGF with high affinity, induced c-Met phosphorylation in the
presence of subthreshold HGF, augmented HGF-dependent cell scattering in the canine kidney cells, and
induced hippocampal spinogenesis and synaptogenesis in the rat neuronal preparations. The effects
were abolished by an HGF antagonist and by short hairpin RNA against c-Met, and the procognitive
effect in the rats was blocked by an HGF antagonist delivered directly into the rat brain
(Benoist CC, Kawas LH, Zhu M, Tyson KA, Stillmaker L, Appleyard SM, Wright JW, Wayman GA, Harding JW.
J Pharmacol Exp Ther. 2014;351(2):390-402. PMID 25187433). Grade D.

**Male Wistar rats, 3-nitropropionic acid model of Huntington's-disease-like symptoms. NEGATIVE
RESULT.** In male Wistar rats, the angiotensin IV analog PNB-0408 (dihexa) **did not protect** against
the deficits induced by 3-nitropropionic acid neurotoxicity, despite the compound's reported activity
in other rodent neurodegeneration models (J Huntingtons Dis. 2024. PMID 38489193). Grade D.

This negative result is given equal weight to the positive findings deliberately. A negative rodent
study is the result most likely to be absent from secondary coverage of a compound, and reporting
only the 2014 findings would leave a reader with a picture of a reliably neuroprotective compound
that the 2024 result does not support. The two are not in scientific conflict: they used different
rat strains, different models, and different insults, and a compound can act in one and not another.
They are in conflict as impressions, which is why both appear here.

**Systematic review of 32 experimental studies, explicitly non-human, screened from 450 articles.** In
non-human experimental models, angiotensin IV showed beneficial effects on passive and conditioned
avoidance and object recognition tasks in normal animals, and improved spatial working memory in
cognitive-deficit models. **The review included no human studies** (Neurosci Biobehav Rev. 2018. PMID
29733881). Grade D.

An important qualification: this is the broader angiotensin IV literature that surrounds dihexa. It is
**not a dihexa-specific efficacy finding**, and it must not be read as one. It explains why the class
attracted attention.

**The size of the literature.** A PubMed search for the term dihexa returned 18 records in total, none
of a clinical trial publication type (checked 2026-07-21). Most were not individually read during this
research, and no claim in this Guide rests on them. They are mentioned to map the literature, not as
substantiated results. Grade G for any specific finding within them until a human reads them.

**A gap, stated as a gap.** No systematic toxicology or carcinogenicity study of dihexa in any species
was located during this research. Its absence was not affirmatively confirmed; it was simply not
found. Given that the central safety concern for this compound is oncologic, this is a material gap
rather than a tidy-up item.

## Contradictions and disagreement

Where sources genuinely disagree, both positions are stated, along with an assessment of which the
retrieved evidence supports.

**1. Whether dihexa has been tested in humans.**

- *Position A.* Popular and vendor-adjacent content sometimes implies that dihexa has clinical trial
  support, usually by pointing at Athira Pharma's Alzheimer's programme.
- *Position B.* Dihexa itself has zero ClinicalTrials.gov registrations and zero PubMed records with
  a clinical trial publication type. The trials belong to fosgonimeton (ATH-1017, formerly NDX-1017),
  a distinct brain-penetrant HGF and MET activating clinical candidate developed by Athira Pharma,
  formerly M3 Biotechnology, which had commercialised dihexa. ALZFORUM describes fosgonimeton only as
  one that may be related to dihexa and does not assert chemical identity.
- *Assessment.* Position B is correct, and this distinction is the most important correction in this
  Guide. Fosgonimeton's registered studies are NCT04488419, NCT04491006, NCT04886063, NCT04831281,
  and NCT05511558. Per ALZFORUM, the ACT-AD Phase 2 trial failed its primary endpoint and the LIFT-AD
  Phase 2/3 trial failed both its primary and key secondary endpoints. So even the related clinical
  candidate did not demonstrate efficacy, which weakens rather than supports any borrowed inference
  about dihexa.

**2. Potency framing.**

- *Position A.* A widely repeated claim describes dihexa as being enormously more potent than
  brain-derived neurotrophic factor in a neurotrophic assay, expressed as a large multiplier. That
  phrasing appeared in a search snippet during this research.
- *Position B.* That is an in-vitro relative-potency figure from a specific assay in a non-human
  system, not a clinical effect size, and its primary source was not retrieved or verified during this
  research.
- *Assessment.* The multiplier is deliberately not reproduced in this Guide. An assay-specific number
  of that kind reads to a lay reader as a claim of superhuman efficacy, and it is exactly the sort of
  precise-sounding figure that survives being copied while its qualifications fall away. If it is ever
  used, a human must verify it against the primary paper and frame it explicitly as an in-vitro assay
  comparison in a non-human system.

**3. Where the preclinical record disagrees with itself.**

- *Position A.* The 2014 work in male Sprague-Dawley rats and rat neuronal preparations reported
  procognitive effects and new synapse formation, with a well-established c-Met dependence.
- *Position B.* The 2024 work in male Wistar rats reported that dihexa did not protect against
  3-nitropropionic acid induced deficits.
- *Assessment.* Both are correct and they are not in scientific conflict, because they used different
  rat strains, different models, and different insults. They are in conflict as impressions. Content
  reporting only the first leaves a false picture, so this Guide reports both with equal prominence.

**4. Where the mechanism is also the risk.**

- Dihexa's proposed mechanism is potentiation of HGF signalling at c-Met, which is why anyone is
  interested in it.
- c-MET is the product of a proto-oncogene, and HGF and Met signalling contributes to oncogenesis and
  tumour progression across multiple tumour types, which is why it is an active target for
  cancer-inhibiting drugs.
- *Assessment.* Both statements come from peer-reviewed sources and neither is disputed. The correct
  treatment is neither dismissal nor alarm. The concern is theoretical only in the precise sense that
  no adverse event has been observed in a person, because no person has been systematically studied.
  It is not speculative: it follows directly from the compound's own proposed mechanism.

## Limitations

Limitations of the evidence base itself:

- There is no human evidence of any design, so there is no human effect size, no responder rate, no
  dose-response relationship, and no duration of effect to report.
- The positive in-vivo work is in a single species, the rat, and largely in one strain in one
  behavioural paradigm.
- The one retrieved study in a different rat strain and a different disease model returned a negative
  result.
- The supporting mechanistic work used laboratory cell lines, including human embryonic kidney cells
  and dog kidney cells. A response in a cell line does not establish a response in intact tissue, let
  alone in a person.
- The 2018 systematic review that is often invoked in support of this class covers angiotensin IV
  generally, not dihexa, and contains no human studies at all.
- No systematic toxicology or carcinogenicity study in any species was located, which is the single
  most consequential absence given the mechanism.

Limitations of this Guide's research process, stated so a reader can judge its reliability:

- **FDA sources could not be retrieved.** Two attempts to fetch the relevant FDA.gov compounding page
  returned HTTP 404. Secondary and vendor-adjacent pages claim a bulk drug substances category
  placement for dihexa. Because no FDA-sourced statement could be confirmed, **no FDA claim about
  dihexa appears anywhere in this Guide**.
- **WADA sources could not be retrieved.** The official Prohibited List page returned empty content
  and two mirror PDFs returned HTTP 403 and unparseable binary. **No anti-doping status is stated.**
- **One expert review could not be read directly.** The Alzheimer's Drug Discovery Foundation
  Cognitive Vitality report on dihexa was fetched successfully but could not be parsed to text in this
  environment, so its content is known only through a summary. It is used as paraphrase only, and no
  wording from it is quoted.
- The primary source for dihexa's reported HGF binding affinity, and for the circulating potency
  comparison against brain-derived neurotrophic factor, was not opened during this research. Neither
  number appears here.
- The reported CAS registry number appears only on vendor pages and was not confirmed against an
  authoritative chemical registry, so it is not reproduced.
- Most of the 18 PubMed records returned for dihexa were not individually read. None is of a clinical
  trial publication type, so a fuller reading is unlikely to change the central finding.
- Only ClinicalTrials.gov was searched. The EU Clinical Trials Register, ISRCTN, the WHO ICTRP, and
  the Japanese and Chinese registries were not searched.

## Quality and identity

This section is about the material itself, separately from the science. It matters because an
unapproved investigational compound is, by definition, obtained outside a regulated supply chain.

**Dihexa is sold as a consumer product despite having no human evidence base.** Research-chemical and
consumer peptide vendors sell dihexa as a nootropic, and vendor pages surfaced prominently in every
search performed during this research. None of that is scientific evidence. Vendor material is a grade
E or G market claim only, and it has no regulatory identity, purity, or content verification behind
it.

**There is nothing to check a product against.** There is no pharmacopoeial monograph for dihexa and
no approved reference standard, so even a well-intentioned supplier has no official specification to
test material against and no authenticated sample to compare it to.

**No analytical study of material sold as dihexa was located.** Nobody appears to have systematically
checked what is in these vials. That is an absence of information, not a favourable result.

**Naming confusion is a live quality and safety risk.** "Dihexa", "PNB-0408", and
"N-hexanoic-Tyr-Ile-(6)-aminohexanoic amide" refer to the same compound, while "fosgonimeton",
"ATH-1017", and "NDX-1017" refer to a **different** clinical candidate. Content that conflates them
will wrongly attribute human trial data to dihexa. This is an identity problem before it is an
evidence problem.

**The information environment around this compound is unusually poor.** Search engines and content
aggregators return confidently worded pages about dihexa "clinical trials", "FDA category status",
and "WADA status" that are hosted on peptide-vendor and search-optimised content domains. Several
such pages appeared during this research. They are treated as unreliable and are not cited as
evidence anywhere in this Guide. A member should assume that a confident, unsourced claim about
dihexa's regulatory status or human evidence is one of these pages.

**A note on circulating numbers.** A widely repeated potency comparison against brain-derived
neurotrophic factor, and a specific HGF binding affinity figure, are both deliberately excluded from
this Guide because their primary sources were not opened and verified during this research. A
precise-sounding number with no traceable source does more harm than good in health content.

See QUALITY_AND_DOCUMENTATION.md for the full documentation standard and the honest current state,
which is that Xenios has confirmed no quality item for this compound.

## Safety categories

Nothing in this section is reassurance. These are categories of concern that remain open because no
one has closed them.

**1. There is no human safety profile of any kind. This is the primary safety finding.** There is no
established adverse event profile, no known contraindication set, no drug-interaction data, no
long-term exposure data, and no organ-system toxicity characterisation in humans. ClinicalTrials.gov
returned zero studies and PubMed returned zero clinical trial records (checked 2026-07-21). **Absence
of trials means absence of systematically collected human safety data. It is not evidence of safety.**

**2. Theoretical oncologic risk from growth-factor pathway potentiation. This is the central and
genuine safety concern for dihexa, and it follows directly from the proposed mechanism rather than
from any observed adverse event.**

The reasoning is short and it is worth following in full.

- Dihexa's proposed mechanism is potentiation of HGF signalling at c-Met (PMID 25187433).
- c-MET is the product of a proto-oncogene, and HGF and Met signalling contributes to oncogenesis and
  tumour progression, promoting proliferation, angiogenesis, invasion, epithelial-mesenchymal
  transition, and metastasis across multiple tumour types. It is an active target for cancer-inhibiting
  drugs (Cecchi F, Rabe DC, Bottaro DP. Expert Opin Ther Targets. 2012. PMID 22530990; Zambelli A,
  Biamonti G, Amato A. Adv Exp Med Biol. 2021. PMID 33123991).
- A compound designed to **amplify** the pathway that oncology drugs are designed to **block**
  therefore carries an inherent theoretical risk.
- That risk **has not been characterised in humans**. No human carcinogenicity data for dihexa was
  retrieved, because no human data of any kind exists.

Two things must be held together here, and neither may be dropped. This is not a speculative worry
invented to pad a safety section: it is mechanistic reasoning grounded in two peer-reviewed oncology
reviews. And it is not a demonstrated harm: no adverse event in a person has been observed, because no
person has been systematically studied. The word "theoretical" describes the state of the evidence. It
does not function as reassurance.

An independent expert review of dihexa by the Alzheimer's Drug Discovery Foundation, in its Cognitive
Vitality series, reported no human clinical trials and flagged HGF and c-Met implication in tumour
progression and metastasis. That document was retrieved but could not be parsed to text during this
research, so this is a paraphrase of a summary and no wording from it is quoted here.
[PARAPHRASE ONLY - requires human source check of the original PDF]

**3. No characterised human pharmacokinetics.** Absorption, metabolism, half-life, and bioavailability
in a person are unknown. No human pharmacokinetic study was found. Whether dihexa crosses the human
blood-brain barrier as reported for rodents is also unknown, so even the basic question of whether it
reaches the tissue of interest in a person is open.

**4. Long-term growth-factor pathway potentiation is entirely unstudied.** Duration is the axis on
which the oncologic concern would most plausibly matter, and there is no long-term exposure data in
humans of any kind.

**5. Product quality as a safety issue in its own right.** Separately from the molecule, material sold
under the name dihexa has unverified identity, purity, and content, with no pharmacopoeial monograph
or approved reference standard behind it and no analytical study of such material located.

**6. Entirely unstudied populations.** No human data of any kind exists for dihexa, which means no data
exists for any specific population, including anyone with a current or past cancer diagnosis, anyone
with a family history that raises their cancer risk, pregnancy, breastfeeding, children, older adults,
and people taking any medication. The cancer-history point deserves separate emphasis given the
mechanism.

## Interactions

No interaction data for dihexa was located in any retrieved source. No study of dihexa alongside any
medication or supplement was found. **Interactions are unstudied, which is not the same as absent.**

One specific signal is worth naming precisely, with its attribution attached. A post-hoc analysis of
the **fosgonimeton** ACT-AD trial suggested that acetylcholinesterase inhibitors, a class of medicine
used in Alzheimer's disease, **might antagonise its effect**. That analysis concerns fosgonimeton, a
different compound, and a post-hoc analysis generates a question rather than an answer. It is recorded
here as a reason the question is worth asking about this class of compound, and explicitly **not** as
a dihexa finding.

The following categories would require review by a qualified professional before anyone considers
anything in this area, because they intersect directly with the proposed mechanism:

- **Any current or past cancer diagnosis**, and any cancer treatment of any kind. The proposed
  mechanism amplifies a pathway that oncology drugs are designed to block.
- **Any known elevated cancer risk**, including family history or a monitored precancerous condition.
- **Any medicine acting on growth-factor signalling**, including c-Met and tyrosine kinase inhibitors.
- **Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors and other medication for cognitive conditions**, on the basis of
  the fosgonimeton post-hoc signal described above.
- **Any other unapproved peptide or research compound taken at the same time**, which makes both
  benefit and harm impossible to attribute.
- **Pregnancy and breastfeeding**, where no data exists at all.

## Questions for a qualified professional

If a member wants to discuss dihexa with a clinician, these are questions that produce a useful
conversation. They are written to be asked as they stand.

1. I have read that there are no registered clinical trials and no human studies of dihexa at all.
   Does that match what you can find, and does it change how you would think about it?
2. The trials I found online turn out to be for a different compound called fosgonimeton, and those
   trials did not meet their endpoints. How much weight would you put on a related compound's failed
   programme?
3. The proposed mechanism amplifies the HGF and c-Met pathway, which I understand is a pathway that
   cancer drugs are designed to block. How do you think about that as a risk, given that nobody has
   studied it in people?
4. Do I have anything in my personal or family history, or in my current monitoring, that would make
   a growth-factor-amplifying compound a particularly poor idea for me?
5. My interest came from research in rats and in cells in a dish, and one of the rat studies was
   negative. How much weight do you put on that kind of evidence when there is no human data behind
   it?
6. What is actually going on with the problem that made me look this up, and is there an approach with
   real human evidence that we have not tried yet?
7. I take the following medications. Which of them would you be most concerned about alongside an
   unstudied compound of this kind?
8. This substance is not approved anywhere I could find, which means any material comes from outside a
   regulated supply chain and there is no official specification to test it against. How do you think
   about unverified identity and purity for something introduced into the body?
9. I compete in a tested sport. Who is the right authority for me to ask about whether this falls
   under the non-approved substances category?
10. If new human evidence on dihexa appears, what would you want to see in it before it changed your
    advice?

This Guide does not give medical advice and does not tell anyone what to do. These questions belong
with a clinician who knows your history.
