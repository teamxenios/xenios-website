# TB-500

A Xenios Research Guide. Written for a member with no scientific training. Our job here is to
tell you what is actually known, what is only proposed, and what nobody knows yet. On this
compound, the third category is by far the largest.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

This Guide is educational. It is not medical advice, it is not a diagnosis, and it is not a
suggestion that you use anything described here.

---

## One-minute summary

TB-500 is a synthetic peptide, a short chain of seven amino acids, based on a small segment of
a larger natural protein called thymosin beta-4. It is sold on the unregulated market and is
discussed mainly in the context of tissue repair and recovery.

The honest evidence verdict is short: **there is no human evidence that TB-500 works for
anything, and no human evidence that it is safe.** That is not a cautious way of saying the
evidence is weak. A full search of the public clinical trial registry on 2026-07-19 returned
exactly one registered human study of TB-500 (NCT07487363). It started in February 2026, it is
still recruiting, its main measurements are counts of adverse events rather than benefits, and
no results have been posted. Human study of this compound has only just begun.

Three further points matter more than anything else in this Guide.

1. **Most of what you have read about TB-500 is probably about a different molecule.** Real
   human trials exist for thymosin beta-4, the full-length 43-amino-acid protein. TB-500 is a
   7-amino-acid fragment. Those trials mostly used eye drops or wound-surface application, in
   groups of nine to a few dozen patients. Treating that research as TB-500 evidence is a
   category error, and commercial sources make it routinely.
2. **Even the animal and laboratory story is unsettled.** One 2024 analytical study found that
   in cultured human fibroblast (skin cell) experiments the TB-500 molecule itself showed no
   wound-healing activity, while one of its breakdown products did. If that holds up, the usual
   explanation of how TB-500 works may be attributing the effect to the wrong molecule.
3. **If you compete under anti-doping rules, this is a documented, concrete risk.** TB-500 is
   named explicitly on the WADA Prohibited List as a prohibited growth factor, prohibited at all
   times, and validated laboratory detection methods exist.

We were unable to verify any US Food and Drug Administration statement this session, because
every fda.gov page our tools requested returned an error. We would rather tell you that than
guess. Nothing about FDA status appears in this Guide as a finding.

## What it is

TB-500 is a laboratory-made peptide. A peptide is a short chain of amino acids, which are the
building blocks proteins are made from. TB-500 is seven amino acids long, written in scientific
shorthand as Ac-LKKTETQ. The "Ac" indicates acetylation, a small chemical cap added to one end
of the chain during manufacture (a modification that does not occur in this position naturally).

That seven-amino-acid sequence corresponds to residues 17 through 23 of a natural human protein
called thymosin beta-4, which is 43 amino acids long. In other words, TB-500 is a short excerpt
of a longer natural protein, made synthetically, with a chemical cap added.

This identity is confirmed in the sources we retrieved. A 2012 doping-control analysis
(Ho et al., PMID 23084823) describes TB-500 as a synthetic version of the naturally occurring
peptide LKKTETQ, corresponding to the segment at positions 17 to 23 of thymosin beta-4, with
artificial acetylation of one end. The official title of the single registered human study of
TB-500 similarly names it as the thymosin beta 4 17-23 fragment.

### Three things that must not be confused

This is the most important framing point in the entire Guide, and it is where most published
consumer material goes wrong.

| | What it is | What evidence attaches to it |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **TB-500** | The synthetic 7-amino-acid acetylated fragment, Ac-LKKTETQ. This is what unregulated vendors sell under the name TB-500. | One registered human study, ongoing, no results. Some animal and cell-culture work. |
| **Thymosin beta-4** (also written Tbeta4 or TB4) | The full-length natural 43-amino-acid protein. A chemically different, much larger molecule. | Nearly all published human trial data attaches here, not to TB-500. |
| **RGN-259 / NL005** | Investigational pharmaceutical products formulated from full-length thymosin beta-4. RGN-259 is an eye solution developed by RegeneRx. NL005 is a recombinant injectable form. | Registered, regulated investigational trials. These are not the same thing as an unregulated vial labelled TB-500. |

TB-500 is also commonly confused with thymosin alpha-1, an unrelated peptide from the thymus
gland with a completely separate history, and it is frequently marketed alongside BPC-157, a
different peptide entirely.

Vendor material and secondary articles frequently use "TB-500" and "thymosin beta-4" as if they
were interchangeable words for one thing. The primary sources we retrieved do not support that
usage.

## Why interest exists

We describe the interest here. We are not endorsing it.

Interest in TB-500 comes from four directions.

**The parent protein has a real research history.** Thymosin beta-4 is a naturally occurring
protein studied for its role in cell movement and tissue repair, and it has been carried into
genuine clinical trials as an investigational eye treatment and wound treatment. That history
gives the name a scientific weight that then transfers, incorrectly, onto the fragment.

**The proposed mechanism is intuitively appealing.** The story told about TB-500 involves cell
migration, new blood vessel formation, and tissue remodelling. Those are recognisable words to
anyone recovering from an injury, and they map neatly onto what a person with a stubborn tendon
problem hopes for.

**It circulates in sport and recovery communities.** TB-500 appears in athletic contexts, which
is precisely why anti-doping authorities named it. Its presence on a prohibited list is
sometimes read backwards by consumers as evidence that it must work, which is not a valid
inference. Prohibited lists include substances based on their potential to affect performance
and their non-approved status, not on demonstrated benefit.

**It is easy to obtain and hard to evaluate.** The unregulated peptide market presents TB-500
with confident claims and no obligation to substantiate them. In that environment, absence of
evidence is not visible to a buyer, because nothing in the marketing surfaces it.

None of these four reasons is evidence of benefit. They explain the interest, not the compound.

## Terminology

Every technical term used in this Guide, in plain English.

**Amino acid.** A basic chemical building block. Chains of amino acids form peptides and
proteins.

**Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids. TB-500 is a peptide of seven amino acids.

**Protein.** A longer chain of amino acids. Thymosin beta-4 is a protein of 43 amino acids.

**Fragment.** A short section taken from a longer sequence. TB-500 is a fragment of thymosin
beta-4. A fragment is not chemically the same thing as the whole, and does not automatically
behave the same way.

**Acetylation.** A small chemical cap added to the end of a peptide chain. In TB-500 this cap
is added artificially during manufacture.

**Synthetic.** Made in a laboratory rather than extracted from a living source.

**Metabolite.** A substance the body produces when it breaks down another substance. If you
take compound A, your body may convert it into metabolites B and C, and those may have their
own activity.

**Actin.** A protein inside cells that forms part of the internal scaffolding, and that cells
use to change shape and move.

**Actin sequestration.** Binding to actin so that it is temporarily held and not available for
building scaffolding. This is the proposed core activity of thymosin beta-4.

**Angiogenesis.** The formation of new blood vessels.

**Extracellular matrix.** The structural material surrounding cells that gives tissue its
shape and strength.

**Fibroblast.** A cell type found in skin and connective tissue, involved in wound repair.
Fibroblasts grown in a dish are a common laboratory model for wound healing.

**In vitro.** Literally "in glass". An experiment performed in cells or chemicals in a dish,
outside a living body.

**In vivo.** An experiment performed in a living animal or person.

**Preclinical.** Research done before human studies, in animals or in laboratory systems.

**Pharmacokinetics.** What the body does to a substance over time: how it is absorbed,
distributed, broken down, and cleared.

**Placebo-controlled.** A study design in which some participants receive an inactive
substance for comparison.

**Double-blind (or double-masked).** Neither the participants nor the researchers assessing
them know who received the active substance.

**Phase 1 / Phase 2 / Phase 3.** Successive stages of human drug testing. Phase 1 is primarily
about safety in a small group. Phase 2 explores whether there is any signal of effect. Phase 3
is the large confirmatory stage. Most substances never complete this path.

**Primary endpoint.** The main outcome a study was designed to measure. It is the result that
counts. Everything else is secondary and more prone to chance findings.

**Statistical significance.** A calculated judgment that a difference between groups is
unlikely to be chance alone, conventionally at a p-value below 0.05. It says nothing about
whether the difference matters to a person.

**Topical.** Applied to a surface, such as the eye or a wound, rather than taken into the body
as a whole.

**Systemic.** Reaching the whole body rather than one surface.

**Neurotrophic keratopathy.** A rare eye condition in which the cornea, the clear front surface
of the eye, loses sensation and fails to heal properly.

**Venous stasis ulcer.** A slow-healing open wound, typically on the lower leg, caused by poor
vein circulation.

**WADA.** The World Anti-Doping Agency, which publishes the international Prohibited List.
**USADA** is the United States national anti-doping organisation.

**Grey market.** Products sold outside the regulated pharmaceutical supply chain, with no
enforced requirement to verify what is in the vial.

**Certificate of Analysis (COA).** A laboratory document stating test results for a specific
manufactured batch. Its value depends entirely on who performed the testing and whether it can
be independently confirmed.

## Proposed mechanism

Everything in this section is **proposed**, not demonstrated, and most of it is inferred from
research on the full-length parent protein rather than shown for the fragment. No human
mechanistic confirmation was retrieved.

The proposed mechanism is actin binding and actin sequestration, with downstream effects on
cell migration, new blood vessel formation, matrix remodelling, and tissue repair. The 2012
equine doping-control paper (Ho et al., PMID 23084823) describes the LKKTETQ segment as the
active site within the thymosin beta-4 protein responsible for actin binding, cell migration
and wound healing. A 2026 narrative review in a sports medicine journal (Mayfield et al.,
PMID 41476424) reports that thymosin beta-4 and its derivative TB-500 promoted angiogenesis and
tissue repair in preclinical models, while stating in the same paper that human orthopaedic
data are lacking.

Note what those two statements are. The first is a description of a protein's known biology
used to introduce an analytical chemistry method. The second is a summary of animal and
laboratory work inside a review article. Neither is a demonstration in a person.

### The complication that is rarely mentioned

A 2024 study (Rahaman et al., PMID 38382158) tested TB-500 and its breakdown products in
cultured human fibroblast wound-healing assays. In those cultured cells, the metabolite
Ac-LKKTE showed significant wound-healing activity, while the parent TB-500 compound did not.
The authors concluded that previously reported wound-healing activity of TB-500 in the
literature may be due to its metabolite Ac-LKKTE rather than the parent form.

If that finding holds, the mechanistic story usually told about TB-500 may be attributing
activity to the wrong molecule. This is a single in vitro result inside an analytical chemistry
paper rather than a dedicated pharmacology study, so it should not be treated as settled either.
The honest position is that the basic question of what the active molecule even is remains open.

**Species and models behind the mechanistic work retrieved here:** rat (in vivo metabolism),
human serum and in vitro enzyme systems, and human fibroblast cell culture. No human
mechanistic study was retrieved.

**Claim grade for the proposed mechanism: D (preclinical), trending toward G (unverified) for
the specific claim that the parent TB-500 molecule is the active agent.**

## Human evidence

**Human evidence for TB-500 is absent.**

That sentence is the finding. It is not a placeholder awaiting better sources.

A full query of the ClinicalTrials.gov registry on 2026-07-19 for thymosin beta 4 returned 18
registered studies. Exactly one of them studies the TB-500 fragment.

### Trials people cite that are not TB-500

The following are real, competently conducted human trials. **None of them studied TB-500.**
Every one studied the full-length thymosin beta-4 protein or a formulated investigational
product made from it, and every one was applied to a surface (an eye or a wound) rather than
given into the body as a whole. We list them so you can recognise them when a vendor or article
presents them as TB-500 evidence.

**Severe dry eye, Phase 2** (Sosne, Dunn, Kim; Cornea, 2015; PMID 25826322). A multicenter,
randomized, double-masked, placebo-controlled trial of an eye solution in nine patients (12
treated eyes, six control eyes) across two US sites. Reported statistically significant
improvement versus placebo in ocular discomfort and corneal staining at day 56, with benefit
persisting 28 days after stopping. **Different molecule (full-length thymosin beta-4), eye drop
route, nine patients.** This says nothing about a fragment given systemically for recovery or
any musculoskeletal purpose.

**Neurotrophic keratopathy, Phase 3** (Sosne et al.; Int J Mol Sci, 2022; PMID 36613994 /
PMC9820614; trial NCT02600429). A multicenter, randomized, placebo-controlled, double-masked
trial of RGN-259 eye solution in 18 subjects with stage 2 to 3 disease, terminated early for
slow recruitment. 60 percent of treated subjects achieved complete corneal healing by day 29
versus 12.5 percent of placebo, which did not reach statistical significance (p=0.0656). At day
43 the difference did reach significance (50.0 percent treated versus 0 percent placebo,
p=0.0359). 16 adverse events occurred across both arms, one considered treatment-related.
**Different molecule, eye drop route, 18 subjects total, terminated early, and the primary
timepoint result was not significant.** The authors themselves note the placebo group was older
with larger lesions and that comfort assessments were subjective. This must never be presented
as evidence that TB-500 heals tissue.

**Venous stasis ulcers** (Guarnera, De Rosa, Camerini; Ann N Y Acad Sci, 2007; PMID 17495250).
A double-blind, placebo-controlled, dose-escalation multicenter trial applied topically, planned
for 72 patients, with 21 enrolled at the time of publication. **This report contains no outcome
data.** It describes methods for a study in progress. It is frequently cited secondhand as
though it demonstrated accelerated healing. The primary record we retrieved does not support
that, and it is a different molecule and a wound-surface route in any case.

The 2026 sports medicine review (Mayfield et al., PMID 41476424) states plainly that human
orthopaedic data are lacking.

## Preclinical evidence

Preclinical means animal or laboratory research. A finding in a rat or in a dish is a reason to
run a human study. It is not a small version of a human result. Every finding below carries its
species or model in the same sentence.

**In rats (in vivo), in human serum and in vitro enzyme systems, and in cultured human
fibroblasts** (Rahaman et al., J Chromatogr B, 2024; PMID 38382158), researchers developed a
mass spectrometry method to measure TB-500 and its breakdown products. In rats, the metabolite
Ac-LK was present at the highest concentration in the first six hours, and Ac-LKK persisted as
a longer-term metabolite detectable up to 72 hours. In cultured human fibroblast wound-healing
assays, the metabolite Ac-LKKTE showed significant wound-healing activity while the parent
TB-500 compound did not, leading the authors to conclude that previously reported wound-healing
activity may belong to the metabolite rather than the parent form.

**In unspecified preclinical models summarised in a narrative review** (Mayfield et al., Am J
Sports Med, 2026; PMID 41476424), thymosin beta-4 and its derivative TB-500 were reported to
have promoted angiogenesis and tissue repair. This is a secondary summary rather than a primary
experiment, the species were not specified in the record available to us, and the underlying
primary studies were not individually retrieved. The same review states that human orthopaedic
data are lacking and that both substances remain banned in sport.

**In horses (equine urine and plasma)** (Ho et al., J Chromatogr A, 2012; PMID 23084823),
researchers developed liquid chromatography mass spectrometry methods to detect acetylated
LKKTETQ and its metabolites, and reported that a non-natural synthesis impurity was detectable
in equine plasma after administration of a TB-500-containing product. This is an analytical and
doping-control study. It is included here for identity and detectability only, and it is **not**
evidence of any therapeutic effect in horses or anywhere else.

**Claim grade for all preclinical findings above: D (preclinical).** None of them supports a
statement about what happens in a person.

## Contradictions and disagreement

Where sources disagree, we show the disagreement rather than picking the tidier answer.

### 1. Is TB-500 itself the active molecule?

**Position A.** TB-500 is the active fragment of thymosin beta-4 and directly drives actin
binding, cell migration, angiogenesis and wound healing. This is the standard framing, reflected
in the 2012 description of LKKTETQ as the protein's active site (PMID 23084823) and in the 2026
review's summary of preclinical models (PMID 41476424).

**Position B.** The 2024 metabolism study (PMID 38382158) found that in cultured fibroblasts the
parent TB-500 compound showed no wound-healing activity while the metabolite Ac-LKKTE did, and
concluded that reported activity may belong to the metabolite rather than the parent form.

**Our assessment.** Unresolved and important. Position B rests on a single in vitro finding
inside an analytical chemistry paper, not a dedicated pharmacology study, so it should not be
treated as settled. But it directly undercuts the simplest version of the TB-500 story, and it
means that even preclinical results obtained with TB-500 may reflect metabolite activity. We
surface it rather than smooth it over.

### 2. Does human trial evidence support TB-500?

**Position A.** Commercial and secondary sources routinely cite thymosin beta-4 trials (dry eye,
neurotrophic keratopathy, wound healing, and an acute heart attack programme) as though they
establish TB-500's efficacy or safety.

**Position B.** Every one of those trials studied full-length thymosin beta-4 or a formulated
investigational product such as RGN-259 or NL005, not the Ac-LKKTETQ fragment. The single
registered TB-500 human study has no posted results.

**Our assessment.** Position B is correct, and this is the central correction this Guide exists
to make. The transfer of evidence from the parent protein to the fragment is not merely
unproven. It is actively challenged by the finding that the fragment and the parent may not even
share the relevant activity. This is the highest-priority accuracy issue on this compound.

### 3. Which anti-doping section prohibits TB-500?

**Position A.** Section S2, specifically S2.3 growth factors, per USADA's published summary of
changes to the 2018 WADA Prohibited List.

**Position B.** Several secondary and commercial sources assert S0 (non-approved substances)
instead, or cite S2 without a subsection, or give a 2011 rather than 2018 listing date.

**Our assessment.** The USADA position is the only one traceable to an anti-doping authority
document we actually retrieved, and we prefer it. WADA's own pages returned empty content to our
tools this session, so the exact 2026 subsection numbering is unconfirmed. The practical point
for a reader is unaffected and safe to state: TB-500 is explicitly named and prohibited at all
times. The precise section citation requires a human check before publication.

### 4. US compounding status

**Position A.** Thymosin beta-4 / TB-500 is a Category 2 substance and barred from 503A
pharmacy compounding.

**Position B.** It was removed from Category 2 in April 2026 after nominators withdrew, and is
scheduled for advisory committee review in July 2026.

**Our assessment.** **Unresolved and unverifiable this session.** Both positions came only from
commercial or vendor-adjacent sources. Every fda.gov page our tools requested returned an error,
and the Federal Register record we did retrieve for the April 2026 Pharmacy Compounding Advisory
Committee meeting notice names no substances at all. **Neither position appears in this Guide as
a finding.** A human must verify against fda.gov directly.

## Limitations

Limitations of the evidence, and limitations of this Guide.

**Of the evidence base**

- There is no completed human trial of TB-500 for any purpose.
- The single registered human study is ongoing, and its primary measurements are adverse event
  counts rather than benefits.
- The human trials that do exist studied a chemically different, larger molecule, mostly applied
  to an eye or wound surface, in samples ranging from nine to a few dozen patients. Several were
  terminated early. One frequently cited paper reports no outcome data at all.
- Sample sizes across all thymosin beta-4 trials are small enough that uncommon harms could not
  have been detected regardless of what was measured.
- Human pharmacokinetics of TB-500 are unknown. Only rat in vivo and human-serum in vitro
  metabolism data were retrieved.
- The preclinical claims summarised by the 2026 review rest on that review rather than on
  primary studies read directly this session.

**Of this Guide**

- FDA.gov was inaccessible to our retrieval tools. Five separate fda.gov pages returned errors.
  Consequently no FDA claim of any kind appears here as verified: not approval status, not

  document. Prohibited status is verified here only indirectly, through USADA and through
  peer-reviewed doping-control literature.
- A specific FDA warning letter concerning thymosin beta-4 appeared in search results and would
  be directly relevant regulatory evidence, but the page could not be retrieved, so its content

## Quality and identity

Even setting aside the question of whether TB-500 does anything, there is a separate and more
immediate question: what is actually in a vial sold under that name? No source we retrieved
answers that. The following is what is documented.

**The naming problem is itself an identity problem.** Because vendors and secondary sources use
"TB-500" and "thymosin beta-4" interchangeably, a purchaser cannot tell from a product name
alone whether a vial is claimed to contain the 7-amino-acid fragment or the 43-amino-acid
full-length protein. These are chemically different molecules with different evidence bases. The
2012 analytical paper (PMID 23084823) notes that numerous products available online claim to
contain either a synthetic acetylated fragment of thymosin beta-4 or the protein itself. No
retrieved source establishes what unregulated vials actually contain.

**Synthesis impurities are documented in TB-500-containing products.** The same 2012 study
reported that a non-natural synthesis impurity was detectable in equine plasma after
administration of a TB-500-containing product. That confirms marketed material carries
manufacturing-derived contaminants. That study did not characterise purity across the consumer
market, so no purity figure can be attached to products generally.

**Unregulated peptide products are a documented counterfeiting problem in general.** A 2015
analysis (Vanhee et al., Talanta; PMID 26003685) developed screening methods for illegal peptide
preparations covering 25 different peptides already seized across Europe or identified in
underground forums, noting that counterfeit and illegal protein and peptide substances could
imply severe health threats. **Important limit:** the record available to us does not state
whether TB-500 or thymosin beta-4 was among those 25 peptides, and does not report what fraction
of samples were mislabelled. No specific mislabelling percentage may be attached to TB-500 on
the basis of that paper, and none is stated here.

**A widely repeated figure is not used in this Guide.** A claim that roughly 30 percent of
unregulated peptide samples are mislabelled, misdosed or contaminated appears frequently in
commercial and secondary material. The underlying analysis was not located, and the figure is
not attached to any primary source in our record. It therefore does not appear here as a
finding. If you encounter that number, treat it as unsourced until someone shows you the study.

The practical summary: an unregulated vial labelled TB-500 comes with no verified identity, no
verified purity, no verified sterility, and no way for a buyer to confirm any of it. See
`QUALITY_AND_DOCUMENTATION.md` in this packet for the full checklist of what a supplier would
have to satisfy, and the current unmet state of every item.

## Safety categories

This section describes **categories of concern**. It is not a list of known side effects,
because no such list exists for this compound in humans. Nothing here should be read as
reassurance, and the absence of a reported harm is not evidence of its absence.

**1. Human safety is unknown, not merely unproven.** No human safety data for TB-500 exist in
the retrieved literature. The full registry query returned exactly one TB-500 study, recruiting,
with no posted results, and its primary endpoints are adverse-event incidence. That a
first-in-class safety study is only now running is itself direct evidence that no established
human safety profile exists.

**2. Safety findings from the parent protein do not transfer.** The tolerability reported in the
neurotrophic keratopathy trial (16 adverse events across both arms, one treatment-related)
concerns a different molecule, given topically to the eye surface, in 18 subjects. Extrapolating
that to a synthetic fragment taken into the body as a whole is unsupported. The small samples
across all thymosin beta-4 trials also mean rare harms could not have been detected.

**3. Anti-doping sanction is a concrete, documented risk.** TB-500 is named explicitly on the
WADA Prohibited List as a prohibited growth factor, and validated detection methods exist for
TB-500 and its metabolites in both equine and mammalian samples, with one metabolite detectable
up to 72 hours in rats. For anyone competing under anti-doping jurisdiction this is a documented
sanction risk, not a theoretical one.

**4. Product quality and contamination.** Because the unregulated supply chain has no enforced
identity, purity, or sterility verification, risks associated with unknown contents, synthesis
impurities, and non-sterile preparation apply independently of anything the intended compound
might do.

**5. Open question, not a finding: long-term risk from the proposed pro-angiogenic activity.**
A mechanism involving the formation of new blood vessels raises an obvious question about
whether sustained signalling of that kind carries any long-term risk, including in the context
of existing tumours. **No retrieved source this session evaluated that question for TB-500
either way.** We record it as an open question, not as a finding.

## Interactions

No interaction studies for TB-500 were located. Nothing is known about how it behaves alongside
medications, supplements, or medical conditions.

The following are **categories that require review by a qualified clinician**, not a list of
known interactions. They are the categories a clinician would reasonably want to consider for a
compound with a proposed effect on blood vessel formation and tissue remodelling, and with no
human data at all.

- Any current or past cancer diagnosis, or ongoing cancer surveillance.
- Any medication or condition affecting blood clotting or bleeding.
- Any medication affecting the immune system, including transplant medication.
- Any active or recent surgical or wound-healing situation under medical management.
- Pregnancy, attempts to conceive, or breastfeeding.
- Any eye disease under specialist care, given that the parent protein's trial history is
  ophthalmic.
- Cardiovascular disease under active treatment.
- Any other prescribed medication, because no interaction data exist to rule anything out.

The honest statement is that a clinician reviewing this compound would be working without data,
and should be told that explicitly.

## Regulatory status

All statements dated **2026-07-19**. Regulatory status changes. Re-verify before relying on any

### Anti-doping (WADA, via USADA) - VERIFIED

USADA's published summary of changes to the 2018 WADA Prohibited List states that thymosin-B4
and its derivatives, giving TB-500 as the example, were added as examples of prohibited growth
factors under section S2.3. Section S2 covers peptide hormones, growth factors, related
substances and mimetics, and substances in S2 are prohibited at all times, both in and out of
competition.

- Jurisdiction: international anti-doping (WADA list, as reported by the US national anti-doping
  organisation)
- Date checked: 2026-07-19
- Source: https://www.usada.org/athlete-advisory/2018-prohibited-list-summary-of-major-changes/
  (VERIFIED 2026-07-19)
- **Caveat:** this verifies the 2018 list change. WADA's own current prohibited-list pages
  returned empty content to our retrieval tools this session, so the exact 2026 subsection
  numbering and current wording were not independently verified. The substance of the statement,
  that TB-500 is explicitly named and prohibited at all times, is supported.

### Anti-doping, secondary confirmation - VERIFIED

The 2012 equine doping-control paper confirms TB-500 is subject to doping control and describes
detection method development in horse urine and plasma. The 2026 sports medicine review states
that thymosin beta-4 and TB-500 remain banned substances in sport.

- Date checked: 2026-07-19
- Sources: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23084823/ and
  https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41476424/ (both VERIFIED 2026-07-19)

### United States, marketing approval - VERIFIED as a negative finding

## Unknowns

The honest list of what nobody currently knows.

1. Whether TB-500 has any efficacy for any indication in humans. No completed human efficacy
   trial of the fragment exists in our record.
2. Whether TB-500 is safe in humans, at any exposure, over any duration. The first registered
   human safety study is ongoing with no results.
3. Whether the parent TB-500 molecule or only its metabolites carry the proposed biological
   activity. Raised directly by the 2024 study and unresolved.
4. Human pharmacokinetics of TB-500. Only rat in vivo and human-serum in vitro data were
   retrieved.
5. Whether findings from full-length thymosin beta-4, applied to an eye or wound surface, have
   any bearing at all on a synthetic fragment taken into the body as a whole. No retrieved
   source attempts to bridge this.
6. What unregulated vials sold as TB-500 actually contain, in what purity, with what
   contaminants. No market-wide analysis was retrieved.
7. Whether any oncologic or other long-term risk attaches to sustained pro-angiogenic
   signalling from this compound. Not assessed in any retrieved source.
8. Current FDA 503A/503B compounding status, and the outcome of any July 2026 Pharmacy
   Compounding Advisory Committee consideration.
9. The exact current WADA 2026 Prohibited List subsection and wording for thymosin beta-4
   derivatives.

## Questions for a qualified professional

If you are discussing this compound with a clinician, these are questions that produce useful
answers. Bring this Guide with you if it helps.

1. Given that the only registered human trial of TB-500 is a safety study that started in 2026
   and has posted no results, what would you consider a reasonable basis for using it now?
2. The human trials most often cited studied the full-length thymosin beta-4 protein, mostly as
   an eye drop. Do you consider those relevant to a synthetic fragment taken into the body as a
   whole, and why or why not?
3. One 2024 laboratory study suggests the breakdown product, rather than TB-500 itself, may
   carry the wound-healing activity seen in cultured cells. Does that change how you think about
   this compound?
4. Given my personal and family history, particularly regarding cancer, is a compound proposed
   to promote new blood vessel formation something you would have concerns about?
5. Do any of my current medications or conditions fall into a category where an unstudied
   peptide would worry you?
6. I compete under anti-doping rules. Can you confirm what this substance's prohibited status
   means for my eligibility and testing?
7. What is actually causing the problem I am trying to solve, and what approaches have evidence
   behind them that this one does not?
8. If I told you I had already used something like this, what would you want to monitor?
9. What would you need to see published before you would consider this differently?
