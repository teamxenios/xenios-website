# TB-500: member questions

Direct answers to the questions members actually ask. Same rules as the Guide: we tell you what
is known, what is proposed, and what nobody knows.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

This page is educational. It is not medical advice and not a recommendation to use anything.

---

## 1. Does TB-500 work?

Nobody knows, and that is the accurate answer rather than a hedge.

There is no completed human trial of TB-500 for any purpose. A full search of the public
clinical trial registry on 2026-07-19 found exactly one registered human study of the TB-500
fragment (NCT07487363). It started in February 2026, it is still recruiting, and it has posted
no results. Its main measurements are counts of adverse events, meaning it is asking a safety
question first, which is what you do with a compound whose effects in people are unestablished.

A 2026 review in a sports medicine journal states plainly that human orthopaedic data are
lacking (PMID 41476424).

So the position is not that TB-500 has been tested and disappointed. It is that it has barely
been tested in people at all.

**Claim grade for any human efficacy claim: G (unverified).**

---

## 2. But I have seen studies. Are those fake?

They are real studies. They are about a different molecule.

TB-500 is a synthetic seven-amino-acid fragment. Thymosin beta-4 is the full-length natural
protein of 43 amino acids. These are chemically different things. Nearly every human trial
people cite for TB-500 studied the full-length protein, or a pharmaceutical product made from
it such as RGN-259, and most of those delivered it as an eye drop or applied it directly to a
wound surface.

Specifically:

- The severe dry eye study (PMID 25826322) enrolled nine patients and used an eye solution of
  the full-length protein.
- The neurotrophic keratopathy study (PMID 36613994) enrolled 18 subjects, used an eye solution
  of the full-length protein, was terminated early for slow recruitment, and did not reach
  statistical significance at its main timepoint (it did at a later one).
- The venous ulcer study (PMID 17495250) is frequently cited as showing faster healing. The
  record we retrieved reports no outcome data at all. It describes a study that was still in
  progress.

Presenting any of those as TB-500 evidence is a category error. It is also the single most
common error in commercial material on this compound.

## 3. Is it safe?

Unknown. Not "probably fine", not "generally well tolerated". Unknown.

There is no human safety data for TB-500 in the published record we searched. The first
registered human safety study is running now with no results posted. When the first-in-class
safety study is only beginning, that is direct evidence that no established human safety profile
exists.

You may see the tolerability numbers from the neurotrophic keratopathy trial quoted as
reassurance. That trial had 18 subjects, used a different molecule, and applied it to the
surface of the eye. It cannot tell you what a synthetic fragment does when taken into the body
as a whole. Samples that small also cannot detect uncommon harms, so a clean safety report from
any of these trials means much less than it appears to.

There is one open question we want to name rather than bury. The proposed mechanism involves
promoting the formation of new blood vessels. That raises an obvious question about long-term
risk, including in the context of existing tumours. **No source we retrieved evaluated that
question for TB-500 either way.** We are recording it as an open question, not as a finding, and
it is a reasonable thing to raise with a clinician.

## 4. Is it legal?

The parts we could verify, and the parts we could not.

**Sport: prohibited, and this is well documented.** TB-500 is named explicitly on the WADA
Prohibited List as a prohibited growth factor. USADA's summary of the 2018 Prohibited List
changes states that thymosin-B4 and its derivatives, giving TB-500 as the example, were added
under section S2.3. Substances in that section are prohibited at all times, in and out of
competition. Validated laboratory detection methods for TB-500 and its breakdown products exist,
and one metabolite was detectable up to 72 hours in rats. If you compete under anti-doping
rules, the sanction risk here is concrete, not theoretical.
(Source: https://www.usada.org/athlete-advisory/2018-prohibited-list-summary-of-major-changes/,
verified 2026-07-19.)

**Medicines approval: no approved product was found.** We located no approved TB-500 product.
The only registered human study is an ongoing early-phase safety study, which is inconsistent
with any approved status.

**US pharmacy compounding status: we could not verify it, so we do not state it.** Conflicting
claims circulate about whether this compound sits in a restricted category, whether it was
removed from one in 2026, and what a July 2026 advisory committee may consider. Every fda.gov
page our tools requested returned an error, and the one government notice we did retrieve names
no substances. We would rather leave a gap than fill it with a guess. A human reviewer must
check fda.gov directly before we say anything about it.

**Outside the United States:** not assessed. Do not infer your local status from this page.

All of the above is dated 2026-07-19 and can change.

## 6. Why is the evidence so thin? Is that suspicious in itself?

It is worth understanding why, because the reasons are ordinary rather than conspiratorial.

Running a human trial is expensive, slow, and requires a sponsor with a commercial or academic
reason to fund it. A short synthetic peptide fragment based on a known natural sequence is hard
to protect commercially, which reduces the incentive for anyone to spend the money proving it
works. Meanwhile the compound is already available on an unregulated market where it sells
perfectly well without any evidence at all. There is no commercial pressure to generate data
when demand exists without it.

Separately, research attention has gone to the full-length parent protein, which is where the
pharmaceutical programmes and the trials actually are.

What that means for you is straightforward. Thin evidence here does not mean the compound is
secretly effective and being suppressed. It means the question has not been asked properly yet.
An honest empty evidence table is a real result, and it is the one this compound currently
produces.

## 7. Does it at least work in animals?

There is preclinical work, and it is more complicated than the marketing suggests. Every finding
below carries its model, because an animal or dish result is not a small human result.

A 2026 review reports that thymosin beta-4 and TB-500 promoted angiogenesis and tissue repair in
preclinical models (PMID 41476424). That is a summary inside a review article rather than a
primary experiment, the species were not specified in the record available to us, and the
underlying primary studies were not retrieved directly.

More interestingly, a 2024 study (PMID 38382158) measured TB-500 and its breakdown products in
rats and in laboratory systems, then tested them in cultured human skin cells. In those cultured
cells, a breakdown product called Ac-LKKTE showed significant wound-healing activity while the
parent TB-500 compound did not. The authors concluded that previously reported wound-healing
activity of TB-500 may be due to its metabolite rather than the parent form.

That is one in vitro result inside an analytical chemistry paper and should not be treated as
settled. But it means the basic question of which molecule is even doing the work is open. The
preclinical story is not a clean foundation waiting for human confirmation.

**Claim grade for all preclinical findings: D (preclinical).**

## 8. How do I know what is actually in a vial sold as TB-500?

You do not, and that is a documented problem rather than a suspicion.

Two things are established in the sources we retrieved. First, a 2012 analytical study found
that a non-natural manufacturing impurity was detectable in blood after administration of a
TB-500-containing product, which confirms that marketed material carries synthesis-derived
contaminants (PMID 23084823). Second, the same paper notes that products sold online claim to
contain either the synthetic fragment or the full-length protein, which means a product name
alone does not tell you which molecule is claimed to be inside.

Beyond that, a 2015 analysis developed screening methods for illegal peptide preparations
covering 25 different peptides seized in Europe or found in underground forums, noting that
counterfeit and illegal peptide substances could imply severe health threats (PMID 26003685).
Important limit: the record available to us does not say whether TB-500 was among those 25, and
does not give a mislabelling rate. So we cannot attach a percentage to TB-500 and we do not.

You may see a figure claiming roughly 30 percent of unregulated peptide samples are mislabelled
or contaminated. We could not locate the underlying analysis, so we do not use that number. Treat
it as unsourced until someone shows you the study.

## 9. If I have already used it, what should I do?

Talk to a clinician, and tell them plainly what you used and over what period. That
conversation is more useful than anything a web page can offer, and clinicians see this more
often than people expect.

It helps to bring the actual product if you still have it, along with any documentation that
came with it, because identity is genuinely uncertain in this market.

Be aware that a clinician evaluating this is working without human data. Saying so directly, and
showing them this Guide, tends to produce a better conversation than presenting the compound as
something with an established profile.

We do not offer monitoring protocols, follow-up schedules, or anything a reader could follow as
instructions. That belongs with a professional who knows your history.

## 10. What would change your mind?

A fair question, and we should answer it specifically rather than gesture at "more research".

We would revise this Guide's evidence verdict if any of the following were published and
verified:

- **Posted results from NCT07487363.** The registered TB-500 safety study reporting adverse
  event data would give this compound its first human safety information of any kind. That alone
  would move safety statements off grade G.
- **A completed, adequately powered, randomized controlled trial of the TB-500 fragment** for a
  defined condition, with a pre-registered primary endpoint and published results. That would be
  the first human efficacy evidence to exist.
- **Resolution of the metabolite question.** A dedicated pharmacology study establishing whether
  the parent TB-500 molecule or its breakdown products carry the proposed activity would either
  restore the standard mechanistic story or replace it.
- **Human pharmacokinetic data.** Currently we have rat in vivo and human-serum laboratory data
  only. Knowing what happens to this compound in a person is a prerequisite for interpreting
  anything else.
- **A published market analysis of what unregulated TB-500 vials actually contain**, with
  identity and purity across a meaningful sample.
- **Retrievable regulatory documentation.** Several statements in this Guide are limited by our
  inability to reach fda.gov and the current WADA list this session. Direct verification would
  either confirm or correct those sections.

What would not change our mind: additional trials of full-length thymosin beta-4, more testimonials,
or vendor-supplied certificates without independent verification. Evidence for the parent protein
is not evidence for the fragment, and that is the central point of this entire Guide.
