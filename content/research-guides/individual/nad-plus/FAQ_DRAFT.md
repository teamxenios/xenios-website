# NAD+ frequently asked questions

These answers are research education. They are not medical advice, and they are not a
recommendation to use anything. Everything here is sourced from the evidence set described in
GUIDE_DRAFT.md, retrieved 2026-07-19.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

### 1. Does it work?

The honest answer depends entirely on which form is being asked about, and for most of them
the answer is either "no, not for the outcomes people want" or "nobody has tested it."

For **oral NMN**, pooled randomized trials found no significant benefit for blood sugar,
insulin, long-term glucose markers, insulin resistance, or blood lipids, and no significant
benefit for muscle size, grip strength, walking speed, or repeated chair rises. Oral NMN does
raise measurable NAD+ in blood, which is real, but the researchers who found it cautioned
that a higher level in blood may not reflect what is happening inside cells.

For **oral NR**, results pointed in opposite directions across small subgroups. It was
associated with a longer six-minute walk distance in people with peripheral artery disease,
and with worse scores on two physical function tests in people with mild cognitive
impairment. Across 10 small trials totalling 240 participants, that reads as noise.

For **intravenous NAD+**, there is no retrieved randomized evidence that it benefits anyone
for anything. The one pharmacokinetic study measured where the molecule went in 11 healthy
men and recorded no clinical outcome at all.

For **injectable and intranasal NAD+**, no human study was found. Nothing can be said.

---

### 2. Is it safe?

Xenios does not describe any compound as safe, and this one has real signals worth naming.

In the one retrieved study of intravenous NAD+ tolerability, **all six** people who received
it reported moderate to severe abdominal cramping, diarrhea, nausea, vomiting, increased
heart rate, throat pain, congestion, and chest pressure. Those symptoms resolved when the
infusion stopped. Six people is far too few to calculate a rate, and it is also too few to
call the symptoms rare.

There is a documented contamination event. A compounded NAD+ injection lot was recalled in
July 2025 for elevated bacterial endotoxin and classified by the FDA in October 2025 as Class
I, the most serious recall category, reserved for products that may cause serious harm or
death.

On the oral side, the systematic review of oral NAD+ and NADH trials reported muscle pain,
nervous system effects, fatigue, sleep disturbance, and headaches as common side effects,
which those authors characterized as not presenting serious health risks. That is a finding
from short trials in a few hundred people, not a general safety statement.

Every retrieved human study was short. No long-term safety data exists for any form, and no
safety data of any kind was found for injectable or intranasal products. No search was run on
drug interactions, contraindications, or use in pregnancy, so silence on those topics here
means the question was not researched, not that the answer is reassuring.

### 3. Is it legal?

That question has different answers for different forms, and it changed recently. All
statements below are dated 2026-07-19 and apply to the United States.

**Oral NMN as a dietary supplement.** On 29 September 2025 the FDA determined that NMN is not
excluded from the definition of a dietary supplement, changing its November 2022 position.
NMN remains a New Dietary Ingredient, so a company must still submit a New Dietary Ingredient
Notification before marketing it. This is about whether the product may lawfully be sold. It
is not an FDA approval, not a safety finding, and not an efficacy finding.

**Intravenous and injectable NAD+.** No FDA-approved NAD+ injection or infusion product for
any indication was identified. These products reach people through pharmacy compounding.
NAD+ is reported to appear on the FDA's list of bulk substances that raise significant safety
risks for compounding, and reported as not eligible for use by registered outsourcing
facilities. Two FDA warning letters in 2026 cited NAD+ injection products.

An honesty note. The FDA website could not be read directly during this research, so every
FDA-attributed statement above other than the NMN determination rests on search snippets or
secondary legal analysis, and needs human confirmation before it is treated as settled.

**Athletes.** Whether NAD+, NMN, or NR appear by name on the WADA 2026 Prohibited List could
not be determined, because the official List returned empty content. Section S0 of that List
prohibits substances with no current approval by any governmental regulatory health authority
for human therapeutic use, which means an unapproved injectable form could plausibly fall
under it regardless of naming. Any athlete should check with their own governing body rather
than relying on this Guide.

### 5. Why is the evidence so thin, when this is sold everywhere?

Three reasons, and they compound.

First, the evidence that does exist is mostly in the **wrong form** for the way the product is
sold. Nearly all randomized human trials are of oral precursors. The clinics selling infusions
are selling something that literature does not cover.

Second, the trials that exist are **small and short**. The largest pooled population in any
retrieved review was 489 participants across 10 different conditions. The intravenous
pharmacokinetic pilot had 11 people. The intravenous tolerability review had 14. Studies at
that size cannot detect uncommon serious events and cannot settle questions of effectiveness.

Third, commercial availability does not require evidence. A dietary supplement can be sold
without proving it works. A compounded infusion can be offered by a clinic without an
approval pathway behind it. One of the retrieved papers itself notes the gap between how
widely available intravenous NAD+ is and how little human data exists on it as a treatment.

Popularity, in this category, is not a proxy for evidence.

### 6. If oral NMN raises NAD+ in my blood, is that not the point?

That is the most important question in this Guide, and the answer is no, not on its own.

Raising a level in blood is a biochemical result. It is not the same as raising NAD+ inside
the tissues anyone actually cares about, and neither one is the same as feeling or functioning
better. The authors of the 2024 meta-analysis said this directly: it is not clear whether the
rise in blood translated to activity at the cellular level, which is likely to differ by
tissue.

What the same body of evidence shows is that the blood marker moved while glucose, insulin,
HbA1c, insulin resistance, lipids, muscle mass, grip strength, gait speed, and chair stand
performance did not. Those two facts belong together in the same sentence. Any content that
shows a member the rising biomarker and stops there has told half the story.

---

### 7. Is intravenous NAD+ better than taking a capsule, since it goes straight in?

That is a reasonable intuition and it is not supported by the retrieved evidence.

There is no retrieved head-to-head randomized comparison showing an intravenous form performs
better than an oral one on any outcome. There is no retrieved randomized evidence that the
intravenous form produces any clinical benefit at all. What was found for the intravenous
route is a pharmacokinetic pilot in 11 healthy men that measured no outcome, and a
retrospective review in which all six intravenous NAD+ recipients had moderate to severe
symptoms during the infusion.

There is also an odd finding worth knowing. In that pilot, plasma NAD+ and its metabolites did
not change until after the first two hours of infusion, which the study authors themselves
called surprising and noted was not what direct delivery would predict.

One randomized preprint comparing intravenous products in healthy adults exists but could not
be retrieved for this Guide, and is a preprint, meaning it has not completed peer review. It
is the single highest-priority outstanding source for a human reviewer.

### 8. Are these products actually what the label says?

Frequently not, on the oral side, and this is one of the strongest findings in the whole
Guide.

Peer-reviewed laboratory testing assayed 18 NMN supplements bought online, bought in
pharmacies, or obtained from manufacturers. Deviation from the adjusted label claim ranged
from minus 100 percent to plus 11.2 percent. Three products contained no detectable NMN. Only
one exceeded its label claim, and 14 contained less than claimed. The authors concluded that
label amounts correspond poorly to detected amounts.

On the injectable side, the problem is not just accuracy but contamination and starting
material. The FDA has communicated that some compounders used food-grade NAD+ from
repackagers to make intravenous products, and food-grade material is not qualified for
sterile injectable manufacture. That FDA page could not be read directly for this Guide and
requires human confirmation.

More dramatic figures circulate in supplement industry marketing. Those appeared on pages
selling competing products and are treated here as market claims, not evidence.

### 9. What is the mechanism, in plain terms, and how confident should I be in it?

The mechanism is a hypothesis, and confidence should be low as far as human clinical effects
are concerned.

NAD+ is a helper molecule that many enzymes in every human cell need in order to work,
including in energy production and DNA repair. NAD+ levels in tissue are widely reported to
fall with age. The hypothesis is that supplying precursors, or NAD+ itself, would restore
those NAD+-dependent processes.

Two cautions. The aging research this reasoning grew out of comes largely from yeast, worm,
and rodent work. No animal or cell study was retrieved for this Guide, so no specific
preclinical finding is cited in it, and a finding in a rodent model is not a finding in a
person. And the human trials that tested the downstream consequences of the hypothesis
largely came back null.

### 10. What would change your mind?

This is a fair question to ask of anyone writing about evidence, so here is a direct answer.

Xenios would revise this Guide in a more favourable direction on seeing:

- Adequately powered randomized, placebo-controlled trials in humans reporting clinical
  outcomes rather than biomarkers, replicated by more than one independent group.
- For the intravenous route specifically, any randomized controlled trial measuring a clinical
  endpoint. Right now that category is empty. The unretrieved randomized preprint may be a
  first step, and it needs retrieval plus conflict of interest scrutiny.
- Direct evidence that raising NAD+ in blood raises NAD+ inside relevant human tissues, which
  is the missing link in the whole mechanism argument.
- Longer trials, since everything retrieved ran weeks to a few months.
- Tolerability data on intravenous NAD+ in a larger, controlled cohort that either confirms or
  contradicts the universal symptom pattern seen in the six-person review.
- Any human data at all on products intended for injection under the skin or into muscle, or
  intranasal products, where the current evidence base is empty.

Xenios would revise this Guide in a more cautious direction on seeing further contamination
events, additional enforcement actions, or a replicated unfavourable functional signal in any
population.
