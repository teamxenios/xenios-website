# NAD+ and its precursors (NR and NMN)

This Guide is member-facing research education. It is not medical advice, it is not a
recommendation to use anything, and it is not a product page. Xenios writes these Guides
so that a member can see the evidence as it actually is, including the parts that are
thin, contradictory, or missing.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## One-minute summary

"NAD+" is sold as if it were one thing. It is not. There are at least three different
products in the market with three different evidence bases and three different regulatory
positions, and evidence for one does not carry over to another.

1. **Oral precursors** (nicotinamide riboside, or NR, and nicotinamide mononucleotide, or
   NMN, both taken by mouth). This is where nearly all of the randomized human trial
   evidence sits.
2. **Intravenous NAD+**, the intact molecule given by infusion at wellness clinics. Very
   little human evidence exists, and none of it tested whether it helps anyone.
3. **Injectable NAD+** (products intended for injection under the skin or into muscle) and
   **intranasal sprays**. No human study of these was retrieved for this Guide at all.

The honest evidence verdict, in one paragraph. For oral NMN, the biochemistry does appear
to move: three systematic reviews retrieved for this Guide found that oral NMN raises
measurable NAD+ levels in blood. The outcomes people actually buy it for did not move.
Pooled randomized trials found no significant benefit for blood sugar, insulin, long-term
glucose markers, insulin resistance, or blood lipids, and no significant benefit for muscle
size, grip strength, walking speed, or the ability to rise repeatedly from a chair. One
review reported that oral NR was associated with *worse* physical performance on two
measures in people with mild cognitive impairment, which is a signal that belongs in the
same summary as the favourable ones. For intravenous NAD+, there is no retrieved randomized
evidence that it benefits anyone for anything, and the one retrieved tolerability review
found that all six people who received it experienced moderate to severe symptoms during
the infusion. For injectable and intranasal NAD+, there is nothing to report because
nothing was found.

Separately, the market itself is a problem. Peer-reviewed laboratory testing of 18 oral NMN
products found that label claims corresponded poorly to what was actually in the bottle,
and three of the products contained no detectable NMN. On the injectable side, a compounded
NAD+ injection lot was subject to the most serious class of United States recall for
contamination with bacterial endotoxin.

What a member should take away: the interest in this compound family is understandable, the
mechanism is a real hypothesis rather than an established human fact, the oral evidence is
mostly null for meaningful outcomes, the intravenous and injectable evidence is close to
absent while the safety and quality signals are real, and Xenios has confirmed nothing about
the quality of any product containing this compound.

## What it is

NAD+ stands for nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide. It is a naturally occurring molecule
present in every human cell. It is described in the retrieved systematic review as a
coenzyme (a helper molecule that other proteins require in order to do their work) involved
in energy production, DNA repair, cell signaling, and the control of which genes are
switched on. (Source: systematic review, American Journal of Physiology, DOI
10.1152/ajpendo.00242.2023, retrieved 2026-07-19.)

The products sold to consumers under the NAD+ heading fall into distinct categories:

- **Oral precursors.** NR and NMN are members of the vitamin B3 family that the body can
  convert toward NAD+. In the United States these are regulated as dietary supplements.
  Related precursors that appear in the retrieved literature include nicotinamide (NAM),
  niacin (nicotinic acid), and NADH (the reduced form of the same molecule).
- **Intravenous NAD+.** The intact NAD+ molecule given by infusion, typically at a drip
  clinic. In the United States this is a compounded drug preparation, not a supplement, and
  no approved product for any indication was identified for this Guide.
- **Injectable and intranasal NAD+.** Distributed through compounding pharmacies, peptide
  vendors, and research chemical channels.

**Commonly confused with.** Niacin (nicotinic acid) is a different B3 vitamin with its own
flushing profile and its own separate research literature. NADH supplements are the reduced
form of the molecule and appear in some of the reviewed trials. "NAD therapy" marketed for
addiction or detoxification is a clinic marketing category, not an approved indication.

**House rule applied throughout this Guide.** Data from oral precursor trials is never
presented as evidence about intravenous or injectable NAD+. The oral NR and NMN literature
says nothing about what an infusion of intact NAD+ does in a human body.

## Why interest exists

The interest is easy to understand, and describing it is not the same as endorsing it.

The starting observation is that NAD+ levels in tissue are widely reported to decline with
age. From that observation follows an intuitive hypothesis: if the decline matters, then
supplying the raw material might slow or offset some of what we call aging. That hypothesis
connects to several processes that people care about, including energy production in
mitochondria, DNA repair, and the activity of a family of proteins called sirtuins that has
been studied extensively in aging research.

The commercial category was built on that hypothesis. It is amplified by three things: the
molecule is genuinely fundamental to human biology, so the story sounds credible; oral NMN
does measurably raise NAD+ in blood, so there is a real number that moves; and intravenous
clinics can offer an experience that feels like a medical intervention.

None of that is evidence of benefit. A plausible mechanism plus a moving biomarker is where
a hypothesis starts, not where it is confirmed. The rest of this Guide is about what
happened when that hypothesis was actually tested in people.

## Terminology

- **NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide).** A coenzyme, meaning a helper molecule that
  enzymes need in order to work, present in all human cells.
- **NADH.** The reduced form of NAD+. It appears as a supplement in some of the trials
  reviewed here.
- **Precursor.** A starting material the body converts into something else. NR and NMN are
  precursors on the path toward NAD+.
- **NR (nicotinamide riboside)** and **NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide).** Two B3-family
  precursors sold as oral supplements.
- **Biomarker.** Something measurable in the body, such as the level of NAD+ in blood. A
  biomarker moving is not the same as a person feeling or functioning better.
- **Randomized controlled trial (RCT).** A study where participants are assigned by chance
  to receive the intervention or a comparison, which is the design best able to separate a
  real effect from wishful thinking.
- **Systematic review and meta-analysis.** A structured search for all relevant trials, with
  the meta-analysis combining their numbers statistically.
- **Pharmacokinetic study.** A study of where a substance goes in the body and how it is
  processed. It does not measure whether anyone gets better.
- **Retrospective review.** A look back through existing records rather than a planned
  experiment. It cannot establish cause and effect.
- **Placebo-controlled and blinded.** Participants receive either the real thing or an
  inactive comparison, without knowing which.
- **Endotoxin.** A component of certain bacteria that can trigger severe inflammatory
  responses if it enters the bloodstream, even when no living bacteria are present.
- **Compounding.** Preparation of a drug product by a pharmacy or outsourcing facility
  rather than manufacture of an approved product by a drug maker.
- **503A and 503B.** Sections of United States law governing pharmacy compounding and
  registered outsourcing facilities respectively.
- **New Dietary Ingredient (NDI).** A United States regulatory category requiring a company
  to notify the FDA before marketing an ingredient that was not in the food supply before a
  statutory cutoff.
- **Sirtuins and PARPs.** Two families of proteins that use NAD+ and that feature in the
  proposed mechanism described below.
- **SPPB, grip strength, gait speed, chair stand test.** Standard physical function
  measures used in older-adult research. SPPB stands for Short Physical Performance Battery.
- **HbA1c and HOMA-IR.** A long-term blood sugar marker, and a calculated estimate of
  insulin resistance.

## Proposed mechanism

Everything in this section is **proposed** and largely unproven as a clinical mechanism in
humans. It is presented because it is the reasoning the category rests on, not because it
has been demonstrated to produce benefit.

The stated rationale is that NAD+ concentrations in tissue decline with age, and that
supplying precursors (NR, NMN) or NAD+ itself would restore NAD+-dependent processes such as
sirtuin activity, PARP-mediated DNA repair, and mitochondrial function. That is the
hypothesis. Grade for the hypothesis as a *clinical* mechanism in humans: **D to G**.

What was actually verified for this Guide:

- Oral NMN does raise measurable NAD+ levels in blood. A 2024 meta-analysis found that 5 of
  8 randomized trials reported increased blood NAD+ after oral NMN. The same authors
  cautioned explicitly that, in their words, "it is not clear whether this translated to
  cellular level activity of NAD+, which is likely to be tissue-specific." So the number in
  the blood moves, and what happens inside the tissues that matter is not demonstrated.
  (PMID 39531138.) Grade for "oral NMN raises blood NAD+": **B**.
- Intravenous NAD+ does raise NAD+ and related substances in plasma, but with a timing
  pattern the researchers themselves called surprising. In a 2019 pilot study, plasma NAD+
  and its metabolites showed no change until after the first two hours of the infusion,
  which the authors noted is not what simple direct delivery to cells would predict.
  (PMID 31572171.) Grade for "intravenous NAD+ raises plasma NAD+": **C**.

The gap that matters. Raising a level in blood is not the same as raising NAD+ inside the
tissues of interest, and neither is the same as producing a clinical benefit. Across the
retrieved reviews, the biomarker moves while the clinical outcomes do not.

**Species note.** The sirtuin and NAD-decline aging literature originates largely in yeast,
worm, and rodent research. No primary preclinical study was retrieved for this Guide, so no
animal or cell finding is cited here. See the Preclinical evidence section.

[UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check] It is widely discussed in
this field that intact NAD+ may not cross intact cell membranes efficiently and may first
need to be broken down outside the cell before its components are taken up. If that is
correct it would undercut the specific rationale for intravenous NAD+. No source
establishing this in humans was retrieved for this Guide, and this line must not be
published as fact without one.

## Human evidence

Human evidence exists in a meaningful quantity for **oral precursors only**. For the
intravenous route it is minimal and does not address benefit. For injectable and intranasal
routes, no human evidence was retrieved at all.

### Oral precursors

**1. Oral NMN and metabolic markers.** Systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized
controlled trials. 8 RCTs, 342 participants (49 percent female, mostly non-diabetic,
predominantly middle-aged, with 3 studies in older subjects and 1 in prediabetic women).
The pooled result found, in the authors' words, "no significant benefit of NMN on fasting
glucose, fasting insulin, glycated hemoglobin, homeostatic model assessment for insulin
resistance and lipid profile." A marginally significant reduction in the insulin resistance
estimate became non-significant once one trial was excluded. Five of eight trials reported
increased blood NAD+.
*Limitations stated by the authors:* few and small trials, publication bias could not be
reliably examined, not all trials measured all markers, regimens and durations and
populations were heterogeneous, only one trial recruited prediabetic participants, and the
findings apply mainly to healthy individuals rather than people with impaired glucose
control. All trials were oral, so this says nothing about intravenous or injectable NAD+.
PMID 39531138 / DOI 10.1007/s11892-024-01557-z.
Grade for "oral NMN improves glucose, insulin, or lipid markers": **PROHIBITED** as a
member-facing claim. The pooled evidence is null.

**2. Oral NMN and NR and physical function in older adults.** Systematic review and
meta-analysis. 10 RCTs, 240 participants, adults aged 60 and above (mean ages ranging from
about 61 to 83), including community-dwelling older adults and people with type 2 diabetes,
prediabetes, mild cognitive impairment, obesity, and peripheral artery disease. NMN showed
no significant effect on skeletal muscle index or handgrip strength, and gait speed and the
five-time chair stand test showed no improvement. The narrative part of the review reported
that NMN did not improve knee extension strength, SPPB, or thigh muscle mass. NR results
were mixed and partly unfavourable: NR was associated with a longer six-minute walk distance
in people with peripheral artery disease, and with *lower* SPPB scores and a *slower*
five-time chair stand test in people with mild cognitive impairment.
*Limitations stated by the authors:* few RCTs available for pooling, inconsistent physical
activity monitoring, most studies did not measure or control diet, variable regimens and
durations, and heterogeneous populations limiting how far the results generalize. Oral route
only. PMID 40275690 / DOI 10.1002/jcsm.13799.
Grade for "oral NMN improves muscle mass or physical function": **PROHIBITED** as a
member-facing claim. Grade for "oral NR improves physical function": **G**, given results
pointing in opposite directions across small subgroups.

**3. Oral NAD+ and NADH supplements across clinical conditions.** Systematic review of
randomized clinical trials (six databases searched, Cochrane risk of bias tool version 2,
two independent reviewers). 10 RCTs, 489 participants, across chronic fatigue syndrome,
aging populations, Parkinson's disease, overweight and obesity, postmenopausal prediabetes,
and Alzheimer's disease. The authors described the supplements as well tolerated and
reported scattered findings including decreased anxiety conditions and maximum heart rate,
increased muscle insulin sensitivity and insulin signaling, and improved quality of life and
sleep among people with chronic fatigue syndrome. Reported side effects included muscle
pain, nervous disorders, fatigue, sleep disturbance, and headaches, which the authors
characterized as not presenting serious health risks.
*Limitations:* the reported benefits are scattered across heterogeneous conditions and small
trials rather than replicated for any one indication, and the review itself states that
further research is needed to clarify clinical benefits for specific diseases. Routes were
oral. PMID 37971292 / DOI 10.1152/ajpendo.00242.2023.
Important boundary: a tolerability conclusion drawn from oral forms is a conclusion about
oral forms. It must not be transferred to intravenous or injectable NAD+, which showed a
very different picture below.
Grade for any single named benefit from this review: **C at best, and G for most**, because
none was replicated across trials.

### Intravenous NAD+

**4. Where the molecule goes during an intravenous infusion.** Pilot study with a randomized
control design (open-label pharmacokinetic and metabolomic pilot). 11 participants total,
8 receiving infusion and 3 as controls, all healthy men aged 30 to 55 with a body mass index
under 30. Plasma NAD+ and its metabolites showed no change until after the first two hours
of infusion, which the authors described as surprising. By the end of the infusion, plasma
NAD+ was substantially elevated above baseline, with comparable rises in nicotinamide,
ADP-ribose, and methyl-nicotinamide, and increased urinary excretion of NAD+ and
methyl-nicotinamide. No adverse events occurred, and minor liver enzyme changes were judged
by the authors to be clinically insignificant.
**Critical framing.** This study measured where the molecule went. It reported no clinical
or symptomatic outcome of any kind.
*Limitations:* very small (11 people), healthy men only, no women, no disease population, no
clinical endpoint, no blinding described, single session. The authors flagged that red cell
NAD+ and certain secondary metabolites were not measured and that purine metabolism was not
evaluated. PMID 31572171 / DOI 10.3389/fnagi.2019.00257.
Grade for "intravenous NAD+ produces any clinical benefit": **G**, because it was not
measured.

**5. Tolerability of intravenous NAD+ compared with intravenous NR.** Retrospective
electronic medical record review in a real-world commercial clinic setting. Not randomized
and not placebo-controlled. 14 people total: 6 received intravenous NAD+ and 8 received
intravenous nicotinamide riboside. Adults over 18, not pregnant, without personal or family
history of cancer. In the intravenous NAD+ group, **all** clients reported moderate to
severe abdominal cramping, diarrhea, nausea, vomiting, increased heart rates, throat pain,
congestion, and chest pressure, which resolved immediately once the infusion ended. The
intravenous NR comparison group experienced only minor tingling in the tongue, jaw, and arm
and minor cramping, plus one event related to menstruation. Intravenous NAD+ required
substantially longer administration time than intravenous NR, consistent with poorer
tolerability. At 30 days there were no clinically significant changes in liver enzymes,
inflammation markers, or kidney function in either group.
*Limitations:* retrospective, very small (6 people in the NAD+ arm), no placebo, no
randomization, commercial entity involvement acknowledged by the authors as a source of
bias, short treatment window, limited follow-up. PMID 41704678 / DOI
10.3389/fragi.2026.1652582.
Grade for "infusion reaction symptoms occur with intravenous NAD+": **C**, and note that in
this small cohort they were universal rather than occasional. The rate cannot be quantified
from 6 people.

### Injectable (under the skin or into muscle) and intranasal NAD+

No human study of these routes was retrieved for this Guide, despite active commercial sale.
The evidence table here is deliberately empty. Grade for every claim about these routes: **G**.

## Contradictions and disagreement

**1. The FDA's own position on NMN as a lawful dietary ingredient changed.**
In November 2022, the FDA announced that NMN was not permitted as a dietary ingredient
because it had been authorized for investigation as a drug. On 29 September 2025, the FDA
issued two letters in response to citizen petitions determining that NMN is *not* excluded
from the dietary supplement definition, applying the "race to market" provision on evidence
of supplement marketing as early as 2017.
*Assessment.* This is a genuine change of position by the same agency, not a misreading of
one document. Any content written between late 2022 and late 2025 states the opposite of the
current position, which is why every regulatory statement in this Guide carries a date. Note
carefully what the change is about: the lawful marketability of oral NMN as a supplement. It
is not an approval, not a safety finding, not an efficacy finding, and it has no bearing on
intravenous or injectable NAD+. Treating "the FDA says oral NMN may be sold as a supplement"
as if it meant "the FDA approves NAD+ therapy" would be the single most likely error a reader
could make about this compound.

**2. Tolerability depends entirely on the form, yet the category is marketed as one thing.**
The systematic review of oral NAD+ and NADH trials (489 participants) concluded the
supplements were well tolerated with a low incidence of side effects. The retrospective
review of the intravenous route found that all 6 recipients experienced moderate to severe
cramping, diarrhea, nausea, vomiting, raised heart rate, throat pain, congestion, and chest
pressure.
*Assessment.* This is not a true contradiction. It is a difference of form and route, and it
is the central analytic point of this Guide. The favourable tolerability finding belongs to
the oral literature only. Note the direction of the error if the two are mixed: the
better-studied form is the milder-appearing one, so borrowing its safety record flatters the
less-studied form.

**3. The biomarker moves and the outcomes do not.**
Oral NMN reliably raises measurable blood NAD+ (5 of 8 trials), which is the mechanism the
category is sold on. The same meta-analysis found no significant benefit on fasting glucose,
fasting insulin, HbA1c, insulin resistance estimate, or lipids, and a second meta-analysis
found no significant effect on muscle index, grip strength, gait speed, or chair stand
performance.
*Assessment.* Both are correct, and together they are the honest story. Oral NMN does what
it says biochemically and has not been shown to do what people buy it for. The meta-analysis
authors themselves cautioned that a raised blood level may not reflect activity inside cells,
which is likely to differ by tissue. These two facts should always be read together, never
the biomarker alone.

**4. The direction of effect for oral NR is not uniformly positive.**
In one review, oral NR was associated with a longer six-minute walk distance in people with
peripheral artery disease, and in the same review with lower SPPB scores and a slower
five-time chair stand test in people with mild cognitive impairment.
*Assessment.* A single review reporting benefit in one small population and worse physical
performance in another, across 10 small trials totalling 240 participants, reads as noise
rather than as population-specific targeting. Neither result is actionable. The unfavourable
direction is named here specifically because consumer content tends to report only the
favourable arm.

**5. What intravenous NAD+ is marketed for, versus what has been tested.**
Intravenous NAD+ is widely marketed by wellness clinics for energy, cognition, addiction
recovery, and anti-aging, and is described in the literature as having a broad range of
anecdotally reported benefits. The only human intravenous studies retrieved were a 2019
pharmacokinetic pilot in 11 healthy men that measured no clinical endpoint at all, and a
2026 retrospective tolerability review of 6 recipients at a commercial clinic.
*Assessment.* This is the largest gap between marketing and evidence in this Guide. One of
the retrieved sources itself notes a paucity of human data examining its use as a treatment
despite wide availability. The accurate framing is that intravenous NAD+ is a commercially
popular intervention whose human evidence base consists of pharmacokinetics in healthy
volunteers and observation of side effects, with effectiveness untested.

## Limitations

Limitations of the evidence base:

- Every retrieved human trial was small. The largest pooled population in any retrieved
  review was 489 participants across 10 heterogeneous conditions.
- All retrieved trials were short, running weeks to a few months. The longest retrieved
  follow-up for the intravenous route was 30 days in 14 people.
- Populations, regimens, and durations varied widely between trials, which limits how
  confidently their results can be pooled.
- The intravenous pilot enrolled healthy men only. There were no women and no disease
  population.
- The intravenous tolerability study was retrospective, uncontrolled, and conducted with
  commercial entity involvement, which the authors acknowledged as a source of bias.
- Publication bias could not be reliably examined in the metabolic meta-analysis.
- Most trials did not measure or control participants' diets, and physical activity
  monitoring was inconsistent.

Limitations of this Guide's research process, stated plainly:

- **The FDA website could not be read directly from the research environment.** Every attempt
  to fetch an fda.gov page returned an error, across at least six different pages. Every
  FDA-attributed statement in this Guide therefore rests on search result snippets or on
  secondary legal and trade analysis, and is marked unverified below. A human must open those
  URLs before this Guide is published.
- **The WADA 2026 Prohibited List could not be searched in full text.** Both the official PDF
  and the list page returned empty content. This Guide therefore cannot state whether NAD+,
  NMN, or NR appear on it by name.
- **A relevant randomized intravenous study could not be retrieved.** A preprint describing a
  randomized, placebo-controlled pilot comparing intravenous products in healthy adults
  (DOI 10.1101/2024.06.06.24308565) returned an access error. It is a preprint, meaning it
  has not completed peer review, and its naming suggests possible industry sponsorship. It is
  the highest-value outstanding retrieval for a human reviewer.
- **A structured clinical trials registry query could not be completed.** This Guide
  therefore contains no count of registered trials, and no registry identifier is cited.
- **No preclinical study was retrieved**, as described above.
- **No dedicated search was run on drug interactions, contraindications, or use in
  pregnancy** for any form. Silence here means the question was not researched, not that the
  answer is reassuring.
- **No source was retrieved on NAD+ nasal sprays specifically**, despite these being a real
  consumer product category.

## Quality and identity

This section is about the market rather than the molecule, and for this compound family the
market findings are among the strongest evidence available.

**Oral NMN products often do not contain what the label says.** Peer-reviewed laboratory
testing assayed 18 NMN supplements (15 non-liposomal and 3 liposomal) purchased online, in
pharmacies, or obtained from manufacturers. Deviation from the adjusted label claim ranged
from minus 100 percent to plus 11.2 percent. **Three products contained no detectable NMN.**
Only one product exceeded its label claim, and the other 14 contained less than claimed. The
authors concluded that the amount of active ingredient indicated on the label corresponds
poorly to the amount detected. (GeroScience, 2024, PMID 38935229, DOI
10.1007/s11357-024-01257-2.) Grade: **B** for the finding that label claims are unreliable in
this category.

**Injectable NAD+ has documented real-world contamination, not just theoretical risk.** A
compounded NAD+ injection lot produced by GenoGenix LLC was voluntarily recalled on 30 July
2025 for elevated endotoxin levels and classified by the FDA as Class I on 21 October 2025.
Class I is the most serious recall category, reserved for products that may cause serious
adverse health consequences or death. Elevated endotoxin in an injected product can trigger
severe inflammatory responses. (Reported by a trade publication; the FDA enforcement record
itself was not retrieved. Requires human confirmation.)

**The identity problem starts upstream of the pharmacy.** The FDA has communicated that it is
aware of compounders using food-grade NAD+ sold by repackagers to make intravenous products,
and that such material is not suitable for compounding sterile drugs without appropriate
processing because of high risk of microbial and endotoxin contamination. (Sourced from a
search snippet; the FDA page could not be read directly. Requires human confirmation.)

**Manufacturing quality failures have been documented at facilities producing this product.**
An FDA warning letter to ProRx LLC dated 7 April 2026, following an inspection conducted 9 to
19 September 2025, cited sterile compounding and manufacturing practice deficiencies,
environmental monitoring failures, inadequate investigations, and lack of sterility
assurance, with NAD+ injection specifically cited for labeling deficiencies. (Search snippet
only. Requires human confirmation.)

**Supplier context matters.** NAD+ injectables are frequently distributed alongside
unapproved substances. The GenoGenix warning letter reportedly cited NAD+ together with other
ineligible injectables. A buyer in that channel is often buying from a vendor whose other
products are outright unapproved, which is itself a signal about the supplier. (Search
snippet only. Requires human confirmation.)

**A note on market claims that are not evidence.** More dramatic quality failure figures
circulate in supplement industry and retailer content, including a claim attributed to a
vendor-commissioned analysis that half of tested NMN products contained none of the
ingredient. Those claims appeared on pages selling competing products and are recorded here
only as market claims, graded **E to G**. The peer-reviewed figure above is the citable one.

Xenios has confirmed none of this for any specific product. See QUALITY_AND_DOCUMENTATION.md.

## Safety categories

This section lists categories of concern. It is not a reassurance, it is not a list of
everything that could happen, and the absence of a category here does not mean it has been
ruled out.

1. **Infusion reactions with intravenous NAD+.** In the one retrieved tolerability study, all
   6 recipients reported moderate to severe abdominal cramping, diarrhea, nausea, vomiting,
   increased heart rate, throat pain, congestion, and chest pressure, resolving when the
   infusion stopped. Chest pressure and a raised heart rate are not symptoms to normalize.
   With 6 people and no control for which product was used, a rate cannot be calculated, and
   the cause cannot be separated from the specific product or infusion conditions.
2. **Contamination of injected products.** Documented, not hypothetical: a Class I recall for
   elevated endotoxin in a compounded NAD+ injection lot. Secondary reporting also describes
   three patients developing low blood pressure, uncontrollable shaking, shivering, and body
   aches in connection with product from the same vial. That patient-harm detail came from a
   secondary summary rather than the FDA record and requires confirmation.
3. **Sterility and manufacturing quality of compounded injectables.** Documented sterility
   assurance and manufacturing practice failures at an outsourcing facility producing NAD+
   injection, and FDA communication about food-grade starting material being used for
   intravenous preparations.
4. **Side effects reported with oral precursors.** The systematic review of oral NAD+ and
   NADH trials reported muscle pain, nervous disorders, fatigue, sleep disturbance, and
   headaches as common side effects, which the authors characterized as not presenting
   serious health risks. Reported as findings from those trials, not as a general statement
   about safety.
5. **A possible unfavourable functional signal with oral NR in one subgroup.** In people with
   mild cognitive impairment, oral NR was associated with lower SPPB scores and a slower
   five-time chair stand test. Small and unreplicated, and named here rather than omitted.
6. **Absence of long-term data for every form.** All retrieved human studies were short. An
   absence of reported harm in studies of 6 to 14 people cannot detect uncommon serious
   events, and short studies say nothing about repeated or chronic exposure.
7. **Complete absence of data for injectable and intranasal routes.** No human safety
   information of any kind was retrieved for products intended for injection under the skin
   or into muscle, or for nasal sprays.
8. **Populations not covered by the retrieved evidence.** The one intravenous study
   explicitly excluded people with a personal or family history of cancer and did not explain
   why. That exclusion is itself worth a reviewer's attention, and it means the retrieved
   safety data does not cover that population. Pregnancy was not researched at all.

## Interactions

**No dedicated search on drug interactions, contraindications, or use in pregnancy was
performed for this Guide, for any form of this compound.** That is a gap in the research, not
a finding of safety. Nothing in this Guide should be read as saying interactions do not
exist.

Categories where a member should seek review by a qualified professional before considering
anything in this category, based on what appears in the retrieved evidence:

- **Blood sugar and diabetes medication.** The retrieved trials measured glucose, insulin,
  HbA1c, and insulin resistance as outcomes, so this is an area where a clinician should be
  involved rather than an area proven to be affected.
- **Cardiovascular history or heart rhythm concerns.** Increased heart rate and chest
  pressure were reported in the intravenous cohort.
- **History of cancer, personal or family.** The one intravenous study explicitly excluded
  this population without explanation, so retrieved evidence does not cover it.
- **Pregnancy, attempting pregnancy, and breastfeeding.** Not researched here at all.
- **Liver or kidney conditions.** Liver enzymes and kidney function were monitored in the
  retrieved studies, which makes them a professional review category rather than a
  reassurance.
- **Any current prescription medication, and any current supplement regimen.** Not researched
  here.
- **Competitive athletes subject to anti-doping rules.** See the regulatory note on WADA
  below, which this Guide could not resolve.

## Regulatory status

All statements dated **2026-07-19**. Regulatory positions change, and this compound family

**United States (FDA), oral NMN as a dietary supplement.** On 29 September 2025 the FDA
issued two letters responding to citizen petitions, determining that beta-nicotinamide
mononucleotide is not excluded from the definition of a dietary supplement. The FDA applied
the "race to market" provision and found sufficient evidence that NMN was marketed as a
dietary supplement in the United States before drug investigation was authorized, with
evidence of supplement marketing as early as 2017. This changed the FDA's November 2022
position. An important constraint remains: NMN is still a New Dietary Ingredient, so
companies must submit a New Dietary Ingredient Notification before marketing and, per the
retrieved analysis, cannot rely on self-affirmed GRAS status to bypass that requirement. The
FDA did not issue accompanying guidance defining the underlying standards, so ambiguity
remains. Checked 2026-07-19. Source:
https://www.venable.com/insights/publications/2025/10/fda-declares-nicotinamide-mononucleotide-is
(secondary legal analysis; the FDA letters themselves were not retrieved).

**United States (FDA), approval status of intravenous and injectable NAD+.** No FDA-approved
NAD+ injection or infusion product for any indication was identified for this Guide. These
products reach patients in the United States through compounding, which is why compounding
rules, warning letters, and the recall are the relevant regulatory frame rather than an
approval pathway. Stated plainly: intravenous and injectable NAD+ is not an FDA-approved
treatment. **Not verified** against a directly read FDA page. Checked 2026-07-19.

unapproved injectable form could plausibly fall under it whether or not the substance is
named. Checked 2026-07-19. Source: https://www.wada-ama.org/en/resources/2026-prohibited-list

## Unknowns

- Whether raising NAD+ in blood raises NAD+ inside the tissues that matter in humans. The
  meta-analysis authors flagged this as unresolved and likely to differ by tissue.
- Whether intravenous NAD+ produces any clinical benefit for any indication. No randomized
  effectiveness trial of intravenous NAD+ was retrieved. Every marketed use, including
  energy, cognition, addiction recovery, and longevity, is untested in the retrieved
  literature.
- Whether intact NAD+ given intravenously is taken up by cells as NAD+, or must first be
  broken down outside the cell. The unexplained two-hour lag in the 2019 pilot is consistent
  with the latter but does not establish it.
- Whether NAD+ given under the skin, into muscle, or intranasally does anything in humans.
  Zero human evidence was retrieved for these, despite active commercial sale.
- Long-term safety of ongoing oral NMN or NR use. All retrieved trials ran weeks to a few
  months.
- Long-term or repeated-exposure safety of intravenous NAD+. The longest retrieved follow-up
  was 30 days in 14 people.
- Whether the universal infusion reactions in the 6-person cohort reflect the molecule
  itself, the infusion conditions, or the specific product used at that clinic. With 6 people
  and no control for product source, these cannot be separated.
- Whether NAD+ precursors are appropriate for people with a history of cancer. The one
  intravenous study excluded that population without explanation.
- Whether NAD+, NMN, or NR appear by name on the WADA 2026 Prohibited List, and whether an
  unapproved injectable form falls under the S0 catch-all.
- Whether the FDA's 503A Category 2 designation for NAD+ is current as of today, and the
  FDA's exact stated safety rationale.
- Drug interactions, contraindications, and use in pregnancy, none of which were researched.

## Questions for a qualified professional

These are questions a member could reasonably take to a physician, pharmacist, or other
qualified clinician. They are written to open a conversation, not to lead to a particular
answer.

1. Given my medical history and the medications I take, is there any reason I should avoid
   this category of product entirely?
2. The oral trial evidence looks mostly null for the outcomes I care about. Do you read it
   the same way, and is there a subgroup or a use case where you think the evidence is
   stronger than the general picture?
3. If I were considering an intravenous infusion, what would you want to know about the
   product, the pharmacy that prepared it, and the clinic before you thought it was
   reasonable?
4. How would you interpret the report that all six people in the one retrieved intravenous
   tolerability study experienced moderate to severe symptoms including chest pressure and a
   raised heart rate?
5. There has been a Class I recall of a compounded NAD+ injection for endotoxin
   contamination. How do you assess contamination risk in compounded injectable products
   generally?
6. Peer-reviewed testing found that oral NMN products often contain much less than the label
   claims, and some contained none. How would you go about assessing whether a specific
   product is what it says it is?
7. Are there any blood tests, baseline measurements, or monitoring you would want if someone
   in my situation were considering this?
8. I have a personal or family history of cancer. The one intravenous study excluded that
   group without explaining why. Does that concern you, and what would you want to know?
9. I am pregnant, breastfeeding, or trying to conceive. What is known, and what is simply not
   known, about this category in that situation?
10. I compete in a sport that tests for prohibited substances. How should I check the current
    status of this category, and who is the right authority to ask?
11. If my underlying goal is energy, cognition, or healthy aging, what interventions do you
    think have better evidence behind them than this one?
