## Approval in one jurisdiction is not approval in another

A determination, listing, approval, or absence of prohibition in one country has no legal or
practical force in any other. It also does not transfer between product forms within the same
country. This second point matters unusually much for this compound, because in the United
States an oral supplement ingredient and a compounded injectable made from the same molecule
sit in two different regulatory positions at the same time.

Nothing in this file should be read as clearance, endorsement, safety validation, or efficacy
validation by any authority.

## The form distinction that drives everything below

| Form | Regulatory character in the United States |
|---|---|
| Oral precursors (NMN, NR, nicotinamide, NADH) | Dietary supplements and dietary ingredients |
| Intravenous NAD+ | Compounded drug product. Not an FDA-approved drug for any indication |
| Injectable (subcutaneous, intramuscular) and intranasal NAD+ | Reaches buyers through compounding pharmacies, peptide vendors, and research chemical channels. No approval identified, and no human evidence retrieved for these routes |

## United States

### US-1. FDA position on NMN as a dietary ingredient

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Jurisdiction | United States, FDA |
| Statement | On 29 September 2025, FDA issued two letters responding to citizen petitions, determining that beta-nicotinamide mononucleotide is not excluded from the definition of a dietary supplement. FDA applied the race-to-market provision and found sufficient evidence that NMN was marketed as a dietary supplement in the United States before drug investigation was authorized, with evidence of supplement marketing as early as 2017. This overturns FDA's November 2022 position that NMN was precluded from being a dietary ingredient because it had been investigated as a drug. NMN remains a New Dietary Ingredient, so companies must still submit a New Dietary Ingredient Notification before marketing, and per the retrieved analysis cannot rely on self-affirmed GRAS status to bypass that requirement. FDA did not issue accompanying guidance defining the underlying standards, so ambiguity remains |
| Date checked | 2026-07-19 |

| Verification | Secondary legal analysis, read in full. FDA's own determination letters were NOT retrieved. No FDA language is quoted verbatim anywhere in this Guide |
| Publishing note | This concerns legal marketability of an oral ingredient. It is not approval, not endorsement, not a safety finding, not an efficacy finding, and has no bearing on intravenous or injectable NAD+ |
