# Evidence Grades

Member education. This file is an AI draft at workflow state `draft`. Nothing here is approved,
published, or member-visible until Samuel Boadu reviews and publishes it.

Every factual statement inside a Xenios Research Guide carries a grade. This page explains what the
grades mean, what they do not mean, and why the grading system is deliberately unflattering to most
of the material you will read.

---

## What are the evidence grades, in one line each?

There are eight labels. Seven are grades. One is a stop.

**A. Established.** The claim is supported by a body of high-quality human evidence, consistent
across multiple independent studies, and is not seriously contested in the literature.

**B. Supported human evidence.** Reasonable human trial evidence exists and points in one
direction, but the body of work is smaller, narrower, or less consistent than grade A requires.

**C. Early human evidence.** Human data exists, but it is preliminary. Small samples, single
studies, short duration, narrow populations, or unreplicated results all sit here.

**D. Preclinical.** The finding comes from animal models or laboratory (in vitro) work only. No
human evidence supports it. A grade D claim tells you what happened in a specific model organism or
cell system, and nothing more.

**E. Manufacturer or supplier reported.** The claim originates from a party that sells or produces
the material. It has not been independently verified. Commercial interest is disclosed rather than
laundered.

**F. Traditional or historical.** The claim comes from long-standing customary use or historical
record. That is a real category of information, and it is not evidence of an effect.

**G. Unverified.** The claim circulates but no source we could retrieve and check supports it. This
is the default resting place for a great deal of what is said online about research compounds.

**PROHIBITED.** Not a grade. It marks a claim that may not appear on any member-facing Xenios
Research surface at all, regardless of how many people repeat it elsewhere.

## Why is a grade attached to a single claim instead of to a product?

Because a product does not have an evidence level. Individual statements do.

Take any single compound. One statement about it might rest on decades of human trials. A second
statement about the same compound might rest on one study in rodents. A third might be something a
supplier put in a product description. Averaging those three into a single badge on a product page
would be an act of misinformation, not simplification. The strong claim would drag the weak ones up,
and the reader would have no way to tell which was which.

So the grade attaches to the sentence. In a Guide, you should be able to look at any specific
statement and see what stands behind that statement. If a product-level rating ever appears on a
Xenios Research surface, it is a defect, and reporting it is useful.

This also means a Guide with mostly grade D and grade G statements is not a Guide about a bad
compound. It is a Guide about a compound that has not been studied much in humans. Those are
different things, and the grading system is designed to keep them separate.

## Why are so many claims graded D or G?

Because that is the honest state of the evidence for most of this material, and the grading process
starts from a conservative position rather than a generous one.

The internal rule is that a claim defaults to G or D unless a specific retrieved human study
supports something better. The burden sits on the upgrade, not on the downgrade. Nobody has to prove
a claim is weak. Somebody has to produce a source to prove it is strong.

You will therefore read Guides where most lines are graded D or G. That is the point of the library.
A library that graded everything B in order to feel substantial would be worth nothing.

## Does a grade A claim mean the thing is safe, or right for me?

No. The grade describes the strength of evidence behind a statement of fact. It says nothing about
safety, suitability, legality, or whether any of it applies to you.

Those are separate questions, and Xenios Research does not answer the last one. No Guide provides
medical, clinical, legal, or financial advice, makes a diagnosis, or tells you what to do. A high
grade on a mechanistic or observational statement is not permission, endorsement, or a
recommendation.

Safety in particular is never asserted as a general fact anywhere in this library. Guides do not say
a compound is safe, well tolerated, or free of side effects, because those are claims about you and
your circumstances, and no document can make them.

## Why do Guides never include dosing?

Dosing information is intentionally excluded from every Guide, without exception.

That means no amounts, no units, no concentrations, no frequency, no timing, no cycle length, no
titration, no loading, no stacking, no reconstitution, and no administration technique or route.
This is a fixed editorial boundary, not an oversight and not a gap waiting to be filled in a later
version.

The reason is the difference between education and instruction. A document that describes what has
been studied is educational. A document that tells you what to take and how is a protocol, and a
protocol is a clinical act performed by a licensed person who knows your history. Xenios Research is
not that, does not employ that, and does not simulate it.

If you find dosing content on a Xenios Research surface, that is a content defect and it should be
reported.

## What makes a claim PROHIBITED rather than just weakly graded?

A weak grade means the claim is permitted with its evidence honestly labelled. PROHIBITED means the
claim is not permitted at all.

Claims land in PROHIBITED when labelling them accurately would still not make them acceptable to
publish. Typical cases include statements that a compound treats, cures, reverses, or eliminates a
disease, statements that guarantee an outcome, and any framing that positions research material as a
therapy for a member to use.

One specific pattern is worth naming, because it is common in this market: using the phrase
"research use only" as decoration while the surrounding copy implies human benefit. That framing is
prohibited here. A disclaimer used as cover for an implied claim is still the claim.

## Who assigns grades, and can a grade change?

Grades are assigned during Guide drafting and are subject to founder review before anything
publishes. AI drafts never publish automatically. Samuel Boadu is the founder reviewer and
publisher, and material sits at draft state until he moves it.

Grades can and do change. New human evidence can move a claim up. A failed replication, a retraction,
or a source that turns out not to hold up can move a claim down. A downgrade is a normal event, not
an embarrassment, and Guides carry a status and a review date so you can see how current the
assessment is.

## What should I do if I think a grade is wrong?

Send it through the member question channel with the specific sentence and, if you have one, the
source you think changes the picture.

Two things to expect. First, a grade challenge is handled as a research question and follows the
same review path as any other, so it is not instant. Second, a source being real is not the same as
a source changing the grade, and some challenges will be answered with an explanation of why the
grade stands.

Citation integrity is the hard rule underneath all of this. Xenios Research cites only sources that
were actually retrieved and checked. Where a claim cannot be traced to a retrievable source, the
claim is either graded G or removed, and it is never propped up with a plausible-looking reference
that nobody verified. A fabricated citation is treated as a more serious failure than a missing one.
