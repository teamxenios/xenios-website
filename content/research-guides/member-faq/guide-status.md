# Guide Status

Member education. This file is an AI draft at workflow state `draft`. Nothing here is approved,
published, or member-visible until Samuel Boadu reviews and publishes it.

Every Guide in the library carries a status label. The label tells you how finished the document is
and how much weight to put on it. This page explains each label, and explains why an unfinished
Guide is left visibly unfinished rather than hidden until it looks complete.

---

## What does each Guide status mean?

**Published.** The Guide has been drafted, reviewed by the founder reviewer, and released to
members. It carries a last-reviewed date. Published does not mean permanent, and it does not mean
every claim inside is strongly graded. It means the document has passed review in its current form.

**Updated.** A previously published Guide that has been revised since its first release. The change
might be new evidence, a corrected claim, a downgraded grade, or a rewrite for clarity. The
last-reviewed date moves with the update.

**In Review.** Drafting is finished and the document is with the founder reviewer. It is not member
visible in this state. A Guide can sit in review for a while, and it can come out of review with
substantial changes or go back to drafting entirely.

**In Development.** Active drafting. Sources are being retrieved and checked and claims are being
graded. Nothing in this state is reliable, and nothing in this state is shown to members as though
it were.

**Coming Soon.** The Guide is planned and named, and work has not started. This label exists so the
library can be honest about the shape of the gap rather than presenting a shorter list and implying
it is complete.

## Does a product need a completed Guide to exist?

No. Guide completion is not a prerequisite for anything.

A product record can exist in the catalog with no Guide, a Guide in development, or a Guide that
never progresses. The catalog state and the Guide state are tracked separately and neither one gates
the other. This is deliberate. Tying the two together would create pressure to rush a Guide to
completion because a record was waiting on it, and that pressure is exactly what produces
low-quality research content.

The reverse is also true, and it matters more. A completed, published Guide does not unlock
anything, does not qualify you for anything, and is not a step in a purchase process. Guides are
member-only education. They are not gates in a funnel.

## Then why does a product appear with no Guide attached?

Because the record exists and the education does not yet. Saying so plainly is better than hiding
the record until the writing catches up.

A catalog record is a factual entry. A Guide is a researched document that takes source retrieval,
verification, grading, and review. The second takes far longer than the first, so the library will
routinely be behind the catalog. When you see a record with no Guide, read it as "we have not
written this yet", not as "there is nothing to say" and not as "this is ready for you".

Where a product fact is unknown, Xenios Research surfaces are required to say NOT CONFIRMED rather
than fill the gap. Composition, concentration, fill volume, shelf life, storage conditions, purity,
certificate content, supplier identity, and price are never estimated or inferred. An empty field is
accurate. A guessed field is not.

## Can I order something I read about in a Guide?

No. Not currently, in any category.

Peptide commerce is not authorized. Quantum commerce is not authorized. Supplement reseller activity
is not authorized. Every commerce lane is either blocked or pending written approval, and no
membership tier, assessment result, Blueprint, or completed Guide changes that.

Fulfillment arrangements for the early period are handled outside the member platform and remain
pending written approval. Nothing about those arrangements creates a purchase path through Xenios
Research, and no Guide should ever be written or read as a route to obtaining a compound. If a Guide
reads that way to you, that is a content defect worth reporting.

No date is committed for commerce opening, because the dependencies are legal review and supplier
documentation, and a date built on those would be a guess presented as a plan.

## Who reviews a Guide, and what does review actually check?

Samuel Boadu is the founder reviewer and publisher. AI drafts never publish automatically, and there
is no automated path from drafting to member visibility.

Review checks whether every claim carries a grade, whether cited sources were actually retrieved and
are real, whether any dosing or protocol content has crept in (it is excluded without exception),
whether animal or laboratory findings are stated with the model named in the same sentence as the
finding, whether any outcome is stated as guaranteed, and whether any unsupported safety claim
appears.

A Guide that fails any of those goes back rather than publishing with a caveat attached.

## How often are Guides reviewed after publication?

Each Guide carries a last-reviewed date, and that date is the honest signal. A Guide reviewed
several months ago is a Guide that has not been checked against anything published since.

Re-review is triggered by new human evidence, a retraction or failed replication affecting a cited
source, a member challenge that holds up, or a regulatory change in a relevant jurisdiction. Any
regulatory statement in a Guide carries a date, a jurisdiction, and a source link, precisely so it
can be checked for staleness rather than trusted indefinitely.

[UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check] A fixed re-review interval is NOT
CONFIRMED. Until one is set and staffed, treat the last-reviewed date as the only reliable currency
signal, and treat an old date as a reason for caution.

## Can I request a Guide, or ask for one to be prioritized?

You can request one through the member question channel. Requests are recorded and they do influence
what gets drafted next.

Two honest limits. Requesting does not create a queue position you can see or track, and member
requests are one input among several rather than the deciding one. Source availability matters more
than demand. A heavily requested topic with almost nothing retrievable behind it produces a short
Guide full of grade G lines, which serves nobody, so it may stay in development while a less
requested topic with better sources moves ahead of it.

## Why do some Guides stay In Development for a long time?

Usually because the sources are not there.

Drafting is fast. Retrieving real studies, verifying that each one says what it is claimed to say,
grading each individual claim conservatively, and cutting the claims that cannot be traced to a
retrievable source is slow. When most of what circulates about a compound turns out to be
unverifiable, the responsible output is a short Guide, and a short Guide often takes longer to
produce than a long one.

The alternative would be to fill the gaps from memory or inference. That is not permitted here.
Invented references and reconstructed study details are treated as the most serious failure this
library can produce, worse than an incomplete document, so a Guide waits rather than guessing.

## What is honestly not settled about the status system yet?

As of 2026-07-19: the re-review cadence, the second-reviewer rule for high-grade claims, the
member-visible change record showing what moved between an original Guide and its Updated version,
and any published turnaround target for the review queue are all NOT CONFIRMED.

The status labels themselves are settled. The operational commitments behind them are not, and they
will not be described here as though they are.
