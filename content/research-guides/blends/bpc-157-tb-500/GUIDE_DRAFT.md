# BPC-157 + TB-500 (blend)

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

> ## The rule that governs this Guide
>
> **A blend is not the sum of its ingredients. It is a new thing.**
>
> When two compounds are put in one container and given together, they form a subject of study in
> their own right. The pair has its own absorption, its own interaction behaviour, its own risk
> profile and its own unanswered questions. None of that can be read off from what is known about
> either ingredient by itself.
>
> So the question for this product is not "what does the research say about BPC-157" and it is not
> "what does the research say about TB-500". It is "what does the research say about the two of
> them given together".
>
> The answer is nothing. No study of any design has tested this combination.
>
> That is not weak evidence, or early evidence, or preliminary evidence. Where nobody has run the
> study, there is no evidence to grade. Everything else in this Guide is background about the
> separate ingredients, and it is labelled as such throughout precisely so that it is never mistaken
> for evidence about the product.

## One-minute summary

This Guide covers a product combining two synthetic peptides, BPC-157 and TB-500. Neither is an
approved medicine anywhere that was checked, and the combination has never been studied.

- **No study of BPC-157 and TB-500 given together was located.** Not a randomised trial, not an open
  label study, not a case series, not even a trial registration. The searches behind this Guide
  covered PubMed, ClinicalTrials.gov, and a United States Food and Drug Administration (FDA)
  regulatory review that itself reports systematic searches of PubMed, Embase, Cochrane and
  ClinicalTrials.gov (checked 2026-07-19).
- **The only human record specific to this pair is an adverse event report.** FDA searched its
  adverse event reporting system through 2025-12-04 and retrieved three reports for BPC-157 in
  total. One concerned a product containing BPC-157 and TB500, in which a 40 year old woman
  developed widespread skin darkening and darkening of the gums. The reaction reproduced when the
  product was taken again and resolved when it was stopped. FDA judged the reaction likely due to
  the product, and stated that because the product contained two peptides it is not possible to say
  which one was responsible. That sentence is the blend problem in a single real case.
- **The composition and ratio of this product are not confirmed.** Nothing in the research record
  establishes how much of each ingredient a blend contains. This is the first question a supplier
  would have to answer, and until it is answered no statement about this product's contents is
  possible.
- **BPC-157's own human evidence base is very small.** FDA identified five human studies in the
  entire published literature. Three exist only as conference abstracts never published in full, one
  is a retrospective review of 17 people at a single private clinic, and one is a two person case
  series. The only randomised controlled trial among them, in ulcerative colitis, did not separate
  from placebo, and FDA called the data inadequate to support efficacy or safety. A PubMed search
  restricted to systematic reviews, randomised controlled trials and clinical trials returned zero
  records for BPC 157.
- **TB-500's human evidence base is empty.** A full trial registry query returned exactly one
  registered human study of TB-500. It began in February 2026, it is still recruiting, its main
  measurements are counts of side effects rather than benefit, and no results are posted.
- **Most human trials cited for TB-500 studied a different molecule.** TB-500 is a seven amino acid
  fragment. Thymosin beta-4 is the full length 43 amino acid protein. The dry eye, corneal and
  wound healing trials that circulate as TB-500 evidence studied the full length protein, usually
  applied topically to an eye or wound surface, in nine to a few dozen patients. Treating those as
  TB-500 evidence is a category error, and one of the most frequently cited among them reports no
  outcome data at all.
- **There is a real preclinical literature for BPC-157**, meaning work in animals and in cells
  rather than in people. FDA's assessment of it is unusually pointed: molecular targets are
  unidentified, mechanisms are poorly understood, and dose response relationships could not be
  established because widely separated amounts produced comparable effects.
- **On safety, the correct statement for both ingredients is that nothing is characterised.** FDA
  states there is insufficient clinical safety information to characterise the safety profile of
  BPC-157, and that nonclinical toxicology is too limited in scope and duration. For TB-500 the
  first human safety study is still running. Absence of reported harm here reflects absence of
  study.
- **Both components appear to be prohibited in sport**, according to the United States Anti-Doping
  Agency, and validated tests exist to detect TB-500 and its breakdown products.

Xenios does not sell these compounds, does not supply them, and does not tell members how to obtain
or use them. This Guide exists so that a member reading about this blend elsewhere has an accurate
account of what is and is not known.

**Claim grade for "this combination is effective for any human purpose": G (Unverified).**
**Claim grade for "this combination has an established safety profile": G (Unverified).**
**Claim grade for "BPC-157 shows tissue repair effects in rodent and cell models": D (Preclinical).**

Grades are given per claim. The product is never graded as a whole, because grading a mixture as a
unit would imply the mixture had been assessed as one.

## What it is

This product combines two separate synthetic peptides. A peptide is a short chain of amino acids,
the building blocks that make up proteins. Neither of these has been approved as a medicine in any
jurisdiction checked for this Guide.

### BPC-157

BPC-157, sometimes written as Body Protection Compound 157, is a synthetic peptide reported to be
composed of 15 amino acids, described as a partial sequence of a larger protein originally isolated
from human gastric juice. FDA records it as also known as Bepecin and PL-14736, an earlier
development code, and notes it is not a United States Adopted Name, meaning it has no standardised
official name (S01).

An identity point that matters more than it sounds. FDA treats BPC-157 free base and BPC-157 acetate
as two different active ingredients, and states that the common name BPC-157 is used commercially
for multiple salts, derivatives, and even different active moieties, meaning different active
molecules. FDA describes inconsistent naming of this kind as a safety risk, because a person may
receive a different substance than intended. The name on a label does not pin down what is in the
container (S01).

### TB-500

TB-500 is a synthetic, N-terminally acetylated peptide of seven amino acids, written as Ac-LKKTETQ.
It corresponds to residues 17 to 23 of a larger protein called thymosin beta-4. This identity is
confirmed directly by the analytical literature and by the official title of the only registered
human study of it (S15, S23).

### The distinction that most commercial material gets wrong

Three different things are routinely treated as one, and separating them is the single most
important accuracy point in this Guide after the blend rule itself.

1. **TB-500.** The short synthetic acetylated fragment above. This is what is sold under the name
   TB-500.
2. **Thymosin beta-4.** The full length naturally occurring 43 amino acid protein. A different and
   much larger molecule. Nearly all published human trial data attaches to this, not to TB-500.
3. **RGN-259 and NL005.** Investigational pharmaceutical products formulated from full length
   thymosin beta-4, RGN-259 as an eye solution and NL005 as an injectable. These are regulated
   investigational products with registered trials, and they are not the same thing as a vial sold
   as TB-500.

Vendor and secondary material frequently uses TB-500 and thymosin beta-4 interchangeably. The
primary sources retrieved for this Guide do not support that usage. A purchaser cannot tell from a
product name alone whether a vial is claimed to contain the seven amino acid fragment or the 43
amino acid protein (S15).

### What the blend is

Unknown. No source retrieved for this Guide establishes the composition or the ratio of any product
sold as a BPC-157 and TB-500 blend, including this one. No proportion is stated anywhere in this
Guide, and none should be inferred from any source that does state one, because no such source was
verified.

What is documented is that products of this kind exist and are common. FDA notes that products
frequently combine BPC-157 with other unapproved peptides, TB-500 or thymosin beta-4 among them, and
raises this specifically as a reason that both benefit claims and harm signals become impossible to
attribute (S01). That is a regulator observing that these are sold together. It is not a regulator
observing that they have been studied together.

## Why interest exists

The interest is easy to describe, and worth understanding without endorsing it.

Both compounds have been proposed, in preclinical work, to support tissue repair. BPC-157 has a
large rodent and cell culture literature reporting protective and wound healing effects across many
tissues. TB-500 is described as corresponding to the active site of a protein involved in cell
migration and wound healing. Set side by side, they suggest a story about recovery from injury, and
that story lands in an area where people have real unmet need, particularly musculoskeletal injuries
that heal slowly or incompletely.

The blend adds a second layer of appeal. Two compounds proposed to support repair, combined into one
product, invites the assumption that the effects add up, or that each covers a different part of the
process. That assumption is the thing this Guide exists to interrupt. It is not supported by
anything, in either direction. Nobody has measured what these two do together.

Four things should be held alongside the interest.

First, an appealing rationale is not an outcome. Interest describes why researchers thought
something was worth studying. It does not describe what happened when it was given to people.

Second, for TB-500, almost nothing has been given to people. The first registered human study began
in February 2026 and is measuring side effects (S23).

Third, for BPC-157, what has been given to people has not shown much. The strongest human study
design in the whole record, a randomised placebo controlled trial in ulcerative colitis, did not
separate from placebo (S01).

Fourth, a good deal of the enthusiasm rests on evidence belonging to a different molecule. That is
covered in the contradictions section below.

## Terminology

Every technical term used in this Guide, in plain English.

- **Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids. Shorter than a protein.
- **Amino acid.** One of the building blocks that link together to form peptides and proteins.
- **Pentadecapeptide.** A peptide of 15 amino acids. BPC-157 is described as one.
- **Fragment.** A short piece of a longer protein, made separately. TB-500 is a fragment of thymosin
  beta-4.
- **Free base and acetate.** Two different chemical forms of the same core molecule. Regulators treat
  them as separate substances because they are not identical.
- **Active moiety.** The part of a substance responsible for its activity. FDA notes that products
  sold under the name BPC-157 have included different active moieties, meaning genuinely different
  molecules.
- **Preclinical.** Research done in animals or in cells in a dish, not in people.
- **In vitro.** In a dish or a test tube, outside a living body.
- **Ex vivo.** In living tissue removed from a body.
- **Randomised controlled trial.** A study where participants are assigned by chance to receive the
  substance or a comparison, which is the design best able to separate a real effect from
  expectation and chance.
- **Placebo controlled.** Some participants receive an inactive comparison.
- **Double blind or double masked.** Neither the participants nor the researchers know who received
  what.
- **Single arm or uncontrolled.** Everyone receives the substance and there is no comparison group,
  which makes it impossible to know what would have happened anyway.
- **Confidence interval.** A range showing how precisely an effect was measured. If the range for a
  difference between two groups includes zero, the study did not show a difference.
- **Pharmacokinetics.** How a substance is absorbed, distributed, broken down and cleared by the body.
- **Metabolite.** A product formed when the body breaks a substance down.
- **Immunogenicity.** Whether the body mounts an immune response against a substance.
- **Aggregation.** Peptide molecules clumping together, which regulators link to immune reactions.
- **Impurity profile.** What else is in a material besides the intended substance, and how much.
- **Certificate of analysis.** A document from a laboratory stating what testing was done on a
  specific batch and what the results were.
- **Monograph.** An official published standard, for example from the United States Pharmacopeia,
  setting out how a substance must be identified and tested. Neither component has one.
- **503A compounding.** A United States framework under which pharmacies may prepare customised
  medicines for individual patients, subject to conditions on which substances may be used.
- **Bulk drug substance.** The raw active ingredient, before it is made into a finished product.
- **FAERS.** The FDA Adverse Event Reporting System, a database of voluntarily submitted reports of
  problems experienced with medicines. Reports do not establish that a substance caused the problem.
- **Rechallenge.** Taking a substance again after stopping it. If the same reaction reappears, that
  strengthens the case that the substance was involved.
- **Angiogenesis.** The growth of new blood vessels.
- **Fibroblast.** A cell type involved in building and repairing connective tissue.
- **Actin.** A structural protein inside cells, involved in cell movement and shape.
- **Systematic review.** A structured review of all studies meeting stated criteria on a question.

## Proposed mechanism

Everything in this section is proposed rather than established, and none of it has been confirmed in
a living human being for either component. There is no proposed mechanism for the combination at
all, because nobody has studied the combination.

### BPC-157

There is no agreed mechanism. FDA's assessment is direct: the molecular targets for BPC-157 related
substances have not been identified, and the mechanisms of action remain poorly understood (S01).

Pathways proposed in preclinical work include VEGFR2 activation and nitric oxide synthesis via the
Akt-eNOS axis, and ERK1/2 signalling, as summarised in a 2025 peer reviewed narrative review of
animal and cell studies (S09). A separate primary study in rat tendon explants and cultured rat
tendon fibroblasts reported increased phosphorylation of FAK and paxillin, two proteins involved in
how cells attach and move, and the authors concluded the observed effect was likely mediated by that
pathway (S10).

An anomaly worth stating plainly. FDA notes repeatedly across the rodent literature that a dose
response relationship could not be established, because widely separated amounts produced comparable
effects (S01). In pharmacology, an effect that does not change as the amount changes is a reason to
look harder at the finding, not a reason for confidence.

### TB-500

The proposed mechanism is actin sequestration and actin binding, with downstream effects on cell
migration, angiogenesis, matrix remodelling and tissue repair. The LKKTETQ segment is described in
the analytical literature as the active site within thymosin beta-4 responsible for actin binding,
cell migration and wound healing (S15). A 2026 review states that thymosin beta-4 and its derivative
TB-500 promoted angiogenesis and tissue repair in preclinical models, without specifying species in
the retrieved record (S17).

This account is largely inferred from work on the parent protein rather than demonstrated for the
fragment, and it is directly challenged from within the preclinical literature. In fibroblast wound
healing assays, the metabolite Ac-LKKTE showed significant activity while the parent TB-500 compound
did not. The authors concluded that previously reported wound healing activity of TB-500 may be due
to its metabolite rather than the parent form (S16). If that is correct, the mechanistic story
commonly told about TB-500 may be attributing activity to the wrong molecule.

### The combination

There is no proposed mechanism for the pair that rests on any evidence. Combining two mechanistic
stories, neither of which is established, does not produce a third one. Any account of how these two
"work together" is invention, however plausible it sounds.

## Human evidence, and its absence

This section is organised to keep three questions apart. Read them in order.

### 1. Human evidence for the combination

**There is none.**

No study of any design tested BPC-157 and TB-500 administered together and measured an outcome. No
randomised trial. No open label study. No case series. No trial registration. The searches behind
this Guide covered PubMed via NCBI E-utilities, ClinicalTrials.gov via its API, and FDA's own
regulatory review, which reports systematic searches of PubMed, Embase, Cochrane and
ClinicalTrials.gov (checked 2026-07-19).

One human record specific to the pair exists, and it is an adverse event report rather than a study.

FDA searched its adverse event reporting system through 2025-12-04 and retrieved three reports
associated with BPC-157 in total, all concerning products given by injection. The third concerned a
40 year old woman who developed widespread skin darkening and darkening of the gums while using a
product containing BPC-157 and TB500. The same reactions reproduced when the product was taken again
and resolved when it was stopped. FDA judged the reaction likely due to the product, precisely
because of that rechallenge pattern, and then wrote the sentence that defines this Guide: because
the product contained two peptides, it is not possible to assess a potential relationship between
BPC-157 and the reported adverse events (S01).

Three things follow, and all three must travel together.

- A single spontaneous report is not a rate. It does not establish causation, it has no denominator,
  and it cannot be turned into an expected outcome. FDA also notes that 503A compounders generally
  do not report adverse events, so underreporting is expected.
- It is nonetheless the only human data point that exists for this pairing, and it is a harm signal,
  not a benefit signal.
- The reason nobody can say which ingredient was responsible is that there were two ingredients.
  That is the structural cost of a blend, appearing in a real person rather than in an argument.

A second report, of injection site redness and swelling in a 55 year old woman, is recorded as
confounded by concurrent thymosin, without specifying whether that was TB-500 or the full length
protein. It is not counted here as a combination report (S01).

One further near miss deserves naming, because it is sometimes presented as combination evidence.
The retrospective review of 17 people with knee pain at a single private clinic included some
subjects who received BPC-157 together with thymosin beta-4, the full length protein, not TB-500
(S01). It is a different pairing, and it is uncontrolled and retrospective in any case.

Finally, an observation that is easy to miss. The one sponsor currently running human research on
both compounds is running two separate studies, one of BPC-157 in hamstring strain and one of TB-500
in cardiovascular disease (S07, S23). Formal research treats these as two distinct investigational
questions.

### 2. Human evidence for BPC-157 alone

**Not evidence about this product.** Included so a member knows what does exist for one ingredient.

FDA identified five human studies in the entire published literature (S01).

Two registrations exist and neither has produced anything. A Phase 1 study registered in 2015 has no
posted results and no located publication a decade later, with an overall status of Unknown (S06). A
randomised placebo controlled Phase 2 trial in acute hamstring strain is recruiting at a single
hospital in China, with estimated study completion in February 2028 (S07). A registration generates
no evidence and must never be cited as support for anything.

A PubMed search restricted to systematic reviews, randomised controlled trials and clinical trials
returned zero records for BPC 157 (S14). An independent 2025 peer reviewed narrative review counted
three human pilot studies in the musculoskeletal context and concluded that BPC-157 should be
considered investigational (S09).

### 3. Human evidence for TB-500 alone

**Not evidence about this product either. And there is almost none of it.**

A full trial registry query returned exactly one registered human study of the TB-500 fragment. It
is a Phase 1/2 randomised, double blind, placebo controlled, escalating cohort study in adults with
stable atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease and endothelial dysfunction. It started on 2026-02-05,
it is recruiting, and no results are posted. Its primary outcomes are counts of treatment emergent
adverse events and serious adverse events (S22, S23).

That last detail is the informative one. The first formal question being asked about TB-500 in
humans is whether it is tolerated at all. A 2026 peer reviewed review in a sports medicine journal
states plainly that human orthopaedic data are lacking (S17).

### 4. The trials that are not TB-500 trials

This subsection exists because these studies are cited constantly in support of TB-500, and a member
will encounter them. They studied full length thymosin beta-4, a different molecule, usually applied
topically to an eye or a wound surface.

| Study | What it actually studied | Why it is not TB-500 evidence |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Phase 2 dry eye trial, 2015 (S18) | Full length thymosin beta-4 as an eye drop, in nine patients across two United States sites, reporting improvement versus placebo in discomfort and corneal staining | Different molecule, topical ocular route, ocular indication, nine patients. It says nothing about a fragment given for tissue repair. |
| Phase 3 neurotrophic keratopathy trial, 2022 (S19) | RGN-259, a formulated full length thymosin beta-4 eye solution, in 18 subjects, terminated early for slow recruitment. 60 percent of treated subjects achieved complete corneal healing by day 29 versus 12.5 percent of placebo, which did not reach statistical significance at that timepoint. A later timepoint difference did reach significance | Different molecule, eye drop, rare eye condition, 18 people, terminated early, and the primary timepoint result was not significant. Authors note the placebo group was older with larger lesions. |
| Venous ulcer study, 2007 (S20) | Full length thymosin beta-4 applied topically. Using any of these as evidence for TB-500 is a category error. Using them as evidence for a two
ingredient blend containing TB-500 stacks a second error on top of the first.

## Preclinical evidence

Preclinical means animals or cells, not people. Every finding below names its model. None of it is
evidence about the combination, because no preclinical study of the combination was located either.

### BPC-157

In rat tendon explants and cultured rat tendon fibroblasts studied in the laboratory, BPC-157 was
reported to accelerate fibroblast outgrowth from tendon tissue, increase cell survival under
hydrogen peroxide stress, and increase cell migration and spreading, with increased phosphorylation
of FAK and paxillin. No effect on cell proliferation was seen by MTT assay. The authors concluded the
effect is likely mediated by activation of the FAK and paxillin pathway. This is a mechanistic cell
and tissue study, not a demonstration of tendon healing in a person (S10).

FDA's own summary of the wider preclinical literature reports that pharmacological studies have
suggested BPC-157 prevents lesions induced by various challenges in rodent gastrointestinal and
cardiovascular systems, has nephroprotective properties in rodents, promotes healing of transected
tendons in rodents, and has neuroprotective properties in rodent models of stroke, encephalopathy
and spinal cord injury. FDA also summarises rat studies on surgically created colonic fistulas and on
prevention of drug induced gastric and liver lesions (S01).

FDA's caveat on all of it is the part that matters, and it should never be separated from the list
above. FDA states it is difficult to assess the biological plausibility of these effects because
dose response relationships have not been established, the molecular targets have not been
identified, and the mechanisms of action remain poorly understood. FDA notes repeatedly that a dose
response relationship could not be established because widely separated amounts produced comparable
effects, and that several studies lacked histopathological confirmation, meaning the tissue changes
were not confirmed under a microscope (S01).

A 2025 peer reviewed narrative review reports regenerative properties across numerous animal models
and proposes the VEGFR2, Akt-eNOS and ERK1/2 pathways, while concluding in its own words that human
data are extremely limited, that only three pilot studies have examined BPC-157 in humans, and that
BPC-157 should be considered investigational and its use approached with caution (S09).

### TB-500

The most informative preclinical study retrieved is an analytical one. Working in rats in vivo, in
human serum, in enzyme systems and in human fibroblast cell culture, researchers quantified TB-500
and its breakdown products. Ac-LK was the primary metabolite at highest concentration in rats in the
first six hours, and Ac-LKK persisted as a long term metabolite detectable up to 72 hours. In the
fibroblast wound healing assays, the metabolite Ac-LKKTE showed significant wound healing activity
while the parent TB-500 compound did not. The authors concluded that previously reported wound
healing activity of TB-500 in the literature may be due to its metabolite rather than the parent
form (S16).

A 2026 narrative review states that thymosin beta-4 and its derivative TB-500 promoted angiogenesis
and tissue repair in preclinical models. The species and models are not specified in the retrieved
record, and the underlying primary studies were not retrieved, so this rests on a review summary
rather than on primary work (S17).

A separate analytical study in horses developed methods to detect acetylated LKKTETQ and its
metabolites in equine urine and plasma, and reported that a non natural synthesis impurity is
detectable in equine plasma after administration of a TB-500 containing product. It is included here
for identity and detectability, not as evidence of any therapeutic effect (S15).

### The combination

No preclinical study of BPC-157 and TB-500 together was located. There is no animal model of this
blend, no cell study of this blend, and no interaction work of any kind.

## Contradictions and disagreement

Full detail is in CONTRADICTIONS.md. The five that matter most to a member are summarised here.

**A blend does not inherit its ingredients' evidence.** Commercial material presents the two research
records side by side and lets the reader conclude the product is well researched. It is not a matter
of degree. Component evidence produces no evidence for the mixture, while creating the impression of
a great deal. In this case there is also very little to inherit: one thin record plus one empty
record does not make a substantial one.

**TB-500 is not thymosin beta-4.** This is not a scientific controversy, it is a widespread error.
Nearly every human trial cited for TB-500 studied the full length 43 amino acid protein, typically
delivered topically to an eye or wound surface, in nine to a few dozen patients. TB-500 is a seven
amino acid fragment. One of the most frequently cited of those trials reports no outcome data at all.

**Whether TB-500 itself is even the active molecule is unresolved.** One preclinical study found the
parent compound inactive in fibroblast wound healing assays while a metabolite was active, and
concluded that reported activity may belong to the metabolite. This is a single in vitro result
inside an analytical paper and should not be overstated, but it undercuts the simplest version of the
TB-500 story and means preclinical results may reflect a different molecule than the one purchased.

**The volume of BPC-157 literature is real, and it is almost entirely preclinical.** Enthusiast
sources cite the volume as though it were clinical support. FDA found five human studies in total,
and a database search restricted to trial publication types returned zero records.

**Absence of reported harm is not a safety record.** Several small BPC-157 studies report no adverse
events, and only three adverse event reports exist. FDA states those studies were short, small, of
unclear safety monitoring, and one had two participants. A study that could not have detected a harm
has not shown its absence.

## Limitations

The limitations of this Guide, stated so a member can judge how much weight it carries.

1. **The central finding is an absence, and absences are harder to prove than presences.** This Guide
   states that no study of the combination exists. That rests on searches of PubMed,
   ClinicalTrials.gov and a regulatory review reporting its own systematic searches. It does not rule
   out an unindexed, unpublished or non English study. A second reviewer should re-run the
   combination search independently and record the result.
2. **The two halves of this Guide are not equally verified.** FDA sources were reachable during the
   BPC-157 research and returned errors during the TB-500 research. BPC-157 regulatory statements
   here rest on primary FDA documents. TB-500 regulatory statements do not exist, because they could
   not be checked.
3. **Four of the five BPC-157 human studies were never read directly.** They exist in this Guide only
   through FDA's summary of them. They are cited as FDA reported, and no publication identifiers were
   constructed for them.
4. **Some TB-500 preclinical claims rest on a narrative review** rather than on primary studies, and
   that review does not specify species in the retrieved record.
5. **No trial registry outside ClinicalTrials.gov was searched** for either component, and one of the
   two BPC-157 trials found there is run in China, which suggests other regional registrations may
   exist.
6. **The anti-doping position was never read from the source document.** Both statements come from
   the United States Anti-Doping Agency rather than from the World Anti-Doping Agency list itself.
7. **Regulatory status is moving.** An FDA advisory committee meeting concerning BPC-157 was scheduled
   for 23 to 24 July 2026 and had not occurred when this Guide was checked. Any statement here may be
   out of date within weeks.
8. **This Guide describes compounds, not a specific product.** Composition and ratio are unconfirmed,
   so nothing here can be read as describing what is in any particular container.

## Quality and identity

Full detail is in QUALITY_AND_DOCUMENTATION.md. The essentials belong here because they are as
important as the evidence question, and in some ways more immediate.

**Neither component has an official standard to be tested against.** There is no United States
Pharmacopeia or National Formulary drug substance monograph for either BPC-157 form, no dietary
supplement monograph, and the substance is not in the European, Japanese or International
Pharmacopeias (S01). Without a monograph there is no official identity, purity or quality
specification that anyone can be held to.

**The name does not identify the substance.** FDA has encountered multiple salts, derivatives and
even different active molecules sold commercially under the common name BPC-157, and describes
inconsistent naming as a safety risk (S01). Separately, TB-500 and thymosin beta-4 are used
interchangeably in commercial material despite being different molecules (S15). A blend of two
ambiguously named ingredients carries both problems at once.

**Certificates of analysis in this market do not control what matters.** FDA states that most
certificates for BPC-157 contain purity testing only, with no impurity limits or results
demonstrating control of the impurity profile, and found the same pattern in certificates published
by commercial peptide sellers (S01). A purity percentage says nothing about what the remainder is.

**Impurities of toxicological concern have not been addressed.** FDA notes that likely impurities of
concern include nitrosamines and potential mutagenic and teratogenic substances, and that available
data do not address these for BPC-157. Solid phase peptide synthesis also introduces residual
solvents, coupling reagents, activators, catalysts, scavengers, and isomeric and deletion peptide
impurities (S01). For TB-500, a non natural synthesis impurity was detectable in equine plasma after
administration of a marketed product (S15).

**Peptides are fragile, and blends compound the problem.** FDA states peptides such as BPC-157 can be
extremely sensitive to formulation, process and environmental conditions, which may lead to
aggregation and degradation, and that significant amounts of aggregates can form especially during
storage or under stress conditions. Aggregation is a driver of FDA's immunogenicity concern (S01).
Two peptides sharing one container introduce questions about physical and chemical compatibility, the
stability of each in the presence of the other, and whether the mixture stays uniform. None of those
questions has been answered for this product.

**No characterisation data were available.** FDA found no particle size, microbial quality, bacterial
endotoxin, sterility, aggregate or container closure data for the proposed BPC-157 product forms, and
concluded both forms are not well characterised from a physical and chemical characterisation
perspective (S01).

**The supply channel is a documented problem.** Counterfeit and illegal peptide preparations are a
recognised issue, and a 2015 analytical study developed screening methods across 25 peptides already
seized in Europe or identified in underground forums (S21). The retrieved record does not state
whether TB-500 or thymosin beta-4 was among those 25, and reports no mislabelling rate, so no
percentage may be attached to either component. Separately, the United States Department of Defense
states that products are often mislabelled not for human consumption or research use only while being
marketed for human use (S11).

**No analytical study of a marketed BPC-157 and TB-500 blend was located.** Nobody appears to have
tested what is actually in these products. That is an absence of information, not a favourable
result.

## Safety categories

The honest position for both components is that safety is uncharacterised. That is a stronger and
more uncomfortable statement than unproven, and it is FDA's own.

**Nothing in this section describes the combination, because the combination has not been studied.**

### The starting point

FDA's stated conclusion for BPC-157 is that there is insufficient clinical safety information to
characterise the safety profile of BPC-157 free base and BPC-157 acetate, that nonclinical
toxicology studies were too limited in scope and duration to inform safety considerations, and that
there is no information to assess human pharmacokinetics beyond a small set of studies using a rectal
route (S01). For TB-500, the first registered human safety study is still recruiting (S23).

### Categories of concern

**Immunogenicity.** FDA's central flagged risk for BPC-157. FDA states that as a 15 amino acid peptide
given by certain routes it may pose a significant risk for an immune response against the peptide,
potentially amplified by aggregation and by peptide related impurities, and that FDA is not aware of
information suggesting BPC-157 does not present these risks (S01, S02). This is a mechanistic and
theoretical concern raised by a regulator, not an observed clinical outcome. No human immunogenicity
testing was identified for either component.

**Reactions reported to FDA.** Three reports in total for BPC-157, all concerning products given by
injection: an injection site reaction confounded by concurrent thymosin, an episode of shortness of
breath resulting in an emergency room visit with no further information provided, and the
hyperpigmentation and gum darkening case involving a product containing both BPC-157 and TB500 (S01).
Three reports is a very small number and must not be read either as reassurance or as a rate.

**The unstudied long term.** Total documented human exposure across all identified BPC-157 studies is
on the order of dozens of people for days to two weeks. For TB-500 there is no completed human
exposure record at all. Uncommon and delayed harms could not have been detected by anything in this
record.

**Cancer related questions.** A pro-angiogenic mechanism, meaning promotion of new blood vessel
growth, is proposed for TB-500. That raises an obvious question about tumour blood supply. No source
retrieved for this Guide evaluated that question in either direction. It is recorded as an open
question, not as a finding and not as a reassurance.

**Carcinogenicity, reproductive and developmental effects, and genetic toxicity.** Unassessed. FDA
states nonclinical toxicology for BPC-157 is too limited in scope and duration, and no equivalent
data was retrieved for TB-500 (S01).

**Unstudied populations.** Behaviour is entirely unstudied for both components and for the
combination in pregnancy, breastfeeding, children, older adults, immunocompromised people, people
with a current or previous cancer, and people taking any regular medicine.

**Product related risk, separate from the molecules.** Because neither component is approved, material
is obtained outside a regulated supply chain, where identity, purity, sterility, endotoxin content and
contamination are unverified. This risk exists regardless of what the molecules themselves do.

**Anti-doping consequence.** Concrete and documented rather than theoretical, and covered under
regulatory status below.

Nothing in this section should be read as a list of expected harms. It is a list of questions nobody
has answered.

## Interactions

**No interaction data exists for either component, and none exists for the pair.**

This section is short because the record is empty, and the emptiness has a specific shape worth
naming.

The most obvious interaction question for this product is the one between its own two ingredients.
Nobody has studied whether BPC-157 changes how TB-500 is absorbed, broken down or cleared, or the
reverse. Nobody has studied whether they interfere with each other, whether they add together, or
whether they are simply independent. That question is not merely unanswered. It has not been asked in
any study located for this Guide.

Beyond that, no study of either component's interaction with any medicine, supplement or other
substance was located. That includes anticoagulants, immune modulating medicines, cancer treatments,
and everything else. Unstudied does not mean absent. It means nobody has looked.

Two specific reasons for caution in conversation with a qualified professional, both flagged in this
Guide rather than inferred:

- The pro-angiogenic mechanism proposed for TB-500 raises unanswered questions for anyone with a
  current or previous cancer.
- FDA's immunogenicity concern for BPC-157 raises unanswered questions for anyone with an autoimmune
  condition or taking immune modulating treatment.

Neither of these is a finding. Both are reasons the conversation belongs with a clinician who knows
the person's history.

## Regulatory status

**Status checked 2026-07-21. Full detail, with jurisdictions, dates and sources, is in
REGULATORY_STATUS.md.** Regulatory positions change, and one item below concerns a review that had
not taken place when this Guide was checked. Every statement here carries a jurisdiction, a date and
a source, and any statement missing one of those three does not belong on a Xenios surface.

### United States, BPC-157

**Not approved.** FDA's briefing document for the Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee meeting of
23 to 24 July 2026 states that neither BPC-157 free base nor BPC-157 acetate is a component of an
FDA approved drug, and that there is no approved product containing BPC-157 related substances in any
country at this time, nor is either form found in the European, Japanese or International
Pharmacopeias. Checked 2026-07-21, sourced to S01.

**Not eligible for 503A compounding.** FDA has stated in at least two enforcement actions that
products compounded with BPC-157 are not eligible for the 503A exemptions, because the substance is
not the subject of an applicable monograph, is not a component of an FDA approved human drug, and
does not appear on the 503A bulks list. Those actions are a warning letter dated 2020-04-01 and an
untitled letter dated 2023-09-14. Checked 2026-07-21, sourced to S04 and S05.

**Currently under evaluation, with no outcome.** Both nominations were withdrawn and FDA is
evaluating BPC-157 free base and BPC-157 acetate on its own initiative. An FDA review memo dated
2026-05-11 was placed before the advisory committee meeting of 23 to 24 July 2026, and FDA's stated
recommendation was that the characterisation, historical use, lack of evidence of effectiveness and
safety information weigh against adding either to the 503A bulks list. **That was a proposal pending
committee input, not a final determination, and the meeting had not occurred when this Guide was
checked.** No outcome is stated anywhere in this Guide. Checked 2026-07-21, sourced to S01 and S03.

A widely repeated claim that BPC-157 has been in Category 2 of the FDA list since September 2023
could not be verified from any FDA source, and FDA's live page now lists it under substances
nominated but withdrawn. The practical consequence stated above is unaffected. Checked 2026-07-21,
sourced to S02.

### United States, TB-500

**No approved product was located,** and the only registered human study is an ongoing Phase 1/2
safety study, which is inconsistent with any approved status. Checked 2026-07-21, sourced to S22 and
S23.

**Compounding status: not established by this Guide.** Every fda.gov page attempted during the TB-500
research returned an error. Conflicting claims circulate in commercial material about Category 2
placement and about removal in 2026, and none could be verified. **This Guide states no position, and
TB-500's status must not be inferred from BPC-157's.** Checked 2026-07-21, gap recorded at S25.

### United States, other federal position

The Department of Defense, through its Operation Supplement Safety programme, states in an article
dated 2025-04-29 that BPC-157 is an unapproved drug that cannot be legally prescribed or sold over
the counter, is not a lawful dietary ingredient, appears on the Department's prohibited dietary
supplement ingredients list and on the World Anti-Doping Agency list under non-approved substances,
and that there is little to no reliable scientific evidence to support the safety or effectiveness of
BPC-157 in humans. Checked 2026-07-21, sourced to S11.

### Anti-doping

**Both components appear to be prohibited, and an athlete should treat this product as prohibited.**

For BPC-157, the United States Anti-Doping Agency states that it is prohibited under the S0
unapproved substances category. The page retrieved did not state the list year or whether the
prohibition is in competition only. Checked 2026-07-21, sourced to S12.

For TB-500, the United States Anti-Doping Agency's summary of changes to the 2018 Prohibited List
states that thymosin-B4 and its derivatives, for example TB-500, were added as examples of prohibited
growth factors under section S2.3. Checked 2026-07-21, sourced to S24.

**The World Anti-Doping Agency list itself was not retrieved in either research session**, so the
current edition's section numbering, scope and wording are unconfirmed, and the two components are
described under different sections by the two sources above. Validated detection methods exist for
TB-500 and its metabolites, with one metabolite detectable up to 72 hours in rats, so this is a
concrete testing risk rather than a theoretical one. Checked 2026-07-21, sourced to S15 and S16.

Any athlete subject to testing should consult their own anti-doping authority directly rather than
rely on this Guide.

### New Zealand

FDA's briefing document quotes the New Zealand regulator Medsafe stating that there are currently no
approved products or pending applications for medicines containing BPC-157 in New Zealand. This is a
second hand citation within an FDA document rather than a retrieved Medsafe record. Checked
2026-07-21, sourced to S01.

## Questions for a qualified professional

If someone is considering this product, or has been offered it, these are questions worth taking to a
clinician who knows their history. They are written to be usable as they stand.

**About the evidence**

1. Are you aware of any study that tested these two peptides together, rather than separately?
2. Given that the only randomised trial of BPC-157 did not separate from placebo, and TB-500's first
   human study is still recruiting, what would you say is actually known here?
3. When a source cites thymosin beta-4 trials in support of TB-500, does that change how you read the
   evidence?

**About me specifically**

4. Does anything in my medical history make an uncharacterised peptide a worse idea than it would be
   for someone else?
5. I have a current or previous cancer, or a family history of one. Does a proposed pro-angiogenic
   mechanism matter for me?
6. I have an autoimmune condition, or take an immune modulating treatment. Does the immunogenicity
   concern raised by FDA matter for me?
7. Am I pregnant, planning a pregnancy, or breastfeeding? What is known about exposure in those
   circumstances?
8. Could this interact with anything I currently take? Is there any way to know?

**About the specific product**

9. What exactly is in this container, and in what proportion? Is that documented anywhere?
10. Is there a batch specific certificate of analysis from a named accredited laboratory, and can it
    be verified with the laboratory directly?
11. Does that certificate cover each ingredient separately, and does it show what else is in the
    material rather than only a purity percentage?
12. Has anyone confirmed that the two ingredients are stable together and evenly distributed in the
    container?
13. Who made this, where, and to what quality standard?

**About my situation**

14. What are the established options for what I am actually trying to address, and how do they
    compare with something that has not been studied?
15. I am subject to anti-doping testing. What is the actual risk here?
16. If I experienced a reaction, what should I do, and who would I report it to?

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides. Any
question about amounts, timing or preparation belongs with a qualified professional, not with this
Guide.
