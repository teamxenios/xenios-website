# BPC-157 + TB-500: questions members actually ask

Plain answers to the questions this product raises. Every answer here is consistent with
`GUIDE_DRAFT.md` and `CLAIM_TABLE.md`, and nothing appears here that is not supported there.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## 1. Does this combination work?

Nobody knows, because nobody has tested it.

That is the whole answer, and it is worth sitting with rather than moving past. No study of any
design has given BPC-157 and TB-500 together and measured what happened. Not a randomised trial, not
an open label study, not a case series, not even a registered trial that has yet to report.

The temptation at this point is to reason from the ingredients: BPC-157 has been studied, TB-500 has
been studied, so something must be known. That reasoning does not hold. Two compounds given together
are a different subject from either one alone, with their own absorption, their own interaction
behaviour, and their own risks. If the pair has not been studied, there is nothing to know about the
pair.

It is also worth noticing what would be inherited even if inheritance worked. BPC-157's human record
is five small studies, four of which exist only as summaries inside a regulatory document, and the
only randomised trial among them did not beat placebo. TB-500's human record is one study that
started in February 2026 and has not reported. There is not much there to borrow.

---

## 2. Is it safe?

Unknown, in both directions. That is a different statement from "it is safe" and a different
statement from "it is dangerous".

FDA's own conclusion for BPC-157 is that there is insufficient clinical safety information to
characterise the safety profile, and that the animal toxicology work is too limited in scope and
duration to help. For TB-500, the first human safety study is still recruiting, which tells you where
the field is.

Two things people commonly read as reassurance, which are not:

**"The studies reported no side effects."** Several of the small BPC-157 studies did report that. FDA
also states those studies were short, small, provided limited safety information, and that safety
monitoring for most of them is unclear. One of them had two participants. A study that could not have
detected a problem has not shown there isn't one.

**"There are hardly any adverse event reports."** There are three in FDA's system for BPC-157, in
total. FDA also notes that the pharmacies compounding these products generally do not report adverse
events, so the number reflects reporting behaviour rather than how often anything happens.

And there is one adverse event report that concerns this exact pairing. See question 4.

---

## 3. If both ingredients are studied for tissue repair, doesn't combining them make sense?

It might. It might not. Nothing establishes either.

This is the most reasonable sounding assumption about blends and the one most worth resisting. When
two substances are combined, several things can happen. They can add together. One can interfere with
how the other is absorbed or cleared. They can do nothing to each other. They can produce an effect
neither shows alone, helpful or otherwise. Which of these occurs is a question you answer by running
a study, not by reading the ingredient list.

For this pair, that study does not exist. So a claim that the two are synergistic, or that they cover
different phases of healing, or that they are commonly stacked for good reason, is not a weakly
supported claim. It is an unsupported one, presented in the grammar of a supported one.

Note also that the mechanism story for one ingredient is itself unsettled. One preclinical study
found that the parent TB-500 molecule showed no wound healing activity in fibroblast assays while one
of its breakdown products did, and concluded that the activity attributed to TB-500 may belong to the
metabolite instead. Reasoning about how two ingredients interact requires knowing what each one does,
and that is not established here.

---

## 4. Is there any human data at all on the two together?

Yes, one record, and it is an adverse event report rather than a study.

FDA searched its adverse event reporting system through December 2025 and found three reports for
BPC-157. One described a 40 year old woman who developed widespread darkening of the skin and
darkening of the gums while using a product containing both BPC-157 and TB500. The same reactions
appeared again when she took the product again, and resolved when she stopped.

FDA judged the reaction likely due to the product, precisely because it reappeared on rechallenge.
And then FDA wrote the sentence that explains why blends are harder to interpret than single
ingredients: because the product contained two peptides, it is not possible to assess a relationship
to either one.

Read that carefully. The strongest signal in the record that the product was responsible sits
alongside a permanent inability to say which ingredient did it. That is not a quirk of this case. It
is what a two ingredient product does to attribution.

Three caveats belong with it. A single report is not a rate. It does not prove causation. And
underreporting in this market is expected. It is still the only human data point specific to this
pairing, and it is a harm signal rather than a benefit one.

## 5. What exactly is in the product, and in what ratio?

Not confirmed. This is the first open question for any supplier.

Nothing in the research behind this Guide establishes the composition or the proportion of either
ingredient in any product sold as a BPC-157 and TB-500 blend. No ratio is stated anywhere in this
Guide, and any ratio encountered elsewhere should be treated as an unverified supplier assertion
until documented.

This matters more than it might seem. Without knowing what is in the container and in what
proportion, no statement about the product is possible at all, and no read across from any single
ingredient study could be attempted even in principle.

There is a further identity problem underneath it. FDA states that the name BPC-157 is used
commercially for multiple salts, derivatives and even different active molecules, and describes that
inconsistent naming as a safety risk. Separately, vendors use TB-500 and thymosin beta-4
interchangeably despite these being different molecules. A blend of two ambiguously named ingredients
carries both problems at once.

## 6. Vendors cite clinical trials for TB-500. Are those real?

The trials are real. They studied a different molecule.

TB-500 is a synthetic seven amino acid fragment, Ac-LKKTETQ, corresponding to a small section of a
larger protein called thymosin beta-4. Thymosin beta-4 itself is 43 amino acids. These are different
molecules, and the research record for the full length protein does not transfer to the fragment.

The trials usually cited are a Phase 2 dry eye trial in nine patients, a Phase 3 corneal condition
trial in 18 subjects that was terminated early and did not reach significance at its primary
timepoint, and a venous ulcer study. All studied the full length protein, generally applied topically
to an eye or wound surface.

The venous ulcer study deserves a specific mention. It enrolled 21 of a planned 72 patients and, in
the record retrieved for this Guide, reports no outcome data at all. It is nonetheless cited
secondhand as though it demonstrated accelerated healing. It did not.

So when a page cites clinical trials in support of a TB-500 product, the honest reading is usually
that it is citing trials of something else. And when that page is selling a two ingredient blend, it
is making two substitutions at once: a different molecule standing in for TB-500, and single
ingredient data standing in for combination data.

## 7. There are hundreds of studies on BPC-157. Doesn't that count for something?

It counts for exactly what it is, which is a large preclinical literature.

The volume is real. Rodent and cell culture work on BPC-157 reports protective and healing effects
across many tissues. That is genuinely a body of research and this Guide does not dismiss it.

What it is not is clinical evidence. FDA searched PubMed, Embase, Cochrane and ClinicalTrials.gov and
identified five human studies in the entire published literature. A PubMed search restricted to
systematic reviews, randomised controlled trials and clinical trials returns zero records. An
independent 2025 review counted three human pilot studies in the musculoskeletal context and
concluded BPC-157 should be considered investigational.

FDA's assessment of the preclinical work is also worth knowing, because it is unusually direct: the
molecular targets have not been identified, the mechanisms remain poorly understood, and dose
response relationships could not be established, because widely separated amounts produced comparable
effects. That last point is a reason to look harder at the findings, not a reason for confidence.

Large preclinical volume with near zero clinical translation is a describable state of a field. It is
not a hidden strength.

## 8. Is it legal?

The full answer, with jurisdictions, dates and sources, is in `REGULATORY_STATUS.md`. In short, as
checked on 2026-07-21:

**BPC-157.** Not an approved drug. FDA states there is no approved product containing BPC-157 related
substances in any country, and it is absent from the United States, European, Japanese and
International Pharmacopeias. FDA has stated in at least two enforcement actions that compounding with
it does not qualify for the 503A exemptions. FDA is currently evaluating whether to add it to the
503A bulk substances list, staff recommended against, and the advisory committee meeting had not
occurred when this Guide was checked. No outcome is stated here, and anyone who reads an outcome
elsewhere should check the primary record rather than a summary of it.

**TB-500.** No approved product was located. Its FDA compounding status could not be established at
all, because every FDA page attempted during research returned an error. This Guide states no
position on that, and TB-500's status should not be assumed to match BPC-157's.

**In sport, treat this as prohibited.** The United States Anti-Doping Agency states BPC-157 is
prohibited under the unapproved substances category, and its summary of the 2018 Prohibited List
changes states that thymosin beta-4 and its derivatives, for example TB-500, were added as prohibited
growth factors. Validated tests exist for TB-500 and its breakdown products, one of which was
detectable up to 72 hours in rats. This is a concrete testing risk. Any athlete subject to testing
should ask their own anti-doping authority directly.

Legal status is not a statement about whether something works or whether it is safe. Those are three
separate questions.

## 10. If the science is thin, what about the product itself? Is that a separate problem?

Yes, and it is a real one, independent of whether the compounds do anything.

Neither ingredient has an official published standard to be tested against. There is no United States
Pharmacopeia or National Formulary monograph for either BPC-157 form and no dietary supplement
monograph, and neither appears in the European, Japanese or International Pharmacopeias. Without a
monograph, there is no official specification anyone can be held to.

FDA also examined certificates of analysis in this market and found that most for BPC-157 contain
purity testing only, with no impurity limits or results showing control of what else is in the
material. A purity percentage tells you how much of the intended substance is present. It says
nothing about what the remainder is. FDA separately notes that impurities of concern for a substance
like this include nitrosamines and potential mutagenic and teratogenic compounds, and that available
data do not address these.

Blends add requirements rather than sharing them. A supplier would need to show identity and content
for each ingredient separately, plus evidence that the mixture is uniform, that each ingredient is
stable in the presence of the other, and that what is in one container matches what is in the next.
None of that has been shown here. No analytical study of a marketed BPC-157 and TB-500 blend was
located at all. Nobody appears to have checked what is in these products.

`QUALITY_AND_DOCUMENTATION.md` sets out the full standard. Every item on it is currently unmet.

## 11. What would change this Guide?

Specific things, and they are worth naming so that this position can be seen as revisable rather than
fixed.

- **A study of the combination.** Any study, of any design, giving both compounds together and
  measuring an outcome. That would create combination evidence where there is currently none.
- **Results from the ongoing trials.** The BPC-157 Phase 2 hamstring trial has estimated completion in
  February 2028. The TB-500 Phase 1/2 study is recruiting. Both would be single ingredient evidence,
  not combination evidence, but both would be real.
- **Resolution of the metabolite question.** A dedicated pharmacology study establishing whether
  TB-500 itself or its breakdown products carry the proposed activity.
- **A confirmed composition for this product**, documented rather than asserted.
- **An analytical study of marketed blends**, testing what is actually in them.
- **A clear regulatory determination**, particularly the outcome of the FDA review of BPC-157 and any
  retrievable position on TB-500.

If those arrive, this Guide changes and the change is recorded in the correction history with a date.
Nothing here is a position held for its own sake.

## 12. So what should I do with all this?

That is a decision for a person and their clinician, and this Guide does not make it.

What it can offer is an accurate summary to bring to that conversation. This product combines two
unapproved compounds. The combination has never been studied. One ingredient's human record is five
small studies whose strongest design did not beat placebo. The other ingredient's human record is one
study that started this year and has not reported. Much of the evidence circulating in support of the
second ingredient belongs to a different molecule. Safety is uncharacterised for both, and there is
one adverse event report specific to this pairing in which nobody can say which ingredient was
responsible. What is in any given container is undocumented. Both components appear to be prohibited
in sport.

The questions in the final section of `GUIDE_DRAFT.md` are written to be taken to a qualified
professional as they stand.
