# BPC-157 + TB-500 Contradictions and Divergences

Where the sources disagree, and what the Guide says when they do. Source ids refer to
SOURCE_REGISTRY.md. Claim ids refer to CLAIM_TABLE.md.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

Some of these are genuine scientific disagreements. Several are not disagreements at all, but
errors that circulate widely enough to need answering by name. Both kinds are recorded, and each
entry says which it is.

---

## C-01. Whether a blend inherits its ingredients' evidence

**This is the foundational disagreement, and it is not a scientific one.**

**Position A.** Commercial material for this pairing presents the two components' research records
side by side, then describes the blend as supported by that combined body of work. The reasoning is
implicit rather than stated: BPC-157 has been studied, TB-500 has been studied, therefore the blend
rests on both.

**Position B.** A mixture is a separate subject of study. Two compounds given together can absorb
differently, persist differently, and act on each other in ways neither shows alone. None of that
can be predicted from separate records, and none of it has been measured here, because no retrieved
source administered BPC-157 and TB-500 together and measured an outcome.

**Assessment.** Position B, and this is not a matter of degree. Position A does not produce weak
evidence for the blend. It produces no evidence for the blend, while creating the impression of a
great deal. The impression is the harm: a reader who sees two research summaries under one product
name reasonably concludes the product has been researched.

There is a further problem specific to this pair. Neither component has a human evidence base worth
inheriting in the first place. BPC-157's amounts to five small studies, four of them known only
through a regulator's summary, with the only randomised trial among them failing to separate from
placebo. TB-500's is a single recruiting study with no results. Adding two thin records together
does not produce a thick one.

**What the Guide says.** No study of the combination exists. See BT-C-01, BT-C-02, and the
prohibitions at BT-X-01 and BT-X-02.

## C-02. Whether the combination has any human record at all

**Position A.** Nothing whatsoever is known about the pair in humans.

**Position B.** One human record exists, and it is an adverse event report. FDA's adverse event
system returned three reports for BPC-157 in total, one of which concerned a product containing
BPC-157 and TB500 in a 40 year old woman who developed diffuse hyperpigmentation and gingival
darkening, with identical reproducible reactions on rechallenge and resolution on discontinuation
(S01).

**Assessment.** Position B is more precise and the Guide uses it. The distinction matters because
Position A, stated flatly, would omit the only pair specific data point that exists, and it happens
to be a harm signal rather than a neutral blank.

Two things must travel with that report every time. First, FDA's own reading: the rechallenge
pattern makes the product a likely cause, but because the product contained two peptides, no
relationship to either individual peptide can be assessed. That is the blend problem appearing in a
real case rather than in the abstract. Second, a single spontaneous report is not a rate, does not
establish causation, and cannot be turned into an expected outcome. FDA also notes that 503A
compounders generally do not report adverse events, so underreporting is expected.

**What the Guide says.** BT-C-03. Present it as the only pair specific human record, describe what
it is, and do not inflate it into a frequency.

## C-03. Whether TB-500 and thymosin beta-4 are the same thing

**Not a genuine controversy. A widespread error.**

**Position A.** Vendor and secondary material uses TB-500 and thymosin beta-4 interchangeably, and
cites human trials of thymosin beta-4 as evidence for TB-500.

**Position B.** They are chemically different molecules. TB-500 is a synthetic, N-terminally
acetylated seven amino acid peptide, Ac-LKKTETQ, corresponding to residues 17 to 23 of thymosin
beta-4 (S15, S23). Thymosin beta-4 is the full length 43 amino acid protein. RGN-259 and NL005 are
investigational products formulated from the full length protein and are different again.

**Assessment.** Position B is correct and the TB-500 research record identifies this as the highest
priority accuracy issue in the entire evidence base. Every human trial commonly cited in support of
TB-500 studied the full length protein or a formulated product made from it, usually delivered
topically to an eye surface or a wound surface, in samples ranging from nine to a few dozen
patients (S18, S19, S20). One of the most frequently cited of those, a venous ulcer study, reports
no outcome data at all in the retrieved record, despite being cited secondhand as though it
demonstrated accelerated healing (S20).

The read across is not merely unproven. It is challenged from the preclinical side as well, which
is C-04.

Note the compounding effect in a blend context. Substituting parent protein data for fragment data
is one category error. Then treating that substituted data as evidence for a two ingredient mixture
is a second. Marketing copy for this product routinely makes both in the same paragraph.

**What the Guide says.** BT-I-04 appears before any discussion of evidence. Section 5 of the claim
table lists the adjacent molecule trials explicitly so a reader recognises them on sight. See the
prohibition at BT-X-04.

## C-04. Whether TB-500 itself is the biologically active molecule

**A genuine and unresolved scientific disagreement.**

**Position A.** TB-500 is the active fragment of thymosin beta-4 and directly drives actin binding,
cell migration, angiogenesis and wound healing. This is the standard framing, reflected in the
analytical literature's description of LKKTETQ as the active site within thymosin beta-4 (S15) and
in a 2026 review's summary of preclinical models (S17).

**Position B.** In fibroblast wound healing assays, the metabolite Ac-LKKTE showed significant
activity while the parent TB-500 compound did not. The authors concluded that previously reported
wound healing activity of TB-500 in the literature may be due to its metabolite rather than the
parent form (S16).

**Assessment.** Unresolved, and important enough to surface rather than smooth over. This is a
single in vitro result inside an analytical chemistry paper, not a dedicated pharmacology study, so
it should not be presented as settled. But it directly undercuts the simplest version of the TB-500
story, and it means that preclinical results obtained with TB-500 may reflect metabolite activity,
which complicates any attempt to reason from animal work toward humans.

For a blend Guide there is an additional consequence. If it is unclear which molecule carries the
activity attributed to one ingredient, then reasoning about how that ingredient interacts with a
second ingredient is speculation resting on speculation.

**What the Guide says.** BT-T-P-01 and BT-T-P-03, with mechanism labelled proposed throughout and
this disagreement stated alongside it.

## C-05. Strength of the BPC-157 evidence base as portrayed by enthusiasts versus by regulators and reviewers

**Not a genuine scientific controversy. A framing gap.**

**Position A.** Marketing and enthusiast sources present BPC-157 as a broadly effective healing
peptide, citing a large volume of published studies.

**Position B.** The volume is almost entirely rodent and in vitro work, much of it from a narrow set
of research groups. FDA identified five human studies in the entire published literature, four of
which are meeting abstracts or tiny uncontrolled clinic series (S01). A 2025 peer reviewed narrative
review independently counted three human pilot studies in the musculoskeletal context and concluded
BPC-157 should be considered investigational (S09). A PubMed search restricted to systematic
reviews, randomised controlled trials and clinical trials returned zero records (S14).

**Assessment.** Position B. The preclinical volume is real, and the Guide says so plainly. Its
translation into human clinical evidence has not happened. FDA's assessment of that preclinical
literature is unusually pointed: molecular targets are unidentified, mechanisms are poorly
understood, and dose response relationships could not be established because widely separated
amounts produced comparable effects, which is itself an anomaly (S01).

**What the Guide says.** The preclinical and human distinction is made explicit on every claim. See
BT-B-H-01, BT-B-H-02, BT-B-P-03 and the prohibition at BT-X-09.

## C-06. Whether BPC-157 is currently Category 2 on the FDA 503A list, and since when

**A live factual dispute about the record.**

**Position A.** Numerous secondary sources, including law firm bulletins and industry blogs, state
that FDA placed BPC-157 in Category 2 in September 2023 and that this is its current status.

**Position B.** FDA's own page, content current as of 2026-04-22, lists BPC-157 not in the Category 2
table but in a separate table headed bulk drug substances nominated but withdrawn, described as
substances previously in category 2 of the interim policies that were withdrawn by the nominators
(S02). FDA's July 2026 briefing document confirms the nominations were withdrawn and that FDA is now
evaluating the substances on its own initiative (S01).

**Assessment.** The practical consequence is the same either way, and the Guide states that
consequence rather than the disputed framing: BPC-157 is not on the 503A bulks list, has no USP or
NF monograph, and is not a component of an FDA approved drug, so compounding with it does not
qualify for the 503A exemptions. Two separate FDA enforcement letters confirm this (S04, S05).

The specific Category 2 since September 2023 framing must not be repeated as current fact. The date
could not be verified from any FDA source, and FDA's own page now presents the position differently.

**What the Guide says.** The consequence, dated and sourced. See BT-X-13.

## C-07. Direction of United States federal policy on these compounds

**Two different kinds of statement, not two readings of one body of evidence.**

**Position A.** FDA staff, in a review memo dated 2026-05-11 placed before the advisory committee
meeting of July 23 to 24, 2026, recommended against adding BPC-157 free base and BPC-157 acetate to
the 503A bulks list, citing physicochemical characterisation, historical use, lack of evidence of
effectiveness, and safety information (S01, S03).

**Position B.** Journalism dated 2026-02-03 reports that HHS leadership has signalled openness to
loosening restrictions on unapproved peptides, and that FDA leadership responses in December 2025
were open yet non-committal, describing a plan to review peptides to determine which are harmless
(S13).

**Assessment.** These are a scientific review recommendation and a political signal. They are not in
conflict on the evidence, and neither one is a change in legal status. The retrieved journalism
provides no evidence that any classification was formally reversed. A web search summary asserting
that HHS announced plans to reverse the classification in February 2026 is not supported by the
article actually retrieved, and is excluded by name at BT-X-11.

Two handling rules follow. First, every regulatory statement in this folder carries the date it was
checked, because this position may move. Second, the outcome of the advisory committee meeting was
not available as of the checks behind this folder, and no outcome is stated anywhere. A staff
recommendation is a proposal, and a committee recommendation is advisory rather than a final agency
determination.

**What the Guide says.** REGULATORY_STATUS.md, with dates. See BT-X-10 and BT-X-11.

## C-08. The FDA compounding status of TB-500 and thymosin beta-4

**Unresolved, and unresolvable from this record.**

**Position A.** Thymosin beta-4 and TB-500 sit in Category 2 of the FDA interim 503A bulks list and
are barred from compounding.

**Position B.** They were removed from Category 2 in April 2026 after nominators withdrew, and were
scheduled for advisory committee review in July 2026.

**Assessment.** Neither position may be stated. Both came only from commercial or vendor adjacent
sources. Every fda.gov URL attempted during the TB-500 research session returned HTTP 404, and the
Federal Register record retrieved for the April 2026 advisory committee meeting notice names no
substances at all (S25). A specific FDA warning letter to GenoGenix LLC concerning thymosin beta-4
appeared in search results and could not be opened, so its content is recorded nowhere.

Note the asymmetry, and note that it is a property of the research sessions rather than of the
compounds. FDA sources were reachable for BPC-157 and unreachable for TB-500. The two halves of this
Guide are therefore not equally verified. It would be a mistake to infer TB-500's status from
BPC-157's simply because BPC-157's was easier to check.

**What the Guide says.** Nothing about TB-500's compounding status, and an explicit statement that
this is a gap. See BT-U-15 and the prohibition at BT-X-12.

## C-09. Which anti-doping section applies, and to which component

**A verifiable inconsistency between two retrieved sources.**

**Position A.** USADA states that BPC-157 is prohibited under the S0 unapproved substances category
(S12).

**Position B.** USADA's summary of the 2018 Prohibited List changes states that thymosin-B4 and its
derivatives, for example TB-500, were added as examples of prohibited growth factors under S2.3
(S24).

**Assessment.** These are not in conflict. They describe two different substances and are both
plausibly correct, since the two components could be captured by different sections. The point for a
reviewer is that this Guide covers a product containing both, so both statements must be checked,
and neither should be generalised to the other component.

What could not be verified in either research session is the current wording. The WADA Prohibited
List document itself returned empty content or was not retrieved on every attempt, so the 2026
edition's section numbering, the scope of prohibition, and whether each component is named
explicitly or captured by a class definition are all unconfirmed. Vendor claims that BPC-157 is
additionally named under S2 were not used and remain unverified.

**What the Guide says.** That both components appear to be prohibited in sport, attributed to USADA
and dated, with the section numbering flagged as unconfirmed, and with athletes directed to their
own anti-doping authority. See BT-T-S-01 and BT-X-19.

## C-10. Whether an absence of reported harm is a favourable safety finding

**Not a disagreement between sources. A disagreement between the record and how the record is read.**

**Position A.** Several of the small BPC-157 human studies report no adverse events, and only three
adverse event reports exist in FDA's system. Commercial material reads this as a clean safety
record.

**Position B.** FDA states directly that those studies were of short duration, had small sample
sizes, evaluated exploratory amounts, provided limited safety information, and that safety
monitoring for most of them is unclear. Total documented human exposure across all identified
BPC-157 studies is on the order of dozens of people for days to two weeks. One of the studies had
two participants. For TB-500, there is no completed human exposure record at all. FDA's conclusion
is that there is insufficient clinical safety information to characterise the safety profile
(S01, S23).

**Assessment.** Position B. A study that could not have detected a harm has not shown its absence.
Three adverse event reports in a system that compounders generally do not report into is a
statement about reporting, not about incidence.

This is the point at which the blend question becomes concrete rather than theoretical. Even if both
components had characterised safety profiles, which neither does, a mixture would still need its own,
because the relevant question is what happens when they are given together. The one report that does
concern the pair is the hyperpigmentation case, and its lesson is precisely that a two ingredient
product makes attribution impossible.

**What the Guide says.** Safety framed as uncharacterised in both directions, never as favourable.
See BT-B-S-01, BT-T-H-03, BT-B-H-10, and the prohibitions at BT-X-05 and BT-X-06.
