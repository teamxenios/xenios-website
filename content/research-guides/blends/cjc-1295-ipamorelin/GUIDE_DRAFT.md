# CJC-1295 and Ipamorelin (blend)

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## The rule this Guide is built on

This is a Guide about a mixture, and a mixture is not its ingredients.

**Knowing what each ingredient does separately does not tell you what the pair does together.** Put
two active compounds in one vial and you have created a new pharmacological object. It has its own
behaviour in the body, its own interaction profile, and its own list of questions nobody has answered.
Research conducted on one ingredient describes that ingredient. It cannot be lent to the combination,
no matter how carefully it was conducted.

So the question that governs this entire Guide is narrow and answerable. Has anyone studied THIS
combination?

Both component research records were searched in full for any study, in any species, of any design,
that gave CJC-1295 and ipamorelin together. **None exists.** Not one.

That means the evidence for this combination is not thin, not early, not preliminary, and not mixed.
There is none. Everything below about either ingredient is clearly labelled as being about that
ingredient alone, and none of it should be read as saying anything about the pair.

## One-minute summary

- **There is no evidence for the combination.** No study of CJC-1295 given together with ipamorelin
  was located, in people or in animals. The pairing is something the market assembled, not something
  research produced.
- **The composition of this product is not confirmed.** Xenios has not verified what is in it or in
  what proportion. No ratio appears anywhere in this Guide because none has been documented. This is
  the first question a supplier would have to answer.
- **The name "CJC-1295" covers two different molecules**, and this confusion is routine. One version
  carries an added group that binds to a blood protein and makes the compound persist far longer.
  Nearly all of the published human research was done on that version. The other version, more
  accurately called modified GRF(1-29), lacks that group, and a 2026 peer-reviewed review states it is
  essentially uncharacterised in the human literature with no controlled clinical studies evaluating
  it at all. A buyer cannot tell from the product name which one they have.
- **Both ingredients are growth hormone secretagogues**, meaning each is proposed to prompt the
  pituitary gland to release the body's own growth hormone rather than supplying growth hormone
  directly. They are proposed to do this through two different receptors. That difference is the
  stated reason for combining them. Whether combining them actually produces a larger effect, a
  redundant one, or an interaction nobody has characterised is unknown, because it has not been
  tested.
- **What the human research on each ingredient measured was hormone levels, not outcomes.** For
  CJC-1295, three human studies exist, all published between 2006 and 2009, all in healthy volunteers,
  all from one investigator group, and all on the longer-acting version. They establish that it raised
  growth hormone and IGF-1 for days. None measured body composition, strength, sleep, recovery, injury
  healing, or aging.
- **The one published human efficacy trial of ipamorelin failed.** It was run for postoperative ileus,
  a gut motility problem after bowel surgery, and it did not meet its primary endpoint. The larger
  follow-up trial of 320 participants completed in 2014 and has never reported results. Development
  was discontinued.
- **Neither ingredient is an approved drug anywhere identified.** The only registered CJC-1295 trial
  was terminated with no results posted. One United States advisory committee voted against making
  ipamorelin available for pharmacy compounding in October 2024.
- **On safety, the honest statement is not that this is safe.** There is no long-term safety data for
  either ingredient, and none at all for the two together. A 2026 review notes a mechanism-based
  question about sustained stimulation of this hormonal axis while stating explicitly that no
  established clinical carcinogenic signal exists.
- **A separate risk attaches to the supply channel rather than to the molecules.** Because neither is
  approved, material circulates outside a regulated supply chain. CJC-1295 has been identified by
  laboratory analysis in an unlabelled seized preparation of unknown origin.
- **Both ingredients are described in the anti-doping literature as prohibited in sport**, and
  ipamorelin is detectable in urine testing. A tested athlete taking this product would be taking two
  prohibited substances.

Xenios does not sell these compounds, does not supply them, and does not tell members how to obtain or
use them. This Guide exists so that a member reading about this blend elsewhere has an accurate
account of what is and is not known.

**Claim grade for "the combination is supported by evidence": G (Unverified), because no combination
study exists.**
**Claim grade for "CJC-1295 with the albumin-binding group raised growth hormone and IGF-1 in healthy
adults": C (Early human evidence), biomarker only, that ingredient alone.**

## What it is

This product is a blend, meaning two separate active compounds supplied together.

### The first ingredient: CJC-1295, which is two molecules under one name

CJC-1295 is a synthetic version of the first 29 amino acids of growth hormone-releasing hormone, a
natural hormone that signals the pituitary gland to release growth hormone. It was developed by
ConjuChem Biotechnologies. It acts at the growth hormone-releasing hormone receptor.

The important thing, and the thing most often confused, is that two chemically different products are
sold under this one name.

**CJC-1295 with DAC.** DAC stands for drug affinity complex, an added chemical group that binds to
albumin, a protein circulating in the blood. Binding to albumin makes the compound persist far longer
in the body. This is the version ConjuChem developed as a drug candidate, and its estimated half-life
in healthy adults was reported as 5.8 to 8.1 days. Essentially all published human data on
"CJC-1295" is data on this version.

**CJC-1295 without DAC**, more accurately called modified GRF(1-29), mod GRF 1-29, or tetrasubstituted
GRF(1-29). It has the amino acid substitutions but not the albumin-binding group, so it is
short-acting. A 2026 review in Frontiers in Endocrinology states plainly that this compound is
essentially uncharacterised in the peer-reviewed human literature and that no controlled clinical
studies have directly evaluated it in humans.

The consequence is direct and it is a safety point, not a naming quibble. Human pharmacokinetic and
safety findings for the longer-acting version do not transfer to the short-acting one, and the reverse
does not hold either. A vendor page, a forum post, or a Guide that cites the 2006 human trial beside a
"no DAC" product is applying data from one molecule to a different molecule. The two are also marketed
very differently, one as sustained elevation and one as a short pulse, so a buyer choosing between
them believes they are choosing between behaviours.

**A purchaser cannot determine which molecule they have from the product name alone.** Every vendor
page encountered during research used the two names interchangeably or as parenthetical synonyms.
Which version is in the Xenios product is not established. See the quality and identity section.

Compounds CJC-1295 is commonly confused with, whose evidence is not its evidence: sermorelin
(unmodified growth hormone-releasing hormone 1-29), tesamorelin (an approved analogue, a different
molecule), and CJC-1293.

### The second ingredient: ipamorelin

Ipamorelin is a synthetic pentapeptide, meaning a chain of five amino acids. It acts at a different
receptor, the growth hormone secretagogue receptor 1a, also called the ghrelin receptor, which makes it
a ghrelin mimetic. It was developed by Novo Nordisk in the late 1990s and characterised in the
peer-reviewed literature as the first selective growth hormone secretagogue.

It is not an approved drug in any jurisdiction identified during research. It is an investigational
compound whose development programme was discontinued.

Compounds ipamorelin is commonly confused with, whose evidence is not its evidence: GHRP-2, GHRP-6,
hexarelin, anamorelin, macimorelin, and ibutamoren, also written MK-677, which is not a peptide at all.

Neither ingredient is growth hormone itself, and neither is IGF-1.

### What makes this a blend rather than a compound

The two ingredients act at two different receptors. A product containing both contains two
pharmacologically active substances, each with its own effects, its own unknowns, and its own
regulatory position. The hyphenated product name can read as though it names a single thing. It does
not.

The exact composition and ingredient ratio of the Xenios product are **NOT CONFIRMED**. No supplier
documentation has been reviewed. No proportion is stated or implied anywhere in this Guide, and none
may be added until a document establishes it.

Reported chemical registry identifiers for ipamorelin, and the reported amino acid substitution
positions for modified GRF(1-29), appeared during research only in vendor material and were not

## Why interest exists

The interest is easy to describe and worth understanding without endorsing it.

Growth hormone declines with age, and it has real roles in tissue repair, body composition, and
metabolism. Supplying growth hormone directly is a regulated medical intervention with known
downsides. The appeal of a secretagogue is the idea of prompting the body to release its own growth
hormone in its own pattern instead, which sounds gentler and more physiological.

The appeal of the pairing specifically rests on the receptor difference. One ingredient acts at the
growth hormone-releasing hormone receptor and the other at the ghrelin receptor. The reasoning offered
is that pushing on two separate inputs to the same system should produce a bigger or steadier response
than pushing on either alone.

Three things belong alongside that interest.

**First, the receptor argument is a rationale, not a result.** It describes why someone thought
combining them was worth trying. It does not describe what happened when they were combined, because
in everything retrieved, nobody has published what happened. Two compounds converging on one hormonal
axis could add together, could overlap redundantly, could blunt each other, or could interact in a way
nobody has characterised. All four remain open.

**Second, raising a hormone is not an outcome.** The human research on both ingredients measured
hormone levels in blood. Whether moving that number changes anything a person would notice was not
measured by any human study retrieved for either compound.

**Third, there is a direct cautionary example inside this Guide.** The one occasion where a proposed
mechanism for either ingredient was actually put to a human test, the argument that ipamorelin's
ghrelin-mimetic action would help gut motility after surgery, produced a trial that did not meet its
primary endpoint. A coherent mechanism is a reason to run a study. It is not a substitute for one.

## Terminology

Every technical term used in this Guide, in plain English.

- **Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids, the building blocks of proteins. Shorter than a protein.
- **Amino acid.** A small molecule that links with others to form peptides and proteins.
- **Pentapeptide.** A peptide made of exactly five amino acids. Ipamorelin is one.
- **Blend.** A product containing more than one active compound supplied together.
- **Growth hormone (GH).** A hormone released by the pituitary gland with roles in growth, tissue
  repair, and metabolism.
- **IGF-1 (insulin-like growth factor 1).** A hormone produced largely in response to growth hormone.
  It is often measured as an indirect marker of growth hormone activity.
- **Pituitary gland.** A small gland at the base of the brain that releases growth hormone and other
  hormones.
- **Secretagogue.** A substance that prompts a gland to release something it already makes. A growth
  hormone secretagogue prompts release of the body's own growth hormone rather than supplying it.
- **GHRH (growth hormone-releasing hormone).** The body's own signal telling the pituitary to release
  growth hormone. CJC-1295 is a synthetic analogue of part of it.
- **Analogue.** A synthetic molecule built to resemble a natural one closely enough to act at the same
  receptor.
- **Receptor.** A structure on or in a cell that a signalling molecule binds to in order to produce an
  effect.
- **Agonist.** A substance that activates a receptor.
- **GHS-R1a (growth hormone secretagogue receptor 1a).** The receptor ipamorelin acts at, also called
  the ghrelin receptor. It is a different receptor from the one CJC-1295 acts at.
- **Ghrelin.** A hormone involved in hunger and growth hormone release. A ghrelin mimetic is a
  synthetic compound that acts at ghrelin's receptor.
- **DAC (drug affinity complex).** An added chemical group that binds to albumin in the blood, greatly
  extending how long a compound persists in the body.
- **Albumin.** The most abundant protein circulating in blood.
- **Modified GRF(1-29).** The name for the version of CJC-1295 that does not carry the
  albumin-binding group. Also written mod GRF 1-29 or tetrasubstituted GRF(1-29).
- **Half-life.** The time it takes for the amount of a substance in the body to fall by half. It
  describes how long something persists. It is not a dosing instruction.
- **Pharmacokinetics.** What the body does to a substance: absorption, distribution, metabolism, and
  elimination.
- **Pharmacodynamics.** What a substance does to the body, such as changing a hormone level.
- **Biomarker.** Something measurable in the body, such as a hormone level, used as an indirect signal.
  A change in a biomarker is not the same as a change a person would experience.
- **Endpoint.** The specific outcome a trial is designed to measure. A primary endpoint is the main
  one, agreed before the trial starts.
- **Primary endpoint missed.** The trial did not show a statistically significant benefit on the
  outcome it was designed to test.
- **Phase 1 trial.** An early human study, usually in healthy volunteers, focused on safety and how the
  body handles a compound.
- **Phase 2 trial.** A later study in people with the relevant condition, testing whether it works.
- **Proof-of-concept trial.** An early test of whether an idea works at all, usually small.
- **Placebo-controlled.** Some participants receive an inactive comparison so the compound's effect can
  be separated from expectation and natural change.
- **Double-blind.** Neither participants nor investigators know who received which, reducing bias.
- **Preclinical.** Research done before any human study, meaning in animals or in cultured cells.
- **In vitro.** Research done in cells or tissue in a dish, outside a living body.
- **Knockout mouse.** A mouse genetically engineered to lack a specific gene. It is a research tool,
  not a model of a healthy adult human.
- **Postoperative ileus.** A temporary loss of normal gut movement after abdominal surgery.
- **ACTH and cortisol.** Hormones involved in the stress response. Whether a secretagogue raises them
  is the basis of the selectivity question.
- **Prolactin.** A pituitary hormone. Also part of the selectivity question.
- **Selectivity.** Acting on the intended target without also acting on others.
- **Pulsatility.** The natural pattern in which growth hormone is released in bursts rather than
  steadily.
- **Immunogenicity.** Whether a substance provokes an immune response against itself.
- **Mitogenic.** Promoting cell division. A mechanism-based reason a growth-signalling pathway raises
  theoretical questions.
- **Narrative review.** A summary article written by experts. Useful, and a lower evidence grade than a
  systematic review, because it does not follow a pre-specified method for finding and weighing
  studies.
- **503A bulk drug substances list.** A United States framework governing which raw substances
  pharmacies may use in compounding.
- **Compounding.** Preparation of a customised medicine by a pharmacy, as distinct from manufacture of
  an approved drug product.
- **Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee.** The advisory committee that reviews substances nominated
  for that list. Its recommendations advise the agency and are not final agency decisions.
- **Federal Register.** The official daily journal of the United States government, where agency
  notices are published.
- **Certificate of analysis (COA).** A document from a testing laboratory stating what a specific batch
  of material was found to contain.
- **Assay.** A laboratory test measuring how much of a specific substance is present.
- **Blend uniformity.** Whether a mixture has the same composition throughout, rather than varying
  within a container or between containers.
- **Endotoxin.** A contaminant from bacterial cell walls that can cause fever and serious reactions in
  an injectable product, even when no live bacteria remain.

## Proposed mechanism

Everything in this section is **proposed**. The receptor pharmacology is genuine. The effect inference
built on it is not established, and the combination inference is not tested at all.

### For each ingredient separately

**CJC-1295** is a synthetic analogue of growth hormone-releasing hormone and acts at that hormone's
receptor on the pituitary, prompting release of the body's own growth hormone. The albumin-binding
version was designed so that binding to albumin in the blood extends how long the compound persists.
The originating preclinical characterisation showed, in rats, that human GRF(1-29) albumin
bioconjugates activate the relevant receptor on the anterior pituitary.

**Ipamorelin** binds the growth hormone secretagogue receptor 1a, the ghrelin receptor, on pituitary
cells and stimulates a pulse of growth hormone release. The distinguishing claim made for it is
selectivity, and the origin of that claim needs stating carefully, because it is the compound's main
selling point and the most commonly misreported thing about it.

In the 1998 study that first characterised it, conducted in primary rat pituitary cells, in
pentobarbital-anesthetized rats, and in conscious swine, ipamorelin released growth hormone with
potency and efficacy comparable to GHRP-6, and did not release ACTH or cortisol at levels significantly
different from those following growth hormone-releasing hormone stimulation, whereas GHRP-6 and GHRP-2
did raise those hormones. No secretagogue tested affected FSH, LH, prolactin, or TSH.

That is a real finding in rats, rat cells, and swine. **It is preclinical.** No human study located
tested ipamorelin's selectivity against ACTH, cortisol, or prolactin. Whether the selectivity holds in
people, and whether it holds with repeated rather than single exposure, are both unstudied. Consumer
material that describes selectivity as a property of the compound in humans is reporting an animal
result as a human clinical property.

### For the combination

The stated rationale for pairing them is that the two act at different receptors, so pushing on both
inputs should produce a larger or more sustained growth hormone response than either alone.

**That is a hypothesis and it has not been tested for this pair in anything retrieved.** It is
recorded here as the reason people give for the pairing, not as a description of what the product does.

There are at least four possible outcomes when two agents converging on one hormonal axis are given
together. Their effects could add. They could overlap so that the second adds little. They could
interact in a way that changes the response pattern of either. Or repeated combined stimulation could
produce tolerance. Nothing retrieved tells us which happens here, and a mechanism argument cannot
settle it, because settling it requires measurement.

One more caution belongs in this section. A human study of the albumin-binding CJC-1295 alone reported
that growth hormone pulsatility was preserved. Whether that holds when a second secretagogue acting at
a different receptor is present is unknown and must not be assumed.

## Preclinical evidence

Every finding below names its species or model. None of it is human evidence, and none of it involves
both compounds together.

### CJC-1295, single ingredient

- **In rats**, human GRF(1-29) albumin bioconjugates were reported to activate the GRF receptor on the
  anterior pituitary, and that work identified CJC-1295 as a long-lasting GRF analogue (PMID 15817669,
  Endocrinology 2005). This is the originating characterisation of the albumin-bioconjugate design.
- **In a genetically GHRH-deficient knockout mouse**, repeated administration of CJC-1295 was reported
  to normalise growth (PMID 16822960, Am J Physiol Endocrinol Metab 2006). A mouse engineered to lack
  the natural hormone is not a model of a healthy adult human, and correcting a deficiency in such an
  animal says nothing about adding stimulation to a person who is not deficient.
- **In horses**, analytical methods were developed to confirm CJC-1295 in equine plasma and to screen
  for CJC-1295 and other GHRH analogues (PMID 30938069 and PMID 30489688, Drug Test Anal 2019). These
  are detection methodology papers. They carry no efficacy or safety finding and are listed only to
  document that veterinary and equine misuse prompted assay development.

### Ipamorelin, single ingredient

- **In primary rat pituitary cells, in pentobarbital-anesthetized rats, and in conscious swine**,
  ipamorelin was reported to release growth hormone with potency and efficacy comparable to GHRP-6,
  without releasing ACTH or cortisol at levels significantly different from those following growth
  hormone-releasing hormone stimulation, while GHRP-6 and GHRP-2 did raise those hormones. No
  secretagogue tested affected FSH, LH, prolactin, or TSH (PMID 9849822, Eur J Endocrinol 1998). This
  animal and cell study is the sole origin of the selectivity claim.
- **In rats**, a study titled as reporting that ipamorelin induces longitudinal bone growth (PMID
  10373343, Growth Horm IGF Res 1999). Title level only, abstract not retrieved, so only the existence,
  title, journal, and year are verified. Rodent longitudinal bone growth does not translate to adults
  with closed growth plates.
- **In ferrets**, a study titled as reporting that anamorelin and ipamorelin inhibit cisplatin-induced
  weight loss (PMID 39043357, Physiol Behav 2024). Title level only.
- **In a rodent model of postoperative ileus**, a study titled as examining ipamorelin's efficacy on
  gastric dysmotility (PMID 27186127, J Exp Pharmacol 2012). Title level only. This is the animal
  rationale that preceded the human ileus trials, which then failed in humans. It is a useful
  illustration of preclinical promise not translating.
- **In a cichlid fish**, a study titled as examining ipamorelin's influence on the
  hypothalamic-pituitary-testicular axis (PMID 38996787, Anim Reprod Sci 2024). Title level only. Very
  low relevance to human physiology, listed only for completeness of the retrieved record.

### For the combination: none

No preclinical study of the two compounds administered together was located, in any species. Where
analytical papers assayed several secretagogues in parallel, they were detecting compounds, not
administering them together, and they carry no efficacy or safety finding.

## Contradictions and disagreement

Set out in full in CONTRADICTIONS.md. In summary.

1. **Whether the blend has an evidence base.** Commercial material implies yes, usually by placing
   component citations under a heading about the blend rather than by stating it. The record says no
   study of the pair exists. This Guide keeps every component finding under its own labelled heading
   for exactly this reason.
2. **Whether "CJC-1295" and "CJC-1295 no DAC" are the same thing.** Vendor pages treat them as
   synonyms. The peer-reviewed record treats them as two molecules with different pharmacology and
   very different evidence bases. This is not a scientific disagreement. It is a naming practice with
   consequences.
3. **Whether ipamorelin's selectivity is a human property.** The claim is preclinical in origin, from a
   1998 study in rat cells, rats, and swine. No human study located tested it.
4. **Whether ipamorelin is "clinically studied and well tolerated".** The trial history is real and the
   framing is misleading by omission, because the published trial failed, the largest trial never
   reported, the indication studied was unrelated to marketed use, and development was discontinued.
5. **The current compounding status of CJC-1295 in the United States.** Unresolved. A peer-reviewed
   review reports a safety-risk category listing. Vendor pages assert removal on two mutually
   inconsistent dates. No primary agency document could be read. No current status is stated here.
6. **Whether either compound is on the July 2026 advisory committee agenda.** Resolved against the
   vendor claims on a primary government notice. Neither compound appears on it.
7. **What category removal would mean.** Commercial material reads it as clearance. A legal analysis
   states that removed substances remain unlawful for compounding until formal addition to the list.
8. **Whether CJC-1295 development stopped for a safety reason.** Unresolved. A tertiary source reports
   a trial subject death, attributed by the attending physician to pre-existing coronary artery disease
   unrelated to treatment. The registry confirms termination without a reason. This may be published
   neither as fact nor as debunked.
9. **Where the mechanism argument outruns the evidence.** The chain from "raises growth hormone" to
   "improves body composition and recovery" has no human measurement behind it for either ingredient,
   and the additivity step at the end has no measurement behind it at all.
10. **Where supply-channel evidence is about the channel rather than the compound.** Two quality
    studies cited here tested other peptides. Neither tested either ingredient.

## Limitations

Limitations of the evidence, and limitations of this Guide.

**Of the evidence.**

- The combination has never been studied, so every statement about the product as a product is a
  statement about an unknown.
- All three CJC-1295 human studies came from one investigator group linked to the original developer,
  were published between 2006 and 2009, enrolled only healthy volunteers, and measured only hormone
  levels or serum proteins.
- The only CJC-1295 trial in a patient population was terminated with no results posted.
- The only published ipamorelin efficacy trial was negative, in an unrelated indication, in acutely ill
  hospitalised patients.
- The largest ipamorelin trial, with 320 participants, has never reported. The largest human dataset on
  that compound is missing from the public record.
- No systematic review or meta-analysis exists for either compound. The 2026 sources relied on here are
  narrative reviews, which is a lower evidence grade.
- The longest human exposure identified for CJC-1295 was 49 days. Every ipamorelin human exposure
  retrieved was short-term. There is no chronic-use data for either, and no post-marketing surveillance
  because there is no approved product.
- Several preclinical records are verified at title level only, so their detailed findings were not
  read and nothing rests on them.

**Of this Guide.**

- The combination finding rests on a thorough search of two component research records, not on a fresh
  registry query naming both compounds together. A human should run that query directly and record the
  result with its date. The expected result is zero.
- Full text of the three CJC-1295 human studies was not obtained, only abstracts. Exact enrollment
  numbers, adverse event tables, and dropout data are therefore unverified.
- Every United States agency statement about ipamorelin here is secondary, because the agency's website
  was completely unreachable during research.
- The agency's actual position and the committee outcome on the five CJC-1295 forms are unknown.
- The anti-doping Prohibited List could not be retrieved for either compound.
- Seven 2026 review articles about CJC-1295 have verified identifiers but unread content, and some may
  contain relevant safety or quality material.
- No trial registry outside ClinicalTrials.gov was searched, and no regulator outside the United States
  was consulted.
- No supplier documentation for the Xenios product has been reviewed, so its composition is unconfirmed.

## Quality and identity

Full requirements are in QUALITY_AND_DOCUMENTATION.md, where every item is currently unmet.

**The first open question is composition.** The exact composition and ingredient ratio of the Xenios
product are NOT CONFIRMED. No supplier document has been reviewed, no assay has been seen, and no
independent testing has been commissioned. No proportion appears in this Guide because none has been
established.

**The second is which molecule.** Because "CJC-1295" names two chemically different compounds, a
product bearing that name does not by itself identify what it contains. This ambiguity is an identity
defect in its own right, not a labelling inconvenience, because the two versions have different
pharmacology and only one of them has any published human data.

**A blend adds a requirement a single-ingredient product does not have.** It is not enough to know that
CJC-1295 is present and that ipamorelin is present. Each component needs its own identity confirmation
and its own quantitative assay, and the mixture needs demonstrated uniformity, meaning the same
composition throughout a container and from container to container. A blend that is correct on average
and uneven in practice is not a controlled product.

**What is documented about the supply channel.**

- CJC-1295 has been identified by mass spectrometry in an unlabelled illicit pharmaceutical preparation
  of unknown origin, submitted by police and customs authorities. The product's contents were unknown
  until analysed (PMID 21204297, 2010). That is direct documentation that this compound circulates in
  unlabelled, unregulated form. It is 16 years old, so read it as evidence that the problem exists
  rather than as a current measurement of the market.
- A 2026 peer-reviewed review states that real-world use of these compounds is complicated by
  quality-control uncertainty and compositional variability characteristic of unregulated peptide
  supply chains, and notes this is especially acute for agents lacking any human validation, which
  includes the short-acting CJC-1295 version.
- The anti-doping analytical literature documents a growing black market and illicit synthesis of
  analogue peptide hormones, citing CJC-1295's identification in confiscated products (PMID 26879649).
- In an analytical quality-control study of commercially obtained synthetic peptides, one of five
  products examined was found to be a totally different peptide than labelled, and two thirds of the
  others had insufficient quality by the study's stated purity and impurity criteria (PMID 18342612).
  **That study tested a different peptide. It did not test either ingredient of this blend.** It is
  evidence about the channel only.
- Counterfeit and illegal injectable peptide preparations are a recognised enforcement problem in
  Europe, sufficient that controlling agencies developed a dedicated screening method covering 25
  peptides already detected in seized products (PMID 26003685). The retrieved abstract did not confirm
  whether either ingredient was among those 25.

**What is not documented.** No independent analytical study of any marketed product containing both
compounds was located. No published purity or identity survey of consumer products labelled as either
ingredient was located. Nobody appears to have checked. That is an absence of information, not a
favourable result.

## Safety categories

Read the first line before any other.

**The primary safety finding for this combination is that no safety data for the combination exists.**
Nothing about the safety of these two compounds administered together can be concluded in either
direction. Absence of reported harm here reflects absence of study, not a favourable record.

Nothing below is a reassurance and nothing below is a prediction of harm. Each item states what kind of
statement it is.

**No long-term data for either ingredient.** A 2026 peer-reviewed review states that no long-term
safety data is available for either CJC-1295 version. That is consistent with what was retrieved
directly: the longest human trial ran 49 days, and the only Phase 2 trial was terminated with no results
posted. For ipamorelin, every human exposure retrieved was short-term. The durations typical of consumer
use have not been studied in a controlled human trial for either compound.

**Mechanism-based question about cell growth.** A 2026 peer-reviewed review flags growth hormone and
IGF-1 signalling as raising a theoretical oncologic question, while explicitly stating that there is no
established clinical carcinogenic signal. Both halves of that sentence are load-bearing. The concern is
mechanism-based, not an observed harm, and neither reassurance nor alarm is supported by data. Both
ingredients stimulate the same axis, which is a reason the question is at least as live for a blend as
for either alone. That is reasoning, not data.

**Glucose handling and fluid retention.** The same review describes these as theoretically plausible
through activation of this axis, while stating that they lack direct peer-reviewed documentation
specifically for CJC-1295 products. Reported here precisely so they are not overstated as observed
effects.

**Immune reactions to injectable peptides.** According to investigative reporting of the agency's
review, the United States Food and Drug Administration cited well-established research that injectable
peptides carry immune reaction risks, ranging from mild rashes to life-threatening anaphylaxis, as part
of the basis for restricting ipamorelin in 2023. This is a class-level concern about injectable peptides
rather than an ipamorelin-specific adverse event series, and it reaches this Guide through secondary
reporting because the agency's pages were unreachable.

**Insufficient data, which is not the same as evidence of harm.** Reporting states the agency concluded
that available data were insufficient to resolve known safety concerns for ipamorelin and recommended
against inclusion on the compounding list, and the advisory committee agreed and voted against inclusion
on October 29, 2024. Insufficient evidence of safety is a distinct finding from evidence of harm, and is
stated here as such.

**Adverse events including death, causation not established.** Reporting states that in the ipamorelin
studies the agency examined, subjects experienced adverse events including death, while noting that
causation was not definitively proven. The context is mandatory and cuts both ways: those trials
enrolled post-surgical bowel resection patients, a population with meaningful baseline mortality and
morbidity independent of any study drug. This is a flag that the agency considered the safety database
inadequate. It is not a demonstration that the compound killed anyone.

**A reported death during CJC-1295 development, unverified.** A tertiary encyclopedia source reports
that development was discontinued following a trial subject's death, that the attending physician
attributed it to pre-existing coronary artery disease unrelated to treatment, and that research was
terminated as a precaution. No primary source for this was retrieved. It is consistent with, but not
established by, the verified fact that the Phase 2 trial was terminated with no posted results and no
stated reason. It is recorded here as unverified and requiring human checking. It may not be treated as
fact and may not be treated as debunked.
[UNVERIFIED - tertiary source, requires human source check]

**Evidence transfer between the two CJC-1295 versions is itself a safety issue.** Because the
short-acting version has no controlled human studies, any safety reassurance derived from the
albumin-binding version's Phase 1 trials is being applied to a compound it was never collected on. The
component record treats this misattribution as a safety problem rather than a labelling one.

**Populations where nothing is known.** No study retrieved for either ingredient addressed pregnancy,
breastfeeding, children, older adults, people with a history of malignancy, people with diabetes or
impaired glucose handling, people with pituitary or endocrine conditions, or people taking hormone or
metabolic medicines. The human studies of both compounds skew heavily male. Two of the three CJC-1295
studies enrolled men only, the third did not report a sex breakdown in the retrieved abstract, and the
ipamorelin pharmacology study enrolled healthy male subjects.

**Anti-doping.** Both ingredients are described in the peer-reviewed anti-doping literature as
prohibited in sport, and ipamorelin metabolites are detectable in urine testing. A product containing
both contains two prohibited substances. Any athlete subject to testing should consult their own
anti-doping authority rather than rely on anything here.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

## Interactions

**No interaction data was located for this combination.** That is the whole of the direct evidence, and
it deserves emphasis rather than a passing mention, because a blend is a permanent interaction question
by construction. Every use of this product is a use of two active compounds at once, and the only study
that could describe what that does has not been done.

Three categories of interaction question are open.

**Between the two ingredients.** Whether they add, overlap, blunt each other, alter each other's
response pattern, or produce tolerance with repeated combined stimulation. Unstudied.

**With medicines and conditions.** No interaction study of either ingredient with any medicine or
supplement was located. Categories that would warrant professional review, on the basis that both
ingredients act on a hormonal axis with metabolic effects, include diabetes and glucose-lowering
medicines, corticosteroids and other hormone therapies, thyroid medicines, and anything prescribed for
a pituitary or endocrine condition. These are named as categories requiring a clinician's judgment.
Naming them is not a finding that an interaction exists, and the absence of a category from this list is
not a finding that it is clear.

**With other growth hormone axis compounds.** The ipamorelin record names this explicitly as an
unknown, including with the widely sold CJC-1295 pairing, which has no located trial evidence in
combination. Anyone considering this product alongside anything else acting on the same system is
compounding an unknown with another unknown.

Unstudied does not mean absent. It means nobody has looked.

## Unknowns

**About the combination**

- Everything. No study of the pair exists, so its effects, its safety, its interaction profile, and its
  behaviour over time are all unknown.
- Whether the two together produce a larger, smaller, or different growth hormone response than either
  alone.
- Whether combining a growth hormone-releasing hormone receptor agonist with a ghrelin receptor agonist
  changes the safety profile relative to either alone.
- Whether the response patterns reported for either ingredient alone, including the preserved
  pulsatility reported for one of them, hold when both are present.

- Which CJC-1295 molecule it contains, the albumin-binding version or the short-acting one.
- Its identity, purity, sterility, endotoxin status, and blend uniformity. None has been assessed.

**About CJC-1295**

## Questions for a qualified professional

If a member wants to discuss this product with a clinician, these questions produce a useful
conversation. They are written to be asked as they stand.

1. This product contains two active compounds, and I have been told there is no study of the two of them
   given together. Does that match what you can find, and how does it change how you would think about
   it?
2. Each ingredient has been studied on its own. How much weight do you give evidence about single
   ingredients when the question is about a mixture?
3. The name CJC-1295 apparently covers two different molecules with different properties, and I cannot
   tell from the label which one is in a product. How would you approach a product whose active
   ingredient you cannot identify with confidence?
4. The human research on both ingredients measured hormone levels rather than outcomes. What would you
   want to see measured before you thought a hormone change mattered for me?
5. One ingredient's only published efficacy trial failed and its development was discontinued. Does that
   history change how you weigh the rest of the material about it?
6. Both ingredients act on the growth hormone axis through different routes. What would concern you
   about stimulating one hormonal system from two directions at once?
7. I have the following conditions and take the following medicines. Which of them would make anything
   acting on the growth hormone axis a particularly poor idea for me?
8. If someone were stimulating growth hormone release over a long period, what would you want to monitor,
   and what would concern you?
9. Neither ingredient appears to be approved anywhere, so any material comes from outside a regulated
   supply chain. How do you think about unverified identity, purity, and sterility for something
   introduced into the body, and does a two-ingredient mixture add anything to that concern?
10. I compete in a tested sport. Who is the right authority for me to ask about both of these compounds?
11. If I decided to do nothing about this for now, what would you want me to watch for, and when should I
    come back?
12. If a study of this combination is published, what would you want to see in it before it changed your
    advice?

This Guide does not give medical advice and does not tell anyone what to do. These questions belong with
a clinician who knows your history.
