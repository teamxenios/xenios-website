# CJC-1295 and Ipamorelin Blend: contradictions and divergences

Where sources disagree, where a widely repeated statement conflicts with the record, and where a
disagreement is unresolved. Each entry states the two positions, what the retrieved record supports,
and how the Guide handles it.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

Source ids refer to SOURCE_REGISTRY.md.

---

## X-00. The disagreement that sits above all the others: whether the blend has an evidence base

**Position A.** The blend is well supported, because both of its ingredients have been studied. This
is the implicit position of essentially all commercial material about the product, and it is usually
made by adjacency rather than by explicit statement: component citations appear beneath a heading
about the blend, and the reader completes the argument themselves.

**Position B.** A mixture of two active compounds is its own pharmacological object. It has its own
behaviour, its own interaction profile, and its own list of things nobody has measured. Evidence
gathered on either ingredient by itself describes that ingredient by itself. It cannot be transferred
to the pair. Unless a study administered both compounds together, the combination has no evidence at
all, of any grade.

**Assessment.** Position B is correct, and Position A is the central error this Guide exists to
prevent. Both component research records were searched in full and neither contains any study, in any
species, of any design, that administered CJC-1295 together with ipamorelin. The ipamorelin record
names the gap explicitly, recording the widely sold blend as an interaction with no located trial
evidence in combination.

The point is worth stating precisely because a hedge here would be worse than nothing. The correct
statement is not that combination evidence is limited, early, or emerging. It is that there is none.

**Handling.** Section 1 of CLAIM_TABLE.md is empty of effect claims and its emptiness is labelled as
the finding. In GUIDE_DRAFT.md the two components are presented in separate, individually headed
sections, each labelled as being about one ingredient alone. No component citation footnotes a
sentence about the blend anywhere in this folder.

**A note for whoever edits this next.** Position A rarely arrives as a sentence you can delete. It
arrives as a layout choice. Watch for a component study cited under a blend heading, a summary bullet
that names both compounds and then a finding from one, and a mechanism paragraph that describes two
receptors and ends with a result. Each of those is the error, even when every individual word is
accurate.

## X-01. Whether the two names "CJC-1295" and "CJC-1295 no DAC" refer to the same thing

**Position A.** They are the same compound, described with an optional modifier. Vendor pages
encountered during research used the two names interchangeably or as parenthetical synonyms, and cited
the same human trial literature for both.

**Position B.** They are two chemically different molecules. One carries an added group that binds
circulating albumin and greatly extends how long the compound persists, and it is the molecule the
published human studies were conducted on, with a reported half-life of 5.8 to 8.1 days (C01). The
other lacks that group, is more accurately called modified GRF(1-29), and a 2026 peer-reviewed review
states it is essentially uncharacterised in the peer-reviewed human literature with no controlled
clinical studies directly evaluating it in humans (C06).

**Assessment.** This is not a genuine scientific contradiction. It is one commercial name covering two
molecules, and it is the single most consequential point in this Guide after the absence of
combination evidence. Position B is supported by the peer-reviewed record. Position A is a marketing
practice.

The mislabelling is consequential rather than pedantic for two reasons. The two forms are positioned
very differently, one as sustained elevation and one as a short pulse, so a buyer choosing between
them believes they are choosing between behaviours. And the human safety and pharmacokinetic findings
that make the compound sound characterised were collected on only one of them. Applying them to the
other applies data to a molecule it was never collected on, which the component record treats as a
safety issue in its own right.

**Handling.** BLEND-I-03 and BLEND-I-04 in CLAIM_TABLE.md, and a dedicated passage in GUIDE_DRAFT.md.
Every CJC-1295 claim in this folder states which molecule it applies to. BLEND-X-04 prohibits the
transfer in either direction. No pharmacokinetic figure for the form without the albumin-binding group
appears anywhere, because none was confirmed by any peer-reviewed or regulatory source.

**Unresolved for the Xenios product specifically.** Which of the two molecules the product contains is
not established. It cannot be resolved from the product name, and no supplier document has been
reviewed. See QUALITY_AND_DOCUMENTATION.md item A2.

## X-02. Whether ipamorelin's selectivity is a human property

**Position A.** Ipamorelin is the most selective growth hormone secretagogue, raising growth hormone
without raising cortisol, ACTH, or prolactin. This is the dominant claim in consumer material and is
the compound's main selling point.

**Position B.** The selectivity data come from a 1998 study in primary rat pituitary cells,
pentobarbital-anesthetized rats, and conscious swine (I07). In those models ipamorelin released growth
hormone comparably to GHRP-6 and did not release ACTH or cortisol at levels significantly different
from those following GHRH stimulation, while GHRP-6 and GHRP-2 did raise those hormones. No human
study located tested selectivity against ACTH, cortisol, or prolactin.

**Assessment.** Position B accurately describes the evidence base. The 1998 study is real, well
conducted, and preclinical. The consumer claim reports an animal result as though it were a
demonstrated human clinical property. Whether the selectivity holds in humans, and whether it holds
with repeated rather than single exposure, are both unstudied.

**Handling.** BLEND-M-03 and BLEND-B-05 describe selectivity as a preclinical characteristic with all
three models named in the same sentence. BLEND-X-06 prohibits stating it as a human property.

## X-03. Whether ipamorelin is "clinically studied and well tolerated"

**Position A.** Ipamorelin is among the better-characterised peptides, having completed Phase 1 and
Phase 2 trials, and the published trial described it as well tolerated.

**Position B.** The trials were run for postoperative ileus, an indication unrelated to anything the
compound is now marketed for. The published Phase 2 trial did not meet its primary endpoint and showed
no advantage on secondary outcomes either (I04). The sponsor discontinued development. The largest
trial, with 320 participants, completed in May 2014 and has never posted results (I02). One regulator
concluded that the safety data were insufficient to resolve known safety concerns and recommended
against compounding eligibility, and the advisory committee concurred (I12, I13).

**Assessment.** Both positions contain true statements, and Position A is materially misleading by
omission. It is true that the compound reached Phase 2 and that the published trial called it well
tolerated in that specific post-surgical setting. It is also true that the programme was abandoned for
lack of efficacy, that the indication studied has nothing to do with current marketed use, and that
regulators judged the safety database inadequate. Citing the trial history as reassurance while
omitting how it ended inverts the meaning of the evidence.

**Handling.** BLEND-B-04 requires the full arc to travel together. BLEND-X-07 prohibits citing the
trial history without the outcome. The tolerability language from the published abstract is confined
to that study and that population and may not be generalised, per BLEND-X-08.

## X-04. The current compounding status of CJC-1295 in the United States

**Position A.** A 2026 peer-reviewed review states that the agency listed CJC-1295 in the
significant-safety-risk category of the bulk drug substances list, citing reports of vasodilatory
reactions and tachycardia (C06).

**Position B.** Vendor and clinic pages assert that CJC-1295 was removed from that category, some
saying September 2024 and others saying April 2026, and describe it variously as awaiting review or as
having no lawful compounding basis in the interim.

**Assessment.** UNRESOLVED, and it must stay unresolved on the page. Every agency URL attempted during
component research returned HTTP 404, and the comment docket returned HTTP 403, so no primary agency
document was read. The two removal dates offered by vendor pages are mutually inconsistent, which is
itself a reason to distrust both, and both come from commercially interested parties.

What is verified, from primary government notices: five CJC-1295 forms were formally considered at the
December 4, 2024 advisory committee meeting (C10), and a January 2025 final guidance ended the
Category 1, 2, and 3 framework for substances nominated on or after its publication date (C11). The
January 2025 notice does not mention CJC-1295 or peptides by name, so it does not by itself establish
this compound's status, and readers encountering category language should treat it as possibly
describing a superseded framework.

**Handling.** No current compounding status for CJC-1295 appears anywhere in this folder.
BLEND-R-06 records the question as unresolved and BLEND-X-12 prohibits stating a status. A human must
open the agency pages directly.

## X-05. Whether either compound is pending review at the July 2026 advisory committee meeting

**Position A.** Vendor and clinic pages assert that CJC-1295 is pending a July 2026 advisory committee
review. Separate commercial pages assert that ipamorelin was reclassified in April 2026 and is now or
soon will be available for compounding.

**Position B.** The Federal Register notice announcing the July 23 to 24, 2026 meeting lists BPC-157,
KPV, TB-500, MOTS-C, DSIP, Semax and Epitalon (C12). Neither CJC-1295 nor ipamorelin appears. A
legal-practice analysis of the April 15, 2026 category action states that ipamorelin was not among the
peptides removed (I14).

**Assessment.** RESOLVED AGAINST THE VENDOR CLAIMS, on a primary government source for the meeting
agenda. The Federal Register notice is verifiable text and neither compound is in it. Treat both
vendor claims as false or, at minimum, unsupported.

The ipamorelin position has additional internal support. That compound already had its advisory
committee review on October 29, 2024 and was voted against (I13), which places it on a different
procedural track from peptides still awaiting a first review. The legal analysis is also a
disinterested source citing a specific docket, where the vendor claims come from parties selling the
product.

**Handling.** BLEND-R-05 states the verified agenda. BLEND-X-12 prohibits the vendor claim. The
ipamorelin position is stated conservatively and date-stamped, because it too could not be confirmed
against the agency directly.

## X-06. What "removal from the safety-risk category" would mean

**Position A.** Removal means the substance is cleared, legal, or agency-sanctioned for compounding.
This is the reading circulating widely in commercial material.

**Position B.** A legal-practice analysis states explicitly that removed substances remain unlawful
for compounding until their formal addition to the bulks list, which requires completion of formal
rulemaking after committee evaluation (I14).

**Assessment.** Position B is correct and Position A is a consequential misreading. Category removal
moves a substance out of an identified-risk bucket. It does not confer compounding authorisation.

**Handling.** BLEND-R-04 states the distinction and BLEND-X-13 prohibits the misreading. Any text in
this folder touching 2026 regulatory news must make the distinction explicitly, because omitting it
misleads a reader about legality.

## X-07. Whether CJC-1295 development stopped for a safety reason

**Position A.** A tertiary encyclopedia source states that Phase II development was discontinued
following a trial subject's death, with the attending physician attributing it to pre-existing
coronary artery disease unrelated to treatment, and research terminated as a precaution (C13).

**Position B.** The registry record for the Phase 2 trial shows it as terminated but gives no reason,
and no results were ever posted (C04).

**Assessment.** UNRESOLVED. The registry confirms termination but not the cause. No primary source for
the death account was retrieved. The account is consistent with, but not established by, the verified
termination.

Both failure modes are prohibited here. Publishing the account as fact would state something
unverified about a serious matter. Publishing it as debunked would be equally unsupported, because
nothing retrieved contradicts it. The honest position is that it is unverified and requires a human
check against a sponsor record, agency correspondence, or contemporaneous reporting.

**Handling.** BLEND-S-08 is graded G, marked not member-facing, and flagged for human verification.
BLEND-X-11 prohibits both directions.

## X-08. Where a mechanism argument outruns the evidence

This is not a disagreement between sources. It is a disagreement between what the mechanism suggests
and what has been measured, and it is the most common way a reader will mislead themselves about this
product.

The argument runs: growth hormone matters for body composition, recovery, and repair. Both of these
compounds raise growth hormone. Therefore the blend improves body composition, recovery, and repair,
and does so more than either compound alone because they act at two different receptors.

Every step in that chain except the last is individually defensible, and the conclusion is still
unsupported. Raising a hormone is a biomarker observation. No human study of either component measured
body composition, strength, sleep, recovery, injury healing, cognition, or any longevity endpoint
(BLEND-A-05, BLEND-B-07). The one occasion where a proposed mechanism for either component was
actually put to a human test, the ghrelin-mimetic rationale for gastrointestinal motility, produced a
trial that did not meet its primary endpoint (I04). That is worth holding on to: it is a direct
demonstration of how much weight a coherent mechanism deserves in the absence of an outcome study.

The final step, the additivity claim, has no support at all. Two compounds converging on one axis
could add, could overlap redundantly, could interact in ways nobody has characterised, or could
produce tolerance. Nobody has measured it for this pair.

**Handling.** BLEND-M-02 records the additive rationale as a named hypothesis and nothing more.
BLEND-C-03 records the additivity question as unknown. BLEND-X-02 and BLEND-X-09 prohibit both the
synergy claim and the outcome claim.

## X-09. Where supply-channel evidence is about the channel, not the compound

Two kinds of quality evidence appear in this folder, and conflating them would be a factual error.

Compound-specific: CJC-1295 was identified by mass spectrometry in an unlabelled illicit preparation
of unknown origin submitted by police and customs authorities (C08). That is direct documentation
about this compound, and it is 16 years old.

Channel-level: an analytical study of commercially obtained synthetic peptides found one of five
products to be a different peptide than labelled, with two thirds of the others of insufficient
quality (I09), and a separate study documents counterfeit and illegal injectable peptide preparations
as an enforcement problem sufficient to warrant a dedicated 25-peptide screening method (I10).
**Neither tested either component of this blend.**

No study testing a marketed product containing both compounds was located, and no purity or identity
survey of consumer products labelled as either component was located.

**Handling.** BLEND-Q-02 through BLEND-Q-06 keep the two kinds of evidence separate and name the
tested compound every time. BLEND-X-14 prohibits implying that the channel studies tested either
component.

## X-10. Jurisdictional and cross-border divergence

No regulatory statement was retrieved for the European Union, the United Kingdom, Canada, Australia,
Japan, or any jurisdiction other than the United States, for either component. No national regulator
outside the United States was consulted during component research, and no trial registry outside
ClinicalTrials.gov was searched.

The absence of an entry means the question was not investigated. It does not mean the compounds are
unregulated, permitted, or approved anywhere. Approval or registration in one jurisdiction is not
approval in another, and an unconfirmed status elsewhere may not be inferred from the United States
position.

**Handling.** REGULATORY_STATUS.md states the omission as a gap rather than as a finding.

## X-11. A note on the integrity of the regulatory record in this folder

The agency's own website was completely unreachable during both component research sessions, with
every attempted URL returning HTTP 404. The comment docket returned HTTP 403. The anti-doping
Prohibited List document could not be retrieved for either compound.

The consequence is uneven and should be understood before this folder is relied on. The CJC-1295
regulatory statements that are verified rest on primary government notices retrieved from an
alternative government source, which is a genuine primary record. Every ipamorelin regulatory
statement rests on secondary accounts: investigative journalism, a trade association report, and a
legal-practice analysis. Those are careful sources and they are still not the regulator.

Anti-doping status for both compounds rests on peer-reviewed analytical papers stating that these
compound classes are on the Prohibited List, not on the List itself. No section number or verbatim
entry may be published.

A human reviewer must open the agency pages and the Prohibited List directly and confirm each
statement before publication. Until then, every affected statement in this folder is labelled with how
it was sourced.
