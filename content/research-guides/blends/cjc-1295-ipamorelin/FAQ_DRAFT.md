# CJC-1295 and ipamorelin: questions members actually ask

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## 1. Does this blend work?

Nobody knows, and the reason is more specific than it usually is.

No study of these two compounds given together was located. Not in people, not in animals, not of any
design. So there is no evidence about the blend to weigh, weak or otherwise.

That is different from saying the evidence is thin. Thin evidence means somebody looked and found
little. Here, nothing was found because, as far as the retrieved record shows, nobody has looked at the
pair at all.

The natural follow-up is: but each ingredient has been studied, so surely that tells us something? It
tells us about each ingredient. A mixture behaves as a mixture. Two compounds in one vial can add up,
overlap, blunt each other, or interact in ways that neither one alone would predict, and the only way
to know which happens is to test the mixture. That test has not been published.

And when you look at what the single-ingredient research actually measured, even the component picture
is weaker than the marketing suggests. See question 3.

## 2. Is it safe?

The correct answer is not that it is safe or that it is unsafe. It is that the safety of the two
together has never been studied, so nothing can be concluded in either direction.

What is known about the ingredients separately:

- There is no long-term safety data for either one. The longest human exposure identified for CJC-1295
  was a 49-day trial. Every human exposure retrieved for ipamorelin was short-term.
- A 2026 peer-reviewed review raises a mechanism-based question about sustained stimulation of the
  growth hormone and IGF-1 axis and effects on cell growth, while stating explicitly that there is no
  established clinical carcinogenic signal. Both halves of that matter. It is a theoretical question,
  not an observed harm, and neither alarm nor reassurance is supported by data.
- The same review describes effects on glucose handling and fluid retention as theoretically plausible
  through this axis while noting they lack direct documentation for these products specifically.
- For ipamorelin, one regulator concluded that available data were insufficient to resolve known safety
  concerns and recommended against making it available for pharmacy compounding. An advisory committee
  agreed in October 2024. Insufficient evidence of safety is a different finding from evidence of harm,
  and it should be read as exactly what it says.

You may encounter a line from a 2006 CJC-1295 trial saying the compound was safe and relatively well
tolerated. That conclusion belongs to that specific short study, in healthy volunteers, on one of the
two molecules sold under that name. It is not a safety statement about a blend product, and it should
not be read as one.

## 3. But both ingredients raise growth hormone. Isn't that the point?

They do appear to raise growth hormone, and that is genuinely established for each one individually in
humans. The question is what follows from it.

Every human study of either compound measured hormone levels in blood. Not one measured body
composition, lean mass, fat loss, strength, sleep quality, recovery, injury healing, skin, cognition, or
any aging endpoint. The uses these compounds are actually marketed for have never been tested in a human
trial for either ingredient.

So the argument being made is: this raises growth hormone, and growth hormone does useful things,
therefore this does useful things. That is a chain of reasoning, not a result. It might be right. It has
not been checked.

There is a good cautionary example inside this compound's own history. Somebody once reasoned that
ipamorelin, acting at the ghrelin receptor, should improve gut movement after abdominal surgery. That
reasoning was sound enough to fund a trial. The trial did not meet its primary endpoint. A coherent
mechanism is a reason to run a study, not a substitute for one.

## 4. Why does everyone sell these two together?

Because they act at two different receptors, and the reasoning is that pushing on two separate inputs to
the same system should produce a bigger or steadier release than pushing on one.

That is a real and sensible-sounding rationale. It is also, so far as the retrieved record shows,
untested for this pair.

It is worth being clear about what the pairing is. It is a commercial arrangement, not a research
finding. These two compounds are sold together far more than they have been studied together, and in
everything retrieved they have not been studied together at all. Nobody ran a trial that concluded the
pair works better and then built a product around it.

## 5. What is the deal with "CJC-1295" versus "CJC-1295 no DAC"?

This is the single most confused thing about this product, and it is worth understanding properly.

Two chemically different molecules are sold under the one name.

One carries an added group, called DAC, that binds to albumin, a protein in the blood. That binding
makes the compound persist far longer. Its estimated half-life in healthy adults was reported as 5.8 to
8.1 days. This is the version the original developer took into clinical trials, and essentially all of
the published human research on "CJC-1295" is research on this version.

The other lacks that group. It is more accurately called modified GRF(1-29), and it is short-acting. A
2026 peer-reviewed review states plainly that it is essentially uncharacterised in the peer-reviewed
human literature and that no controlled clinical studies have directly evaluated it in humans.

Why this matters practically: if a page shows you the human trial data and then sells you the version
without the albumin-binding group, it is showing you evidence collected on a different molecule. The two
are marketed for opposite behaviours, one as sustained elevation and one as a short pulse, so this is
not a technicality. A buyer choosing between them believes they are choosing between behaviours, and one
of those behaviours has never been characterised in a person.

**You cannot tell which one you have from the product name.** Every vendor page encountered during
research used the two names interchangeably. Which version is in this product is not established.

## 6. What is actually in the Xenios product, and in what proportion?

Not confirmed, and that is the honest and complete answer.

Xenios has not reviewed supplier documentation for this product, has not seen an assay, and has not
commissioned independent testing. No composition and no ratio appear anywhere in this Guide, because
none has been established. If you see a ratio quoted for a blend anywhere, ask what document it came
from.

This is the first question a supplier would have to answer, and it is listed first in the quality
document for that reason.

A blend also carries a quality requirement a single-ingredient product does not. It is not enough to
show that both compounds are present. Each one needs its own identity confirmation and its own
measurement of how much is there, and the mixture needs to be demonstrably uniform, meaning the same
composition throughout a container and from container to container. None of that has been established
here.

## 7. Is it legal?

The regulatory picture is genuinely complicated and parts of it could not be confirmed. What can be said,
checked 2026-07-19 and reviewed 2026-07-21:

- Neither ingredient is an approved drug product in the United States, and no approval was identified in
  any jurisdiction. No product containing both is approved anywhere.
- For ipamorelin, an advisory committee voted against adding it to the pharmacy compounding bulk
  substances list on October 29, 2024, and the agency had recommended against inclusion.
- For CJC-1295, five different forms were formally considered at an advisory committee meeting on
  December 4, 2024. That much is confirmed from the Federal Register. What the agency proposed, how the
  committee voted, and the current status are all unknown, because the agency's own website was
  unreachable throughout research.
- One point that is widely misreported: removal of a substance from a safety-risk category does not make
  it legal to compound. A legal analysis retrieved during research states that removed substances remain
  unlawful for compounding until they are formally added to the bulks list, which requires completion of
  formal rulemaking.
- Vendor claims that either compound is pending review at the July 2026 advisory committee meeting are
  contradicted by the Federal Register notice announcing that meeting. Neither compound is on its agenda.

If you compete in a tested sport, both ingredients are described in the peer-reviewed anti-doping
literature as prohibited, and ipamorelin is detectable in urine testing. A product containing both
contains two prohibited substances. Consult your own anti-doping authority rather than relying on
anything here.

Nothing in this answer is legal advice, and nothing in it addresses any country other than the United
States, because no other jurisdiction was checked.

## 8. Vendors describe ipamorelin as clinically studied and well tolerated. Is that wrong?

It is true in its parts and misleading as a whole.

The compound did reach Phase 2. The published trial did describe it as well tolerated in the setting it
was studied in. Both statements are accurate.

What is usually left out is how the story ends. The trials were run for postoperative ileus, a gut
motility problem after bowel surgery, which has nothing to do with what the compound is marketed for
now. The published trial did not meet its primary endpoint, and showed no advantage on secondary
outcomes either. A second, much larger trial of 320 participants completed in May 2014 and has never
reported results. Development was discontinued.

So citing the trial history as reassurance, while omitting that the trials failed and the programme was
dropped, does not simplify the evidence. It inverts its meaning.

## 9. What about the claim that ipamorelin is the selective one that does not raise cortisol?

That claim traces to a single 1998 study, and the study is real and well conducted. It was done in
primary rat pituitary cells, in anesthetized rats, and in conscious swine. In those models ipamorelin
released growth hormone comparably to another secretagogue without raising ACTH or cortisol above what
the natural releasing hormone produced, while two other compounds tested did raise them.

That is an animal and cell finding. No human study located tested ipamorelin's selectivity against
cortisol, ACTH, or prolactin. Whether it holds in people, and whether it holds with repeated rather than
single exposure, are both unstudied.

So the selectivity claim is not fabricated. It is preclinical, and it is routinely presented as though it
were a demonstrated human property. Those are different things.

## 11. If the ingredients are unapproved, what does that mean for what is in the vial?

It means the material comes from outside a regulated supply chain, so identity, purity, sterility, and
contamination are unverified by anyone with authority to verify them.

What is documented specifically: CJC-1295 has been identified by laboratory analysis in an unlabelled
illicit preparation of unknown origin submitted by police and customs authorities. Nobody knew what was
in it until it was analysed. That study is from 2010, so read it as evidence that the problem exists
rather than as a measurement of today's market.

What is documented about the channel generally: in an analytical study of commercially obtained synthetic
peptides, one of five products was found to be a completely different peptide than labelled, and two
thirds of the others were of insufficient quality by the study's criteria. **That study tested a
different peptide. It did not test either ingredient of this blend.** Separately, counterfeit and illegal
injectable peptide preparations are a recognised enforcement problem in Europe, sufficient that agencies
built a dedicated screening method covering 25 peptides found in seized products.

What is not documented: no independent analysis of any marketed product containing both compounds was
located, and no purity or identity survey of consumer products labelled as either ingredient was
located. Nobody appears to have checked. That is missing information, not a good result.

## 12. Why is the evidence so thin for compounds that have been around this long?

Different reasons for each, and both are informative.

CJC-1295 was a genuine drug candidate. The company ran early trials, published three of them, started a
Phase 2 study in patients with a real condition, and then that study was terminated with no results ever
posted. Development stopped. There is a tertiary account suggesting a safety-related reason involving a
trial subject's death, which the attending physician reportedly attributed to a pre-existing heart
condition unrelated to treatment. That account could not be confirmed by any primary source, so it is
recorded here as unverified. It cannot be stated as fact and it cannot be dismissed either.

Ipamorelin's story is more ordinary. It was developed, taken into Phase 2 for a specific medical
indication, tested, and the trial did not work. That is the normal fate of most drug candidates. The
programme stopped.

In both cases the compounds then continued to circulate commercially, marketed for uses that were never
the subject of any trial. The evidence is thin for the marketed uses because those uses were never
studied. The evidence exists for what was actually tested, and what was actually tested was hormone
levels in healthy volunteers, and gut motility after bowel surgery.

## 13. What would change your mind?

Concretely, and in order of how much weight it would carry:

1. **A controlled human trial of the combination**, measuring an outcome a person would notice rather
   than a hormone level, in a population resembling the people who use it, with results published in
   full. That would create the first combination evidence that exists.
2. **Confirmation of what is in the product**, meaning composition, ratio, which CJC-1295 molecule, and
   identity and quantity for each component from an accredited laboratory on the specific batch, with
   demonstrated blend uniformity.
3. **The results of the unreported 320-participant ipamorelin trial.** It is the largest human dataset on
   that compound and it has been unavailable since 2014.
4. **Long-term safety data for either ingredient.** Currently the longest human exposure identified is
   49 days for one and short in-hospital courses for the other.
5. **A human study testing whether ipamorelin's selectivity holds in people**, and whether it holds with
   repeated exposure.
6. **Primary regulatory records**, read directly, resolving what the agency actually concluded about the
   five CJC-1295 forms and confirming the ipamorelin position that currently reaches us secondhand.

If those arrived, this Guide would be revised and the revision would be recorded with its date in the
correction history.

## 14. What should I do with all this?

That is a decision for you and a clinician who knows your history, and this Guide does not make it.

What it can offer is the honest shape of the situation. There is no evidence about the combination. The
evidence about each ingredient separately establishes that each moves a hormone, and does not establish
that moving it benefits anyone. One ingredient's only published efficacy trial failed and its development
was abandoned. The other's identity is ambiguous by name, and half of what is sold under that name has
never been characterised in a person. Neither is approved anywhere identified. Long-term safety is
unstudied for both and entirely unstudied for the pair. What is in any given vial is unverified, and for
this product specifically, what is in it and in what proportion is not confirmed.

The Guide includes a list of questions written to be asked of a qualified professional as they stand. If
you want to take one thing from this document into a conversation, take that list.
