# Blend Contradictions and Divergences: Thymosin Alpha-1 + KPV + LL-37

Where the sources disagree, where commercial material disagrees with the published record, and
where an apparent disagreement is not actually a live one. Each entry states both positions and
then gives the assessment, so a member can see the reasoning rather than being handed a verdict.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

Source IDs refer to SOURCE_REGISTRY.md.

---

## C-01. Whether a combination of ingredients inherits the evidence for its ingredients

**Position A.** Each of these three compounds has been researched. Thymosin Alpha-1 has been
studied in randomised trials and is an approved medicine in some countries. LL-37 has been
studied in randomised trials. KPV has a preclinical literature going back decades. So a
preparation containing all three rests on that combined body of research.

**Position B.** No study of the combination exists (CB-01, CB-02, CB-03). A mixture is a
different thing from its parts. It has its own behaviour in the body, its own interactions
between the components, and its own unknowns, and none of those has been examined.

**Assessment.** Position B, and the gap between the two positions is not a matter of degree.
Position A performs a substitution that looks reasonable and is not: it treats separate facts
about three molecules as a single fact about one preparation. Nothing licenses that step. The
components have not been given together to a person, to an animal, or to cells in a dish, so
far as any of the three research records shows. There is no result to be cautious about, no
small study to weigh, no preliminary signal to hedge. There is nothing.

This is the most important entry in this document, and it is why this Guide exists as a
separate document rather than as a link to three component Guides. Three accurate component
summaries placed side by side will be read as a case for the mixture unless the reader is told
explicitly that they are not one.

Two further points sharpen it. First, the component evidence that does exist is largely
negative: the strongest trial of Thymosin Alpha-1 found no effect (TA-01), the largest trial of
LL-37 missed its primary endpoint (LL-01), and KPV has never been given to a person in any
recorded study (KP-01, KP-02). A blend is not being assembled from three established agents.
Second, because there is no registered trial of KPV of any kind, a registered three-component
trial containing KPV cannot exist in the trial registry record. That is not an inference about
biology. It is a consequence of two verified zero-result queries.

## C-02. Whether human data on KPV exists at all

**Position A.** Vendor, clinic, and peptide-marketing pages retrieved during research assert
that limited clinical data suggest good tolerability in humans, with preliminary evidence of
therapeutic benefit in inflammatory bowel disease and skin conditions, and reference more than
50 peer-reviewed publications establishing efficacy.

**Position B.** A direct trial registry query returned zero studies for KPV (KP-01). A
bibliographic search restricted to the clinical trial publication type returned zero records
(KP-02). The reported regulatory rationale for restricting KPV was an absence of human exposure
data (KP-07). Two peer-reviewed reviews describe KPV only as a candidate suitable for possible
future treatment (KP-05).

**Assessment.** Position B is correct and Position A is unsupported. The publication count
conflates the whole alpha-MSH literature, much of it on the full hormone and on the separate
compound KdPT, with KPV-specific evidence, and it conflates preclinical publications with
clinical ones. The claim of demonstrated human tolerability is not merely unproven. It is
contradicted by the regulator's own reported finding that human exposure data are absent.

## C-03. Whether Thymosin Alpha-1 reduces mortality in sepsis

**Position A.** A 2013 multicentre single-blind trial in 361 patients with severe sepsis
reported 28-day mortality of 26.0 percent versus 35.0 percent, relative risk 0.74, 95 percent
confidence interval 0.54 to 1.02, with the primary endpoint at P equals 0.062 and supporting
analyses reported as favourable. Its authors concluded it may improve outcomes in a targeted
population (TA-02).

**Position B.** A 2025 double-blind, placebo-controlled phase 3 trial in 1,089 adults with
sepsis reported 28-day mortality of 23.4 percent versus 24.1 percent, hazard ratio 0.99, 95
percent confidence interval 0.77 to 1.27, P equals 0.93, and concluded there is no clear
evidence that Thymosin Alpha-1 decreases 28-day all-cause mortality (TA-01).

**Assessment.** Resolved in favour of the null. This is not a live controversy and must not be
presented to a member as one. The later trial is roughly three times larger, double-blind,
placebo-controlled, phase 3, and it is the confirmatory study the earlier trial's own authors
called for. Its point estimate of 0.99 is essentially perfectly null rather than merely
non-significant. The earlier result is best read as a promising smaller single-blind signal
that did not survive rigorous confirmation, which is a common trajectory. Any text must lead
with the null trial, and citing the earlier trial without it would materially mislead.

## C-04. Whether Thymosin Alpha-1 reduces mortality in COVID-19

**Position A.** A 2023 meta-analysis pooling 8 studies reported lower mortality, risk ratio
0.59, 95 percent confidence interval 0.37 to 0.93, p equals 0.02 (TA-05).

**Position B.** The one randomised trial retrieved in this indication was null on its primary
endpoint of time to clinical recovery, subdistribution hazard ratio 0.80, 95 percent confidence
interval 0.42 to 1.55, p equals 0.52, with no significant differences in mortality, mechanical
ventilation, or intensive care admission (TA-03).

**Assessment.** Unresolved, but weighted toward no established benefit. Three things argue
against accepting the positive pooled estimate. Heterogeneity was substantial at I-squared of
84 percent, which materially undermines the pooled number. The randomised versus observational
composition of the pooled studies could not be confirmed and appears to lean observational, and
observational COVID-19 treatment data were highly vulnerable to confounding by which patients
received treatment and by rapidly changing standard care. And the meta-analysis authors
themselves state that randomised trials are still required.

A positive pooled estimate from largely observational data sitting beside a null randomised
trial is a recognised pattern of confounding, not corroboration. The null trial was itself a
small pilot terminated early for administrative reasons, so it does not definitively exclude a
modest effect either. The honest reading is that efficacy in this indication was never
established by adequate randomised evidence before attention moved on.

## C-05. Whether Thymosin Alpha-1 is an approved medicine

**Position A.** It is reported to be an approved prescription medicine under a brand name in a
number of countries, principally for chronic hepatitis B, and is referenced in connection with
orphan drug designations (TA-09).

**Position B.** It has no United States marketing approval, and in the United States it has
been treated as a compounding substance placed in Category 2 in 2023 on safety-risk grounds
(TA-08).

**Assessment.** Both are true, and the distinction is the point. This is the single most
important framing issue for this component and the one most exploited in marketing. Approval in
another country reflects that regulator's decision on that regulator's evidence standard and
does not transfer. Orphan drug designation is a development incentive status, not marketing
approval, and it is routinely presented as though it were approval.

There is a third step that matters even more here, because this is a blend. Approval of a
single agent, wherever it holds, is not approval of a preparation that contains that agent
alongside two others. No regulator anywhere has evaluated this combination.

A verification caveat belongs with this entry. Every fda.gov URL returned HTTP 404 during
research, so the United States statements rest on secondary reporting and on a secondary drug
reference. A primary regulator record confirming the non-US approval list was also not
obtained, and the commonly repeated country count traces to marketing material and is not
reproduced anywhere in this folder.

## C-06. The current United States compounding position for Thymosin Alpha-1

**Position A.** Numerous vendor and clinic websites state that Thymosin Alpha-1 was
reclassified out of Category 2 in February 2026 and is legally available by prescription
through compounding pharmacies.

**Position B.** The only independent journalism retrieved, dated 2026-04-03, describes the
regulator as poised to reverse the 2023 categorisation under current leadership, with the
change contested by former agency officials. It does not describe a completed February 2026
reclassification (TA-08).

**Assessment.** Unresolved, and the vendor claim is unverified and commercially interested. A
source with a financial interest in a product being available is the weakest possible source
for the claim that it has become available. The chronology is also internally inconsistent: an
April 2026 investigative piece describing a reversal as pending is hard to reconcile with a
completed February 2026 reclassification.

This status is genuinely in flux. A human reviewer must verify the current position against
primary regulator sources immediately before publication, because a Guide that states the wrong
legal status could directly influence a purchasing decision.

## C-07. The status of the July 2026 advisory committee review of KPV

**Position A.** Multiple search-engine summaries and vendor status-tracker pages state in the
past tense that the committee discussed KPV on 23 July 2026 and that a position was proposed.

**Position B.** The meeting was scheduled for 23 to 24 July 2026 and, as of the KPV research
date of 2026-07-20, had not taken place. The two contemporaneous trade sources, both dated
2026-04-16, describe it in the future tense (KP-07, KP-08).

**Assessment.** Position B. Position A is either speculative pre-publication commentary or a
date-handling artifact. No outcome is stated anywhere in this folder. Anyone updating this
Guide must read the actual record rather than a summary of it, and should note that a committee
recommendation is advisory and is not a final regulatory determination in any case.

## C-08. Whether KPV acts through melanocortin receptors

**Position A.** KPV is derived from alpha-MSH, the classical melanocortin hormone, which
invites the assumption that it acts as a melanocortin agonist.

**Position B.** A 2010 review states KPV lacks the entire sequence motif required for binding to
any of the known melanocortin receptors. The murine colitis work found effects partially
independent of melanocortin-1 receptor signalling. A study in cultured human keratinocytes found
calcium responses without the accompanying second messenger of the classical pathway. A study in
a cultured human bronchial epithelial cell line found that a related peptide acted through a
melanocortin receptor while KPV did not (KP-04, KP-05).

**Assessment.** Position B is supported by the retrieved literature, and the same 2010 review
concedes that KPV's actual mechanism is unknown. This matters twice. It removes any inference
drawn from melanocortin agonist compounds such as melanotan-type peptides. And it means the
honest description is that the mechanism is unresolved rather than well characterised. Marketing
material describing a confident mechanism overstates the published science.

## C-09. Whether the largest LL-37 trial was a positive result

**Position A.** Vendor and affiliate pages present the phase IIb venous leg ulcer trial as
supportive, quoting 28.1 percent on LL-37 versus 8.1 percent on placebo as the headline result.

**Position B.** The published trial reports that the primary endpoint was not met. Confirmed
complete wound closure was 26.5 percent and 24.7 percent in the two active groups versus 25.3
percent in placebo, not statistically significant. The quoted figure comes from a post-hoc
analysis restricted to patients with larger wounds (LL-01).

**Assessment.** The primary source decisively contradicts the vendor framing. This is a negative
trial on its prespecified primary endpoint. The quoted numbers are real, but they are reproduced
stripped of the fact that they are post-hoc, subgroup-restricted, and generated after the
primary endpoint had already failed. Post-hoc subgroup findings are hypothesis-generating and a
well-known source of false positives, and this one has not been replicated. Stating plainly that
the largest human trial of LL-37 did not meet its primary endpoint is the single most important
correction for this component.

## C-10. Whether the early LL-37 efficacy signal replicated

**Position A.** A 2014 first-in-man trial in 34 participants reported roughly sixfold higher
healing rates than placebo in its lowest-strength group (LL-02).

**Position B.** The 2021 phase IIb trial in 148 treated patients, by overlapping authors in the
same indication, failed to confirm this on its primary endpoint (LL-01).

**Assessment.** A textbook example of a promising small early-phase signal failing to replicate
at adequate sample size. The earlier result should be treated as superseded rather than as
independent supporting evidence. Presenting the two trials additively as two positive trials
would be misleading. It is also worth noting that in the earlier trial the response did not
increase with strength, a pattern the later trial did not resolve in favour of efficacy.

## C-11. Whether LL-37 is protective or harmful

**Position A.** LL-37 is framed as a host-defence peptide with antimicrobial, wound-healing,
and immune-modulating benefit (LL-06).

**Position B.** LL-37 is described as an autoantigen in psoriasis, is implicated in the
mechanisms of systemic lupus erythematosus through complexes with a person's own DNA that drive
type I interferon, is reported at elevated levels in several inflammatory diseases, and damages
human red blood cells, lymphocytes, and fibroblasts in laboratory culture at concentrations near
those needed for its antimicrobial effect (LL-06, LL-07).

**Assessment.** Both are supported in the retrieved peer-reviewed literature. This is a
genuinely dual-natured, context-dependent and concentration-dependent molecule, not a case of
one side being wrong. Any account presenting only the host-defence framing is materially
incomplete. The source literature itself frames the molecule as sitting between the two roles.

A qualification belongs with position B: the autoimmunity evidence is an association and a
proposed mechanism in people who already have autoimmune disease, not a demonstrated consequence
of administering LL-37 to someone. The source review also notes that some studies find no
association with disease activity. Both of those qualifications should travel with the claim.

## C-12. Mechanism plausibility against measured effect in people

**Position A.** LL-37 is characterised mechanistically as a broad-spectrum antimicrobial
peptide (LL-06).

**Position B.** In the one retrieved human trial that directly measured it, LL-37 applied to
diabetic foot ulcers did not significantly reduce bacterial colonisation compared with placebo,
and did not significantly reduce inflammatory markers either (LL-03).

**Assessment.** A notable gap between mechanism and measured human outcome. Mechanistic
plausibility did not translate on the endpoint most directly derived from the mechanism. This
entry is included because it is the clearest single illustration in this folder of why a
mechanistic story is not evidence of an effect, which is the same reasoning error that produces
combination claims.

The same pattern appears on the Thymosin Alpha-1 side. In the two trials where an immune
biomarker moved, the clinical endpoint did not follow (TA-02, TA-03).

## C-13. Where a preclinical result does not mean what it appears to mean

**Position A.** KPV reduced mucosal damage and inflammatory signalling in a mouse model of
ulcerative colitis, which supports its use for gut inflammation (KP-04).

**Position B.** The tested article in that study was KPV carried in an engineered
hyaluronic-acid-functionalised targeting nanoparticle, not plain KPV, and the paper's own
framing is that the targeting vehicle drove the advantage over non-targeted formulations
(KP-04).

**Assessment.** Position B. The result does not transfer to unformulated KPV and must never be
cited as though it did. A second caution belongs alongside it: rodent colitis models have a long
history of failing to predict human inflammatory bowel disease outcomes, so even the plain-KPV
mouse results (KP-04) carry limited predictive weight.

## C-14. Where supply-channel evidence is about the channel, not the compound

**Position A.** A peer-reviewed 2024 market-surveillance study found that every vial purchased
from illegal online pharmacies was a probable substandard or falsified product, with measured
purity far below the labelled claim and contamination in every sample. That describes what these
products are like (KP-06).

**Position B.** That study tested a different peptide entirely. It tested none of the three
components of this blend.

**Assessment.** Both readings need to be held at once, and the distinction must be stated every
time the study is cited. It is real, peer-reviewed evidence about how the unregulated online
supply channel performs, and that channel is where unapproved material is typically obtained. It
is not evidence about the identity, purity, or contamination of any product containing these
three compounds. No study testing material sold as Thymosin Alpha-1, as KPV, or as LL-37 was
located for any of the three. That is a gap, not a favourable result.

## C-15. Where a source is genuinely absent rather than merely thin

Three absences in this folder are frequently misread, and they are different from one another.

1. **The combination has no evidence.** Nobody has studied it. Nothing is known in either
   direction (CB-01, CB-02, CB-03).
2. **KPV has no human evidence.** Nobody has given it to a person in any recorded study
   (KP-01, KP-02).
3. **The Thymosin Alpha-1 preclinical table in this folder is empty because no primary
   preclinical study was retrieved in that research session, not because none exists.** This is
   a limit of this Guide, and it must be written as one. It is the only one of the three
   absences that is a search limitation rather than a finding.

Conflating the third with the first two would misrepresent the literature. Conflating the first
two with anything reassuring would misrepresent safety. Absence of study is not evidence of
safety, and it is not evidence of danger. It is absence of information.

## C-16. A note on the integrity of the regulatory record

Across all three component research sessions, every attempt to reach the United States
regulator's own web pages failed. Direct fetches returned HTTP 404 and a mirror returned HTTP
403, in a pattern consistent with automated-access blocking rather than the documents not
existing. A government notice was blocked by a redirect and its public-inspection document was
unparseable. The anti-doping list could not be retrieved on any attempt.

The consequence is that every regulatory statement in this folder rests on secondary sources:
investigative journalism, regulatory trade press, a professional society, an advocacy
submission, and a secondary drug reference. Those are reasonable sources for reporting what a
regulator did, and they are not the regulator.

This is recorded here as a contradiction entry rather than buried in a gap list because it is a
disagreement between the standard this Guide sets for itself and what the research was able to
achieve. It is not resolved. A human reviewer must open the primary pages directly before
publication, and no regulatory statement in this folder should be treated as settled until they
have.
