# Thymosin Alpha-1 + KPV + LL-37: questions members actually ask

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## 1. Has this combination been studied?

No.

We searched three web-verified research records covering all three components for any study of
the three together, any study of any two of them together, and any work on how they interact,
whether they are compatible in one preparation, or how the body handles them together. Nothing
was found in any of those categories.

There is one structural point worth knowing, because it makes the answer firmer than a failed
search usually would. KPV has no registered clinical trial of any kind. A direct trial registry
query returned zero studies (verified 2026-07-20). So a registered trial of a combination
containing KPV cannot exist in that record.

---

## 2. But all three ingredients have been researched. Doesn't that count for something?

It counts for the ingredients. It does not count for the mixture.

This is the point the whole Guide is built around, so it is worth being precise. When you put
active compounds together, you get a preparation that behaves in its own way. The components can
affect each other. They can change how the body handles each of them. Questions come into
existence that did not exist for any of them alone. None of that is answered by research on the
ingredients separately, because none of it was ever the subject of a study.

The everyday version: knowing what three drugs do individually does not tell a pharmacist
whether it is fine to take them together. That is why interaction checking is a separate job
from knowing what each drug does. Here, that job has not been done at all.

So when a page lists impressive-sounding findings for each ingredient in turn, notice what is
happening. Every individual sentence can be true while the overall impression, that there is a
case for the combination, is false.

## 3. What is actually in the product?

We do not know, and neither does anyone reading the label alone.

The exact composition and ingredient ratio of this product is **not confirmed**. No proportion is
stated anywhere in this Guide because none is known to us. That is the first question any
supplier would have to answer in writing, and the answer would then need to be checked
analytically rather than accepted.

Until that is settled, the product cannot be described accurately, and nothing downstream of the
description can be assessed.

---

## 4. Isn't Thymosin Alpha-1 an approved drug? Doesn't that make this legitimate?

Three separate things are true and they are easy to run together.

**It is a real pharmaceutical.** Thymosin Alpha-1, in its synthetic form called thymalfasin, is
reported to be an approved prescription medicine in some countries outside the United States,
principally for chronic hepatitis B (checked 2026-07-19). That is genuinely unusual in this
category.

**It is not approved in the United States.** It has no United States marketing approval for any
indication (checked 2026-07-19). Approval elsewhere reflects that regulator's decision on that
regulator's evidence standard. It does not transfer.

**Approval of one ingredient is not approval of a mixture containing it.** No regulator anywhere
has evaluated this three-component combination.

Two more distortions worth recognising. Orphan drug designation, which is sometimes cited in
connection with this compound, is a development incentive status and not marketing approval, and
it is routinely presented as though it were. And the material sold under this name outside a
regulated supply chain is not necessarily the same substance as the approved product. Because a
real regulated drug exists, unregulated material can borrow its credibility without being it.

---

## 5. What does the evidence on each ingredient actually show?

Shorter and less encouraging than the marketing suggests.

**Thymosin Alpha-1.** The largest and best-designed trial of this compound in any indication was
a double-blind, placebo-controlled phase 3 trial in 1,089 adults with sepsis. It found
essentially no effect on 28-day death rates: 23.4 percent versus 24.1 percent, hazard ratio 0.99,
P equals 0.93. That trial was the confirmatory study an earlier, smaller, single-blind trial's
authors had specifically called for, and the earlier favourable signal did not survive it. In
COVID-19, the one randomised trial retrieved was null on its primary endpoint. Every trial
studied ill people; none studied healthy adults.

**KPV.** Zero human studies. A trial registry query returned zero studies and a literature search
restricted to clinical trials returned zero records (verified 2026-07-20). When the United States
regulator restricted it, the reported reason included an absence of human exposure data. There is
a real preclinical literature in mice and in cultured cells, and a peer-reviewed review from 2010
states that even its signalling mechanism is unknown.

**LL-37.** Real randomised trials exist, and they are narrow and mostly negative. The largest, in
148 treated patients with hard-to-heal leg ulcers, **missed its primary endpoint**. In a diabetic
foot ulcer trial, a peptide described as antimicrobial did not significantly reduce bacterial
colonisation compared with placebo. A melanoma study enrolled four people over five years, one of
whom withdrew for lack of efficacy.

---

## 6. I have seen a striking number quoted for LL-37: 28 percent versus 8 percent. Is that real?

The numbers are real. The framing usually attached to them is not.

That comparison comes from the 148-patient phase IIb trial. What is normally left out is that the
trial **missed its prespecified primary endpoint**. Complete wound closure was 26.5 percent and
24.7 percent in the two active groups against 25.3 percent in placebo, which is no difference.

The 28.1 percent versus 8.1 percent figure comes from an analysis done afterwards, restricted to
patients with larger wounds. Analyses decided on after the results are in can generate ideas but
cannot confirm them, because if you slice a dataset enough ways one slice will eventually look
favourable by chance. No benefit was seen in smaller wounds, and the subgroup finding has never
been replicated.

Quoting the subgroup number without the failed primary endpoint alongside it is the most common
distortion attached to this compound. When you see it, that is what is happening.

---

## 7. Is it safe?

We cannot tell you it is safe, and nobody honestly can.

For the combination, nothing at all is known. No study has given these three together to
anything. Absence of reported harm is not a safety record; it reflects the fact that nobody has
looked.

For the components, the reassuring-looking data is real but narrow. Trial safety findings were
unremarkable in a 1,089-patient sepsis trial, in an earlier sepsis trial, in a COVID-19 pilot, and
in the LL-37 wound trials. **All of those describe short-course use in acutely ill, medically
supervised hospital patients, or application to a wound over weeks.** None of it describes
elective long-term use by a healthy adult, which is how these compounds are actually promoted.
Adjusting immune function in someone whose immune system is working normally is not a low-stakes
intervention.

There are also specific concerns worth naming. Labelling for the Thymosin Alpha-1 pharmaceutical
describes it as contraindicated in people being deliberately immunosuppressed, including organ
transplant recipients. LL-37 is described in the peer-reviewed literature as an autoantigen in
psoriasis and is implicated in the mechanisms of lupus, and whether giving it could trigger or
worsen such conditions has not been studied. In laboratory culture, LL-37 damages human red blood
cells, lymphocytes, and fibroblasts at concentrations similar to those needed for its
antimicrobial effect.

---

## 8. Do the three ingredients interact with each other?

Nobody knows. That is the honest answer and it is not a formality here.

No study examined any pairing of these three. Two of them are described in the literature as
acting on immune function, and not obviously in the same direction. One is proposed to enhance
immune competence. Another is studied preclinically for suppressing inflammatory signalling. A
third is described as both host defence and, in other contexts, an autoimmune driver.

What happens when those are combined has not been looked at. It cannot be assumed to be the sum
of the parts, and the absence of a study is not a finding that there is no interaction.

---

## 9. Is it legal?

That depends on the jurisdiction, and the position is genuinely unsettled in places. Everything
here is dated because regulatory status expires.

**The combination.** Not an approved medicine anywhere, and no regulator has evaluated it as a
combination (checked 2026-07-21).

**Thymosin Alpha-1.** No United States marketing approval (checked 2026-07-19). Placed in a
restricted compounding category in 2023 on safety-risk grounds. The current position is
unsettled: independent reporting from April 2026 describes a reversal as anticipated and
contested, while multiple vendor sites assert a completed February 2026 reclassification that
could not be verified in any independent source. A source with a financial interest in a product
being available is the weakest possible source for the claim that it has become available.

**KPV.** Not approved. Placed in the same restricted compounding category. Scheduled for advisory
committee review on 23 to 24 July 2026, which had not occurred as of the research date of
2026-07-20. **No outcome is stated in this Guide**, despite several websites already describing
one in the past tense. A committee recommendation is advice, not a final determination.

**LL-37.** No approved product identified. Secondary sources report a review scheduled before the
end of February 2027, which could not be confirmed because the relevant pages were unreachable.

An integrity note: every attempt to reach the United States regulator's own web pages failed
across all three research sessions. Every regulatory statement here rests on secondary reporting
and needs human confirmation at source.

## 10. What about anti-doping? I am a tested athlete.

**This Guide states no anti-doping status for any of these three components, or for the
combination.**

The prohibited list could not be retrieved or parsed in any of the three research sessions. Every
source we encountered asserting a status was a vendor or peptide-directory page, which is not
acceptable evidence for a claim of this kind.

If you are subject to testing, check the current prohibited list directly and speak to your own
anti-doping authority. Do not rely on anything a seller tells you, and do not rely on the absence
of a statement here.

One thing worth knowing: Thymosin Beta-4, also sold as TB-500, is a different peptide with a
different anti-doping position, and the two are constantly confused.

## 12. If it comes from things the body already makes, doesn't that make it gentler?

That reasoning is intuitive and it does not hold.

Two of the three components are related to substances present naturally in the human body.
Thymosin Alpha-1 is present physiologically. LL-37 is produced naturally by human surface-lining
and immune cells. KPV corresponds to a fragment of a natural hormone.

What matters is not origin but what happens when a substance is introduced from outside, in a
different amount, at a different time, and in a different place from where the body would produce
it. LL-37 is the clearest illustration in this Guide. The body makes it, and the peer-reviewed
literature also describes it as an autoantigen in psoriasis, as implicated in lupus, as elevated
in several inflammatory diseases, and as damaging to human red blood cells, lymphocytes, and
fibroblasts in laboratory culture at concentrations near its antimicrobial range. The same
molecule, in the same body, is described as protective in one context and harmful in another.

Naturally occurring is not a safety argument.

## 14. What would change your mind about this blend?

A study of the combination. That is the whole answer, and it is worth stating what would count.

- A study, in any species or model, that gave these three components together and recorded what
  happened. Even a preclinical study would change the picture from nothing to something, though
  a preclinical result would still not describe what happens in a person.
- Work on whether the components interact, whether they are chemically compatible in one
  preparation, and how the body handles them together.
- Confirmed composition and ratio, documented and independently verified.
- Analytical testing showing what is actually in the product, with identity and content
  established for each component separately, and the mixture shown to be uniform.

Separately, for the components, we would want to see human evidence of any kind for KPV, and
prospective replication of the LL-37 subgroup finding rather than the post-hoc analysis that
currently stands in for it.

None of these exists today. If any appears, this Guide will be updated and the change recorded in
its correction history.

## 15. So what should I do with all this?

That is your decision and a clinician's, not ours. What this Guide can offer is what to hold on
to.

The combination has never been studied. The composition is not confirmed. Of the three
ingredients, the strongest trial of one found no effect, the largest trial of another missed its
primary endpoint, and the third has never been given to a person in any recorded study. The
safety information that looks reassuring comes from ill, supervised patients over short periods,
not from healthy adults over long ones. The legal position is unsettled and the anti-doping
position is unknown to us.

If you are considering this anyway, the Guide has a list of questions worth taking to a clinician
who knows your history. Being able to ask precise questions is the most useful thing this
document can give you.
