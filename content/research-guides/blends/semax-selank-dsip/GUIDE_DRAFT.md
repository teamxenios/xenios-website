# Semax + Selank + DSIP

This Guide is educational. It is not medical advice, and it is not a recommendation to use
anything described in it. Xenios does not sell, supply, source, or obtain this product or any
of the compounds named in it, and this Guide contains no guidance on how to obtain them.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## One-minute summary

This is a blend product containing three named compounds. Two facts about it matter more than
anything else in this Guide, and both are absences.

**First, nobody has ever studied this combination.** Every source consulted was searched for a
study that administered Semax, Selank, and DSIP together. There is none. No human study, no
animal study, no laboratory study, no trial registration anywhere, and no regulator has
evaluated the mixture as a product. There is no two-component evidence either: nothing tested
Semax with Selank, Semax with DSIP, or Selank with DSIP as a co-administered preparation.

Here is why that matters more than it first looks. **Put three compounds into one preparation
and you have made a fourth thing.** The mixture has its own pharmacology, because the
components can change how the body handles each other. It has its own interaction profile,
because they can act on each other and not only on the body. And it has its own unknowns, which
are not the unknowns of any one of them alone. Only a study of the actual mixture can speak to
any of that. **Unless a study tested this combination, there is no combination evidence. Not
weak evidence. Not preliminary evidence. None.**

**Second, what is actually in the product is not confirmed.** No supplier specification, no
certificate of analysis, and no manufacturing record was available for this Guide. So this
Guide states no ingredient ratio, no proportion, and no strength, because nobody has shown us.
That is the first open question for the supplier and it is listed as such below.

A specific point that follows from the three compounds involved: **all three are reported to
act on the central nervous system.** Three centrally acting compounds in one preparation is a
reason for more caution, not less, and the combined neurological and psychiatric interaction
profile has never been studied in anyone.

What does exist is evidence about the three compounds studied separately. It is thinner, older,
and more negative than most people expect, and it is set out honestly below. In summary:

- **Semax.** Four human studies, none described as randomized, placebo-controlled and blinded
  with the method stated. Two are Russian-language clinical studies in stroke and
  cerebrovascular illness. Two are small brain imaging studies in healthy volunteers that
  measured brain connectivity, not cognition, mood, or symptoms. Essentially the whole human
  literature traces to two affiliated Russian research groups, one of which appears to include
  the compound's originators. Zero Semax trials are registered on ClinicalTrials.gov.
- **Selank.** Genuine human trials exist, which is unusual in this category, so this Guide does
  not present an empty table for it. Three trials enrolling roughly 192 patients in total, all
  Russian-language, all from one overlapping research network, with **no placebo-controlled
  trial anywhere** and zero trial registrations.
- **DSIP.** Every human sleep study located dates from 1981 to 1992. That is more than three
  decades with no modern replication and zero registered trials. The most-cited positive result
  came from an open, uncontrolled study of 7 people. When the question was put to two
  double-blind controlled tests, the effect largely evaporated, and both sets of authors
  concluded the benefit was not clinically meaningful. Underneath that, no DSIP gene, precursor,
  or receptor has ever been identified in any species.

On regulation, two things must be kept strictly apart. Semax and Selank were reported to be
registered medicines in **the Russian Federation**, and two independent Russian trade outlets
report that those registrations were **cancelled in January 2026** at the request of the
certificate holders. **Registration in Russia, current or historical, confers nothing anywhere
else.** In **the United States**, none of the three compounds is FDA approved. Semax free base
and Semax acetate were nominated by an outside party for possible inclusion on a list governing
what pharmacies may use in compounding, with a committee meeting scheduled for 23 to 24 July
2026 that had not occurred as of the date of this Guide. A nomination is not an approval and no
outcome exists.

And separately from all of that: **registration of two single agents, in one country, at some
point in the past, is not registration of a three-component blend.** No regulator anywhere has
evaluated this product.

There is no overall grade for this product. Grades are assigned to individual claims in
CLAIM_TABLE.md, and deliberately not to the blend, because a summary grade would imply that
component evidence adds up into product evidence. It does not.

## What it is

This is a blend sold under a single product name, containing three compounds in one
preparation. Beyond that, the honest description is short.

**Composition is reported, not confirmed.** No supplier specification, certificate of analysis,
or manufacturing record was available for this Guide, so nothing about the amounts or the
relative proportions of the three components is stated here. A Guide cannot describe what
nobody has documented.

**The three named components.**

- **Semax** is described as a synthetic heptapeptide, meaning a chain of seven amino acids. It
  is described as an analogue of a fragment of adrenocorticotropic hormone, a natural hormone,
  extended at one end with three further amino acids. It was first described in the scientific
  literature in 1991. The sequence and the analogue description come from a tertiary source and
  have not been confirmed against a primary chemical registry.
  [UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]
- **Selank** is described as a synthetic heptapeptide derived from tuftsin, a naturally
  occurring immune peptide. It appears in the United States substance registry under its own
  name, which confirms it is a recognised discrete substance rather than a name invented by
  sellers. **The commonly cited amino acid sequence and the tuftsin-derivation detail appeared
  only in vendor and blog sources during research**, because the registry records themselves
  rendered no readable content.
  [UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]
- **DSIP**, or delta sleep-inducing peptide, is described as a nonapeptide, meaning a chain of
  nine amino acids. It was first isolated in 1974 from the cerebral venous blood of rabbits
  during induced sleep. In regulatory documents it appears under the name **emideltide**, which
  matters because a member searching regulatory records will not find it under DSIP.

**An unusual problem with DSIP specifically, at the level of basic identity.** In the more than
fifty years since its isolation, **no DSIP gene, precursor peptide, or receptor has been
identified in any species.** A 2006 review in the Journal of Neurochemistry treats this as the
central unresolved problem and notes that the link between DSIP and sleep was never further
characterised, in part because the gene was never isolated. What that means practically is that
the synthetic peptide sold under this name cannot be confirmed to correspond to a real
signalling molecule in the human body. That is a weakness at a deeper level than thin clinical
evidence, and it is worth understanding before anything else about DSIP.

**A name is not an identity.** Regulatory filings treat a substance's free base and its acetate
salt as separate substances. The retrieved United States notice lists "Semax (free base)" and
"Semax acetate" as two distinct nominated substances, and the substance registry carries both
SELANK and SELANK DIACETATE. A blend label carries several such names at once, so the names on a
label do not by themselves settle what is in a unit.

**No official quality standard exists.** No pharmacopoeial monograph was located for any of the
three components, so there is no official identity, purity, or quality specification that any
material can be held to.

## Why interest exists

Interest in a blend of this shape comes from three places. Two of them are reasoning errors,
and it is worth naming all three plainly.

**The story is coherent.** Each component has a described biological rationale, and laid side
by side they read as a designed system: something for cognition, something for anxiety,
something for sleep. That impression is produced by presentation, not by evidence. Nobody
designed and tested this system. Three separate research literatures, from different decades
and different countries, have been arranged next to each other.

**Each name has publications behind it.** Semax has roughly 230 records in PubMed. Selank has
genuine clinical trials. DSIP has been studied since 1974. Volume is not the same as strength,
and it is certainly not the same as relevance to a mixture. Of the first 20 Semax results
reviewed, roughly 19 were preclinical or narrative review and one was a human clinical study.
For Selank, a filtered PubMed search returns six records of which three are unrelated false
positives, so a raw count overstates the base by half. For DSIP, the entire human literature is
three small studies, all from 1981 to 1992.

**The arithmetic assumption.** The most common reason a blend feels attractive is the belief
that three studied ingredients make a better-evidenced product than one. This is the assumption
this Guide exists to interrupt. Evidence attaches to what was actually administered. A mixture
that has never been administered in any study has no evidence, whatever its ingredients have
been through separately. Combining compounds does not add their evidence together. In practical
terms it does the opposite: it removes the ability to attribute any effect, good or bad, to
anything in particular.

## Terminology

Plain-language definitions of every technical term used in this Guide.

**Blend.** A product containing more than one active compound in a single preparation. For
evidence purposes a blend is its own distinct object, not a collection of its parts.

**Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids, which are the building blocks of proteins. All three
components here are peptides.

**Heptapeptide.** A peptide of exactly seven amino acids. Semax and Selank are described as
heptapeptides.

**Nonapeptide.** A peptide of exactly nine amino acids. DSIP is described as a nonapeptide.

**Analogue.** A molecule deliberately made to resemble another one, usually with a small change.

**Endogenous.** Occurring naturally within the body. A compound can be synthetic and still be
described as endogenous if the same molecule occurs naturally, which for DSIP was never
established.

**Preclinical.** Research done in animals, in cultured cells, or in isolated tissue rather than
in living people. A preclinical finding says nothing directly about what happens in a person.

**In vitro.** Literally "in glass". Work done in a dish rather than in a living body. Cells
taken from humans and grown in a dish are still preclinical.

**Cell line.** Cells grown indefinitely in a laboratory. A human-derived cell line is still
cells in a dish, not a person.

**Randomised controlled trial.** A study in which people are assigned by chance to receive
either the thing being tested or a comparison, so that differences between the groups are less
likely to be explained by who ended up in which group.

**Placebo-controlled.** A study with a comparison group receiving an inactive preparation.
Without one, improvement cannot be separated from expectation or from the natural course of the
condition.

**Blinded.** A study in which the participants, the assessors, or both do not know who received
the active compound. Blinding matters most when the outcome involves judgement, which sleep and
anxiety outcomes very much do.

**Open-label and uncontrolled.** A study in which everyone knows what they are receiving and
there is no comparison group. It is the weakest common design.

**Crossover.** A study in which each participant receives both the tested compound and the
comparison at different times, acting as their own comparison.

**Post hoc analysis.** A result found after the fact by looking at part of the data that was not
the pre-specified question. It can generate a hypothesis. It cannot confirm one, and it is a
well-known source of false positives.

**Trial registration.** Publicly recording a trial's design and intended outcome measures before
it runs. Without it there is no protection against reporting only the results that came out
well.

**Systematic review and meta-analysis.** Structured summaries that appraise the quality of
studies and combine their results. Neither exists for any of these three compounds.

**Narrative review.** An article summarising a field without a structured method for appraising
bias. Useful for orientation, not evidence of effect.

**Pharmacokinetics.** What the body does with a substance: how much is absorbed, where it goes,
how it is broken down, and how quickly it leaves.

**Immunogenicity.** Whether a substance provokes an immune response against itself, such as
antibody formation.

**Anxiolytic.** Anxiety-reducing.

**Nootropic.** A marketing term for a substance claimed to improve cognition. It is not a
regulatory or scientific category.

**Benzodiazepine.** A class of prescription sedative and anti-anxiety medicines. Medazepam,
phenazepam, and diazepam, all named in the Selank literature, are benzodiazepines.

**GABAergic.** Relating to GABA, the brain's main calming signalling chemical.

**Monoaminergic.** Relating to signalling chemicals including dopamine and serotonin.

**Slow wave sleep, also called delta sleep.** The deepest stage of non-REM sleep. DSIP is named
for it.

**Stage 2 sleep.** A lighter stage of non-REM sleep.

**REM sleep.** The sleep stage associated with dreaming.

**Polysomnography.** Overnight sleep recording that measures sleep stages objectively rather
than by self-report.

**Sleep latency.** How long it takes to fall asleep.

**Sleep efficiency.** The proportion of time in bed actually spent asleep.

**Resting-state functional MRI.** A brain scan that measures which regions vary together while a
person lies still doing nothing. It measures brain activity patterns, not thinking, memory, or
symptoms.

**Default mode network.** A set of brain regions that are active when a person is at rest rather
than doing a task.

**Surrogate endpoint.** Something measurable that stands in for an outcome a person would
actually notice. A brain scan difference is a surrogate. Sleeping better is an outcome.

**503A bulk drug substances list.** A United States framework governing which raw substances
pharmacies may use in compounding. It is a compounding-eligibility mechanism, not a drug
approval pathway.

**Nomination.** A submission by an outside party asking that a substance be considered for that
list. A nomination is not an approval and not a finding of any kind.

**Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee.** The FDA advisory committee that reviews substances
nominated for that list. Its recommendations advise FDA and are not final FDA decisions.

**Marketing authorisation and registration.** The permissions a national regulator grants for a
medicine to be sold in that country, and only in that country.

**Certificate of analysis.** A document from a testing laboratory stating what a specific batch
of material was found to contain.

**Blend uniformity.** Whether every portion of a mixed product contains the same proportion of
each component.

## Proposed mechanism

**There is no proposed mechanism for this combination, because nobody has studied it.** Any
mechanistic account of this product is three separate component stories placed next to each
other. That is presentation, not evidence, and it is worth being explicit that assembling three
plausible stories does not produce a plausible fourth one.

Everything below is proposed, derives from animals or cultured cells, and has not been confirmed
in a living human being.

**Selank and GABA signalling.** In IMR-32 human neuroblastoma cells studied in a dish, GABA,
selank, and olanzapine were reported to affect the expression of genes involved in GABAergic
neurotransmission, which is the brain's main calming signalling system. Human-derived cells in
a dish are still preclinical, and this is a gene expression observation rather than a
demonstrated effect on anxiety in anyone.

**Semax mechanism is not publishable from what was retrieved.** Preclinical titles for Semax
describe effects on a growth factor, on dopaminergic and serotoninergic systems, on immune
response genes, and on opioid receptor gene expression. **The species for those individual
records was not confirmed during research**, because they were read from a search results
listing rather than fetched individually. Under house rules a preclinical finding cannot be
published without naming its model in the same sentence, so none of them appears in this Guide
as a finding. What can be said is that the breadth of these themes is itself an open safety
question rather than a selling point, which is addressed in the safety section.

**DSIP mechanism points away from the compound.** A 2006 critical review reports that certain
structural analogues of DSIP showed sleep-promoting activity in animal models while DSIP itself
did not demonstrate clear effects, and the authors propose that a DSIP-like peptide rather than
DSIP may account for the reported results. That is the most honest available summary of the
mechanism question for DSIP, and it points away from the molecule rather than toward it.

**DSIP across species, in a 1984 review.** A narrative review reported delta-sleep induction in
rabbits, rats, and mice, with a more pronounced REM sleep effect in cats, so the response was
not consistent across species. The same review described a non-monotonic activity curve, meaning
more was not more, and framed DSIP as a multifunctional neuroregulatory peptide rather than a
specific sleep agent.

**No human pharmacokinetic data exists for any of the three.** Nothing was retrieved about
absorption, distribution, metabolism, elimination, or duration for Semax, Selank, or DSIP, and
nothing about whether meaningful exposure in the central nervous system is achieved by any
route at all. Routes are named here only to state that evidence about them is absent.

## Human evidence

### For the combination: there is none

**No human study of Semax with Selank with DSIP exists.**

| Study | Design | Population | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| *(no records)* | | | |

The table is empty, and the empty table is the finding. There is no combination human evidence
to grade, to caveat, or to describe. There is also no two-component human evidence.

**One study needs naming, because it is the single most likely thing to be misread.** A
resting-state functional MRI study in 52 healthy adults examined **Selank and Semax** and
reported differences in connectivity between certain brain regions, which the authors described
as both general and specific effects of each peptide, identified through post hoc analysis
(PMID 32342318). Two compounds examined alongside each other, with their effects distinguished
from one another, is a comparison. It is the opposite of a combination: the design exists
precisely to tell them apart. The two were not administered together, and DSIP was not involved
at all. This study is not evidence for combining anything.

### For the components, studied separately

Everything in this section concerns one compound studied by itself. None of it is evidence about
this product, and reading down the three sections and adding them together is exactly the error
this Guide exists to prevent.

#### Semax: four studies, none independent, none registered

Four human studies were located.

**110 adults after ischemic stroke.** In a study of 110 adults (43 men and 67 women, mean age
approximately 58 years), the authors reported that Semax together with high levels of a growth
factor was associated with faster improvement and a better final outcome on functional recovery
measures. The study was not randomized, had no placebo group, and did not state blinding, and
early initiation of rehabilitation independently improved outcomes in the same study. Because
rehabilitation timing and Semax exposure were not separated by randomization, the reported Semax
effect cannot be disentangled from rehabilitation intensity or from differences between the
groups at the start (PMID 29798983, Russian-language, English abstract only).

**187 patients with chronic cerebrovascular insufficiency.** In a 2005 study, the authors
reported clinical improvement, stabilization of disease progression, a reduced reported risk of
stroke and transient ischemic attacks, and good tolerability across age groups. The design was
not stated as randomized or placebo-controlled. **Outcome claims of that magnitude would
normally require a randomized, adequately powered, prospectively registered trial**, and this
study has none of those properties. It has not been independently replicated outside Russia in
anything retrieved. Treat it as hypothesis-generating only (PMID 15792140, Russian-language,
English abstract only).

**24 healthy adults, brain imaging.** A small placebo-comparison imaging study (14 receiving
Semax and 10 placebo, mean age approximately 44 years) reported a greater volume of one
subcomponent of a resting brain network in the Semax group. **This is a neuroimaging signal, not
a clinical outcome. No cognitive, functional, or symptom benefit is demonstrated by it.**
Whether the study was randomized is genuinely unresolved from the abstract, so this Guide does
not describe it as a randomized controlled trial (PMID 30225715).

**52 healthy adults, brain imaging.** The Selank and Semax connectivity study described above,
whose findings the authors identify as post hoc. Post hoc connectivity findings at this sample
size are exploratory and require independent replication (PMID 32342318).

**The defining weakness is independence, not sample size.** The two imaging studies share almost
the same author list, and one of those authors is named in search summaries as a leader of the
group that originally developed Semax. The two clinical studies share the same senior author. So
essentially the entire retrieved human literature for Semax reduces to roughly two Russian
research groups, one of which appears to include the compound's originators, with no replication
by an unaffiliated group in another country.

**Three further facts about the Semax evidence base.** There is no systematic review or
meta-analysis of Semax: a PubMed search filtered to review types returned 14 records and none
was one. **Zero Semax trials are registered on ClinicalTrials.gov**, so pre-specified endpoints
cannot be checked against what was published, which matters acutely for findings the authors
themselves describe as post hoc. And **no study of Semax with a cognitive endpoint in healthy
people was located at all**, which is precisely the use the consumer market promotes.

#### Selank: real trials, small, and not independent

Selank is the one component where an empty evidence table would be dishonest. Genuine clinical
trials exist. They are small, none is placebo-controlled, and none is independent.

**62 adults with generalized anxiety disorder and neurasthenia.** In a comparison in which 30
received selank and 32 received the benzodiazepine medazepam, the authors reported that the
anxiolytic effects of the two were similar, with selank additionally reported to have
antiasthenic and psychostimulant effects. Only the abstract was retrievable, so the
randomization method, blinding, allocation concealment, effect sizes, and confidence intervals
could not be assessed (PMID 18454096, 2008, Russian-language).

**60 patients with anxiety disorders.** A comparison against the benzodiazepine phenazepam
reported pronounced anxiolytic and mild nootropic effects, with the anxiolytic effect reported
to persist for about a week after treatment ended, and a reported positive impact on quality of
life. This record is not indexed as a randomized controlled trial. Abstract-only retrieval
yielded no numeric outcomes, no variance, no dropout figures, and no adverse-event data
(PMID 25176261, 2014, Russian-language).

**70 patients with anxiety disorders.** In a comparison of phenazepam alone (30 patients) against
phenazepam plus selank (40 patients), the authors reported that the combined treatment decreased
the level of undesirable side effects of phenazepam, with a reported positive impact on quality
of life. **This is a question about reducing another medicine's side effects, not a
demonstration of standalone anxiolytic efficacy, and there was no placebo arm** (PMID 26356395,
2015, Russian-language).

**Patients with anxiety-asthenic disorders, immunological endpoints.** A study combining
laboratory work with observations in patients reported cytokine-regulating effects and suggested
selank could be investigated as an immunomodulator. In laboratory work, the compound was
reported to suppress a particular gene's expression by blood cells from patients with depression
but not from healthy controls. **The sample size was not stated in the retrieved record**, so
this study cannot be weighted, and its endpoints are mechanistic rather than clinical
(PMID 18577961, 2008, Russian-language).

**What binds these four together, and limits them.** All come from an overlapping Russian
institutional research network, and the compound's originator is a co-author on the immunological
patient study. There is no non-Russian human trial and no independent replication group. Three
of the four appeared in a single Russian journal, and all four were readable only as English
abstracts, so full methods, adverse-event tables, and statistical analysis plans were never
reviewed. **No placebo-controlled trial of Selank was located anywhere.** Zero Selank trials are
registered on ClinicalTrials.gov: two separate registry queries returned only unrelated fuzzy
text matches. And no systematic review or meta-analysis exists.

One counting caution worth carrying: a filtered PubMed search for Selank trials and reviews
returns six records, but three of them concern strength training periodization, lithium
augmentation in depression, and anaesthesia, and have nothing to do with Selank. Anyone citing a
raw PubMed count overstates this evidence base by half.

#### DSIP: the age of the evidence is itself the finding

**Every human sleep study of DSIP that could be located dates from 1981 to 1992.** That is more
than three decades with no modern human replication, and **zero registered DSIP trials** of any
kind. A hypothesis that generated active human study in the 1980s and then generated none for
thirty years is best read as a line of inquiry that was tried and quietly set aside, not as a
promising idea awaiting rediscovery. The methodological standards of that era, in particular
single-digit uncontrolled open trials, would not be accepted as evidence of efficacy today.

**7 patients, open and uncontrolled, 1984.** In all but one case sleep was reported as
normalized over follow-up periods of three to seven months, with reported improvement in daytime
mood and performance. **This is the most frequently cited positive DSIP result and it is
methodologically the weakest of the three.** Seven patients, no control group, no blinding, and
subjective endpoints. A design of this type cannot separate drug effect from placebo response,
regression to the mean, or investigator expectancy. The paper also flags complications in
patients with a long-standing history of drug dependence (PMID 6391926, European Neurology).

**Chronic insomniacs, double-blind crossover with sleep recording, 1987.** Reductions in
nocturnal awakenings, sleep latency, and waking time after sleep onset were reported, and total
sleep time and non-REM sleep increased, driven primarily by an increase in lighter stage 2
sleep. **Slow wave sleep and REM sleep were not modified**, which is notable given that
promoting deep delta sleep is the peptide's namesake claim. **The authors concluded that the
sleep improvement was of little clinical significance**, and no significant differences were
detected against either baseline or placebo on the primary comparisons. The sample size was not
stated in the retrieved abstract (PMID 3583493, Int J Clin Pharmacol Res).

**16 chronic insomniac patients, double-blind parallel groups, 1992.** Higher sleep efficiency
and shorter sleep latency were observed versus placebo, and one measure of subjectively estimated
tiredness decreased. **None of the other measures changed, including subjective sleep quality**,
which is arguably the outcome that matters most to a person with insomnia. The authors
explicitly stated the statistically significant effects were weak and could in part be due to an
incidental change in the placebo group, and concluded that short-term treatment of chronic
insomnia with DSIP is not likely to be of major therapeutic benefit (Bes et al, Neuropsychobiology
1992;26(4):193, DOI 10.1159/000118919).

**A fourth early human study exists and has not been examined.** A record titled as an
investigation of synthetic DSIP in disturbed human sleep, from approximately 1981, could not be
retrieved during research because the fetch returned a bot-check page instead of the record. No
author, journal, sample size, or result is asserted from it anywhere in this Guide. It is named
here only so that its existence is on the record as an open gap (PMID 7028502).

**How to read the DSIP picture.** Not as "mixed". The positive result comes from an open,
uncontrolled study of 7 people. The negative results come from the two double-blind controlled
studies, whose authors concluded, respectively, little clinical significance and no likely major
therapeutic benefit. When a striking open-label result fails to survive blinding and placebo
control, the standard inference is that the original result reflected placebo response and
expectancy rather than drug effect. Describing this as a live scientific disagreement would
misrepresent the record.

## Preclinical evidence

Every finding below names its species or model in the same sentence. None of it establishes an
effect in a person, and **none of it involves the combination**, which has never been studied in
any animal or any laboratory model either.

**In rats under unpredictable chronic mild stress**, selank was reported to enhance the
anxiety-reducing effect of diazepam, a benzodiazepine (PMID 28280289, Behav Neurol, 2017). This
is simultaneously a preclinical finding and a drug-interaction signal, and it is treated as both
in the safety section below.

**In DBA/2 mice**, selank was reported to inhibit ethanol-induced hyperlocomotion and the
manifestation of behavioral sensitization (PMID 27878720, Bull Exp Biol Med, 2016).

**In rats in a morphine withdrawal model**, selank was reported to attenuate aversive signs of
withdrawal (PMID 36322304, Bull Exp Biol Med, 2022).

**In rats subjected to chronic foot-shock stress**, selank was studied for its effect on
morphological parameters of the liver. **The direction of the effect was not retrievable from
the record obtained**, so nothing about the result is stated here in either direction
(PMID 31243679, Bull Exp Biol Med, 2019). A liver endpoint whose direction is unknown is a
reason for care, not a reason for comfort.

**In IMR-32 human neuroblastoma cells studied in a dish**, GABA, selank, and olanzapine were
reported to affect the expression of genes involved in GABAergic neurotransmission
(PMID 28293190, Front Pharmacol, 2017). Human-derived cells in a dish are still preclinical.

**In 48 male Kun-Ming strain mice with insomnia induced by a chemical agent**, an engineered DSIP
fusion peptide reduced wakefulness time, normalized several neurotransmitter measures, decreased
anxiety-like and depressive-like behaviours, and improved hippocampal tissue appearance
(Frontiers in Pharmacology, 2024, DOI 10.3389/fphar.2024.1439536). **Two caveats change what this
means entirely.** The tested article was a fusion construct specifically engineered to cross the
blood-brain barrier, which is a different molecule from DSIP. And **the fusion peptide
outperformed plain DSIP in that same mouse model**, which is an implicit finding that unmodified
DSIP performed less well. This is the only recent primary research located for DSIP, and it
cannot be presented as modern validation. If anything it points the other way.

**Across rabbits, rats, mice, and cats**, a 1984 narrative review reported delta-sleep induction
in rabbits, rats, and mice, with a more pronounced REM effect in cats, so the response was not
consistent across species (PMID 6145137, Neurosci Biobehav Rev).

**In animal models**, a 2006 critical review reports that certain structural analogues of DSIP
showed sleep-promoting activity while DSIP itself did not demonstrate clear effects
(PMID 16539679, J Neurochem).

**The Semax preclinical literature could not be carried into this Guide.** A PubMed search for
Semax returned approximately 230 records, overwhelmingly animal, cell, and biochemical work.
Preclinical themes visible in the titles include a growth factor, dopaminergic and serotoninergic
systems, immune response genes, opioid receptor gene targeting, copper binding, and behavioural
models. **Those records were read from a results listing rather than fetched individually, so
their species were never confirmed.** Under house rules a preclinical finding cannot appear
without its model named in the same sentence, so none of them is reported here as a finding.
This is a gap for a human reviewer to close, not a judgement about the quality of that work.

**No systematic review or meta-analysis exists for any of the three compounds.** There is no
evidence-synthesis layer above any of these primary studies.

## Contradictions and disagreement

Full detail is in CONTRADICTIONS.md. The four that matter most to a member are these.

**1. Whether anything supports the combination.** A study exists whose title names both Selank
and Semax, and it would be easy to cite as combination evidence. It compared them as separate
agents and distinguished their effects from one another, and DSIP was absent from it. Two
compounds compared side by side is the opposite of two compounds combined. **Assessment: there
is no combination evidence, and the one citation that looks like it is not.**

**2. Whether Semax and Selank are currently approved in Russia.** Essentially every vendor page
retrieved states that they are, commonly citing a 2009 approval for Selank and a place on a
Russian vital medicines list for Semax. Two independent Russian pharmaceutical trade outlets
report that on 20 to 21 January 2026 the Russian Ministry of Health cancelled the registration of
the Peptogen Semax and Selank products and excluded selank as a substance from the State Register
of Medicines, at the request of the certificate holders rather than for any reported safety
reason. **Assessment: prefer the Russian regulatory reporting and treat the vendor claim as
stale.** Two independent outlets naming the same company, the same two products, and the same
stated reason is specific and corroborated. The vendor claim is generic and uncited. **This Guide
does not describe either compound as currently approved anywhere.**

**3. What the United States 503A activity means.** Vendor pages frame the July 2026 advisory
committee consideration as these peptides being under FDA review and heading toward legitimacy.
The primary Federal Register notice records only that certain substances were nominated by
outside parties for possible inclusion on a compounding-eligibility list, with a meeting
scheduled for 23 to 24 July 2026. **Assessment: the primary source wins decisively.** A
nomination is not review of a drug application and not an approval, and as of the date of this
Guide the meeting had not occurred, so no outcome exists to report in any direction.

**4. Whether DSIP improves sleep.** An open, uncontrolled study of 7 people reported sleep
normalized in nearly all of them. Two double-blind controlled studies concluded, respectively,
that the improvement was of little clinical significance and that treatment is not likely to be
of major therapeutic benefit. **Assessment: this is not a genuine standoff and should not be
presented as "mixed evidence".** The controlled studies get decisive weight.

One further point that belongs here rather than in a footnote. **The human controlled data do not
support the claim embedded in DSIP's own name.** The double-blind study that measured sleep
stages found slow wave sleep was not modified, with the increase in total sleep coming from
lighter stage 2 sleep instead. Material that leans on "delta sleep" as a mechanism is leaning on
the name, not on the human measurement.

## Limitations

**Limitations of the evidence base itself.**

- **There is no combination evidence of any kind.** Everything below concerns components studied
  separately, and none of it transfers to the product.
- No placebo-controlled trial exists for Selank, and no Semax study was described as randomized,
  placebo-controlled, and blinded with the method stated.
- Zero trials of any of the three compounds are registered on ClinicalTrials.gov, so selective
  outcome reporting cannot be excluded for any of them.
- The Semax and Selank human literatures are not independent. Each traces to an overlapping
  Russian research network, and the compounds' originators appear as co-authors within them.
  There is no replication by an unaffiliated group in another country for either.
- Every Semax and Selank human study was readable only as an English abstract of a
  Russian-language paper. Full methods, randomization procedures, blinding, adverse-event tables,
  and statistical analysis plans were never reviewed, so risk-of-bias assessment could not be
  performed on any of them.
- Human sample sizes across all three compounds are small: 24, 52, 110, and 187 for Semax; 60,
  62, and 70 for Selank; 7, 16, and one unstated for DSIP.
- The entire DSIP human literature predates 1993, and the two controlled studies in it are
  negative on clinical significance by their own authors' conclusions.
- The only recent primary DSIP research is a mouse study of an engineered fusion construct that
  is not DSIP and that outperformed plain DSIP.
- No systematic review or meta-analysis exists for any of the three compounds.
- The Semax preclinical records were read from a results listing without species confirmation, so
  none could be reported as a finding.

**Limitations of this Guide's research process, stated so a reader can judge its reliability.**

- The primary research was performed in prior sessions and supplied to this Guide as three
  structured records, one per component. **The zero result for combination evidence was
  established by searching those three records exhaustively, not by running fresh database
  queries.** A human must re-run a direct combination search against live databases and record the
  queries and the zero result with a date.
- **Only one primary regulatory document was successfully retrieved anywhere in this work**: the
  Federal Register full text for the 503A nominations. Every fetch to www.fda.gov returned HTTP
  404, the EMA endpoint returned HTTP 401, the WADA Prohibited List returned empty content on
  every attempt across all three searches, the Russian State Register was never successfully
  queried, and the substance registry records for Selank rendered nothing.
- The composition of the product is unconfirmed, so this Guide describes three named compounds
  rather than a characterised preparation.
- One early DSIP human study could not be retrieved at all, so one piece of the DSIP literature
  remains entirely unexamined.
- No trial registry outside ClinicalTrials.gov was searched, for any component or for the
  combination. The EU Clinical Trials Register, ISRCTN, the WHO ICTRP, the Russian registry, and
  the Japanese and Chinese registries were not consulted.
- Search results for all three compounds were dominated by retailers, clinic marketing, and
  peptide-encyclopedia sites. None was used as evidence. This pollution is worth noting in its
  own right, because a member researching independently will meet those sources first and will
  find them far more confident than the actual literature.

One thing that did work, recorded alongside the failures: each of the three research sessions ran
an adversarial control, querying a deliberately fabricated study identifier. In each case the
fabricated record failed while genuine records returned full content, which confirms the
retrieval path was real and did not invent sources.

## Quality and identity

This section is about the material itself, separately from the science. Full detail is in
QUALITY_AND_DOCUMENTATION.md, where every checklist item is currently unmet.

**The composition is not confirmed, and that is the first problem.** No supplier specification,
certificate of analysis, or manufacturing record was available. Nothing is known to Xenios about
what is in this product or in what proportion.

**A blend is harder to document than a single compound, not easier.** Everything a single
compound requires must be satisfied **for each component separately**, and then two further
things are needed that a single compound never needs: **blend uniformity**, meaning that every
portion of the mixed product contains the same proportion of each component, and **content
measured per component** rather than as a combined total. Neither is documented here, and neither
is the per-component identity and assay underneath them.

**No independent product testing was located for any component.** No peer-reviewed study testing
material sold as Semax, as Selank, or as DSIP was found, and none for the blend. Nobody appears
to have checked what is in these vials. That is an absence of information, not a favourable
result.

**There is no regulator currently overseeing quality for two of the three.** Following the
reported January 2026 cancellation of the Russian registrations for the Peptogen Semax and Selank
products, **no current national marketing authorization was identified in any jurisdiction for
either compound.** For DSIP, no approval was identified anywhere at any time. Where no regulator
has approved a product, no regulator is inspecting a production line against a filed standard.

**No pharmacopoeial standard exists to test against.** No monograph was located for any of the
three components, so there is no official identity, purity, or quality specification that
material can be held to.

**Names do not fix identity.** Free base and acetate forms are treated as separate substances in
regulatory filings, and a blend label carries several such names at once. DSIP additionally
appears in regulatory documents under a different name entirely, emideltide.

**A note on the information environment.** Research for this Guide found that vendor and
peptide-blog pages for all three compounds publish confident efficacy and safety claims, and in
several cases publish administration protocols. None of that has been reproduced anywhere in this
Guide, and none of it was used as a source.

## Safety categories

Nothing in this section is reassurance. These are categories of concern that remain open because
nobody has closed them.

**1. There is no safety data for this combination, because the combination has never been
studied.** This is the primary safety finding and it comes before everything else. There is no
combination safety data, no combination pharmacokinetic data, and no way to attribute any effect
or any adverse event to any one component if something happens.

**2. All three components act on the central nervous system, and their combined neurological and
psychiatric interaction profile is unstudied.** This is a specific open risk, not a general
caveat, and it is the reason this particular combination warrants more caution rather than less.
The retrieved literature separately describes GABAergic themes for Selank, monoaminergic and
opioid-related and immune themes in Semax preclinical titles, and sleep and neurotransmitter
effects for DSIP in animal models. Nobody has studied what those surfaces do to each other in a
person.

**3. There is no adequate human safety dataset for any component individually either.** No
adverse-event tables, no dropout counts, no laboratory monitoring, no long-term follow-up, no
pharmacovigilance data, and no regulator safety review was retrieved for any of the three.

**4. The one tolerability statement in the whole record cannot bear weight.** A 2005
non-randomized Semax study in 187 patients described a minor percentage of side effects and good
tolerability across age groups. That is an uncontrolled impression from a single non-randomized
study, not a safety characterization, and it is the entire retrieved safety signal for Semax.

**5. Absence of reported harm reflects absence of study.** Combined human exposure across the
entire retrieved DSIP literature is on the order of a few dozen people. Total healthy-volunteer
exposure to Semax is in the dozens. A literature this small cannot detect uncommon or delayed
harms even if they exist, so silence in it is not a safety record.

**6. Interaction with sedative and anti-anxiety medicines is an open question.** One human trial
studied selank added to a benzodiazepine, and one study in rats examined selank combined with a
different benzodiazepine and reported that it enhanced that drug's anxiety-reducing effect.
Combination use with sedative and hypnotic medicines is a recognised interaction context that has
not been characterised outside those small studies.

**7. Mechanistic breadth is an unresolved question, not a feature.** A compound reported to touch
monoaminergic, opioid-related, immune, and metal-handling pathways in animal and cell models has a
correspondingly wide surface for unstudied interactions in people, including with psychiatric and
neurological medicines. **No human drug-interaction study was retrieved for any of the three
components.**

**8. Immunogenicity is uncharacterised.** Whether the body mounts an immune response against any
of these substances has not been assessed for any of them, alone or combined. Secondary reporting
attributed a concern of this kind to regulatory briefing material for one component, but that
document could not be retrieved during research, so nothing here is attributed to any regulator.
[UNVERIFIED - requires human source check against the briefing document]

**9. Entirely unstudied populations.** No safety or effect data of any kind was located for
pregnancy, breastfeeding, adolescents, older adults, or people with cardiovascular, psychiatric,
endocrine, neurological, hepatic, or seizure conditions, for any component or for the combination.

**10. Product quality is a safety issue in its own right.** Separately from the molecules,
material obtained outside a regulated supply chain has unverified identity, purity, sterility, and
content, and no regulator was identified as currently overseeing any of these three anywhere.

## Interactions

**No human interaction study was located for any of the three components, and none for the
combination.** Interactions are unstudied, which is not the same as absent.

Two specific signals exist in the retrieved literature and are worth naming precisely, because
they are the closest thing to interaction data that exists here.

- **Benzodiazepines.** One human trial (70 patients) studied selank added to phenazepam, a
  benzodiazepine, and reported reduced side effects from that medicine. One study in rats reported
  that selank enhanced the anxiety-reducing effect of diazepam, a different benzodiazepine. These
  are small, non-independent, and in one case an animal finding. They establish that this is an
  interaction context, not that the interaction is understood.
- **The combination itself is an interaction question.** Three centrally acting compounds in one
  preparation is a set of interactions before anything else is taken alongside it, and none of
  those internal interactions has been studied.

The following categories would require review by a qualified professional, because they intersect
with the central nervous system activity described across the three components.

- **Sedatives, hypnotics, sleep medicines, and benzodiazepines.**
- **Antidepressants and any medicine acting on serotonin or dopamine signalling.**
- **Antipsychotics, mood stabilisers, and other psychiatric medication.**
- **Anti-seizure medication**, and any history of seizures.
- **Opioid medicines**, given the opioid-related themes in the preclinical titles.
- **Alcohol**, given the animal work on ethanol-induced behaviour.
- **Stimulants**, prescribed or otherwise, given the reported psychostimulant description in one
  Selank trial.
- **Anticoagulant medication**, flagged as an open interaction question in the underlying research.
- **Immune-modulating medication**, given the immune themes in the Selank and Semax literature.
- **Any medicine for a hormonal or endocrine condition**, given that one component is described as
  an analogue of a hormone fragment and long-term effects on hormonal axes are unstudied.
- **Any other unapproved peptide or research compound taken at the same time**, which makes both
  benefit and harm impossible to attribute.
- **Pregnancy and breastfeeding**, where no data exists at all.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

## Unknowns

An explicit list of what is not known. It is longer than the evidence section, which is itself the
most accurate summary of the state of knowledge.

**About the combination**

- Whether this combination does anything at all in a person. Nothing has ever tested it.
- What the three compounds do to each other in one preparation: whether they change how the body
  handles each other, whether their effects add, cancel, or amplify, and whether the mixture
  behaves like any of its parts.
- The combined neurological and psychiatric interaction profile of three centrally acting
  compounds.
- Any safety profile for the combination, at any duration.
- What is actually in this product and in what proportion.

**About Semax**

- Whether it has any clinically meaningful cognitive, mood, or nootropic effect in healthy people.
  The healthy-volunteer studies measured brain connectivity, not cognition or symptoms.
- Whether the reported stroke and cerebrovascular benefits would survive randomization, blinding,
  and placebo control. No such trial was found.
- The human safety profile at any level of detail, including adverse-event frequency, serious
  adverse events, and effects with repeated or prolonged exposure, including on hormonal axes over
  time.
- The species and methods behind the individual preclinical records, none of which could be
  confirmed.

**About Selank**

- Whether the reported anxiolytic effect would survive a placebo-controlled trial. None exists.
- Whether the reported GABAergic and serotonergic mechanism operates in living humans. The
  retrieved mechanistic work is in rodents and in a human neuroblastoma cell line, not in people.
- Adverse event rates, dropout rates, and tolerability data from the three human trials, none of
  which were present in the retrieved abstracts.
- The verified amino acid sequence and the tuftsin-derivation claim from a primary chemical source.
- Whether the January 2026 Russian deregistration was purely commercial or administrative, or had
  any undisclosed driver. The reported reason is a holder request and no safety rationale was
  reported. This Guide does not speculate in either direction.

**About DSIP**

- Whether DSIP is a genuine endogenous mammalian peptide at all. No gene, receptor, or precursor
  has been identified in over fifty years.
- Whether any reported human sleep effect would survive a modern, adequately powered, blinded
  trial. This has never been tested.
- Whether findings in the 2024 mouse work on an engineered fusion peptide have any bearing on
  unmodified DSIP in humans. The construct was specifically engineered to cross the blood-brain
  barrier, which implies unmodified DSIP does so poorly.
- The content of the one early human study that could not be retrieved.

**Across all three**

- Human pharmacokinetics: absorption, central nervous system exposure, metabolism, and duration.
  Nothing was retrieved for any of them by any route.
- Interactions with any medicine or supplement.
- Immunogenicity.
- Behaviour in pregnancy, breastfeeding, adolescents, older adults, and people with cardiovascular,

- The identity, purity, and content of material sold under any of these names, or as this blend.
- The exact Russian registration particulars for Semax and Selank, and the effective date of the

## Questions for a qualified professional

If a member wants to discuss this with a clinician, these questions produce a useful
conversation. They are written to be asked as they stand.

1. This is a blend of three compounds and I cannot find any study of the combination itself. Does
   that match what you can find, and how should that change how I think about it?
2. Is there any reason to expect three compounds that each act on the brain to behave differently
   together than they would separately, and is that something anyone could predict without a study?
3. If I took a mixture and something good or bad happened, would there be any way to tell which
   component was responsible?
4. I take the following medications and have the following conditions. Which of them would make
   anything acting on the central nervous system a particularly poor idea for me?
5. I currently take a sedative, sleep, or anti-anxiety medicine. What would you want to know before
   anything else acting on the same systems was introduced?
6. The sleep component is named for promoting deep sleep, but the one controlled human study that
   measured sleep stages found deep sleep was not changed. How much weight do you put on a
   compound's name versus its measured effect?
7. Most of the human research on two of these compounds comes from one country and one research
   network, with no independent replication anywhere else. How do you weigh evidence that has never
   been reproduced by an unaffiliated group?
8. What is actually going on with my sleep, my anxiety, or my focus, and is there an approved
   treatment with real human evidence that we have not tried yet?
9. None of these is approved where I live, which means any material comes from outside a regulated
   supply chain. How do you think about unverified identity, purity, and sterility for something
   introduced into the body?
10. I compete in a tested sport. Who is the right authority for me to ask about these substances?
11. If I decided to do nothing about this for now, what would you want me to watch for, and when
    should I come back?
12. If new human evidence appeared, what would you want to see in it before it changed your advice?

This Guide does not give medical advice and does not tell anyone what to do. These questions belong
with a clinician who knows your history.
