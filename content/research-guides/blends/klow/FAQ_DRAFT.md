# KLOW: questions members actually ask

Educational only. Not medical advice. Xenios does not sell, supply, or source KLOW or any of
the compounds named here, and this page contains no guidance on obtaining them.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

## 1. What is in KLOW?

We cannot tell you, and that is the honest answer rather than a dodge.

A legacy internal Xenios catalog entry reports that KLOW contains TB-500, BPC-157, GHK-Cu, and
KPV, at stated strengths. No supplier specification, certificate of analysis, or manufacturing
record supporting that entry was available when this Guide was prepared. A catalog line is a
commercial description. It is not a documented composition, and we do not publish it as one.

Two further things make the picture less certain rather than more. The research records
supplied for this Guide cover LL-37, which is not one of the four names in the catalog entry,
while no research record was supplied for TB-500, which is. And a regulator has recorded that
one of these component names is a common commercial name rather than a standardised one, under
which multiple salts, derivatives, and even different active moieties have been encountered.

Confirming the composition is the first question for the supplier, and until it is answered the
subject of this Guide is not fully defined.

## 2. What proportion of each ingredient does it contain?

We do not state a ratio, and we will not imply one.

Nobody has shown us a composition document, so any proportion we printed would be a guess
dressed as a specification. It is listed as an open supplier question alongside the ingredient
list itself.

## 3. Does KLOW work?

Nobody knows, because nobody has studied it.

Every source consulted for this Guide was searched for a study of this combination. There is
none. No human study, no animal study, no laboratory study, no trial registration, and no
regulatory evaluation of the mixture as a product.

That is a different answer from "the evidence is weak". Weak evidence means someone looked and
found little. Here, nobody looked at this combination at all.

## 4. But four studied ingredients must add up to something, surely?

This is the most common and most reasonable-sounding assumption, and it is wrong.

Evidence attaches to what was actually administered in a study. When compounds are combined
into one preparation, that preparation is a new thing. The components can interact, affect each
other's stability and how the body handles them, and produce effects that none of them produces
alone. Only a study of the actual mixture can address any of that.

So a blend does not inherit its ingredients' evidence. It starts from zero and has to earn its
own.

There is a sharper version of the point in the record. A regulator described an adverse event
reported on a product containing two peptides. The reaction reproduced when the product was
used again and resolved when it was stopped, so the regulator judged it likely related to the
product. It then stated that because two peptides were present, the event could not be
attributed to either one. The same regulator recorded a review of clinical records where some
people received two peptides together and noted no effect could be attributed to either.

Combining compounds did not double the evidence in either case. It destroyed the ability to
interpret what happened.

## 5. Is it safe?

Unknown, and that is the only defensible answer.

The safety of this combination has never been studied. Nobody has taken it in a study, so there
is no adverse event record, no tolerability data, and no information about what happens over
any period of time.

The component picture does not rescue this. For BPC-157, a regulator concluded that there is
insufficient clinical safety information to characterise the safety profile, that the
non-clinical toxicology work was too limited in scope and duration to inform safety, and that
there is no human data on how the body handles the substance for most proposed routes. Total
documented human exposure across every study that regulator could identify is on the order of
dozens of people for days to two weeks. For GHK-Cu, no human trial of a systemically
administered form was located at all. For KPV, no human study of any design was located. For
TB-500, no research was supplied for this Guide.

Please read the absence of reported problems as a measure of how little has been looked at, not
as a good safety record. They are not the same thing.

## 6. What about interactions?

There are none documented, in either direction, and that is itself worth pausing on.

No study of how any two of these components affect each other was located. Which means a blend
is, in effect, an untested interaction: combining compounds is precisely the situation an
interaction study exists to examine, and for this product none has been done.

There is also no data on interactions with any medication, supplement, or medical condition.
That does not mean interactions are absent. It means the question has never been asked. If you
take any medication or have any medical condition, that absence is a reason to talk to a
qualified professional, not a reassurance.

## 7. Is it legal?

The combination as a product has no regulatory status anywhere, because it has never been
submitted to or evaluated by any regulator as a product. The evaluations that exist assess
individual bulk substances one at a time.

As at 2026-07-21, and for the United States only:

- Per the Food and Drug Administration's own briefing document for its July 23-24, 2026
  advisory committee meeting, BPC-157 free base and BPC-157 acetate are not components of any
  approved drug, no approved product containing them exists in any country, and neither is in
  the European, Japanese, or International pharmacopoeias. Two enforcement letters, dated
  04/01/2020 and 09/14/2023, cited firms for compounding with the substance. A Department of
  Defense article dated April 29, 2025 states it is an unapproved drug and not a lawful dietary
  ingredient. A national anti-doping organisation states it is prohibited in sport under the
  non-approved substances category.
- An advisory committee review of these substances was scheduled for July 23-24, 2026. As of
  2026-07-21 that meeting has not taken place. Some sources describe an outcome in the past
  tense. No outcome could be verified, and this Guide states none. A committee recommendation
  is in any case advisory rather than a final agency determination.
- For GHK-Cu and KPV, agency pages were unreachable during the underlying research, so those
  statements rest on trade reporting rather than on the regulator and are labelled that way.
- For TB-500, no research was supplied and no statement is made.

We did not check the European Union, the United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, or anywhere else.
Not checked is not the same as permitted. If you are subject to anti-doping testing, ask your
own anti-doping authority rather than relying on anything here.

## 8. Can I get it from Xenios?

No. Xenios does not sell, supply, source, obtain, or arrange access to KLOW or to any compound
named in these Guides, and we do not tell members where to get them.

These Guides exist so that a member reading claims elsewhere has an honest, sourced account to
read alongside them.

## 9. Why does everything in these Guides sound so negative?

Because we report what the record contains rather than what a product page would prefer.

Two specific patterns produced most of what looks negative here. First, several apparently
positive results carry documented defects: manufacturer-sponsored uncontrolled studies of
multi-ingredient products, a trial that missed its main endpoint with the favourable numbers
drawn from a subgroup examined afterwards, an animal result the authors themselves credited
substantially to a delivery vehicle rather than the compound, and a trial whose confidence
interval crossed zero. Second, the largest single fact about this product is an absence: nobody
has studied the combination.

If a well-designed study of this combination reported a benefit, we would say so with the same
directness. Nothing in our approach is set against a positive finding. There simply is not one.

## 10. Some of the component research looked genuinely promising. What happened?

In two cases the promising result was tested properly and did not hold.

For GHK-Cu, the animal wound-healing literature was encouraging across several species. When a
copper tripeptide complex was then tested in a randomised, evaluator-blinded human trial in
people with venous stasis ulcers, the copper arm did not differ meaningfully from an inert
vehicle. In a second controlled trial after a skin resurfacing procedure, there was no
measurable improvement in redness, wrinkles, or skin quality.

That second trial contains something genuinely interesting. Participants using the compound
reported significantly higher satisfaction, while blinded and computer-based assessment found
no objective improvement. Both results are real. They explain how enthusiastic personal
accounts can coexist quite honestly with flat trial data, and why controlled trials exist.

For LL-37, an early small trial reported markedly better healing, and the signal did not
survive when the same question was tested at larger sample size by overlapping authors.

## 11. If nothing is known, why write a Guide at all?

Because "nothing is known" is information, and it is information a member is unlikely to get
anywhere else.

Someone encountering this product will meet confident mechanism explanations, ingredient lists
presented as specifications, and study counts that turn out to be animal work. Having a sourced
account of what the record actually contains, including what it does not contain, is more
useful than silence.

An honest empty evidence table is a result. We do not pad it.

## 12. What would change the answer?

Specific things, in rough order of how much they would change.

1. A study that administered this actual combination to people, designed properly, with a
   comparison group and prespecified outcomes. This is the only thing that could produce
   evidence about KLOW.
2. Failing that, any study of the combination at all, in any system, so that interaction
   questions could begin to be addressed.
3. A supplier specification, certificate of analysis, and manufacturing record, so the product
   has a documented identity, plus evidence that the mixture is uniform and that each component
   is present at its stated content.
4. Adequately powered, controlled human trials of the individual components. Two are under way
   for individual compounds, neither has reported, and neither would tell anyone anything about
   the blend.
5. Independent analytical testing of material sold under these names. No such study was located
   for any of them, which is a real gap rather than a good sign.

If any of these appear, this Guide returns to review and is updated with the date and the
source.

## 13. What should I do with all this?

Take it to a qualified professional who knows your medical history, and take the questions
listed at the end of the main Guide with you.

What we would ask you not to do is treat the length of an ingredient list as a measure of
evidence, or read the absence of reported problems as a safety record. Those two inferences are
the ones this Guide is built to interrupt.
