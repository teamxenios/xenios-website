# KLOW Contradictions and Divergences

Where sources disagree, or where a common understanding is contradicted by the retrieved
record. Each entry states the two positions and then states which one the evidence supports,
rather than leaving a member to referee it.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

## C-01. Whether a blend inherits the evidence of its ingredients

**Position A.** A blend of four studied compounds is better evidenced than any one of them,
because the evidence for each ingredient adds up.

**Position B.** Evidence attaches to what was actually administered. A mixture is a different
preparation with its own interaction profile and its own unknowns. Nothing in the retrieved
record studied the KLOW combination, so there is nothing to inherit.

**Assessment.** Position B, and this is not a close question. The retrieved record contains
two direct demonstrations of the point, both of them cautionary rather than supportive. A
regulator recorded an adverse event on a product containing two of the reported components,
judged it likely related to the product because it reproduced on rechallenge, and then stated
that because two peptides were present the event could not be attributed to either. The same
regulator recorded a chart review in which some people received two peptides and no effect
could be attributed to one. In both cases combining the compounds did not add evidence. It
removed the ability to interpret what happened. This is the single most important entry in
this document and the Guide should not soften it.

## C-02. What KLOW actually contains

**Position A.** The legacy Xenios catalog entry lists four components at stated strengths, so
the composition is known.

**Position B.** No supplier specification, certificate of analysis, or manufacturing record
supports that entry. A catalog line is a commercial description, not a documented composition.
The internal content review declined to guess and this Guide follows that decision.

**Assessment.** Position B. The composition is unconfirmed and is the first open supplier
question. Two further facts sharpen it. First, the research records supplied for this packet
cover LL-37, which is not one of the four names in the catalog entry, while no research record
was supplied for TB-500, which is. Second, a regulator has recorded that one of the reported
component names is a common commercial name rather than a standardised one, and that multiple
salts, derivatives, and even different active moieties have been encountered sold under it.
So even a confirmed ingredient list would not by itself establish which substances are
present. No composition, strength, or ratio is stated anywhere in this folder.

## C-03. Whether a well-developed mechanism implies a real effect

**Position A.** Each of the reported components has a coherent, decades-old mechanistic story,
and the stories fit together.

**Position B.** For GHK-Cu, a detailed mechanistic account has existed for decades while the
two identified randomised, blinded, controlled human trials were both null on their objective
measures. For BPC-157, a regulator stated that molecular targets have not been identified and
mechanisms remain poorly understood, and that a dose-response relationship could not be
established because widely separated amounts produced comparable effects. For KPV, a
peer-reviewed review states plainly that the signalling mechanism is unknown.

**Assessment.** Position B. A mechanism is a hypothesis about why something might work. It is
not a finding that it does. For these components the mechanistic literature and the human
outcome literature have run in opposite directions, and where they were tested directly the
outcome data won. The move from mechanism to product claim is also where blends go wrong most
persuasively, because several plausible stories layered together read as more convincing than
any of them is. That layering is prohibited in CLAIM_TABLE.md.

## C-04. Whether the components have a human safety record

**Position A.** These compounds have been used for years with no reported problems, so the
combination is probably fine.

**Position B.** For BPC-157, total documented human exposure across all studies a regulator
could identify is on the order of dozens of people for days to two weeks, with safety
monitoring described as unclear, and the regulator concluded that safety cannot be
characterised. For GHK-Cu, no human trial of a systemically administered form was located at
all. For KPV, no human study of any design was located. For TB-500, no research record was
supplied. Absence of reported harm across a body of study that small is a measure of how
little was looked at.

**Assessment.** Position B. The premise of Position A is wrong on the facts, and the inference
would be invalid even if the premise held, because the combination was never the thing
administered. This is exactly the reasoning the Guide must interrupt.

## C-05. Whether the strongest-looking component evidence is what it appears to be

**Position A.** Several of these components have positive human studies behind them.

**Position B.** Each of the apparently positive results carries a specific defect that the
retrieved record documents.

- The recent favourable GHK-Cu reports are open-label, uncontrolled, sponsored by the
  manufacturer of the tested products, and test multi-ingredient formulations from which no
  effect can be attributed to the copper peptide.
- In the one controlled trial where the divergence could be seen directly, participants
  reported significantly higher satisfaction while blinded and computer-based assessment found
  no objective improvement.
- The largest LL-37 trial missed its prespecified primary endpoint, and the favourable figures
  quoted from it come from a post-hoc subgroup analysis produced after that failure.
- The strongest KPV animal result used an engineered targeting nanoparticle rather than the
  plain compound, and the authors attributed the advantage substantially to the delivery
  vehicle.
- The only randomised controlled trial of BPC-157 that a regulator could identify exists as a
  conference abstract whose between-group confidence interval crosses zero.

**Assessment.** Position B in every instance. None of these is a fringe objection. Each is
recorded in the source itself or in the regulator's own review of it. A Guide that lists the
studies without their defects would be more misleading than one that listed no studies at all.

## C-06. Whether a cosmetic or topical evidence base transfers to another product form

**Position A.** GHK-Cu is a long-established reviewed cosmetic ingredient with a safety
assessment behind it, so the compound is well characterised.

**Position B.** A cosmetic ingredient assessment addresses a cosmetic product used as
described in that assessment. All retrieved human evidence for this component is topical. No
human trial of a systemically administered form was located, and the research record flags the
substitution of cosmetic and topical evidence for injectable products as the single most
common way a reader is misled about this compound.

**Assessment.** These are statements about two different products and only conflict when one
is used to vouch for the other. The Guide should make the substitution explicit rather than
leaving a member to notice it. Note also that the cosmetic assessment itself was not retrieved
in the supplied research and is recorded as unverified.

## C-07. Whether the regulatory picture is settled

**Position A.** Numerous secondary sources state flatly that these substances hold a specific
regulatory category as of a specific 2023 date, and some sources describe the July 2026
advisory committee outcome in the past tense.

**Position B.** The regulator's own current page lists BPC-157 not in the category table but
in a separate table of nominations that were withdrawn. The advisory committee meeting
scheduled for July 23-24, 2026 has not occurred as of 2026-07-21. The KPV research record
documents multiple search summaries asserting a past-tense outcome that could not be verified,
while the two contemporaneous trade reports describe the meeting in the future tense.

**Assessment.** Position B. The Guide states that the review is pending and asserts no
outcome. The practical consequence is unchanged either way for BPC-157: it is not on the
relevant bulk substances list, has no pharmacopoeial monograph, and is not a component of an
approved drug, which is why two enforcement letters were issued. But a specific category and
date must not be repeated as current fact, and a past-tense outcome must not be repeated at
all. Note additionally that a committee recommendation, when it comes, is advisory and is not
a final agency determination.

## C-08. Whether quality problems are documented for these products specifically

**Position A.** Independent testing has established specific failure rates for grey-market
peptide products, quoted as percentages.

**Position B.** Every one of those figures traced to a vendor blog, a Substack post, or a
testing-service marketing page, with no locatable primary source. The peer-reviewed evidence
that does exist concerns an entirely different peptide bought from illegal online sellers, and
a category-level white paper on compounded peptide products that names none of these
substances.

**Assessment.** Position B. The named figures are prohibited by name in CLAIM_TABLE.md so they
cannot re-enter later. What can honestly be said is narrower and still serious: the supply
channel through which unapproved peptides are typically obtained has documented failures, the
compounded peptide category carries documented contamination and mislabelling risk, and no
analytical study of material sold under any of these component names was located at all. That
last point is a genuine gap, not a clean bill of health. Nobody appears to have systematically
checked.

## C-09. A note on the integrity of the record itself

Two of the four supplied research records could not reach the regulator's own pages. FDA
sources returned HTTP 404 and HTTP 403 during the GHK-Cu and KPV research, so the regulatory
statements for those two components rest on trade reporting rather than on the agency. The
BPC-157 record, by contrast, retrieved the agency's briefing document, list page, meeting
page, and two enforcement letters directly, and its regulatory statements are correspondingly
stronger.

This unevenness is recorded rather than smoothed over. Where a statement rests on trade
reporting, the Guide says so in the sentence that makes the statement. A reviewer should close
the gap before publication rather than accepting a uniform-looking regulatory section built on
uneven foundations.
