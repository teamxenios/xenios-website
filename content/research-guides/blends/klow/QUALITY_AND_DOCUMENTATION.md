# KLOW: quality and documentation requirements

Educational only. Not medical advice. Xenios does not sell, supply, or source KLOW or any
compound named here.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

## Purpose of this document

This is the documentation a product like KLOW would need before anyone could say what it is,
let alone what it does. It is written as a checklist so that a reader can see exactly what is
known and what is not.

**Every item on this checklist is currently unmet.** Not partially met, not met with caveats.
Unmet. No supplier specification, certificate of analysis, manufacturing record, stability
record, or test result of any kind was available when this Guide was prepared.

That is not a criticism of a particular supplier. It is a description of the state of the
record, and the correct response to it is to obtain the documents rather than to assume they
would say something reassuring.

## The starting position

**The composition is not confirmed.** A legacy internal Xenios catalog entry reports that KLOW
contains TB-500, BPC-157, GHK-Cu, and KPV, at stated strengths. Nothing supports that entry. It
is recorded in this Guide's source registry as non-evidence, and no composition, strength, or
ratio appears anywhere in this document set as fact. Confirming the composition is item A.1
below and is the first thing a supplier must produce.

**There is no standard to test against.** For BPC-157 there is no monograph in the United
States, European, Japanese, or International pharmacopoeias, per a regulator's own statement.
Where no official specification exists, no material can be held to one, and a certificate of
analysis has nothing authoritative to compare its results against.

**A name does not identify a substance.** The same regulator has recorded encountering multiple
salts, derivatives, and even different active moieties sold commercially under one of these
component names, and described inconsistent naming as a safety risk because a person may
receive a substance other than the one intended. It treats a free base and its acetate salt as
two different active ingredients. A blend label carries several such names simultaneously.

**Certificates of analysis in this market do not do what buyers assume.** The regulator examined
certificates published by commercial sellers and found they typically report purity only, with
no information demonstrating control of the impurity profile, and that impurities of
toxicological concern for a bulk drug substance are not addressed. It found no particle size,
microbial quality, endotoxin, sterility, aggregate, or container-closure data for the proposed
forms, and concluded the substances are not well characterised.

**The category has documented failures.** A white paper on compounded peptide products
documents contamination risk, mislabelling, and lack of standardisation relative to approved
products. It names none of the compounds discussed here, so it is category-level evidence. A
peer-reviewed study of products bought from illegal online sellers found every purchased vial
to be a probable substandard or falsified product, with measured purity far below the label
claim and endotoxin present in every sample. That study tested an entirely different peptide
and is evidence about a supply channel, not about any compound named here.

**No analytical study of material sold under any of these component names was located.** Nobody
appears to have systematically checked. That is a gap, not a clean result.

## What a blend requires that a single compound does not

This is the section that distinguishes this document from the quality document for a
single-compound Guide, and it is the reason a blend is harder to document rather than easier.

For a single compound, the baseline is identity and assay of that one substance: proving it is
what the label says, and measuring how much is there.

For a blend, that baseline must be met **for every component individually**, and then two
further requirements sit on top of it.

**Blend uniformity.** Whether every portion of the mixed product contains the same proportion of
each component. Without uniformity data, one unit may differ substantially from another, and
neither the supplier nor the user can know which they have.

**Content per component.** How much of each individual component the product actually contains,
measured rather than asserted on a label. A stated strength is a claim. A measured content is a
result.

Neither of these is documented for KLOW. Neither is the per-component identity and assay
underneath them. This is recorded as section A of the checklist and is the specific blend
requirement referenced throughout this document set.

## The checklist

Every box is currently unmet. Where a regulator's finding bears on an item, it is noted so a
reviewer can see why the item is on the list.

### A. Identity, including the blend-specific requirements

- [ ] A.1 A supplier specification stating the complete composition of KLOW, in a document, not
      a catalog line. **UNMET. This is the first blocker for the entire Guide.**
- [ ] A.2 Confirmation of which chemical form of each named substance is present, including free
      base or salt, since a regulator treats these as different active ingredients. **UNMET.**
- [ ] A.3 Identity testing for each component individually, by a stated analytical method, with
      results. **UNMET. This is the per-component identity requirement specific to a blend.**
- [ ] A.4 Assay for each component individually, establishing measured content per component
      rather than a label claim. **UNMET. This is the per-component assay requirement specific
      to a blend.**
- [ ] A.5 Blend uniformity data showing that the mixture is consistent throughout, so that one
      portion matches another. **UNMET. Specific to a blend and entirely absent.**
- [ ] A.6 A named analytical method for each of A.3, A.4, and A.5, with its validation. **UNMET.**
- [ ] A.7 Resolution of the discrepancy between the reported ingredient list and the research
      supplied, specifically TB-500 listed but not researched and LL-37 researched but not
      listed. **UNMET.**
- [ ] A.8 A statement of which pharmacopoeial standard, if any, each component is tested
      against. Note that for BPC-157 no monograph exists in the United States, European,
      Japanese, or International pharmacopoeias, so this item cannot currently be satisfied by
      any supplier. **UNMET and currently unsatisfiable.**

### B. Purity and impurities

- [ ] B.1 A stated purity for each component individually, by a named method. **UNMET.**
- [ ] B.2 A defined impurity profile for each component, with impurities identified rather than
      only quantified in aggregate. A regulator found that market certificates for one component
      report purity only and do not demonstrate control of the impurity profile. **UNMET.**
- [ ] B.3 Testing for impurities of toxicological concern, which a regulator identifies as
      including nitrosamines and potentially mutagenic and teratogenic substances. **UNMET.**
- [ ] B.4 Residual solvent testing, relevant because the synthesis route for peptides of this
      kind introduces solvents, coupling reagents, activators, catalysts, and scavengers.
      **UNMET.**
- [ ] B.5 Testing for related peptide impurities, including isomeric and deletion sequences.
      **UNMET.**
- [ ] B.6 Heavy metal testing. **UNMET.** Note that no locatable primary source exists for the
      heavy metal failure percentages circulating in this market, and those figures are
      prohibited by name in CLAIM_TABLE.md. Testing is required on its own merits, not because
      of an unverified statistic.
- [ ] B.7 Testing for cross-contamination between components introduced during blending.
      **UNMET. Specific to a blend.**

### C. Sterility and microbiological safety

- [ ] C.1 Sterility testing of the finished product, by a named method. **UNMET.**
- [ ] C.2 Bacterial endotoxin testing with a stated limit and result. A peer-reviewed study of a
      different peptide bought from illegal online sellers detected endotoxin in every sample.
      **UNMET.**
- [ ] C.3 Bioburden and microbial quality data. A regulator found none available for the
      proposed forms of one component. **UNMET.**
- [ ] C.4 Particulate matter and visible particle testing. **UNMET.**
- [ ] C.5 Container closure integrity data. A regulator found none available. **UNMET.**

### D. Stability, including the blend-specific requirement

- [ ] D.1 A stability programme for the finished blend, with a stated shelf life and the data
      supporting it. **UNMET.**
- [ ] D.2 Evidence that each component remains within specification over that shelf life, tested
      individually rather than as a total. **UNMET. Specific to a blend, and the item most
      likely to be overlooked.**
- [ ] D.3 Evidence that blend uniformity is maintained over time and is not lost through
      settling or separation. **UNMET. Specific to a blend.**
- [ ] D.4 Aggregation data. A regulator states that peptides of this kind can be extremely
      sensitive to formulation, process, and environmental conditions including pH, temperature,
      and concentration, that significant amounts of aggregates can form during storage or
      stress, and that aggregation drives its immunogenicity concern. **UNMET.**
- [ ] D.5 Degradation data with degradation products identified. **UNMET.**
- [ ] D.6 Stated storage and transport conditions with data showing they were maintained.
      **UNMET.**

### E. Chain of custody and manufacturing provenance

- [ ] E.1 The identity of the manufacturer of each component substance. **UNMET.**
- [ ] E.2 The identity of the facility that performed the blending. **UNMET.**
- [ ] E.3 The regulatory registration status of those facilities. **UNMET.**
- [ ] E.4 Batch and lot identification traceable from finished product back to each component.
      **UNMET.**
- [ ] E.5 A manufacturing record for the blending operation. **UNMET.**
- [ ] E.6 Disclosure of any enforcement history against facilities in the chain. Relevant
      because a regulator recorded that material used in at least one small published human
      study of one component was compounded by a firm that subsequently received a warning
      letter naming that substance, and that material in two other studies came from a pharmacy
      that declined to be named. **UNMET.**

### F. Certificate of analysis and independent verification

- [ ] F.1 A certificate of analysis for the finished blend, not for individual raw components.
      **UNMET.**
- [ ] F.2 That certificate reporting identity, assay, and content for every component separately.
      **UNMET. Specific to a blend.**
- [ ] F.3 That certificate reporting blend uniformity. **UNMET. Specific to a blend.**
- [ ] F.4 That certificate reporting the impurity profile rather than purity alone. A regulator
      found that market certificates for one component do not do this. **UNMET.**
- [ ] F.5 Independent third-party testing by a laboratory with no commercial relationship to the
      supplier. **UNMET.**
- [ ] F.6 The testing laboratory named, with its accreditation stated. **UNMET.**
- [ ] F.7 Raw analytical data available for review, not only a summary certificate. **UNMET.**

### G. Labelling and claims

- [ ] G.1 A label stating the complete composition accurately. **UNMET, and cannot be assessed
      while the composition is unconfirmed.**
- [ ] G.2 A label free of any claim to treat, prevent, or improve any condition. **UNMET, not
      assessed.**
- [ ] G.3 A label free of any implication that combination evidence exists. **UNMET, not
      assessed.**
- [ ] G.4 Absence of a "research use only" or "not for human consumption" marking used while the
      product is marketed for people. A government consumer safety article records this as a
      common pattern in this category. **UNMET, not assessed.**
- [ ] G.5 Absence of any stated ratio or relative strength that is not supported by measured
      content data. **UNMET.**

### H. Governance and adverse events

- [ ] H.1 A named person accountable for product quality. **UNMET.**
- [ ] H.2 A documented procedure for receiving and recording adverse event reports. **UNMET.**
      Relevant because a regulator noted that compounders generally do not report adverse events
      to it, so underreporting is expected.
- [ ] H.3 A recall and withdrawal procedure. **UNMET.**
- [ ] H.4 A complaint handling record. **UNMET.**
- [ ] H.5 A documented procedure for attributing an adverse event to a component in a
      multi-component product, or an acknowledgement that attribution may not be possible. A
      regulator has stated exactly that, on a two-peptide product where a reaction reproduced on
      re-use. **UNMET. Specific to a blend.**

## The open supplier questions, in order

1. **What does KLOW contain?** Provide the specification. This blocks everything else.
2. **In what proportion, measured rather than stated?**
3. **Which chemical form of each component?**
4. **Why does the supplied research cover LL-37, which is not on the reported list, while no
   research exists for TB-500, which is?**
5. **Where is the certificate of analysis for the finished blend, showing identity, assay, and
   content for each component separately, plus blend uniformity?**
6. **Where is the stability data for the mixture, showing each component individually?**
7. **Who manufactured each component, who blended them, and what is their regulatory status?**
8. **Where is the independent third-party testing?**
9. **What is the procedure if an adverse event is reported and cannot be attributed to a
   component?**
