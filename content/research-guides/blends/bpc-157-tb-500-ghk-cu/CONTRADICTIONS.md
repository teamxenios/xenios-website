# BPC-157 + TB-500 + GHK-Cu: contradictions and divergences

Where sources disagree, this page records both positions and states which one the retrieved
record supports. Some entries are genuine scientific disagreement. Several are not
disagreements at all but substitutions, where a claim about one thing is presented as a claim
about another. Those are recorded here too, because they will reach a reader in the same form.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## C-01. Whether combining ingredients combines their evidence

**Position A.** A product containing three compounds, each with a published literature, is
supported by three literatures. The evidence for the parts is evidence for the whole, and a
blend of well-researched ingredients is therefore better researched than any of them alone.

**Position B.** Evidence for individual ingredients does not establish evidence for the
combination. A mixture is its own pharmacological entity. It has its own behaviour in the body,
its own interactions between components, and its own unknowns that do not exist for any
component studied alone. Only a study of the mixture can support a statement about the mixture.

**Assessment.** Position B is correct, and this is the central point of the entire packet.
Position A is not a weaker reading of the evidence, it is a category error. No study of this
three-component combination was located in any registry, index, species or route. That is not
a small amount of combination evidence. It is none.

The error is easy to make because it does not require anyone to write a false sentence. Three
accurate component sections placed under one product name will be read as a case for the
product. The claim table therefore prohibits the arrangement as well as the assertion, and the
editorial checklist asks reviewers to check the composed page rather than only the text.

There is a further point that cuts against Position A specifically. In this case the small
amount of retrieved material involving co-administration of two components points the other
way. FDA records a chart review in which co-administration made it impossible to attribute the
outcome to anything, and an adverse event report on a two-peptide product where the same
reason made it impossible to attribute the harm. Combining compounds did not strengthen the
evidence in either case. It destroyed the ability to interpret it.

## C-02. Whether the strength of a component's evidence tells you anything about the blend

**Position A.** GHK-Cu has a decades-old research literature and an established position as a
reviewed cosmetic ingredient. BPC-157 has hundreds of published studies. That weight of
research supports a product containing them.

**Position B.** Weight of research about a component says nothing about a mixture, and in this
case the component literatures do not say what Position A assumes either. The BPC-157
literature is overwhelmingly rodent and laboratory work, with FDA identifying five human
studies in total, of which three were never published in full. The GHK-Cu human literature is
topical, small, and negative on objective measures in both of the controlled trials retrieved.
TB-500 has no completed human study at all.

**Assessment.** Position B. Both halves matter. Even if the component literatures were strong,
they would not transfer to the mixture. As it happens they are also much weaker than the
volume of publication suggests, which means Position A is wrong twice over.

---

## C-03. Whether GHK-Cu's human evidence applies to an injected product

**Position A.** GHK-Cu is well studied in humans, is an established skincare ingredient, and
has been through a cosmetic safety review.

**Position B.** Every identified human study of GHK-Cu used the topical route. No human trial
of injected or otherwise systemically administered GHK-Cu was located in either the trial
registry or the published literature. Cosmetic ingredient review, if confirmed, concerns
cosmetic topical use. None of this speaks to a product given another way.

**Assessment.** Position B, and this is the sharpest single risk in this packet. These are not
two readings of the same evidence, they are statements about two different products that share
a name.

Two further points make the substitution worse rather than merely unsupported. First, the
topical evidence that does exist is largely negative on objective measures, so the borrowed
support would be weak even if borrowing were legitimate. Second, the cosmetic safety review
could not be primary-verified during research and its conclusion is not asserted anywhere in
this packet.

Naming the route in order to say the evidence is absent is permitted and required. Writing
around the route is not.

## C-04. Whether TB-500 evidence exists, or whether it belongs to a different molecule

**Position A.** TB-500 is supported by human trials in dry eye, corneal disease, wound healing
and heart attack, and has a substantial clinical record.

**Position B.** Every one of those trials studied full-length thymosin beta-4, a 43-amino-acid
protein, or a formulated investigational product made from it, typically delivered topically to
an eye or a wound surface. TB-500 is a seven-amino-acid acetylated fragment. The single
registered human study of the fragment began recruiting in February 2026, has adverse event
counts as its primary outcomes, and has posted no results.

**Assessment.** Position B is correct and this is the highest-priority accuracy issue for that
component. The substitution is routine in commercial material.

The read-across is not merely unproven, it is actively challenged. In fibroblast wound-healing
assays the parent TB-500 compound showed no activity while its metabolite did, and the authors
concluded that previously reported activity may belong to the metabolite rather than the parent
form. That is a single in vitro finding inside an analytical chemistry paper and should not be
treated as settled, but it means even the preclinical story for this component is unsettled,
and it undercuts the assumption that fragment and parent protein are interchangeable.

## C-05. Whether BPC-157's evidence base is large or thin

**Position A.** Marketing and enthusiast sources present BPC-157 as a broadly effective healing
peptide supported by a large volume of published studies.

**Position B.** The volume is real and it is almost entirely rodent and laboratory work, much of
it from a narrow set of research groups. FDA searched multiple databases and identified five
human studies in the entire published literature. A PubMed search restricted to systematic
reviews, randomized controlled trials and clinical trials returns zero records. An independent
2025 peer-reviewed review reached a similar count in the musculoskeletal context and concluded
the compound should be considered investigational.

**Assessment.** Position B, and this is not a genuine scientific controversy. The preclinical
literature is real. Its translation into human clinical evidence has not happened. The
distinction between preclinical and human must be unmistakable on every claim.

FDA's own reading of the preclinical literature is sharper than a reader might expect: molecular
targets are unidentified, mechanisms are poorly understood, and a dose-response relationship
could not be established because widely separated exposures produced comparable effects. That
last observation is an anomaly worth naming rather than passing over.

## C-06. Where a preclinical result does not mean what it appears to mean

**Position A.** GHK-Cu accelerated wound healing in animals, with reported effect sizes far
above control.

**Position B.** The same compound was tested in a randomized, evaluator-blinded human trial in
86 evaluable patients with venous stasis ulcers, and the copper complex showed no meaningful
difference from inert vehicle while a comparator did significantly reduce ulcer size.

**Assessment.** Both can be true, and together they are the clearest illustration in this packet
of why animal results do not carry across. This is not a contradiction to be resolved in favour
of one side. It is the normal pattern of a compound whose preclinical promise did not translate.

Note also that the animal figures were retrieved only through a tertiary encyclopedia summary
and the primary papers were not individually verified, so those figures are not published in
this packet at all.

## C-07. Objective measurement against subjective experience

**Position A.** People who used GHK-Cu after laser skin resurfacing were significantly more
satisfied than those who did not.

**Position B.** The same trial found no significant improvement in redness, wrinkles or overall
skin quality on blinded and computer-based assessment.

**Assessment.** This is a clean within-study dissociation and is genuinely informative rather
than contradictory. It is consistent with a real subjective experience without a measurable
tissue-level change. It also explains, without accusing anyone of dishonesty, how enthusiastic
personal reports coexist with flat trial data. That is worth explaining to a member directly,
because they will encounter both.

## C-08. Whether BPC-157 is currently in Category 2 of the FDA compounding framework

**Position A.** Numerous secondary sources state that FDA placed BPC-157 in Category 2 in
September 2023 and that this is its current status.

**Position B.** FDA's own page, content current as of 04/22/2026, lists the substance not in the
Category 2 table but in a separate table of bulk drug substances nominated but withdrawn,
described as substances previously in category 2 of the interim policies that were withdrawn by
the nominators. FDA's July 2026 briefing document confirms the nominations were withdrawn and
that FDA is now evaluating the substances on its own initiative.

**Assessment.** The practical consequence is the same either way, and it is what the Guide
states: the substance is not on the 503A bulks list, has no applicable monograph, and is not a
component of an approved drug, so compounding with it does not qualify for the section 503A
exemptions. That is confirmed by two separate FDA enforcement letters.

The specific September 2023 framing should not be repeated as current fact. The date could not
be verified from an FDA source during research, and FDA's own page now presents the matter
differently. A human should check the Federal Register and docket record before any date is
asserted.

## C-09. The direction of US federal policy on unapproved peptides

**Position A.** FDA staff, in a review memo dated 5/11/2026 placed before the advisory committee
meeting of 23 to 24 July 2026, recommended against adding BPC-157 free base and BPC-157 acetate
to the 503A bulks list, citing characterisation problems, lack of evidence of effectiveness,
immunogenicity risk, and the existence of approved alternatives for the evaluated condition.

**Position B.** Journalism dated 3 February 2026 reports that federal health leadership has
signalled openness to loosening restrictions on unapproved peptides, and describes agency
responses in December 2025 as open yet non-committal.

**Assessment.** These are not two readings of the same evidence. One is a scientific review
recommendation and the other is a political signal about a possible future direction. They can
both be accurate at once.

State explicitly: the journalism provides no evidence that any formal reversal of
classification occurred. A search summary encountered during research asserted that a
classification was reversed in February 2026. The article actually retrieved does not support
that, and the assertion should be treated as false or unverified.

Regulatory status may change. Every regulatory statement in this packet carries the date it was
checked, and the advisory committee outcome is stated nowhere because as of the date on this
file the meeting has not occurred.

## C-10. The compounding status of TB-500 and GHK-Cu

**Position A.** Thymosin beta-4 or TB-500, and GHK-Cu, are Category 2 substances barred from
compounding.

**Position B.** Their status changed during 2026 after nominations were withdrawn, and they are
scheduled for or have undergone advisory committee review. For GHK-Cu, secondary sources add
that status has been handled differently according to route, with injectable use treated as
raising immunogenicity and impurity concerns while non-injectable use was treated more
permissively.

**Assessment.** Unresolved and unverifiable from the retrieved record. Both positions came only
from commercial or vendor-adjacent sources. Every attempted FDA page returned an error during
research for both components, and the Federal Register notice that was retrieved names no
substances at all.

Neither position may be stated. The honest output is the gap itself. Note that the route-based
distinction described in Position B, if it were confirmed, would reinforce rather than soften
the central caution about GHK-Cu: it would mean the regulator treats the injectable route as
the concerning one.

## C-11. Whether being naturally occurring implies anything

**Position A.** GHK-Cu occurs naturally in human plasma and declines with age, and BPC-157 is
described as a partial sequence of a protein found in human gastric juice. These are body-native
molecules rather than foreign drugs.

**Position B.** Origin is not a safety property and not an efficacy property. A molecule that
occurs in the body at some level tells you nothing about what happens when a synthetic version
of it, of unconfirmed purity, is introduced by a different route, in the presence of two other
unstudied compounds.

**Assessment.** Position B. This framing appears frequently and is persuasive precisely because
it sounds like a reason rather than a claim. It should be addressed directly in the Guide and
in the FAQ rather than left unsaid.

## C-12. A note on the integrity of the source environment

This is not a contradiction between sources. It is a warning about a class of them.

During research on GHK-Cu, retailer and affiliate pages confidently described specific human
trials with specific statistics: a 2022 randomized controlled trial in androgenetic alopecia
with a stated hair count increase and a stated p-value, a 2023 phase II trial with a stated
scar volume reduction, and a human trial with a stated wrinkle volume reduction against a named
comparator. None could be located in PubMed or in any trial registry, and none of the sites
making the claims offered any identifier.

Separately, a figure that roughly 30 percent of grey-market peptide samples are mislabelled,
misdosed or contaminated appears widely and traces to no primary analysis.

These are excluded from this packet entirely. They are recorded here so that a reviewer
encountering them later knows they were seen and rejected, and so that any uncited statistic
from that source class is treated with suspicion by default. Numbers that cannot be traced do
not become usable by being repeated.

## C-13. What the ongoing trials mean

**Position A.** Multiple trials are now under way across all three compounds, which shows the
science is advancing and the compounds are being validated.

**Position B.** Registrations are not results. A Phase 1 study of BPC-157 registered in 2015 has
no posted results and no located publication a decade later. The Phase 2 hamstring trial began
in 2026 with estimated completion in 2028. The only TB-500 human study began recruiting in
February 2026 with adverse event counts as its primary outcomes. The controlled topical GHK-Cu
gel trial is recruiting with no results. Registry entries are sponsor-supplied.

**Assessment.** Position B. Ongoing trials are evidence that human study has only recently
begun, which is itself informative and belongs in the Guide. They are not evidence of anything
about the compounds, and they are not evidence about the combination, since none of them
studies the combination.

The 2015 registration is the cautionary case: a registered trial can simply never report.

## C-14. Jurisdictional and status differences

Status differs by jurisdiction, by route, by product category and by date, and this packet does
not claim a complete picture.

What is stated with a source and a date: US approval status for BPC-157, its position on FDA's
bulk substances page, two enforcement letters, the pending advisory committee review, and
anti-doping statements from a national anti-doping organisation for two components.

What is explicitly not stated: the compounding status of two components, the anti-doping status
of one component, and status in every jurisdiction outside the United States for all three.
These are recorded as not checked. Not checked never means permitted.
