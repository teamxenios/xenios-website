# BPC-157 + TB-500 + GHK-Cu

A blend of three synthetic peptides. This Guide covers what is known, what is not known, and
one distinction that matters more than everything else on this page.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## One-minute summary

**No study has ever tested this combination.** Not a clinical trial, not a case series, not an
animal study, not a laboratory study. Searches across trial registries and the published
literature for each of the three ingredients returned nothing that tested them together.

That single fact governs how the rest of this page should be read. **Evidence for individual
ingredients does not establish evidence for the combination.** When three compounds are put
into one preparation, the result is a new thing. It has its own behaviour in the body, its own
interactions between the components, and its own set of unknowns that do not exist for any of
the three studied alone. Only a study of the combination can speak to the combination, and no
such study exists. That is not weak evidence for this blend. It is no evidence at all.

The ingredient evidence, taken separately, is also much thinner than its reputation.

- **BPC-157.** Not an approved drug anywhere in the world. FDA searched the published
  literature and trial registries and identified five human studies in total. Three exist only
  as conference abstracts never published in full. One is a chart review of 17 people at a
  single private clinic. One is a two-person series. The only randomized controlled trial FDA
  could find did not separate from placebo, and FDA called its data inadequate to support
  efficacy or safety. A literature search restricted to systematic reviews, randomized trials
  and clinical trials returns zero records.
- **TB-500.** No completed human study exists. The single registered human study of this
  molecule began recruiting in February 2026, has counts of adverse events as its main
  outcomes, and has published no results. Human trials frequently cited as TB-500 evidence
  studied a different, much larger molecule, given as eye drops or applied to a wound.
- **GHK-Cu.** All identified human evidence is topical. No human study of this compound given
  by any other route was located. The two controlled, blinded topical trials that exist were
  both negative on objective measures.

The exact composition and ingredient ratio of this product is **not confirmed**. No ratio
appears anywhere in this Guide, and none may be added.

On safety, the correct statement is not that this blend is safe or that it is dangerous. It is
that nobody knows. No human safety data for the combination exists. FDA has stated there is
insufficient information to characterise the safety profile of one ingredient, the first human
safety study of another is still recruiting, and the systemic safety of the third is
uncharacterised because it has only ever been studied on the skin.

Two ingredients are prohibited in sport according to national anti-doping guidance. The status
of the third could not be determined.

Xenios does not sell these compounds and does not say how to obtain them.

## What it is

This product is described as containing three synthetic peptides. Each is briefly described
below. Nothing in these three descriptions should be read as describing the mixture.

### BPC-157

A synthetic peptide reported to consist of 15 amino acids, described as a partial sequence of a
larger protein originally isolated from human gastric juice. FDA records the molecular formula
of the free base as C62H98N16O22 with a molecular weight of 1419.5 g/mol, and notes that
BPC-157 is not a United States Adopted Name.

A point that sounds technical and is not: FDA treats **BPC-157 free base and BPC-157 acetate as
two different active pharmaceutical ingredients**, and states that the common name BPC-157 is
used commercially for multiple salts, derivatives, and even different active moieties. FDA's
own comment is that inconsistent naming represents a safety risk, because a person may receive
a different substance than the one intended. There is no USP or NF monograph for either form,
no USP dietary supplement monograph, and FDA states the substance is not found in the European,
Japanese or International Pharmacopeias. So there is no official specification anyone can be
held to.

### TB-500

A synthetic, chemically modified seven-amino-acid peptide corresponding to a short segment of a
larger protein called thymosin beta-4.

Three things must not be run together, and confusing them is the most common error made about
this ingredient:

1. **TB-500**, the short synthetic fragment described here. This is what is sold under the name
   TB-500.
2. **Thymosin beta-4**, the full-length naturally occurring 43-amino-acid protein. A different,
   much larger molecule. Nearly all published human trial data attaches to this one, not to
   TB-500.
3. **Investigational pharmaceutical products formulated from full-length thymosin beta-4.**
   These are regulated investigational products with registered trials. They are not a vial
   sold as TB-500.

Vendor and secondary material frequently uses TB-500 and thymosin beta-4 as though they were
the same word. The retrieved primary sources do not support that usage. A further practical
consequence: a purchaser cannot tell from a product name alone whether a vial is claimed to
contain the seven-amino-acid fragment or the 43-amino-acid protein.

### GHK-Cu

The copper complex of a three-amino-acid peptide, glycyl-L-histidyl-L-lysine. It occurs
naturally in human plasma, saliva and urine, and plasma levels are described as declining with
age. In cosmetics it carries the ingredient name Copper Tripeptide-1.

The distinction that matters most for this ingredient is not chemical but practical. **GHK-Cu as
a topical cosmetic ingredient and GHK-Cu in an injectable product are treated as the same thing
in consumer marketing, and they have completely different evidence and regulatory footing.**
Every human study of this compound that was located used the topical route. That point recurs
throughout this Guide because it is the sharpest risk on this page.

It is also distinct from plain GHK without copper, and from other cosmetic copper peptides such
as palmitoyl copper peptide or copper hexapeptide. Findings for those are not findings for
GHK-Cu.

### The product itself

The exact composition and ingredient ratio has not been confirmed. Which chemical form of each
component is present, including salt form, has not been confirmed. This is the first open
question for any supplier, and it comes before every other question, because until the
composition is fixed and verified there is no defined article to describe, test, or write
about.

## Why interest exists

Interest in this blend comes from three places, and it is worth naming them plainly because
each is a real reason people find the idea persuasive.

**A large animal literature.** BPC-157 in particular has a genuinely substantial body of rodent
and laboratory work reporting protective and wound-healing effects across many tissues. GHK-Cu
has decades of animal and cell research. That volume is real. What has not happened is its
translation into human clinical evidence.

**A plausible mechanical story.** Each ingredient has a proposed mechanism that sounds coherent:
tissue repair signalling, cell migration, blood vessel formation, copper delivery. Coherent
mechanisms are persuasive. They are not evidence of a clinical effect. GHK-Cu is the clearest
case: its mechanistic account has been developed over decades while the controlled human trials
that exist have been largely negative.

**The appeal of combination itself.** A blend implies that its parts cover different aspects of
the same problem and reinforce one another. That is an appealing story and there is nothing
behind it here. No study has examined whether these three compounds interact at all, in any
direction.

Interest also exists because these compounds are marketed heavily, frequently with citations
that do not hold up. During research for this Guide, several specific human trial statistics
for GHK-Cu found on retail pages could not be located in any index or registry, and none of the
sites making the claims offered any identifier. Those claims are excluded from this Guide
entirely, and they are the reason the sourcing rules for these Guides are strict.

## Terminology

Plain definitions for the technical terms used on this page.

**Peptide.** A short chain of amino acids. Amino acids are the units proteins are built from. A
peptide is smaller than a protein, and where the line falls is a matter of convention rather
than a sharp rule.

**Amino acid residue numbering.** When a source says a fragment corresponds to residues 17 to
23 of a protein, it means the fragment is a copy of that specific stretch of the larger
molecule, counted from one end.

**Preclinical.** Research in animals, in cells grown in a dish, or in tissue outside a living
body. It is not evidence about people. Every preclinical finding in this Guide names its
species or model in the same sentence.

**In vitro and ex vivo.** In vitro means in a dish. Ex vivo means in tissue removed from a
living body. Neither is human evidence, even when the cells or tissue came from a human being.

**Topical.** Applied to a surface such as skin, a wound, or the eye. Topical evidence describes
what happened at that surface. It does not describe what happens when a substance is
administered another way.

**Randomized controlled trial.** A study in which participants are assigned by chance to
receive the thing being tested or a comparison, so that the two groups can be compared fairly.
It is the design that best separates a real effect from expectation and from natural recovery.

**Blinded, or masked.** Participants, assessors, or both do not know who received what. This
matters most when the outcome is subjective.

**Placebo-controlled and vehicle-controlled.** The comparison group receives an inactive
version. Vehicle means the cream or liquid without the active ingredient.

**Open-label.** Everyone knows who is receiving what. Open-label results are much weaker,
particularly for outcomes based on how people feel.

**Confidence interval.** A range of values consistent with the data. When a confidence interval
for the difference between two groups includes zero, the study did not demonstrate a difference.

**Pharmacokinetics.** What the body does to a substance: how much enters, where it goes, how it
is broken down, how it leaves.

**Metabolite.** A product formed when the body breaks a substance down. A metabolite can behave
differently from the original substance, which matters here for one ingredient.

**Immunogenicity.** The tendency of a substance to provoke an immune response against itself.
It is a recognised concern for peptides, and it can be increased by clumping and by impurities.

**Aggregation.** Peptide molecules clumping together, which can happen during manufacture,
formulation or storage. It is one of the drivers of the immunogenicity concern.

**Certificate of analysis.** A document from a laboratory stating test results for a batch of
material. What it covers varies enormously, and a certificate showing only purity says nothing
about what the remaining material is.

**Impurity profile.** The identity and quantity of everything in a batch that is not the
intended substance. Purity alone is not an impurity profile.

**Bulk drug substance and compounding.** A bulk drug substance is a raw active ingredient.
Compounding is the preparation of a medicine by a pharmacy for a specific person. Certain legal
exemptions apply only to substances that meet defined criteria.

**Monograph.** An official published standard for a substance, setting out identity, purity and
quality specifications. Where no monograph exists, there is no official standard to test
against.

**Adverse event reporting system.** A database of voluntarily submitted reports of problems
experienced by people using a product. A report does not establish that the product caused the
problem, and many events are never reported at all.

**Rechallenge.** Re-exposure to a substance after a reaction resolved. If the same reaction
recurs, that strengthens the case for a link.

**Advisory committee.** A panel that reviews evidence and gives a recommendation to a regulator.
A recommendation is advice. It is not a final decision by the agency.

## Proposed mechanism

Everything in this section is proposed. Every part of it comes from animal work, cell work, or
inference from a related molecule. None of it has been confirmed in a living human being for
any of the three ingredients, and none of it addresses the mixture.

**BPC-157.** FDA's assessment is the clearest available summary: the molecular targets have not
been identified and the mechanisms of action remain poorly understood. Pathways proposed in
animal and cell studies include signalling through a receptor involved in blood vessel
formation, nitric oxide production, and two named cell signalling routes. In rat tendon
explants and cultured rat tendon fibroblasts, one study reported changes in cell survival and
migration and proposed a particular signalling pathway as the explanation. These are proposals
drawn from rodent and cell systems, not established human mechanisms.

**TB-500.** The proposed mechanism is binding to actin, a structural protein inside cells, with
downstream effects on cell movement and tissue repair. This is inferred largely from work on
the full-length parent protein rather than demonstrated for the fragment in humans, and the
inference step from parent protein to fragment is not established.

That story is also directly challenged. In fibroblast wound-healing assays, a metabolite of
TB-500 showed significant activity while the parent TB-500 compound did not. The authors
concluded that previously reported wound-healing activity may be due to the metabolite rather
than the parent form. That is a single laboratory finding inside an analytical chemistry paper
and should not be treated as settled. It does mean the mechanistic account commonly given for
this ingredient may be attributing activity to the wrong molecule, and it complicates any
attempt to reason from animal results to people.

**GHK-Cu.** The proposal is that it modulates copper delivery into cells, with the complex
described as reducing copper's reactivity so the metal can be carried in a less reactive form.
It has been hypothesised to act as a signal released from damaged tissue after injury. Effects
reported in preclinical models include increased collagen production and blood vessel
formation. Broader claims circulating in the consumer market about wide-ranging effects on gene
activity were not verified against primary literature and are not asserted here.

It is worth saying plainly: a plausible mechanism is not evidence of a clinical effect. The
mechanistic story for GHK-Cu has been well developed for decades, and the controlled human
trials that exist have been largely negative.

**The combination.** Whether the three components interact mechanistically, whether the
presence of one affects a proposed pathway of another, and whether the mixture behaves in any
way like the sum of its parts are all entirely unstudied. No interaction, compatibility or
pharmacokinetic study between any pair of these three compounds was located. There is nothing
to report here in either direction, and speculation about synergy would be invention.

## Human evidence

### For the combination

**None.** No study of BPC-157 with TB-500 with GHK-Cu was located, of any design, in any
registry or index. There is no human evidence for this combination, and there is no animal or
laboratory evidence for it either.

This is the finding. It is not a shortfall in the research and it should not be read as one. An
honest empty evidence table is the correct outcome when nothing has been studied, and filling
it with findings about the separate ingredients would misrepresent the state of knowledge.

Two records document use of **two** of these compounds together, and neither supports the
mixture. Both are worth stating precisely, because they show what combination use has actually
produced so far.

In a retrospective chart review of knee pain in 17 people at a single private clinic, FDA
records that some subjects received BPC-157 combined with thymosin beta-4, and states in
consequence that effects cannot be attributed to BPC-157 alone. Combining did not add evidence.
It removed the ability to interpret the result. Note that this concerns the full-length protein
as described, not the TB-500 fragment, and no GHK-Cu was involved.

In a report to FDA's adverse event system, a 40-year-old woman developed widespread skin
darkening and darkening of the gums while using a product containing both BPC-157 and TB500,
with the same reaction recurring on re-exposure and resolving when she stopped. FDA judged the
reaction likely due to the drug product, and stated that because the product contained two
peptides it is not possible to assess a relationship to BPC-157 specifically. A separate report
of an injection site reaction was also confounded by a concurrent second peptide.

So the retrieved human record of multi-peptide products of this kind consists of one
uninterpretable series and two confounded reports, one of which a regulator considered likely
product-related. There is nothing that could honestly be read as combination benefit.

### For BPC-157 alone

No BPC-157 product is approved as a drug anywhere in the world. FDA states there is no approved
product containing BPC-157-related bulk drug substances in any country.

FDA searched multiple databases and trial registries and identified **five human studies in the
entire published literature**:

1. **An uncontrolled pilot in 12 women with interstitial cystitis**, ages 39 to 76, at a single
   private clinic. The authors reported symptom resolution in all 12 by a self-rated
   questionnaire. This is the only study of this compound indexed in PubMed. It was
   uncontrolled, unblinded, tiny, at one site, with a subjective self-report as its main
   outcome, in a condition where placebo and procedural effects are substantial. FDA records
   that the material came from a compounding pharmacy of undisclosed identity. It is
   hypothesis-generating and cannot establish efficacy or safety.
2. **A randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled study in ulcerative colitis**, 53
   randomized and 46 completed, published only as a 2005 conference abstract. This is the only
   randomized controlled trial FDA identified. The confidence interval for the difference
   between groups crossed zero, meaning no separation from placebo was demonstrated. FDA's
   conclusion: the data are inadequate to support the efficacy and safety of BPC-157.
3. **A placebo-controlled Phase 1 tolerability study in 32 healthy subjects**, of whom 24
   received the compound, reported in conference abstracts only. Authors reported no
   significant adverse events attributable to treatment, with headache and flatulence most
   frequent and no obvious difference from placebo. The compound was not detected in blood
   samples.
4. **A retrospective chart review of knee pain in 17 people** at a single private clinic, where
   some subjects also received a second peptide, described above.
5. **A single-arm study in two people** assessed over three days. An n of 2 is a case series and
   cannot support any safety conclusion.

A PubMed search restricted to systematic reviews, randomized controlled trials and clinical
trials returned **zero records**.

Two registrations exist and neither has produced anything. A Phase 1 study registered in 2015
has no posted results and no located publication a decade later. A Phase 2 trial in hamstring
strain began recruiting in 2026, with estimated completion in 2028. Registry entries are
supplied by sponsors, and a registered trial can simply never report, as the 2015 registration
illustrates.

An independent 2025 peer-reviewed review reached a similar count in the musculoskeletal context
and concluded the compound should be considered investigational.

### For TB-500 alone

**There is no completed human study of TB-500.** A full trial registry query returned exactly
one registered human study of this molecule. It began recruiting in February 2026, its main
outcomes are counts of adverse events over defined periods rather than any measure of benefit,
and no results are posted. That a first safety study is only now running is itself the evidence
that no established human profile exists.

A 2026 peer-reviewed review in a sports medicine journal states directly that human
orthopaedic data for this compound and its parent protein are lacking.

The trials routinely cited as TB-500 evidence are not studies of TB-500. Each studied
full-length thymosin beta-4 or a formulated investigational product made from it, and each was
delivered to a surface rather than systemically:

- A Phase 2 randomized, double-masked, placebo-controlled trial of an **eye drop** in nine
  patients with severe dry eye reported significant improvement versus placebo on discomfort
  and corneal staining.
- A Phase 3 randomized, placebo-controlled, double-masked trial of an **eye drop** in a rare
  corneal condition was terminated early for slow recruitment with 18 subjects total. The
  difference in healing at the primary timepoint did not reach statistical significance,
  reaching it only at a later timepoint. The authors note the placebo group was older with
  larger lesions.
- A frequently cited **topical** venous ulcer study reports methodology for an in-progress
  trial with 21 of a planned 72 patients enrolled, and **no outcome data at all**. It is
  routinely cited secondhand as though it demonstrated accelerated healing. The primary record
  does not support that.

Presenting any of these as TB-500 evidence is a category error. Presenting them as evidence for
this blend adds the combination error on top of it.

### For GHK-Cu alone

**All identified human evidence for GHK-Cu is topical.** No human trial of this compound
administered by any other route was located in either the trial registry or the published
literature. Naming the route here is necessary in order to state accurately that the evidence
for other routes is absent.

The two identified randomized, blinded, controlled human trials of a copper tripeptide complex
were both **negative on objective measures**:

- In a 1992 trial in 86 evaluable patients with venous stasis ulcers, a comparator produced a
  statistically significant reduction in ulcer size while the copper complex showed no
  meaningful difference from inert vehicle.
- In a 2006 trial in 13 people after laser skin resurfacing, there was no significant reduction
  in redness by computer analysis or by blinded evaluators, and no significant improvement in
  wrinkles or overall skin quality.

The 2006 trial contains one of the more informative findings in this whole Guide. Patient
satisfaction was significantly higher in the treated group despite the absence of any objective
benefit. That dissociation is consistent with a real subjective experience without a measurable
tissue-level change, and it goes some way to explaining how enthusiastic personal accounts
coexist with flat trial data.

Recent positive human reports are much weaker than they appear. Two studies from 2024 and 2025
reported improvements in scalp condition and in scarring. Both were open-label and
uncontrolled, both were short, both tested multi-ingredient proprietary formulations rather
than the peptide alone, and both came from a single author group sponsored by the manufacturer
of the tested products, with author financial relationships disclosed. Because a combination
product was tested in each case, nothing can be attributed to the copper peptide. One reported
effect size exceeds 100 percent, which points to a reporting or denominator problem.

One properly designed topical trial is under way, comparing a gel against a vehicle placebo in
60 people with standardised wounds. It has no posted results. It is the study worth waiting for
on the topical question, and it will not answer any question about this blend.

## Preclinical evidence

Every finding in this section names its species or model. None of it is evidence about people,
and none of it is evidence about the combination.

**No preclinical study of the three components together was located, in any species or model.**
A reader may reasonably assume that at least animal work on the mixture exists. It does not.

**BPC-157, rodents and rat tissue.** FDA summarises that studies in rodents have reported
prevention of lesions induced by various challenges in the gastrointestinal and cardiovascular
systems, healing of divided tendons, and protective effects in models of stroke, brain injury
and spinal cord injury. In rat tendon explants and cultured rat tendon fibroblasts, one study
reported accelerated cell outgrowth, increased cell survival under oxidative stress, and
increased cell migration and spreading, with no effect on cell multiplication by the assay
used, and proposed a specific signalling pathway.

FDA's assessment of that literature belongs in the same breath as the literature itself.
Biological plausibility is difficult to assess because dose-response relationships have not
been established, the molecular targets have not been identified, and the mechanisms remain
poorly understood. FDA notes repeatedly that a dose-response relationship could not be
established because widely separated exposures produced comparable effects. That is an unusual
pattern and worth naming rather than passing over. FDA also notes that several studies lacked
confirmation by examination of tissue.

**TB-500, review level and rats.** A 2026 narrative review reports that the parent protein and
its derivative promoted blood vessel formation and tissue repair in preclinical models, while
stating that human data are lacking. The species behind that summary are not specified in the
record available, and the underlying primary studies were not retrieved, so this rests on a
review rather than on primary sources. In rats, one metabolite of TB-500 was the most abundant
early on and another persisted for up to 72 hours, which is relevant chiefly to anti-doping
detection rather than to any benefit claim.

The most important preclinical finding for this ingredient is the one that complicates it. In
fibroblast wound-healing assays, the parent TB-500 compound showed no activity while a
metabolite did.

**GHK-Cu, various animals and cells.** The preclinical literature includes records in rabbits,
mice, nematodes and zebrafish larvae, and in cultured fibroblasts and laboratory-maintained
human skin. One of those records is a preprint that has not been peer reviewed. One is a
laboratory and tissue study and is not human evidence, even though the tissue is human in
origin. These records were retrieved at title level, their abstracts were not read, and no
finding from any of them is asserted in this Guide.

Animal wound-healing figures for this compound circulate widely. They were retrieved only
through a tertiary encyclopedia summary, the primary papers were not verified, and the figures
are therefore not published here.

**What happened when the animal work was tested in people.** This compound offers the clearest
illustration in this Guide of why preclinical results do not carry across. Animal wound-healing
results have been reported for decades. The best-controlled human wound-healing trial of a
copper tripeptide complex found no meaningful difference from inert vehicle. Both things are
true, and the pattern is the ordinary one for a compound whose animal promise did not
translate.

## Contradictions

Where sources disagree, the disagreements are set out in full in CONTRADICTIONS.md. The ones a
member is most likely to encounter are summarised here.

**Does combining ingredients combine their evidence?** No. This is not a disagreement between
sources, it is a category error that appears wherever blends are sold. Three ingredient
literatures do not add up to a combination literature. The mixture has its own pharmacology and
its own unknowns, and only a study of the mixture addresses them.

**Is GHK-Cu well studied in humans?** It depends entirely on the route, and the answer for any
route other than topical is no. Cosmetic ingredient status and topical trial data describe
topical cosmetic use. They say nothing about a product given another way. This is the single
most likely way a reader will be misled on this page.

**Does TB-500 have human trials?** No. The trials cited as its evidence studied a different,
larger molecule, delivered as eye drops or applied to a wound. The read-across is not merely
unproven, it is challenged by the laboratory finding that the parent compound showed no
activity where its metabolite did.

**Is the BPC-157 evidence base large?** The volume of publication is real and it is almost
entirely rodent and laboratory work. The human clinical evidence is five studies, three of them
never published in full, and zero indexed randomized trials, systematic reviews or clinical
trials. This is not a genuine scientific controversy.

**Does the animal evidence mean it works?** For GHK-Cu the question has been partly answered:
animal wound-healing results were reported, a controlled human wound-healing trial was run, and
it was negative for the copper complex. That is the ordinary pattern.

**Is BPC-157 currently in Category 2 of the US compounding framework?** Secondary sources say
it was placed there in September 2023. FDA's own page presents the substance under nominated
but withdrawn rather than in the dated Category 2 table. The practical consequence is the same
either way and is stated in the regulatory section below. The specific date could not be
verified from an FDA source and is not repeated here as fact.

**Are the compounding statuses of TB-500 and GHK-Cu known?** No. Every attempted FDA page
returned an error during research for both. Competing claims exist and all of them come from
commercial sources. No position is stated here in either direction.

**A note on the information environment.** Several specific human trial statistics for GHK-Cu
found on retail sites could not be located in any index or registry, and none of the sites
offered an identifier. A widely repeated figure about grey-market peptide failure rates traces
to no primary analysis. All of these are excluded. Any uncited statistic from that class of
source should be treated with suspicion by default.

## Limitations

These limitations apply to this Guide, not only to the compounds.

**The combination search is the load-bearing one and it should be repeated.** The conclusion
that no combination study exists rests on searches of the registries and the published
literature for each ingredient. A combination study would be expected to appear under its
components, so the conclusion is reasonably founded, but a reviewer should personally re-run a
dedicated combination search and record the date.

**The product is not defined.** With no confirmed composition or ratio, this Guide describes
three named ingredients rather than a specified article.

**Several regulatory questions could not be answered.** Every attempted FDA page returned an
error during research for two of the three ingredients, so no compounding-status claim is made
for either. The anti-doping list could not be read directly for any ingredient, so anti-doping
statements rest on national anti-doping guidance rather than on the list itself, and one
ingredient's status is not stated at all.

**Four of the five BPC-157 human records were not read directly.** They are known through FDA's
description of them. They are cited here as reported by FDA, and no identifier was constructed
for any of them.

**Several GHK-Cu preclinical records were retrieved at title level only.** Their abstracts were
not read and no finding from them is asserted.

**Some sourcing is second-hand.** The identity and mechanism material for GHK-Cu comes in part
from a tertiary encyclopedia entry citing primary literature that was not individually
retrieved. The preclinical summary for TB-500 comes from a narrative review rather than from
primary studies.

**A cosmetic safety review could not be retrieved.** Its conclusion is not asserted anywhere
here. Even if confirmed, it would concern cosmetic topical use.

**Additional registries were not searched**, including the EU register, the UK register, the WHO
platform, and the Japanese and Chinese national registries, for any ingredient or for the
combination.

**Dates need reconciling.** The underlying research was conducted on 2026-07-19 and this Guide
carries a review date of 2026-07-21. Regulatory statements in particular must be re-checked and
re-dated immediately before publication.

**This Guide is an AI draft.** It has not been reviewed by a scientific, claims, quality, legal
or founder reviewer. Nothing here is member-facing until that review is complete.

## Quality and identity

Full requirements are set out in QUALITY_AND_DOCUMENTATION.md. What follows is what the
retrieved sources establish, and what a blend adds.

**The name does not identify the substance.** FDA states that BPC-157 is a common name rather
than a standardised one, and that it has encountered multiple salts and derivatives, including
different active moieties, sold commercially under that same name. FDA's own framing is that
inconsistent naming is a safety risk because a person may be given a different substance than
intended. Separately, a product name does not reveal whether a TB-500 vial is claimed to
contain the short fragment or the full-length protein.

**There is no standard to test against.** No monograph exists for BPC-157 in the US, European,
Japanese or International Pharmacopeias, so there is no official identity, purity or quality
specification.

**Certificates of analysis in this market do not prove what a reader assumes.** FDA examined
certificates published by commercial peptide sellers and found that most contain purity testing
only, with no information about impurity limits or testing to demonstrate control of the
impurity profile. A purity figure says how much of the material is the intended substance. It
does not say what the rest is.

**Impurities of concern are not addressed.** FDA notes that likely impurities of concern for a
bulk substance include nitrosamines and potentially mutagenic and teratogenic substances, and
that available data do not address these. Peptide synthesis also introduces residual solvents,
reagents, catalysts, and truncated or modified peptide impurities.

**Basic characterisation is missing.** FDA reports that no particle size, microbial quality,
endotoxin, sterility, aggregate or container data were available for the proposed product
forms, and concluded that both forms of that ingredient are not well characterised from a
physical and chemical perspective.

**Synthesis contaminants have been detected in a marketed product of one ingredient.** A
non-natural synthesis impurity was detectable in horse blood plasma after administration of a
TB-500-containing product. That study did not characterise purity across the consumer market.

**Compounded and grey-market peptide products carry documented category-level risk.** A 2026
white paper on compounded peptide products addresses contamination, mislabelling and quality
variability, and cites a 2012 contaminated-compounding outbreak as precedent for real harm. It
does not name any ingredient in this blend, and it is cited here only at the category level.

**Consumer products are often mislabelled.** Products are commonly labelled research use only
or not for human consumption while being marketed for human use. That framing is not used on
any Xenios surface as an implicit signal of benefit.

**No product testing exists for this blend or its ingredients.** No study testing material sold
under these names for identity, purity, sterility or endotoxin content was located. That is a
gap, not a clean result. Nobody appears to have checked.

**What a blend adds.** A mixture creates quality requirements a single-ingredient product does
not have, and they are the requirements most likely to be skipped:

- identity confirmed **for each component separately**, not for the mixture as a whole
- assay confirmed **for each component separately**, so the content of each is known
- **uniformity** of the mixture, so that the composition is the same throughout the batch and
  from unit to unit
- **stability of the components in one another's presence**, since a mixture can degrade
  differently from any ingredient alone

None of this has been confirmed for this product. Neither has the composition it would be
confirmed against.

## Safety categories

**The primary safety finding is that no human safety data for this combination exists.** Nothing
about the safety of these three compounds taken together can be concluded in either direction.
Absence of reported harm reflects absence of study, not a safety record.

The sections below are categories of concern that a qualified professional would consider. None
is a prediction that harm will occur, and none is reassurance.

**Uncharacterised safety profiles for every ingredient.** FDA's conclusion for BPC-157 is that
there is insufficient clinical safety information to characterise its safety profile, that
non-clinical toxicology studies were too limited in scope and duration to inform safety
considerations, and that human data on how the compound behaves in the body is absent for most
routes. For TB-500 there is no established human safety profile at any exposure over any
duration, and the first registered human safety study is still recruiting. For GHK-Cu, systemic
safety, immune response and the consequences of copper delivery by any route other than
topically are uncharacterised, because no such human study exists.

**Immunogenicity.** FDA's specific flagged concern for BPC-157 is immunogenicity, meaning an
immune response directed against the peptide itself, potentially amplified by peptide clumping
and by impurities. FDA states it is not aware of information suggesting the substance does not
present these risks. This is a regulator-flagged mechanistic concern, not an observed clinical
outcome.

**Reported adverse events, and why so few is not reassuring.** FDA retrieved three adverse event
reports for BPC-157 through December 2025, all involving injectable products, and all
confounded. One was an injection site reaction with a concurrent second peptide. One was
shortness of breath resulting in an emergency room visit, with no further information. One was
widespread skin darkening and gum darkening on a two-peptide product, recurring on re-exposure
and resolving on stopping, which FDA judged likely due to the drug product while noting that
the two-peptide composition made attribution impossible. FDA's own caveats apply: adverse event
reports do not establish causation, and compounders generally do not report adverse events, so
underreporting is expected.

**The absence of serious events in the small human studies is uninformative.** FDA notes that no
serious adverse events appear to have been reported in the identified BPC-157 studies, and
immediately adds that those studies were short, small, used exploratory exposures, provided
limited safety data, and had unclear safety monitoring. Total documented human exposure across
all of them is on the order of dozens of people for days to about two weeks. That is far too
little to detect uncommon or delayed harms.

**Copper.** Copper is a physiologically active metal with an upper tolerance, so a
copper-carrying compound is not pharmacologically inert. This is general toxicology reasoning
rather than a retrieved finding about this compound, and it is flagged here as an open question
requiring a proper source. [UNVERIFIED - background knowledge, requires human source check]

**Blood vessel formation.** Promotion of new blood vessel growth is a proposed mechanism for
more than one ingredient here. Whether any long-term risk attaches to sustained signalling of
that kind was not assessed in any retrieved source, in either direction. It is recorded as an
open question, not as a finding.

**Combination uncertainty.** Combining three uncharacterised substances multiplies uncertainty
rather than dividing it. Each brings its own unknown profile, and the mixture adds a further set
of unknowns about how they behave together that does not exist for any of them alone. This is
an argument from the structure of the evidence rather than a finding, and it is stated as such.

**Product quality as a distinct risk.** Even setting aside what the compounds do, there is no
confirmed composition, no official standard, no impurity control demonstrated in market
certificates, and no testing of material sold under these names. What is in a given vial is not
established.

**Populations entirely unstudied.** For every ingredient and for the combination: pregnancy,
breastfeeding, children, older adults, immunocompromised people, people with cancer or a cancer
history, people with liver or kidney impairment, people with a disorder of copper metabolism,
and people taking any medicine.

**Anti-doping.** For an athlete subject to testing, this is a documented and concrete risk
rather than a theoretical one. See the regulatory section.

## Interactions

No interaction study of any kind was located, for any ingredient or for the combination. That
includes interactions between the three components themselves.

This is not a statement that no interactions occur. It is a statement that nobody has looked.
Unstudied does not mean absent.

Categories that a qualified professional would need to consider, none of which has been studied
for these compounds:

- **Interaction between the three components.** Whether one alters how another is absorbed,
  distributed, broken down or eliminated. Whether an effect of one is amplified or cancelled by
  another. Whether the mixture produces something none of them produces alone.
- **Prescription medicines of every class**, including anticoagulants and antiplatelet
  medicines, immune-modulating medicines, cancer therapies, and anything with a narrow margin
  between a useful and a harmful exposure.
- **Copper status and anything affecting it**, including copper or zinc supplementation, since
  zinc and copper interact in the body, and any condition affecting copper handling.
- **Other peptides and other unapproved substances**, which is directly relevant here because
  these compounds are commonly sold and used alongside further peptides. FDA's records show
  that co-administration has already made attribution impossible in both a clinical series and
  an adverse event report.
- **Supplements and over-the-counter products**, which are frequently omitted when people
  describe what they are taking.
- **Existing conditions**, particularly any cancer or cancer history given the proposed effects
  on blood vessel formation, any autoimmune or inflammatory condition, and any condition
  affecting the liver or kidneys.

Anyone considering these compounds should give a qualified professional a complete list of
everything they take, including supplements and anything obtained outside a pharmacy.

## Questions for a qualified professional

If you are considering this or discussing it with a clinician, these are questions worth asking.
They are written to be used as they stand.

**About the evidence**

1. Is there any study of this specific combination? If not, what does that mean for how I should
   weigh anything I have read about the individual ingredients?
2. For each ingredient separately, what human evidence exists, and what was actually studied:
   which molecule, given how, to how many people, for how long?
3. Is the human evidence I have been shown for one ingredient actually about that ingredient, or
   about a related molecule or a different route?
4. Are the trials being cited to me completed with published results, or are they registrations
   with nothing reported?

**About safety**

5. What is known about the safety of these three compounds taken together? What is known about
   each one alone?
6. What would we monitor, and would monitoring actually detect the risks that concern you?
7. Does anything in my history, or anything I currently take, make this a worse idea for me
   specifically?
8. What is the concern about immune responses to peptides, and does it apply here?
9. Is there any concern related to copper for me specifically?
10. Is there any concern about promoting blood vessel growth given my personal and family
    history?

**About the product**

11. What exactly is in it, in what form, and in what proportion? Who has verified that, and how?
12. Has each ingredient been separately identified and measured in this product, and is the
    mixture uniform?
13. What does the certificate of analysis actually test for, and does it control impurities or
    only report purity?
14. Who made it, where, and to what standard? Can the certificate be confirmed directly with the
    laboratory that issued it?
15. Is it sterile, and has endotoxin been tested and found within a stated limit?

**About the decision**

16. What would you need to see before you would consider this reasonable?
17. What are the alternatives with actual evidence behind them for what I am trying to address?
18. If I am subject to anti-doping testing, what is the risk, and where should I check?
19. What would you want me to stop and report immediately?
20. If I decide against this, what would you suggest instead?

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.
