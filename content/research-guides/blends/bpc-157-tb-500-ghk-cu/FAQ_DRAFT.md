# BPC-157 + TB-500 + GHK-Cu: questions members actually ask

Plain answers, with the same sourcing rules as the main Guide. Nothing here is medical advice.

Dosing and administration information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research Guides.

---

## 1. Does this blend work?

Nobody knows, because it has never been tested.

No study of BPC-157 with TB-500 with GHK-Cu was located. Not a clinical trial, not a case
series, not an animal study, not a laboratory study. Searches of the trial registries and the
published literature for each ingredient returned nothing that tested them together.

This is a real answer, not an evasion. When a question has not been studied, the honest response
is that it is unanswered, and the alternative would be to assemble findings about the separate
ingredients into something that reads like an answer without being one.

---

## 2. But each ingredient has research behind it. Doesn't that count for the blend?

No, and this is the single most important thing on this page.

Evidence for individual ingredients does not establish evidence for the combination. Putting
three compounds into one preparation creates a new thing. It behaves in its own way in the body,
the components may interact with one another, and it carries unknowns that none of the three
carries alone. Only a study of the combination can tell you about the combination.

There is no combination evidence here. Not thin evidence, not early evidence, not preliminary
evidence. None.

It is worth adding what the little combination-adjacent material that does exist actually shows.
FDA records a chart review in which some people received two of these kinds of compounds
together, and states that because of this, effects could not be attributed to either one. FDA
also records an adverse event report on a product containing two peptides, where the same
problem made it impossible to say which was responsible. In both cases, combining did not
strengthen the evidence. It made the result impossible to interpret.

---

## 3. What is actually in the product?

Not confirmed. The exact composition and ingredient ratio of this product has not been
established, and no ratio appears anywhere in these documents.

That sounds like a technicality and it is not. Until the composition is fixed and verified in
writing, there is no defined product to describe, test or evaluate. It is the first question for
any supplier, and it comes before every other question.

There is a related problem with names. FDA treats BPC-157 free base and BPC-157 acetate as two
different active ingredients, and states that the common name BPC-157 is used commercially for
multiple salts, derivatives and even different active molecules. Separately, a product name does
not reveal whether a TB-500 vial is claimed to contain the short seven-amino-acid fragment or
the much larger 43-amino-acid protein. Those are different molecules with different evidence.

---

## 4. Is it safe?

Nobody knows, and that is the accurate answer rather than a cautious one.

There is no human safety data for the combination. For the ingredients separately:

- FDA's conclusion for BPC-157 is that there is insufficient clinical safety information to
  characterise its safety profile, and that non-clinical toxicology was too limited in scope and
  duration to inform safety. FDA's specific flagged concern is an immune response against the
  peptide, potentially made worse by peptide clumping and by impurities.
- For TB-500 there is no established human safety profile at all. The first registered human
  safety study only began recruiting in February 2026 and has posted no results.
- For GHK-Cu, safety by any route other than topical is uncharacterised, because every human
  study of it used the topical route.

A point people often get backwards: the fact that few problems have been reported is not
reassuring here. FDA found three adverse event reports for BPC-157 in total, all confounded by
other substances, and notes that compounders generally do not report adverse events so
underreporting is expected. Total documented human exposure across all the identified BPC-157
studies is on the order of dozens of people for days to about two weeks. That is far too little
to detect anything uncommon or delayed. Silence is not a safety record.

---

## 5. GHK-Cu is in skincare products everywhere. Doesn't that mean it is well established?

For skin, applied to skin, it is at least studied. For anything else, no.

Every human study of GHK-Cu that was located used the topical route. No human trial of this
compound given any other way was found in either the trial registry or the published literature.
Cosmetic ingredient status describes cosmetic topical use, and it is not a drug approval.

This matters here because this product is not a cosmetic applied to skin. Vendors routinely cite
the topical and cosmetic record in support of products given other ways, and that substitution
is the most common way a reader gets misled about this ingredient.

There is a second half to this answer that is less commonly heard. The topical evidence itself is
not strong. The two randomized, blinded, controlled trials that exist were both negative on
objective measures. In a 1992 trial in 86 evaluable patients with leg ulcers, a comparator
significantly reduced ulcer size while the copper complex showed no meaningful difference from
inert vehicle. In a 2006 trial in 13 people after laser skin resurfacing, there was no
significant improvement in redness, wrinkles or overall skin quality on blinded and
computer-based assessment.

So the borrowed support would be weak even if borrowing were legitimate.

---

## 6. People swear by it. How can that be true if the trials are negative?

One of the retrieved studies answers this unusually clearly.

In the 2006 laser resurfacing trial, patient satisfaction was significantly higher in the treated
group, while the objective assessments showed no improvement in redness, wrinkles or skin
quality. Same trial, same people, opposite directions.

That dissociation is consistent with a real subjective experience without a measurable
tissue-level change. It does not mean anyone is lying or imagining things. It means that how
something feels and what can be measured are two different questions, and that the first can be
influenced by expectation, attention, natural recovery and everything else happening at the same
time. It is exactly why controlled and blinded trials exist.

---

## 7. TB-500 has clinical trials though, doesn't it?

Not for TB-500 itself. This is a case of two different molecules sharing a conversation.

TB-500 is a synthetic seven-amino-acid fragment. Thymosin beta-4 is the full-length,
naturally occurring 43-amino-acid protein. The trials usually cited studied the full-length
protein, or an investigational product formulated from it, and delivered it as eye drops or
applied it to a wound:

- a Phase 2 eye drop trial in nine patients with severe dry eye
- a Phase 3 eye drop trial in a rare corneal condition, terminated early with 18 subjects, which
  did not reach significance at its primary timepoint
- a topical leg ulcer paper that is frequently cited as showing accelerated healing and in fact
  reports no outcome data at all

There is exactly one registered human study of the TB-500 fragment. It began recruiting in
February 2026, its main outcomes are counts of adverse events rather than any measure of
benefit, and it has published nothing. A 2026 review in a sports medicine journal states
directly that human data are lacking.

There is also a finding that complicates the story from the other end. In laboratory
wound-healing assays, the parent TB-500 compound showed no activity while one of its breakdown
products did. The authors suggested that previously reported activity may belong to the
breakdown product rather than to TB-500 itself. That is one laboratory result and should not be
treated as settled, but it means even the animal and cell story for this ingredient is unsettled.

---

## 8. BPC-157 has hundreds of studies. Are they all worthless?

Not worthless, but they are almost all rodent and laboratory studies, and that is a different
thing from human evidence.

FDA searched multiple databases and trial registries and identified five human studies of
BPC-157 in the entire published literature. Three exist only as conference abstracts that were
never published in full. One is a chart review of 17 people at a single private clinic. One is a
two-person series. The only study indexed in PubMed is an uncontrolled, unblinded pilot in 12
women whose main outcome was a self-rated questionnaire.

The only randomized controlled trial FDA could find, in ulcerative colitis, did not separate
from placebo. FDA's own words are that the data are inadequate to support efficacy and safety.

A literature search restricted to systematic reviews, randomized controlled trials and clinical
trials returns zero records.

FDA's reading of the animal literature is also sharper than most summaries suggest: molecular
targets have not been identified, mechanisms remain poorly understood, and a dose-response
relationship could not be established because widely separated exposures produced comparable
effects. An independent 2025 peer-reviewed review concluded the compound should be considered
investigational.

---

## 9. Won't three ingredients work better together than one?

There is no evidence for that, and no evidence against it, because the question has never been
studied for these three compounds.

Two things are worth saying about the assumption itself.

First, combination is not automatically additive. Compounds can interfere with one another,
alter how each other is absorbed or broken down, or produce effects that neither produces alone.
None of this has been examined here. No interaction, compatibility or pharmacokinetic study
between any pair of these three compounds was located.

Second, combining uncharacterised things multiplies uncertainty rather than dividing it. Each
ingredient arrives with its own unknown profile, and the mixture adds a further set of unknowns
about how they behave together.

Any claim of synergy for this product would be invented rather than reported.

---

## 10. Two of these occur naturally in the body. Doesn't that make them gentler?

Origin is not a safety property.

GHK-Cu does occur naturally in human plasma, and BPC-157 is described as a partial sequence of a
protein found in human gastric juice. Neither fact tells you what happens when a synthetic
version of unconfirmed purity is introduced by a different route, at a level the body did not
choose, alongside two other unstudied compounds.

This framing is persuasive because it sounds like a reason rather than a claim. It is a claim,
and nothing in the retrieved evidence supports it.

## 11. Is it legal?

It depends on the jurisdiction and the question, and part of the answer could not be determined.
Everything below was checked on 2026-07-19 and should be re-checked before relying on it.

In the United States, BPC-157 is not an approved drug, and FDA states there is no approved
product containing BPC-157-related bulk drug substances in any country. It is not on the
compounding bulks list, and FDA has cited compounding with it as not eligible for the relevant
exemptions in at least two enforcement letters, dated 2020 and 2023. FDA staff recommended
against adding it to that list in a memo dated 5/11/2026, placed before an advisory committee
meeting scheduled for 23 to 24 July 2026. As of the date on this page that meeting has not
occurred, so no outcome is stated. A committee recommendation is advice, not a final agency
decision.

No approved product was located for TB-500, and no approved drug product containing GHK-Cu was
located for any indication by any route.

The compounding status of TB-500 and GHK-Cu could not be verified. Every attempted FDA page
returned an error during research for both. Conflicting claims circulate online and all of them
come from commercial sources, so none is repeated here.

Nothing outside the United States was checked. Not checked never means permitted.

The combination itself has not been evaluated or classified by any regulator anywhere, because
no such product has been submitted for evaluation.

## 12. I compete in a tested sport. What do I need to know?

That this is a concrete risk rather than a theoretical one, and that you should check with your
own anti-doping authority rather than relying on any guide.

Checked 2026-07-19. USADA states that BPC-157 is prohibited under the unapproved substances
category. USADA's summary of changes to the 2018 prohibited list states that thymosin beta-4 and
its derivatives, giving TB-500 as an example, were added as prohibited growth factors. Validated
detection methods exist for TB-500 and its breakdown products, one of which persisted for up to
72 hours in rats.

GHK-Cu's status could not be determined. The prohibited list itself could not be read during
research, and every search result addressing this question was a retailer or affiliate page. No
status is stated here in either direction, and no status should be inferred from that silence.

## 14. If a supplier gives me a certificate of analysis, does that settle it?

It answers a different question from the one most people think it answers, and here it would not
answer that one either.

A certificate of analysis, at best, tells you what is in a vial. It does not create evidence
about what the contents do. Perfect documentation for this blend would establish its composition.
It would not create the human evidence that does not exist, and it certainly would not create
combination evidence.

Even on its own terms, certificates in this market prove less than a reader assumes. FDA examined
certificates published by commercial peptide sellers for one of these ingredients and found that
most contain purity testing only, with no information about impurity limits or testing to show
control of the impurity profile. A purity figure says how much of the material is the intended
substance. It does not say what the rest is.

A blend adds requirements that a single-ingredient product does not have: identity and content
confirmed for each component separately, uniformity of the mixture so that every unit is the
same, and stability of the components in one another's presence. None of that has been confirmed
here, and there is no confirmed composition to confirm it against.

## 15. Why is the evidence so thin if these have been around for years?

Several reasons, and they are worth separating.

Human trials are expensive, slow, and mostly funded by parties who expect to recover the cost
through an approved product. None of these compounds has an approval pathway under way that
would justify that expense for the parties currently selling them.

The existing market does not require evidence. These compounds sell without it, so there is
little commercial pressure to generate any.

Animal and laboratory work is much cheaper, and it accumulates. That is why the publication
count looks impressive while the human column stays empty.

And blends specifically are almost never studied. A combination study is harder to design and
interpret than a single-ingredient study, and no regulator is requiring one here.

The result is a large body of research that reads as substantial and answers almost none of the
questions a person actually has.

## 16. What would change this answer?

For the combination: a study that administered these three compounds together, with a control
group, in people, reporting both what happened and what went wrong. Nothing less would do,
because nothing less addresses the mixture.

For the ingredients: completed, controlled, adequately sized human trials with published
results. Several are registered. The Phase 2 hamstring trial for BPC-157 has an estimated
completion in 2028. The TB-500 safety study began in 2026. A properly controlled topical GHK-Cu
gel trial against a vehicle placebo is recruiting. None has reported.

A caution on registered trials: a Phase 1 study of BPC-157 registered in 2015 has no posted
results and no located publication a decade later. Registration is not a result, and some
registered trials simply never report.

For the product: a confirmed composition, per-component identity and content, demonstrated
uniformity, an impurity profile rather than a purity figure, and independent testing.

If any of this arrives, these documents get revised and the revision is recorded with a date.

## 17. What should I do with all this?

Take it to a qualified professional who knows your full history, and take the questions in the
main Guide with you. They are written to be used as they stand.

The honest summary is short. This combination has never been studied. The ingredients have much
less human evidence than their reputations suggest, and for one of them the human evidence is
about a different route entirely and is largely negative in any case. The product's composition
is not confirmed. Two ingredients carry a documented anti-doping risk, and the third could not be
checked.

None of that means these compounds do nothing. It means nobody has established that they do
anything, and nobody has established what happens when they are combined. Those are different
statements from a claim that they are useless, and they are also very different from the claims
being made in the market.
