## Prohibited claims

The standing baseline, all nine items, applies in full. See PRODUCT_CONTENT_SCHEMA.md section 7.

SKU specific additions. Each may not appear on any member-facing surface for this SKU.

1. Any statement that this item is an "exercise mimetic", replicates exercise, or delivers the
   benefits of training. Reason: the framing originates in preclinical work and marketing
   language, and presenting it to a member is a preclinical to human leap and an implied
   guaranteed outcome.
2. Any statement that this item causes fat loss, weight loss, or a change in body composition.
   Reason: unsupported fat-loss guarantee. The retrieved metabolic findings on administered
   MOTS-c come from mice, and the retrieved human work did not administer the peptide at all.
3. Any statement that this item improves, restores, or enhances insulin sensitivity, blood
   sugar, or glucose control in humans. Reason: the retrieved insulin sensitivity finding is
   from diet-induced obese male mice. Stating it without the species is a prohibited
   preclinical to human leap, and stating it as a human outcome is a prohibited disease claim.
4. Any statement that this item treats, prevents, or manages diabetes, prediabetes, insulin
   resistance, metabolic syndrome, or fatty liver disease. Reason: prohibited disease claim.
5. Any statement about muscle growth, strength, endurance, VO2, or athletic performance.
   Reason: unsupported muscle-growth and performance guarantee. The retrieved human athlete
   study was observational, measured naturally occurring blood levels rather than any
   administered product, and reported that professional athletes had lower MOTS-c than
   sedentary controls, which does not support a performance claim in any direction.
6. Any statement that this item reverses, slows, or stops aging, or extends lifespan or
   healthspan. Reason: unsupported longevity claim.
7. Any statement about energy, fatigue, or mitochondrial function presented as a member-facing
   benefit. Reason: mechanism framing used as an implied outcome claim. Mechanism may be
   described in the Guide as mechanism, graded, and never as a result a member should expect.
8. Any presentation of a finding about naturally occurring MOTS-c levels in the body as
   evidence about an administered product. Reason: an observational measurement of an
   endogenous molecule is not evidence for a supplied item, and conflating the two is the most
   likely way this record gets misused.
9. Any presentation of exercise-trial results as results for this item. Reason: the retrieved
   human randomized trial tested an exercise programme and measured MOTS-c as an outcome
   marker. The intervention was exercise, not this item.
10. Any statement that this item is safe, well tolerated, or free of side effects. Reason:
    unsupported safety claim. No retrieved human trial administered this item.
11. Any use of "research use only" framing to imply that a member would benefit. Reason:
    prohibited framing.
12. Any reference to a human clinical trial of an administered MOTS-c or MOTS-c analogue
    without a retrieved, verified primary source. Reason: several secondary sources describing
    such trials were encountered during source review and could not be verified against a
    primary record. They are logged as open question 9 and may not be cited.

## Product FAQ

**How much should I take, and how often?**

Dosing information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research content. That includes
amounts, frequency, timing, cycle length, and route. Questions about any of those belong with a
qualified professional who knows your history.

**Is this safe for me?**

Xenios Research does not make general safety statements and cannot assess an individual
situation. No retrieved human trial administered this item, so there is no human safety record
here to characterize even if Xenios Research made such characterizations. The Guide lists
categories of consideration discussed in the literature so you can raise them with a qualified
professional.

**What is the current legal or regulatory status?**

Not established to the standard Xenios Research requires, and logged as open supplier question
6 below. No regulatory statement is made in this record. Secondary reporting on the compounding
status of this peptide was encountered during source review, was contradictory across sources,
and could not be confirmed against a retrievable primary agency document, so it has not been
used.

**Has this been tested in people?**

Not as an administered product, in anything retrieved and verified for this record. Two human
studies were retrieved, and neither gave participants this item. The first was observational:
105 people (75 professional athletes and 30 sedentary controls) had blood levels of naturally
occurring MOTS-c measured, and athletes showed lower levels than controls. The second was a
randomized trial in which the intervention was a 16 week exercise programme in 49 breast cancer
survivors, with MOTS-c measured as a marker. The findings that involve giving MOTS-c to a body
come from diet-induced obese male mice.
Sources, VERIFIED 2026-07-19: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11273660/ and
https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8376922/ and
https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6640593/

**Why is this described as exercise related if the evidence is early?**

Because the compound was identified as a molecule the body produces, and its blood levels move
in relation to physical activity. That is a reason researchers find it interesting. It is not a
reason to expect that taking a supplied version reproduces exercise, and Xenios Research will
not say that it does.

**Why does the catalog show this if I cannot buy it?**

Because catalog state and commerce state are separate on purpose. Peptide commerce is not
authorized, so nothing here is for sale, and the presence of this record is not an offer.
