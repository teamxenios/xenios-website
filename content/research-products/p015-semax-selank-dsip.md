Three named components. Each is a separate substance with its own literature, its own regulatory
entry, and its own risk profile, and this record keeps them separate wherever possible:

- Semax. Described in the retrieved literature as a synthetic peptide analog of a fragment of
  adrenocorticotropic hormone, sequence Met-Glu-His-Phe-Pro-Gly-Pro (source 1).

- DSIP, delta sleep-inducing peptide. Described as a nonapeptide, sequence
  Trp-Ala-Gly-Gly-Asp-Ala-Ser-Gly-Glu, first described in 1974 from rabbit cerebral venous blood
  (source 4). It appears in the United States regulatory document cited below under the name
  Emideltide (source 5).

section, not here. One name per SKU.

## Product FAQ

**How much should I take, and how often?**

Dosing information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research content. That includes amounts,
frequency, timing, cycle length, and route. Questions about any of those belong with a qualified
professional who knows your history. On this item the exclusion runs deeper than usual, because
xenios does not know the proportion of the three components, so there is no specification here that
anyone could reason from even in principle.

**What is actually in this, and in what proportion?**

Three named synthetic peptides: Semax, Selank, and DSIP. The proportion is not confirmed. No written
composition specification has been received from a supplier, so Xenios Research does not state a
ratio and does not estimate one. That question is first on the open list, and until it is answered
this record describes an item whose exact composition is unknown.

**Has this combination been studied?**

No study of these three components together was located during this review. Each component has its
own separate literature. Adding those literatures together does not produce evidence for the
combination, and Xenios Research does not present it that way.

**What has each component been studied for?**

Semax has been studied in rodent models within cognition and brain research, and is described in a
peer-reviewed review as a registered nootropic drug in the Russian Federation. Selank has been
studied in male Wistar rats for effects on the expression of genes involved in a major inhibitory
neurotransmitter system, and is described in that same review as a registered anxiolytic drug in the
Russian Federation. DSIP was first described in 1974 from rabbit blood and has been studied within
sleep research, where the retrieved source describes the evidence on its involvement in sleep as
conflicting. Each of those is a statement about a component and a species, not about this product
and not about you.

**They are approved medicines in Russia. Does that mean they are approved here?**

No. A registration attaches to a specific medicine, made by a specific manufacturer, supplied under
one country's framework. It is not an authorization anywhere else, it is not evidence of effect, and
a catalog record is not that registered medicine or a route to it. The statement is recorded with
its source because leaving it out would be its own kind of dishonesty, not because it counts in
favour of the item.

**What is the current legal or regulatory status?**

In the United States, in a document dated 29 September 2023, Semax (heptapeptide), Selank Acetate
(TP-7), and Emideltide (DSIP) each appear in Category 2 of the Food and Drug Administration Section
503A bulk drug substances categories, a placement the document attributes to identified significant
safety risks. Source, VERIFIED 2026-07-19:
https://join.a4pc.org/hubfs/PDFs/503A-Categories-Update-for-September-2023-FINAL.docx.pdf On 15
April 2026 the agency announced removal of twelve peptide substances from that category and

with Semax and Emideltide (DSIP) named for the July meeting. Sources, VERIFIED 2026-07-19:

Removal from that category does not place a substance on the bulks list, does not put it in Category
