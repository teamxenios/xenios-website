## Product FAQ

**How much should I take, and how often?**

Dosing information is intentionally excluded from Xenios Research content. That includes amounts,
frequency, timing, cycle length, and route. Questions about any of those belong with a qualified
professional who knows your history. For this compound there is no human trial to reason from, so
there is no human dose response anywhere for anyone to point to.

**What is dihexa?**

It is a compound derived from angiotensin IV, developed in academic research and studied in animals
as a possible procognitive agent. It is sold online as a cognitive product. That marketing is not
evidence, and Xenios Research does not repeat it.

**What has it actually been studied for, and in what?**

In the work retrieved for this record it was studied in rats, including older rats and rats given a
drug that impairs learning, and in cultured rat neurons and hippocampal slice preparations. Those
are animal and laboratory findings. No human clinical trial of dihexa itself was located during this
review.

**I have read that the way it works is well established. Is that right?**

No, and this is the most important answer on this page. The paper most often cited for that
mechanism, published in 2014 in the Journal of Pharmacology and Experimental Therapeutics, was
retracted by the journal in 2025. Retraction notice, VERIFIED 2026-07-19:
https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40312093/ Separately, four papers published between 2011 and 2014
in that journal, sharing a co-author, received expressions of concern in September 2021 over
possible image manipulation. Report, VERIFIED 2026-07-19:
https://retractionwatch.com/2021/09/24/four-papers-by-athira-ceo-earn-expressions-of-concern/ A
retracted paper is not weak evidence. It is withdrawn evidence, and Xenios Research does not build a
claim on it.

**What is the current legal or regulatory status?**

In the United States, in a document dated 29 September 2023, Dihexa Acetate is listed among
substances placed in Category 2 of the Food and Drug Administration Section 503A bulk drug
substances categories, a placement the document attributes to identified significant safety risks.
Source, VERIFIED 2026-07-19:
https://join.a4pc.org/hubfs/PDFs/503A-Categories-Update-for-September-2023-FINAL.docx.pdf On 15
April 2026 the agency announced the removal of twelve peptide substances from Category 2 and
scheduled advisory committee meetings for 23 and 24 July 2026 and before the end of February 2027,
with dihexa named among the substances due for review in the later meeting. Sources, VERIFIED
2026-07-19: https://www.orrick.com/en/Insights/2026/04/FDA-Announces-Removal-of-12-Peptides-from-Category-2-and-Schedules-PCAC-Meetings
and https://www.thefdalawblog.com/2026/04/fdas-peptide-rally-what-compounders-and-industry-need-to-know-post-1-of-2/
Removal from that category does not place a substance on the bulks list, does not put it in
Category 1, and does not authorize anything. This framework concerns pharmacy compounding, not
retail, and none of it is an approval. The position is moving, so this answer carries its dates and
is re-checked at each review.

**Is this the same as the Alzheimer's drug I read about?**

No. Fosgonimeton, also known as ATH-1017 and NDX-1017, is a separate investigational medicine whose
development lineage is described as running from dihexa through the company that later became
Athira Pharma. It is a different compound, developed by a named sponsor, and it is not approved. Its
phase 2/3 LIFT-AD trial did not meet its primary endpoint, with results announced on 3 September
2024, and its status for Alzheimer's disease and related conditions is listed as discontinued.
Source, VERIFIED 2026-07-19: https://www.alzforum.org/therapeutics/fosgonimeton A catalog record is
not that medicine and is not a route to it. If you are asking because of memory concerns for
yourself or someone close to you, the route is a physician, not a catalog.

**Is this safe for me?**

Xenios Research does not make general safety statements and cannot assess an individual situation.
For this compound no human safety data was located, and a United States regulator has recorded
identified significant safety risks for a named form of the substance in the compounding context
described above. That is stated plainly rather than left out.
