## 2. Canonical name

Internal canonical name: Gonadorelin.

Member-facing display name is not yet fixed and must be set by the founder reviewer at publication.

Compound identity, as described in the retrieved literature:

- Gonadorelin is the synthetic form of gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH), described in the
  literature as a decapeptide produced in hypothalamic neurons that drives downstream sex hormone
  production through the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis (Casteel and Singh, StatPearls, 2023,
  VERIFIED 2026-07-19, https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32644418/).
- GnRH acts through the GnRH receptor on pituitary cells. The receptor has been identified as a
  therapeutic target, and both agonist and antagonist drugs have been developed against it (Tzoupis
  et al, Current Medicinal Chemistry, 2020, VERIFIED 2026-07-19,
  https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31309882/).

A distinction that must not be lost in any downstream copy. Gonadorelin is the native decapeptide
sequence. The GnRH agonist analogues named in the anti-doping literature (buserelin, deslorelin,
goserelin, histrelin, leuprorelin, nafarelin, triptorelin) are different molecules with different
pharmacology and different regulatory histories. Literature about those analogues is not literature
about this product, and may not be carried across. Which molecule the supplied material actually is
is NOT CONFIRMED - see open supplier questions.

Whether any FDA-approved human gonadorelin drug product is currently marketed in the United States
was NOT established from any primary source retrieved for this record. See the retrieval gaps
section. No statement about human approval status appears anywhere in this record.

## 18. Product FAQ

Five honest questions, answered without dosing and without promises.

**What is this compound, in plain terms?**
Gonadorelin is described in the literature as the synthetic form of GnRH, a decapeptide made in the
hypothalamus that sits at the top of the hormonal axis governing the gonads (Casteel and Singh,
StatPearls, 2023). Describing where a compound sits in a system is not a statement about what it
would do for any person.

**Is this the same thing as leuprolide, triptorelin, or goserelin?**
No. Those are GnRH agonist analogues, which are different molecules with different pharmacology and
different regulatory histories. Research about them does not describe this compound, and this record
does not carry their evidence across. We also have not confirmed which molecule the supplied material
actually is, which is an open supplier question.

**What has this actually been studied for?**
The retrieved human work sits in reproductive endocrinology. A 2023 review of pulsatile GnRH reported
outcomes in anovulatory women, including an overall ovulatory rate of 75 percent across 600 treatment
cycles in 292 patients, and reported normalization of androgen secretion and testicular volume in most
men with idiopathic hypogonadotropic hypogonadism (Filicori, F&S Reports, 2023). That is clinical
research in defined patient populations under a specific administration pattern supervised by
clinicians. Studied for is not the same as works for, and none of it is a statement about what would
happen to any individual.

**Can you tell me how to use it, or whether it works with testosterone therapy?**
No, on both counts. Dosing information is intentionally excluded from this record and from every
guide. We do not publish amounts, frequency, timing, cycle length, reconstitution, or administration
technique, and we do not describe how a compound would be combined with any other therapy. That is a
question for a licensed clinician who knows your history.

**Why can I see this product but not buy it?**
Catalog state and commerce state are separate on purpose. This record exists so the information is
organized and reviewable. Peptide commerce is not authorized, so commerce for this product is
disabled pending written approval.

**Is it safe?**
We cannot answer that, and we will not assert it. There is no verified quality documentation on file
for this product, no confirmed composition, no confirmed supply chain provenance, and no general
safety finding that would apply to an individual. Anyone considering a compound in this class should
talk to a licensed clinician.
