# Intimacy and Vitality for Women

This is a companion page. The shared foundation, which applies to everyone and carries the most
important material, is at `content/research-goals/intimacy-and-vitality`. Read that page as well.

## 1. What this goal actually means

Women arrive here describing several distinct situations that get collapsed into one word.

Desire that has faded, either gradually or after a specific life change. Arousal that no longer
follows desire, or physical response that has changed. Discomfort, dryness, or pain. Changes tied
to a particular period of life: hormonal contraception, pregnancy and the year after it,
breastfeeding, perimenopause, menopause, or a course of medication. And a general depletion, often
described as having nothing left at the end of the day, which is a capacity problem more than a
sexual one.

These have different causes and different next steps, and the difference matters more here than
almost anywhere else in the library, because one of them is a pain condition and pain conditions
are not lifestyle matters.

Two things are worth establishing at the start.

**This is common and it is under-discussed clinically.** Genitourinary syndrome of menopause, the
current term for a cluster including vaginal dryness and painful sex, affects approximately 27 to
84 percent of postmenopausal women and can significantly impair health, sexual function, and
quality of life. The North American Menopause Society position statement notes plainly that it is
likely underdiagnosed and undertreated (Menopause, 2020). Many women have never been asked about
this in a clinical appointment. That is a gap in care, not a sign that nothing can be done.

**Female sexual response is not a smaller version of male sexual response.** It is not primarily a
plumbing question, the useful outcome measures are different, and the assumption that it works the
same way is the reason a great deal of product development in this area has failed. Anything
marketed to women here as an equivalent of a men's product should be read with that in mind.

## 2. Lifestyle foundation

**Pain is a clinical finding. It is never something to push through, and it is never something to
manage with a product from us.** Pain during or after sex, pain with insertion, burning, bleeding,
new or changed discharge, pelvic pain, or urinary symptoms all warrant assessment by a clinician.
There is a long history of women being told that discomfort in this area is normal, expected, or
psychological, and being sent away. It is none of those things by default. Endometriosis,
vulvodynia, vaginismus, infection, pelvic floor dysfunction, and genitourinary syndrome of
menopause are recognised conditions with recognised pathways, and identifying which one you are
dealing with is the entire task. If you take one thing from this page, take this paragraph.

**Know that genitourinary syndrome of menopause is treatable and that established options exist.**
The 2020 North American Menopause Society position statement describes available therapies
including vaginal lubricants and moisturisers, vaginal estrogens and dehydroepiandrosterone,
systemic hormone therapy, and the estrogen agonist and antagonist ospemifene (Menopause, 2020).
Several of those are prescription treatments and all of the prescription options are decisions for
you and a clinician, with your own history taken into account. We name them here only so that you
know a real clinical conversation is available. Xenios does not supply, recommend, or advise on any
of them.

**Take perimenopause seriously as an explanation.** Changes in this area frequently begin years
before periods stop, alongside sleep disruption, mood changes, and altered energy, and they are
often attributed to stress or ageing instead. A clinician who takes menopause care seriously is
one of the highest value relationships available in this goal area.

**Review medications and contraception with your prescriber, and change nothing on your own.**
Antidepressants are the clearest documented example. In a 2025 literature review, sexual problems
were reported in 26 to 31.8 percent of subjects described as normal, 45 percent of untreated
depressed patients, and 62 to 63 percent of treated depressed patients (Tran et al., Cureus, 2025).
Note what those figures also show, which is that depression itself affects this area independently
of treatment. Hormonal contraception is another common contributor that is worth raising directly.
None of this means stopping anything. It means the conversation belongs with the person who
prescribed it, and many people never start it because they assume the cause is themselves.

**Treat exhaustion and mental load as a physiological input, not a character flaw.** Desire is
responsive to context. Sleep debt, caregiving load, an unequal division of domestic work, financial
stress, and unprocessed resentment are among the most common reasons this area goes quiet, and none
of them is addressed by a supplement. This is stated as a matter of respect rather than dismissal.
Naming a real cause is more useful than selling around it.
[UNVERIFIED - background knowledge on responsive desire, requires human source check]

**Sleep and general cardiovascular health still apply.** The shared foundation page covers both. The
American Academy of Sleep Medicine and the Sleep Research Society recommend that adults sleep 7 or
more hours per night on a regular basis, and general adult activity guidance is at least 150 to 300
minutes of moderate intensity aerobic activity per week plus muscle strengthening on at least 2
days per week (ODPHP, Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans, second edition). These are the
unglamorous inputs and they remain the ones with the strongest general support.

**Where the difficulty sits between two people, that is the appropriate place to work on it.** A
qualified sex therapist or couples therapist addresses things no physiological intervention
reaches. This is a professional referral, not a consolation prize, and for a substantial share of
members in this goal area it is the highest value step available.

**The postpartum year is its own category.** Recovery, sleep deprivation, breastfeeding, and pelvic
floor changes all sit in it simultaneously. A pelvic health physiotherapist is a recognised
specialty and is frequently under-used. Any plan we produce works around that period rather than
through it.

## 3. Fitness and nutrition considerations

These are the areas your Xenios 30 plan is built to address in this goal. The plan itself is
produced from your assessment, so the specific structure is set there rather than described here.

- Sleep duration, timing, and consistency, treated as a primary lever
- Realistic total load, because the most common finding here is a capacity problem
- Resistance training and cardiovascular conditioning for general health, including bone and
  metabolic health through the menopause transition
- Life stage explicitly accounted for: cycle, contraception, pregnancy, postpartum,
  breastfeeding, perimenopause, menopause
- Any history of pain, pelvic floor dysfunction, surgery, or birth injury, adapted for rather than
  trained through, with referral where appropriate
- Adequate energy intake, since under-eating is a common and under-recognised contributor to low
  energy, disrupted cycles, and low interest
- A general cardiovascular and metabolic eating pattern rather than a goal-specific diet, because
  no goal-specific diet is established here
- Mental load, caregiving, and workload treated as scheduling inputs rather than obstacles to
  overcome
- A referral prompt to review current medications and contraception with your prescriber
- Clear criteria for what belongs with a clinician rather than with a plan

One regulatory fact is recorded here precisely, because this is the page where it is most likely to
be misread as an endorsement.

In the United States, VYLEESI (bremelanotide injection) carries an initial US approval year of 2019
and is indicated for the treatment of premenopausal women with acquired, generalized hypoactive
sexual desire disorder, as characterized by low sexual desire that causes marked distress or
interpersonal difficulty and is not due to a co-existing medical or psychiatric condition, problems
with the relationship, or the effects of a medication or drug substance. The label's stated
limitations of use are that it is not indicated for the treatment of hypoactive sexual desire
disorder in postmenopausal women or in men, and that it is not indicated to enhance sexual
performance. Source: US Food and Drug Administration prescribing information as published on
DailyMed, label revised March 2024, retrieved 2026-07-19 from
https://dailymed.nlm.nih.gov/dailymed/drugInfo.cfm?setid=f1d0c1b5-2f39-4bad-a6a4-0066e3ad5dcf

Four things follow from that, and they are the reason it appears here at all.

First, hypoactive sexual desire disorder is a specific diagnosis made by a clinician, and the
indication excludes low desire attributable to a medical or psychiatric condition, to the
relationship, or to a medication. Those exclusions describe a large share of the situations
described in section 2.

Second, the approval covers premenopausal women only, and explicitly not postmenopausal women.

Third, it is a prescription medicine obtained through a clinician and a pharmacy. That approval
says nothing about material sold outside that channel, and it is not a justification for obtaining
anything.

Fourth, the label states it is not indicated to enhance sexual performance. We have quoted the
indication and the limitations together so that neither can be read alone.

**Dosing information is intentionally excluded** from this page and from every Guide.

Compound-specific regulatory status is jurisdiction-dependent, dated, and changes. Beyond the
approval recorded above, we could not verify current status for compounds in this group from a
primary regulatory source in this session, so no further regulatory claim is made on this page.
Each Guide records status with a date, a jurisdiction, and a source URL.

## 6. What Xenios will not promise

- **We do not promise increased libido or desire.** Desire responds to health, sleep, medication,
  life stage, workload, and relationship, and no plan or product we offer is presented as capable of
  producing it.
- **We do not promise hormone balancing.** The phrase has no clinical meaning. Hormonal conditions
  are diagnosed and managed by clinicians, and a supplement category is not a substitute for that
  assessment.
- **We do not promise relief from menopause symptoms.** Not hot flushes, not night sweats, not
  vaginal dryness, not painful sex. Established treatment pathways exist for these and they belong
  with a clinician who knows your history.
- **We do not promise anything about pain.** We do not treat pain, we do not manage pain, and we do
  not offer anything for pain during sex. Pain is a reason to be assessed, and we would rather lose
  the sale than have you spend another year on something a clinician could identify.
- **We do not promise a natural or non-prescription equivalent of any approved medicine.** If a
  supplement genuinely produced a drug's effect, it would be a drug.
- **We do not promise to restore your sex drive, your youth, or your energy.** That verb belongs to
  marketing rather than to evidence, and it implies a certainty that does not exist here.
- **We do not promise that low desire is a deficiency to be corrected.** For many people it is a
  reasonable response to exhaustion, an unequal load, a medication, a health condition, or a
  relationship, and framing it as a personal deficiency is both inaccurate and unkind.
- **We do not promise that anything on this page is appropriate during pregnancy or
  breastfeeding.** Those are clinical contexts and require your own clinician's judgement.
- **We do not diagnose or treat sexual dysfunction, hormonal conditions, endometriosis, pelvic
  pain, menopause, or infertility.** The assessment is not a diagnosis and is not a prescription.
  If your history or your symptoms raise a clinical question, the correct next step is a licensed
  clinician, and we will say so plainly.

## 8. Take the assessment

The assessment is what makes any of this personal. Your life stage, your medical history, your
medications and contraception, your sleep, whether pain is present, and what your week actually
demands of you change the right answer completely, and none of that is visible from a page.

It takes about 10 minutes. It is required within 3 days of activation. It is not a diagnosis, it is
not a prescription, and it does not replace your clinician.

If pain is part of your picture, the assessment will tell you to see a clinician, and it will not
offer you anything else in its place. That is the correct answer and we are not going to soften it.
