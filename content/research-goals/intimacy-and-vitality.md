# Intimacy and Vitality

This page carries the foundation that applies to everyone. Two companion pages carry the framing
that differs by physiology:

- `content/research-goals/intimacy-and-vitality-men`
- `content/research-goals/intimacy-and-vitality-women`

If your situation does not map cleanly onto either page, read both, and raise it in the assessment.
The split exists because the common causes and the clinical pathways differ, not because we assume
anything about you.

## 1. What this goal actually means

This goal covers desire, arousal, physical function, comfort, energy, and the sense of being
present with someone. Members arrive at it from very different places. Some describe a specific
physical change. Some describe interest that has quietly gone flat. Some describe discomfort or
pain. Some describe a general loss of vitality that they have attached to this area because it is
where they noticed it first.

Three things are true and are worth stating before anything else.

**This is common.** In the Massachusetts Male Aging Study, a community based random sample survey
of men aged 40 to 70 conducted near Boston between 1987 and 1989, the combined prevalence of
minimal, moderate, and complete impotence was 52 percent (Feldman et al., Journal of Urology,
1994). Genitourinary syndrome of menopause, the current term for a cluster of symptoms including
vaginal dryness and painful sex, affects approximately 27 to 84 percent of postmenopausal women
and is likely underdiagnosed and undertreated (The North American Menopause Society position
statement, Menopause, 2020). Whatever you are dealing with, you are not unusual, and the silence
around it is not evidence that it is rare.

**This is frequently medical.** Changes in this area are often the first visible sign of something
systemic. Vascular health, blood pressure, blood sugar, thyroid function, hormonal status, pelvic
health, and prescribed medications all sit upstream of it. That is the single most important fact
on this page, and section 2 develops it.

**This is also relational and psychological, and that is not a lesser explanation.** Desire is
contextual. It responds to sleep, stress, safety, resentment, grief, workload, and what is
happening between two people. Treating it as purely mechanical is a mistake in both directions,
because it leads people to buy something for a problem that a conversation would address, and to
have a conversation about a problem that a clinician would identify.

We have written this page with restraint on purpose. This subject is surrounded by an industry
that trades on embarrassment, urgency, and implied performance. We are not going to do that.

## 2. Lifestyle foundation

**Start from the possibility that this is a medical sign, not a preference.** Erectile dysfunction
in particular is discussed in the cardiovascular literature as an early warning marker. A 2023
editorial in JACC: Advances states that erectile dysfunction often precedes cardiovascular events
and can be used as an early marker to identify men at high risk of major cardiovascular disease
(Cortese, Costantino, and Luzi, 2023). The reason is mechanical in the most literal sense. The
vessels involved are small, so they show impairment earlier than larger vessels do. This is the
clearest example of why the first step in this goal area is a clinician rather than a product, and
why buying something that masks a symptom can cost you the warning.

For women, the equivalent point is that pain, bleeding, changes in discharge, urinary symptoms, or
a sudden change in function are clinical findings. Genitourinary syndrome of menopause is
recognised, describable, and has established treatment pathways, and it is likely underdiagnosed
and undertreated (NAMS, Menopause, 2020). Discomfort during sex is not something to tolerate
quietly or to push through.

**Review your medications with the person who prescribed them, and do not stop anything on your
own.** Sexual side effects are common with several widely prescribed drug classes. In a 2025
literature review of antidepressant-induced sexual dysfunction, sexual problems were reported in
26 to 31.8 percent of subjects described as normal, 45 percent of untreated depressed patients,
and 62 to 63 percent of treated depressed patients (Tran et al., Cureus, 2025). Blood pressure
medications, hormonal contraception, and several other classes are also relevant. None of this
means any of those medicines are wrong for you. It means the conversation belongs with your
prescriber, who can consider alternatives, and it is a conversation many people never start because
they assume the cause is themselves.

**Sleep is upstream of this, measurably.** In a laboratory study of 10 healthy young men with a
mean age of 24.3 years, one week of sleep restriction was associated with a 10 to 15 percent
decrease in daytime testosterone levels compared with the rested condition (Leproult and Van
Cauter, JAMA, 2011). That is a very small study in a narrow population and should not be read as a
general result. It is included because it points at something people consistently underrate. Beyond
hormones, exhaustion affects desire, attention, patience, and interest in being touched, and no
product competes with a chronic sleep deficit.

**What is good for your arteries tends to be good here.** In a randomised controlled trial of 110
obese men aged 35 to 55 in Italy, a lifestyle intervention aimed at weight reduction through
reduced caloric intake and increased physical activity was associated with an improvement in mean
International Index of Erectile Function score from 13.9 to 17, while the control group remained
essentially unchanged at 13.5 to 13.6. The authors concluded that lifestyle changes were associated
with improvement in sexual function in about one third of the obese men with erectile dysfunction
at baseline (Esposito et al., JAMA, 2004). About one third is a real and useful figure, and it is
also not everyone, which is why the clinician step stays first.

**Alcohol and smoking both sit directly in the path of this goal.** Smoking damages exactly the
vascular function this area depends on. Alcohol degrades sleep and, in the moment, function.
Neither of these is a moralising point. They are simply the two behaviours with the most direct
line to the outcome.

**Treat stress, workload, and relationship context as part of the clinical picture, not as an
excuse.** A period of intense work, a new child, bereavement, financial strain, or untreated
anxiety or depression will affect this area, and the correct response is to address the cause
rather than to add something on top of it. Where the difficulty sits between two people, a
qualified therapist or a specialist in sexual health is a far better use of your money than
anything sold for this purpose.

**Be patient with timelines and impatient about new symptoms.** Gradual change over years and
sudden change over weeks are different situations. Sudden onset, pain, bleeding, numbness, a
noticeable curvature or lump, or a change accompanied by other systemic symptoms should be seen by
a clinician promptly rather than managed with a plan or a product.

## 3. Fitness and nutrition considerations

These are the areas your Xenios 30 plan is built to address in this goal. The plan itself is
produced from your assessment, so the specific structure is set there rather than described here.

- Cardiovascular conditioning, since this is substantially a vascular outcome
- Resistance training as a general health and body composition input
- Body composition where it is clinically relevant, addressed without aesthetic framing
- Sleep duration, timing, and consistency, treated as a primary lever
- Alcohol intake as an explicit line item
- Stress load, workload, and recovery capacity as scheduling inputs
- Pelvic health and any history of pain, surgery, childbirth, or injury, adapted for rather than
  trained through
- A prompt to review current medications with your prescriber, which is a referral rather than
  advice
- A general eating pattern that supports cardiovascular and metabolic health rather than a
  goal-specific diet, because no goal-specific diet is established here
- Clear criteria for what belongs with a clinician rather than with a plan

One regulatory fact is worth recording precisely, because it is routinely misused.

In the United States, VYLEESI (bremelanotide injection) carries an initial US approval year of
2019 and is indicated for the treatment of premenopausal women with acquired, generalized
hypoactive sexual desire disorder, as characterized by low sexual desire that causes marked
distress or interpersonal difficulty and is not due to a co-existing medical or psychiatric
condition, problems with the relationship, or the effects of a medication or drug substance. The
label's stated limitations of use are that it is not indicated for the treatment of hypoactive
sexual desire disorder in postmenopausal women or in men, and that it is not indicated to enhance
sexual performance. Source: US Food and Drug Administration prescribing information as published
on DailyMed, label revised March 2024, retrieved 2026-07-19 from
https://dailymed.nlm.nih.gov/dailymed/drugInfo.cfm?setid=f1d0c1b5-2f39-4bad-a6a4-0066e3ad5dcf

Read that carefully, because three things follow from it and all three are commonly ignored.

First, the approval is narrow. It covers one specific diagnosed condition in one specific
population, and the label states explicitly that it is not indicated to enhance sexual performance.

Second, an approved prescription medicine is a regulated product obtained through a clinician and
a pharmacy. The existence of that approval says nothing whatsoever about material sold outside that
channel, and it is not a justification for obtaining anything.

Third, this is precisely the kind of fact that marketing in this category takes out of context. We
have quoted the indication and the limitations of use together for that reason.

**Dosing information is intentionally excluded** from this page and from every Guide.

Compound-specific regulatory status is jurisdiction-dependent, dated, and changes. Beyond the
approval recorded above, we could not verify current status for compounds in this group from a
primary regulatory source in this session, so no further regulatory claim is made on this page.
Each Guide records status with a date, a jurisdiction, and a source URL.

## 6. What Xenios will not promise

- **We do not promise improved sexual performance.** Not duration, not frequency, not intensity,
  not stamina, not anything a competitor would put in a headline. This is the single most oversold
  claim in this category and we do not make it in any form.
- **We do not promise increased desire.** Desire is contextual, it responds to sleep, stress,
  health, medication, and relationship, and no plan or product we offer is presented as capable of
  producing it.
- **We do not promise to restore anything.** Not youthful function, not hormone levels, not
  vitality, not what things used to be like. That verb belongs to marketing, not to evidence.
- **We do not promise any physical change to your body's dimensions or appearance.** No product in
  this area has credible evidence for that, and it is a claim used specifically to exploit
  insecurity.
- **We do not promise that this is not medical.** We promise the opposite. Difficulty in this area
  can be an early sign of cardiovascular or other systemic disease, and our first response to it is
  to point you toward a clinician, even though that is not the commercially useful response.
- **We do not promise to fix a relationship.** Where the difficulty sits between two people, a
  qualified therapist is the appropriate professional, and no amount of physiology addresses it.
- **We do not promise a natural alternative to a prescription medicine.** If a supplement genuinely
  produced a drug's effect, it would be a drug, and in this category products making that
  implication have been a recurring source of adulteration findings.
- **We do not promise hormonal optimization.** Hormone therapy is medical care that requires
  diagnosis, monitoring, and informed consent about tradeoffs, including effects on fertility. It
  is not a lifestyle upgrade and we do not present it as one.
- **We do not diagnose or treat sexual dysfunction, hormonal conditions, pelvic pain, infertility,
  or any related condition.** The assessment is not a diagnosis and is not a prescription. If your
  history or your symptoms raise a clinical question, the correct next step is a licensed
  clinician, and we will say so plainly.
