# Recover Faster

## 1. What this goal actually means

"Recover faster" usually covers three different situations that people describe with the same
words.

The first is day-to-day recovery between training sessions. You want to arrive at Thursday able
to train well rather than dragging Tuesday behind you. The second is soreness, which is
uncomfortable but is a poor measure of anything. Soreness tracks novelty more than damage, which
is why a new movement leaves you sore and a hard session you are adapted to often does not. The
third is injury and tissue healing, which is a different category entirely and is a clinical
matter rather than a programming one.

These are worth separating because they respond to different things and because they carry very
different stakes. Being tired on Thursday is a scheduling problem. A joint that has hurt for six
weeks is not, and it is not something a plan or a product should be asked to solve.

There is also a framing worth stating directly. Recovery is not primarily something you add. It
is mostly something you stop interfering with.

## 2. Lifestyle foundation

**Sleep is the intervention, and nothing else on this page is close.** The American Academy of
Sleep Medicine and the Sleep Research Society recommend that adults sleep 7 or more hours per
night on a regular basis. In a study of eleven male collegiate basketball players at Stanford who
extended their nightly sleep across five to seven weeks, sprint times, free throw accuracy, and
three-point accuracy improved, and measured reaction time, daytime sleepiness, and fatigue scores
all improved as well (Mah et al., SLEEP, 2011). That is a small study in a narrow population and
it should not be read as a general result for everyone. It is included because it points in the
same direction as the rest of the sleep literature and because no product in this category has
evidence of that character behind it.

If you are sleeping poorly, everything else in this section is a rounding error.

**Recovery is a function of the load you put on it.** Most recovery problems are training design
problems wearing a different name. If the weekly load exceeds what your sleep, nutrition, and
life circumstances can absorb, no modality fixes it. The answer is to adjust the load, which
people resist because it feels like going backwards. It is not. It is the only version of this
that works.

**Eat enough, and eat consistently.** Under-eating slows recovery in a way that is easy to miss
because it presents as low motivation and poor session quality rather than as hunger. Adequate
total energy and adequate protein are the material conditions for repair.

**Move on your off days.** Complete rest is often less useful than easy movement. General
activity guidance for adults is at least 150 to 300 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic
activity each week plus muscle-strengthening on at least 2 days (Physical Activity Guidelines for
Americans, 2nd edition, ODPHP). Some of that aerobic work can be genuinely easy, and easy work is
frequently the thing that makes the hard work repeatable.

**Manage the stress you can manage, and account for the stress you cannot.** Your body does not
maintain separate budgets for training stress and life stress. A brutal work quarter, a new baby,
or a period of poor mental health all draw from the same account. The correct response is usually
to reduce planned training load during those periods rather than to add something to compensate.

**Alcohol degrades sleep architecture,** which puts it directly in the path of the most important
item on this list.

**Be patient with the timeline, and be impatient about persistent pain.** Normal training fatigue
resolves in days. Normal soreness resolves within about a week. Pain that persists, pain that
wakes you, pain with swelling or loss of function, or any injury that is not steadily improving is
a reason to see a clinician, not a reason to look for a better recovery protocol. We would rather
lose the sale than have you manage a real injury with a supplement.

## 3. Fitness and nutrition considerations

These are the areas your Xenios 30 plan is built to address in this goal. The plan itself is
produced from your assessment, so the specific structure is set there rather than described here.

- An honest audit of current training load against current recovery capacity
- Sleep quantity, timing, and consistency, treated as the primary lever
- Session distribution across the week rather than clustering hard work together
- Genuinely easy days that are actually easy, which is harder than it sounds
- Planned lighter periods built into the schedule in advance
- Total energy and protein intake sufficient to support repair
- Hydration and electrolytes where training volume or heat make them relevant
- Existing injuries and restrictions, with movements adapted rather than pushed through
- Life stress and work load as scheduling inputs, not as things to be overcome
- Clear criteria for when a symptom stops being a training question and becomes a clinical one

## 6. What Xenios will not promise

- **We do not promise faster healing of an injury.** Nothing on this page is presented as capable
  of accelerating tissue healing in a human, and an injury belongs with a clinician rather than
  with a plan or a product.
- **We do not promise pain relief.** Not for joint pain, not for tendon pain, not for back pain.
  We do not treat pain, and persistent pain is a reason to be assessed rather than a reason to buy
  something.
- **We do not promise the elimination of soreness.** Soreness is a normal response to unfamiliar
  work. It is also a poor indicator of training quality, and chasing its absence tends to make
  programs worse rather than better.
- **We do not promise you can train harder with adequate recovery support.** That framing inverts
  the actual relationship. Recovery capacity sets the ceiling on training load. It is not a
  resource you purchase in order to raise the ceiling.
- **We do not promise that any peptide repairs tissue in humans.** Regardless of what the animal
  literature suggests, and regardless of how confidently it is stated elsewhere, that claim is not
  one we will make.
- **We do not promise recovery metrics from wearables mean what they appear to mean.** Readiness
  scores are proprietary, variably validated, and easy to over-read. They are one input, not a
  verdict on your body.
- **We do not promise you can recover your way out of doing too much.** If the load exceeds what
  your life can absorb, the load is the problem, and we will tell you that rather than sell you
  around it.
- **We do not diagnose or treat injuries, tendinopathy, autoimmune conditions, chronic
  inflammatory conditions, or chronic fatigue.** The assessment is not a diagnosis. If your
  history or your symptoms raise a clinical question, the correct next step is a licensed
  clinician, and we will say so plainly.
