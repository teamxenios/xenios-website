# Intimacy and Vitality for Men

This is a companion page. The shared foundation, which applies to everyone and carries the most
important material, is at `content/research-goals/intimacy-and-vitality`. Read that page as well.

## 1. What this goal actually means

Men typically arrive here describing one of four things: erections that are less reliable than they
were, interest that has flattened, energy and drive that have dropped in a way that feels different
from ordinary tiredness, or a general suspicion that their testosterone is low because that is the
explanation the internet offers for all of the above.

Those four descriptions have different causes and different next steps, and they overlap less than
the marketing in this category suggests.

It is worth removing the shame from this at the start, because shame is what the industry runs on.
In the Massachusetts Male Aging Study, a community based random sample survey of men aged 40 to 70
conducted near Boston between 1987 and 1989, the combined prevalence of minimal, moderate, and
complete impotence was 52 percent (Feldman et al., Journal of Urology, 1994). This is one of the
most common experiences in adult male life, and the fact that men do not discuss it is a social
fact, not a medical one.

There is one framing this page insists on. Erectile function is largely a vascular measurement. It
is not a measure of masculinity, effort, attraction, or character, and treating it as one is both
inaccurate and the reason many men delay seeing a doctor for years.

## 2. Lifestyle foundation

**Treat a change in erectile function as a reason to have your cardiovascular health assessed.**
This is the most valuable sentence on this page. A 2023 editorial in JACC: Advances states that
erectile dysfunction often precedes cardiovascular events and can be used as an early marker to
identify men at high risk of major cardiovascular disease, and it cites an umbrella review
reporting increased risk in men with endothelial dysfunction across coronary heart disease,
cardiovascular mortality, myocardial infarction, and stroke (Cortese, Costantino, and Luzi, 2023).
The vessels involved are small, which is why impairment can appear here before it appears anywhere
you would notice.

The practical consequence is uncomfortable and we are going to state it directly. A product that
improves the symptom without an assessment can remove the only prompt you were going to get. Book
the appointment first. Blood pressure, blood glucose or HbA1c, lipids, and a medication review are
the ordinary starting points, and they belong with a clinician, not with us.

**Do not self-diagnose low testosterone, and be sceptical of anyone who diagnoses it quickly.** The
American Urological Association guideline on evaluation and management of testosterone deficiency
(published 2018, reviewed and validity confirmed 2024, United States) requires at least two serum
total testosterone measurements taken in an early morning fashion on separate occasions, sets a
defined total testosterone threshold as a reasonable cut-off in support of the diagnosis, and
states that the clinical diagnosis is made only when low total testosterone levels are combined
with symptoms or signs. The specific threshold figure is a laboratory reference value and is
deliberately not reproduced here, because it is not something to self-interpret. Laboratory numbers
alone are not a diagnosis, and a single afternoon draw is not an adequate basis for one.

That standard exists because testosterone varies substantially through the day and between draws.
Any service that offers a diagnosis from one convenient sample is operating below the published
standard of care in this area.

**Understand the fertility tradeoff before, not after.** Exogenous testosterone suppresses the
hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis and reduces sperm production. One review reports that
testosterone can suppress spermatogenesis and lead to azoospermia in 65 percent of normospermic men
within four months of use, and notes that both the Endocrine Society and the American Urological
Association recommend against prescribing testosterone to men who desire fertility within the next
6 to 12 months (Patel, Leong, Ramos, and Ramasamy, World Journal of Men's Health, 2018). This is
not a rare complication or a footnote. It is a predictable pharmacological effect, and it is
frequently underplayed when testosterone is marketed as an optimisation product rather than as
medical treatment for a diagnosed condition.

**Protect your sleep, because it is upstream of the hormone you are asking about.** In a laboratory
study of 10 healthy young men with a mean age of 24.3 years, one week of sleep restriction was
associated with a 10 to 15 percent decrease in daytime testosterone levels compared with the rested
condition (Leproult and Van Cauter, JAMA, 2011). That is a very small study in a narrow population
and it should not be read as a general result for all men. It is here because men frequently
consider hormone therapy before they have ever tried sleeping properly for a month, and because
untreated obstructive sleep apnoea is a common and specifically treatable contributor in this area.

**Body composition and general fitness are the lever with actual trial evidence behind them.** In a
randomised controlled trial of 110 obese men aged 35 to 55 in Italy, a lifestyle intervention aimed
at weight reduction through reduced caloric intake and increased physical activity was associated
with a mean International Index of Erectile Function score improvement from 13.9 to 17, while the
control group was essentially unchanged at 13.5 to 13.6. The authors concluded that lifestyle
changes were associated with improvement in sexual function in about one third of the obese men who
had erectile dysfunction at baseline (Esposito et al., JAMA, 2004). One third is an honest number.
It is meaningfully better than most of what is sold here, and it is not everyone.

**Review your prescriptions with your prescriber, and change nothing on your own.** Sexual side
effects are well documented across several common drug classes, including antidepressants. In a
2025 literature review, sexual problems were reported in 26 to 31.8 percent of subjects described as
normal, 45 percent of untreated depressed patients, and 62 to 63 percent of treated depressed
patients (Tran et al., Cureus, 2025). Stopping an antidepressant or a blood pressure medicine
without medical supervision carries its own serious risks.

**Alcohol, smoking, and chronic stress all act directly against this goal.** Smoking damages the
vascular function this depends on. Alcohol degrades sleep and function. Sustained stress and
untreated anxiety or depression affect desire and performance, and performance anxiety in
particular becomes self-sustaining once it starts.

**See someone promptly for anything sudden, painful, or structural.** Sudden loss of function, pain
with erection, a new curvature or palpable lump, testicular pain or swelling, or symptoms alongside
chest pain, breathlessness, or leg swelling are reasons to seek medical assessment rather than to
adjust a plan.

## 3. Fitness and nutrition considerations

These are the areas your Xenios 30 plan is built to address in this goal. The plan itself is
produced from your assessment, so the specific structure is set there rather than described here.

- Cardiovascular conditioning as a primary component, given the vascular nature of the outcome
- Resistance training for general health and body composition
- Body composition where clinically relevant, addressed as a health variable rather than an
  aesthetic one
- Sleep duration, consistency, and any prompt to screen for sleep-disordered breathing with a
  clinician
- Alcohol intake as an explicit line item
- Training load kept within recovery capacity, since chronic under-recovery works against this goal
- Stress, workload, and performance anxiety acknowledged as real inputs rather than dismissed
- A general cardiovascular and metabolic eating pattern rather than a goal-specific diet, because
  no goal-specific diet is established here
- A referral prompt to review current medications with your prescriber
- Clear criteria for what belongs with a clinician rather than with a plan

One regulatory fact belongs on this page specifically, because it is misused in marketing aimed at
men.

In the United States, VYLEESI (bremelanotide injection) carries an initial US approval year of 2019
and is indicated for the treatment of premenopausal women with acquired, generalized hypoactive
sexual desire disorder. The label states, as limitations of use, that it is **not** indicated for
the treatment of hypoactive sexual desire disorder in postmenopausal women **or in men**, and that
it is **not** indicated to enhance sexual performance. Source: US Food and Drug Administration
prescribing information as published on DailyMed, label revised March 2024, retrieved 2026-07-19
from https://dailymed.nlm.nih.gov/dailymed/drugInfo.cfm?setid=f1d0c1b5-2f39-4bad-a6a4-0066e3ad5dcf

That approval is therefore not a men's indication and not a performance indication, and any
material presenting it as either is misrepresenting the label.

**Dosing information is intentionally excluded** from this page and from every Guide.

Compound-specific regulatory status is jurisdiction-dependent, dated, and changes. Beyond the
approval recorded above, we could not verify current status for compounds in this group from a
primary regulatory source in this session, so no further regulatory claim is made on this page.
Each Guide records status with a date, a jurisdiction, and a source URL.

## 6. What Xenios will not promise

- **We do not promise better erections.** Not harder, not more reliable, not more frequent. This is
  the central promise of this entire market and we do not make it.
- **We do not promise higher testosterone.** No supplement category on this page is presented as
  raising testosterone, and we do not frame hormone therapy as an upgrade.
- **We do not promise more stamina, longer duration, or improved sexual performance.** The
  performance frame is where this category does the most damage, and we decline it entirely.
- **We do not promise any change in size or appearance.** No credible evidence supports such a
  claim, and it exists in this market only to exploit insecurity.
- **We do not promise a return to how things were at 25.** Some change with age is ordinary. Some
  is a treatable medical finding. Distinguishing the two is a clinician's job, and neither answer is
  a product.
- **We do not promise more drive, dominance, alpha status, or any other trait this industry sells
  as a hormone level.** Testosterone is a hormone with a clinical range, not a personality.
- **We do not promise that treating the symptom addresses the cause.** In this goal area more than
  any other, the symptom can be the warning, and masking it can cost you the warning.
- **We do not promise that hormone therapy is without tradeoffs.** Exogenous testosterone
  suppresses sperm production, and both the AUA and the Endocrine Society advise against it for men
  who want fertility in the next 6 to 12 months. Anyone selling it without raising that with you
  first is not giving you the full picture.
- **We do not diagnose or treat erectile dysfunction, testosterone deficiency, infertility,
  cardiovascular disease, or any related condition.** The assessment is not a diagnosis and is not
  a prescription. If your history or your symptoms raise a clinical question, the correct next step
  is a licensed clinician, and we will say so plainly.

## 8. Take the assessment

The assessment is what makes any of this personal. Your age, your cardiovascular and metabolic
history, your medications, your sleep, whether the change was gradual or sudden, and whether
fertility matters to you now or later all change the right answer completely, and none of that is
visible from a page.

It takes about 10 minutes. It is required within 3 days of activation. It is not a diagnosis, it is
not a prescription, and it does not replace your clinician.

For a meaningful number of men in this goal area, the most useful output of the assessment is a
clear recommendation to see a doctor before doing anything else. We would rather give you that than
sell you a plan.
