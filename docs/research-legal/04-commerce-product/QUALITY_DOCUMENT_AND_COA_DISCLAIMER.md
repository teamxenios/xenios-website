## 1. Purpose

Xenios may display quality documents for products, including certificates of analysis. This
notice explains what those documents are, what they show, and what they do not prove, so that
no member reads more into them than they actually say.

## 2. What a COA is

A certificate of analysis (COA) is a document reporting test results for a product or a
specific lot of a product. It typically covers things like identity (the material is what it
is labeled as), purity, and potency, based on the tests named in the document, performed on
the sample tested, at the time of testing.

## 3. Who generates these documents

COAs and other quality documents shown by Xenios are supplier-generated: they come from the
manufacturer, the supplier, or a laboratory engaged by one of them. Unless a document
expressly states that Xenios commissioned independent testing, Xenios did not perform or
commission the testing, and no independent certification by Xenios or anyone else is implied.
If Xenios ever commissions independent testing, the resulting document will be labeled as
independent and will name the laboratory.

## 4. What a quality document shows

- the results of the specific tests listed, and nothing beyond them,
- for the specific sample or lot tested,
- at the time of testing,
- using the methods stated in the document.

Where a COA is lot-specific, it applies to that lot. Xenios's quality process is designed to
track lots and expiry so that a displayed COA can be matched to the lot shipped, but you
should check the lot number on your product against the document. If they do not match,
contact Xenios before use.

## 5. What a quality document does not prove

1. It is not FDA approval, and it does not make any product FDA approved, clinically proven,
   or safe for everyone.
2. It is not a safety or effectiveness conclusion for any use. A purity result is not a
   statement about what a product does or whether it is appropriate for you.
3. It is not a guarantee of every unit. Testing is sample-based; a COA reports on what was
   tested.
4. It is not a statement about the product's condition after testing. Storage, shipping
   conditions, time, and handling after the test are outside the document's scope. Stability
   and expiry are separate matters covered by the product's dating and storage information.
5. It does not resolve a product's classification or permitted marketing lane. For research
   products, classification remains under formal review regardless of what a COA shows.
6. It is not medical advice, and it does not replace your own professional's judgment.

## 6. How Xenios uses quality documents internally

Xenios's quality process is designed to include approved-supplier qualification, written
specifications, receiving checks, quarantine and release, lot and expiry tracking,
first-expiry-first-out handling, temperature-excursion review, complaint handling, corrective
action, and recall support. Displaying a supplier COA is one part of that process, not a
substitute for it. This description is of intended operating practice and is not a warranty.

## 7. If a document looks wrong

If a COA is missing, does not match your lot, appears altered, or conflicts with what you
received, do not use the product. Report it under the Product Concern and Adverse Event
Instructions (XR-COM-019) with the product name, SKU, lot number, order number, and photos of
the label. Verified quality issues are handled under the returns policy: affected product is
never restocked, and verified cases may receive full replacement or full refund, subject to
applicable law.

## 8. Legal posture

This notice does not limit rights you have under applicable law and does not relieve Xenios of
duties imposed by law, including duties about product quality and truthful claims. It is a
description of what displayed documents are, so that they are read accurately.
